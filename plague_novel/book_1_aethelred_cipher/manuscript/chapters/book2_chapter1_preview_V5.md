# CHAPTER 1 - THE BURNING

**November 9, 2019 — New Haven, Connecticut**
**1:47 AM**

---

Sarah smelled the accelerant before she saw the flames.

Not wood smoke. Not the honest smell of a fireplace or a campfire or anything that belonged in November. This was chemical — kerosene or something close to it, the kind of smell that bypassed seven years of graduate training and went straight to the part of her brain that still lived in caves and understood: *fire means death. Run.*

She ran toward it.

Linda Marsh's driveway cut through Connecticut woods — gravel and pine needles, dark except for the orange light bleeding through the trees ahead. Sarah's sneakers slipped on frozen ground. Her laptop bag hammered against her hip with each stride. Her breath came in white bursts that November shredded away.

She'd gotten Marsh's encrypted message eighteen hours ago: *Found something at GenVault. Genetic research. Wrong numbers. Need pattern analysis. Come NOW.*

Eighteen hours too slow.

Her phone buzzed in her pocket. She glanced at the screen without breaking stride: **Martin Reeves — FBI**. Her contact. The man who'd connected her with Marsh two weeks ago.

She silenced it. Martin could wait. Whatever was happening at the end of this driveway couldn't.

The trees opened.

The entire second floor was engulfed. Flames poured from every window — not the yellow of natural fire but the white-blue of something burning at temperatures a house shouldn't reach. Black smoke rolled across the front lawn in a low fog, thick enough to lose your feet in.

No fire trucks. Not yet. Just the house dying and Sarah standing at the edge of the driveway with nothing useful in her hands.

"MARSH!"

Her voice cracked against the roar. No answer. Just fire crackling and something deep inside the structure groaning — a sound like the house was trying to hold itself up and failing.

Sarah walked toward it.

(Every part of her body was screaming *stop.* Every part except the part that had gotten her through MIT and doctoral defense and six months of investigating GenVault — that part said *go.*)

The heat hit her at the front steps. Not warmth. Violence. A wall of it that evaporated the sweat on her face and made her skin feel tight, shrinking, about to split. Her eyes streamed. Her throat clamped shut against the smoke pouring through the open front door — blown outward by pressure or kicked in by whoever had been here first.

Sarah pulled her jacket over her mouth. Useless. The smoke found ways in. Down her throat. Into her lungs. Tasting like burning plastic and melting insulation and underneath it something sweet and organic that her brain refused to name because naming it meant admitting what was burning.

She dropped low where the smoke thinned from black to gray and went inside.

The entryway was an oven. Three feet of visibility. The floor was hot through her sneakers. Wallpaper curled from the walls in blackened strips. Above her, the ceiling blistered and bulged and made sounds that meant it was going to come down soon.

"MARSH! WHERE ARE YOU?"

Nothing.

Then — a shape. Ten feet from the door. On the floor.

A body. Face down. White hair matted dark. One arm stretched forward toward the door as if she'd been crawling. The other arm bent at her side at an angle that living people didn't make.

And beside her, clutched against her hip: a small metal box.

Marsh had been trying to reach the door. She'd been carrying the box and trying to get out and something had stopped her ten feet short.

"Linda." Sarah crawled to her. The heat pressed down on her back like a hand. "Linda, I'm getting you out."

She grabbed Marsh's outstretched arm. Gripped the forearm through the sleeve of her cardigan — wool, or something that had been wool before the heat transformed it.

Pulled.

The sleeve came apart in her hands.

Not tore. *Came apart.* The fabric had fused with the skin underneath, melted into it by heat until fabric and flesh were the same ruined thing, and when Sarah pulled, the sleeve separated from the arm like a glove being peeled from a hand that wasn't a hand anymore. What was underneath was blistered and split and wet and *wrong* — the texture of paper that had been soaked and left to dry and soaked again until it forgot it was ever solid.

Sarah dropped it.

The sound she made wasn't a scream. It was something before screaming — a short, involuntary noise from lungs that had stopped working. She fell backward. Caught herself on her hands. The floor was hot enough to feel through her palms.

(Move. The ceiling is coming down. Move or die in here with her.)

But her brain — the part that never shut off, the part that recognized patterns in data sets and genetic sequences and population models — was already processing what it had seen in the half-second between pulling and dropping.

A wound. Small. Neat. At the base of Marsh's skull. Entry wound.

The fire hadn't killed Linda Marsh.

Someone had put a bullet in her head. Then set the house on fire to burn the evidence.

Something cracked overhead — the sound of support beams giving up. A section of ceiling plaster crashed to the floor three feet away. Sparks and ember rain. The fire reaching down from the second floor like fingers closing.

Sarah's eyes went to the metal box beside Marsh's body. Fireproof. The size of a shoebox. Marsh had been carrying it toward the door when they shot her. Whatever was inside, she'd thought it was worth dying for.

(Worth dying *with.* She'd been trying to get it out.)

Sarah grabbed the box with both hands and ran.

Through the entryway. Through smoke so thick she navigated by memory — she'd been crawling toward the door, the door was behind her, she turned and ran. Her shins hit something. She stumbled. Kept moving. The front door was a rectangle of cold air and darkness and she threw herself through it as the ceiling behind her surrendered.

The collapse sounded like the end of something.

Sarah made it to the lawn. Fell. Rolled onto her back in wet grass. Coughed so hard her vision went white, then coughed again, then couldn't stop coughing. Her eyes streamed. Her lungs felt scraped. Smoke poured out of her in ragged breaths as if the fire had followed her out and was still burning inside her chest.

(The sleeve. The texture of it. The way the skin and the fabric had become the same thing. The way it peeled.)

(Stop.)

She vomited into the grass. Her hands were shaking — both of them, pressing into the earth, fingers digging into cold dirt while her stomach emptied itself.

(Don't think about what you just held.)

(She couldn't stop thinking about it.)

Sirens. Growing louder. Red and white lights flickering through the trees at the end of the driveway. Fire trucks arriving twenty minutes after the fire had won.

Sarah sat up. The metal box was beside her on the lawn. She didn't remember setting it down, but her hands must have dropped it when the coughing took over.

She picked it up. Held it against her chest.

Watched Linda Marsh's house collapse into itself. Sparks rose into the Connecticut sky and died. The second floor was gone. The first floor was going. Everything Marsh had spent thirty-seven years gathering — evidence, research, whatever she'd found at GenVault — was ash now.

Everything except what was in this box.

A paramedic appeared. Young. Efficient. "Ma'am? Were you inside? Do you need—"

"I heard screaming." Sarah's voice wasn't her own. Flat. Controlled. The voice of someone who had already decided to lie. "Thought someone was trapped."

The paramedic studied her — soot-covered face, streaming eyes, the particular look of someone who'd just been inside a burning building and wouldn't stop smelling it for days.

"We'll need you to—"

"I'll be over there." Sarah pointed vaguely and stood up. Walked toward the street. The paramedic let her go because the burning house was a bigger problem than one disoriented woman walking in the wrong direction.

At the street, Sarah stopped.

A sedan idled across the road. Black. Tinted windows. Engine running, lights off.

Just sitting there.

She stared at it. The sedan stared back — two headlights like closed eyes, exhaust curling into November dark.

Then it pulled away. Slow. Unhurried. Making sure she saw it leave. Making sure she understood: whoever was inside had watched the fire, watched her go in, watched her come out carrying the box.

Message delivered: *We know. We saw.*

The sedan turned the corner and was gone.

---

Sarah sat in her car three blocks away. Doors locked. Engine off. The metal box on the passenger seat.

Her hands wouldn't stop shaking.

She gripped the steering wheel. Squeezed until her knuckles went white. Held on like the car might move without her. The shaking traveled up her forearms, into her shoulders, into the place behind her sternum where breathing was supposed to happen automatically but wasn't.

(The sleeve. The way it separated.)

She let go of the wheel. Looked at her palms. Soot in the creases. Burns she hadn't noticed — two on the right hand, raw patches where she'd caught herself on the hot floor. She couldn't feel them yet. Shock was handling that.

The lockbox sat on the passenger seat where Marsh had died carrying it. A fireproof container with a simple clasp, latched but not locked — the hiding place had been the security, and Marsh hadn't had time to lock it because she'd been running.

Sarah reached for the clasp. Her fingers slipped. She wiped her hands on her jeans — soot smeared across denim — and tried again.

It opened.

Inside: a thumb drive. A folded note in careful handwriting. A photograph. A small iron key with a paper tag reading **YUL-1912-ARIS**.

She picked up the note first. Unfolded it with fingers that left black smudges on the paper.

*Sarah — The thumb drive has everything. Deployment schedules. Locations. The numbers are real. I've been gathering evidence for 37 years and this is what it comes down to. The key opens a box at Yale Sterling Library, Rare Books vault. Copies of everything they'll try to destroy. Find the pattern. Stop this. — M*

*Find the pattern.*

Marsh had known Sarah would come. Had prepared for this — backup evidence in a vault where fire couldn't reach, and a key she'd carried against her body while crawling toward the door with a bullet in her skull.

(The sleeve.)

(Stop.)

Sarah set down the note. Picked up the photograph.

Two men in a conference room. Marsh had written names on the back in the same careful handwriting:

- **Dr. James Morrison** — GenVault Director of Genetic Research
- **Dr. David Lagos** — Lead Research Coordinator

Smiling. Conference table between them. A photograph that said *colleagues* — or *conspirators.* Marsh had kept it in a fireproof box with evidence of something worth killing for.

Her phone buzzed again. Martin. Third time since the driveway.

She stared at his name on the screen. Martin Reeves. FBI. She trusted Martin — but right now she couldn't form words. Her throat was raw from smoke and she'd have to explain what had happened inside that house and she couldn't. Not yet.

She declined the call. Texted: **At Marsh's. Too late. She's dead. I'm OK. Will call tomorrow.**

Then she plugged the thumb drive into her laptop.

Files loaded. Dozens. Research documents. Genetic distribution maps. Population data organized by region. A folder labeled **THRESHOLD — Deployment Targets**.

She opened it.

Maps. Forty-seven locations marked in red. Casualty projections beside each one — numbers so large they stopped meaning what numbers are supposed to mean. They looked like coordinates. Like data points in a model. Not like human lives.

She scrolled. The numbers kept growing.

The document header read: **THRESHOLD Protocol — Population Optimization Through Targeted Genetic Selection.**

*Population optimization.*

Sarah closed the laptop.

The car was quiet. Three blocks away, fire trucks pumped water into a structure already lost. Red and white lights pulsed through the trees. Someone was dead inside that structure and no one was coming to save her because the fire had already won and the people who'd set it were driving away in a black sedan.

(The sleeve. The texture of paper that had been soaked and left to dry and soaked again. The way it peeled from the arm like it was the most natural thing in the world, like skin was meant to separate from muscle, like the human body was just layers that came apart when you pulled.)

Sarah opened the car door and vomited onto the asphalt.

Closed the door. Wiped her mouth. Sat in the dark breathing through her nose until the shaking slowed to something she could work around.

She opened the laptop again.

*Population optimization through targeted genetic selection.* Forty-seven locations. Casualty projections in the billions. Deployment schedules dated three weeks from now.

This was what Marsh had found. This was what the bullet was for. This was what the fire was meant to destroy.

*Find the pattern. Stop this.*

Sarah looked at the iron key on the passenger seat. Old. Heavy. The kind of key that opened something that had been locked for a long time.

**YUL-1912-ARIS.**

YUL. Yale University Library.

Yale was ten minutes from where she sat.

Whatever Marsh had locked in that vault — whatever she'd duplicated and hidden where fire couldn't reach — was waiting. Had been waiting. And Marsh had trusted Sarah to find it because Marsh had spent thirty-seven years gathering evidence and she'd chosen Sarah's name for the note and Sarah's skills for the pattern and Sarah's key for the lock.

Sarah closed the laptop. Put the photograph and the note back in the lockbox. Kept the thumb drive and the key.

Started the car.

She didn't call Martin back. Didn't call anyone.

The drive took eight minutes. November streets. Empty and dark. Her headlights cutting through the kind of quiet that only existed at 2 AM in a college town between semesters.

Sarah's hands shook on the steering wheel the entire way.

She parked two blocks from Sterling Memorial Library. Got out. The November air hit her lungs like a slap — cold and clean after the smoke, and her body wanted to stand there breathing it forever, but there was a key in her pocket and a dead woman's evidence in a vault and the sedan's headlights were still behind her eyelids every time she blinked.

She walked toward the library.

Behind her, three blocks away, Linda Marsh's house was still burning.

**END CHAPTER 1**
