# Chapter 3: The Seizure (1455)

**Location:** Mainz, Holy Roman Empire
**Timeline:** November 1455 - The transfer of power
**POV Characters:** Johann Fust (Gen 114), Johannes Gutenberg, Peter Schöffer (Gen 115)

---

## The Mainz Court, November 6, 1455

The hearing lasted forty-seven minutes.

Johann Fust stood before the arbitrator—a commercial magistrate named Heinrich Günther who specialized in debt disputes—and presented documents proving that Johannes Gutenberg owed sixteen hundred gulden plus five years of accumulated interest. The contracts were clear, properly witnessed, legally binding. The collateral terms were explicit: failure to repay by November 1455 resulted in workshop forfeiture.

Gutenberg's lawyer argued technical points about interest calculation, about whether production delays constituted force majeure, about the reasonableness of the repayment timeline. The arguments were competent but ultimately irrelevant. Contract law was contract law.

"The debt is valid," Magistrate Günther ruled. "The collateral terms are enforceable. Herr Gutenberg has failed to repay the principal plus interest within the specified timeline. Therefore, ownership of the printing workshop—including all equipment, inventory, and associated intellectual property—transfers to Herr Fust effective immediately."

The gavel fell.

Johannes Gutenberg sat motionless, his life's work legally seized.

"However," Günther continued, "the court recognizes Herr Gutenberg's contributions to the enterprise. We order Herr Fust to employ Herr Gutenberg as master printer for a minimum of three years at fair wages, with the option to extend employment as both parties agree."

A consolation prize. Employment instead of ownership.

"Additionally," Günther said, consulting his notes, "Herr Gutenberg retains personal ownership of experimental equipment not financed by the loans—specifically, the smaller press used for initial type development and any personal tools predating the 1450 investment."

Gutenberg looked up at that. The experimental press was where he'd perfected movable type, where he'd solved the ink formulation problems, where he'd developed the techniques that made the Bible possible. It wasn't worth much commercially—old equipment, outdated technology—but it had sentimental value.

"Court is adjourned," Günther announced.

Fust collected his documents, carefully avoiding eye contact with Gutenberg. This was a victory, but not one to celebrate publicly. The defensive network had secured control of printing technology, but at the cost of alienating the man who'd invented it.

Strategic necessity. Personal cost.

Outside the courthouse, Peter Schöffer waited with a hired cart.

"It's done?" he asked quietly.

"It's done." Fust climbed into the cart. "The workshop is ours. Gutenberg stays on as employed master printer. We maintain continuity while gaining operational control."

"How did he take it?"

"Like a man who just lost everything." Fust's voice was hollow. "Which is accurate."

They rode in silence toward the workshop—now legally Fust's property, the foundation of a printing empire that would reshape European civilization. The cart wheels rattled over cobblestones, the November cold biting through winter cloaks.

"Did we have to do it this way?" Peter finally asked. "The loans, the foreclosure, the public humiliation?"

"Yes." Fust's certainty came from three thousand years of defensive network experience. "Because gradual transfer doesn't work. If I'd proposed partnership, Gutenberg would have negotiated for years. If I'd offered to buy him out, he'd have demanded impossible prices. If I'd asked him to voluntarily train master printers, he'd have delayed and perfectionist everything to death."

He paused. "The defensive network learned millennia ago that sometimes you can't negotiate with individuals. Sometimes you have to *act*—decisively, legally, irreversibly—and accept that people will hate you for it. The mission matters more than being liked."

"Does it?" Peter's voice carried doubt. "We just destroyed the man who gave us printing. Made him a hired employee in his own workshop. History will remember Gutenberg as the inventor and you as the banker who stole his invention."

"I know." Fust's acceptance was complete. "And I'm fine with that. Because in five hundred years, historians will study Gutenberg's technical brilliance. They'll admire the Bible he printed. They'll call him a genius. And they'll call me a ruthless financier who exploited that genius for profit."

"Doesn't that bother you?"

"Not at all." Fust smiled grimly. "Because those historians will be wrong about my motives. They'll think I wanted money. They'll never realize I wanted *distribution*. They'll study Gutenberg's invention without understanding that the defensive network turned that invention into a weapon against information monopoly."

The cart turned onto the street where the workshop stood—three stories of brick and timber, smoke rising from the furnace where type metal was being cast, the rhythmic thump of presses audible from outside.

"We're the villains in Gutenberg's story," Fust continued. "And that's acceptable. Because we're the heroes in a longer story he'll never know about. The story that starts with Tausret three thousand years ago and continues through Sarah Chen five hundred years from now. Our reputation in 1455 matters less than the Reformation in 1517."

Peter absorbed this. "Seven-generation thinking."

"Exactly." Fust climbed down from the cart. "Now let's make sure the workshop continues operating smoothly. Master printers are scheduled to arrive next week for advanced training. We can't afford disruption just because ownership changed."

They entered the workshop, where six apprentices were printing a psalter—the next commercial project after the Bible. The work continued regardless of legal proceedings, regardless of who owned the equipment, regardless of personal feelings.

Printing didn't care about drama. It just distributed information.

## The Workshop Office, November 7, 1455

Johannes Gutenberg arrived early—before dawn, before the apprentices, before anyone else could witness his arrival at a workshop he no longer owned.

Fust was already there, reviewing accounts by candlelight.

"You don't have to hide," Fust said quietly, not looking up from the ledgers. "The apprentices know about yesterday's ruling. They'll adjust."

"I wasn't hiding." Gutenberg's voice was carefully neutral. "I was checking the type casting furnace. Temperature needs to be consistent for quality."

"You're still master printer," Fust reminded him. "Technical operations remain your authority. Nothing changes except who signs the contracts and manages finances."

"Everything changes." Gutenberg finally entered the office, closing the door behind him. "I built this. Every innovation, every solution, every technical breakthrough—that was my work. And now it's yours."

"It was always going to be the world's," Fust said. "The only question was whether one man controlled it or whether it became an industry. I chose industry."

Gutenberg leaned against the doorframe. "Did you ever intend to let me repay the loans?"

Fust considered lying, decided truth was more respectful. "No. The repayment timeline was deliberately impossible. You would have needed to liquidate all assets, stop production, and abandon future projects to raise sixteen hundred gulden. Which would have defeated the purpose of the investment."

"So it was always seizure. From the beginning."

"It was always controlled transfer of technology," Fust corrected. "Seizure is the legal mechanism. But the goal was never to take your invention away from you—it was to take it away from exclusive control. Make it too distributed to suppress, too valuable to destroy, too useful to ban."

He gestured at the workshop visible through the office window. "In your hands, printing would have remained a craft. Beautiful, precise, limited. You'd spend forty years perfecting technique, training maybe ten apprentices who'd maintain your standards. By the time you died, there might be five workshops in Europe producing exquisite work at slow pace."

"And in your hands?"

"By the time I die in 1480, there will be eighty workshops. By 1500, two hundred. By 1517, five hundred—producing materials so quickly, so cheaply, so ubiquitously that information control becomes impossible." Fust met Gutenberg's gaze directly. "That's the difference between craft and industry. Between art and weapon."

"You keep calling it a weapon."

"Because that's what it is." Fust stood, moved to the window overlooking the main workshop floor. "Johannes, the defensive network has been preserving knowledge for three thousand years. We've watched libraries burn, manuscripts destroyed, entire civilizations lose their collected wisdom. We've seen how power structures maintain control through information monopoly—by deciding who learns to read, what books exist, which ideas circulate."

He paused. "Printing ends that monopoly. Not immediately. Not obviously. But inevitably. Because once books become cheap enough, fast enough, distributed enough, censorship becomes impossible. Burn one copy, and a hundred copies remain. Ban one printer, and ten others continue. Arrest one distributor, and the underground market flourishes."

"The Church won't tolerate that."

"The Church will try to prevent it," Fust agreed. "They'll establish censorship boards, create *Index Librorum Prohibitorum* lists of banned books, threaten printers with excommunication or worse. And they'll fail. Because by the time they realize printing is a threat, it'll be too embedded in the economic system to eliminate."

Gutenberg processed this. "How long?"

"Until what?"

"Until someone uses printing to openly challenge Church authority? To print heretical materials? To distribute banned ideas?"

"Sixty-two years," Fust said. "1517. A monk in Wittenberg will nail ninety-five theses to a church door criticizing papal indulgences. Within three months, three hundred thousand printed copies will circulate across Europe. The Church will try to suppress it. They'll excommunicate the monk, ban the theses, arrest printers. And they'll fail completely."

Gutenberg stared. "That's... very specific."

"The defensive network thinks in generations," Fust said simply. "We plan sixty years ahead the way other people plan six months ahead. 1517 isn't a guess—it's a calculated convergence point. The printing infrastructure we're building now matures by then. The reading public we're cultivating grows large enough. The economic incentives align correctly. And somewhere, someone will have both the courage and the timing to weaponize printing against ecclesiastical corruption."

"You can't know that happens."

"I know it *can* happen," Fust corrected. "And we're making sure the infrastructure exists when the moment arrives. Planting trees whose shade we'll never see."

Silence filled the office. Outside, dawn was breaking—gray November light illuminating the workshop where six apprentices would soon arrive to continue printing work, indifferent to yesterday's legal drama.

"Three hundred gulden annually," Gutenberg said finally. "You promised that in writing."

"I have the contract ready." Fust pulled out the employment agreement. "Three hundred gulden per year, paid quarterly. Housing allowance. Workshop access. Authority over all technical decisions. Renewable annually as long as both parties agree."

"And if I train the master printers you want? If I cooperate with the expansion strategy?"

"Then you become the most respected printer in Europe," Fust said. "The standard against which all others measure themselves. Your techniques adopted everywhere. Your name synonymous with quality. Not ownership, Johannes—but legacy."

Gutenberg took the contract, read it carefully. The terms were generous by any standard. More than a master craftsman typically earned. Security, respect, authority over his domain.

Everything except control.

"I want one addition," he said finally.

"Name it."

"The experimental press—the small one I keep as personal equipment per the court's ruling. I want that documented as mine permanently. Not workshop property. Not subject to future seizure or sale. Mine until I die, then passed to my designated heir."

"Agreed." Fust made the notation immediately. "The experimental press remains your personal property in perpetuity. You can will it to anyone you choose."

"Then I'll sign." Gutenberg accepted the pen. "And I'll train your master printers. And I'll help build your printing empire. Because you're right about one thing—printing matters more than pride."

He signed with a steady hand despite everything.

"Welcome to the long war," Fust said quietly. "Population: everyone who ever thought knowledge should be free."

## The Master Printer Meeting, November 12, 1455

Six men sat around the workshop's largest table, studying the contracts Fust had prepared.

They were the future of European printing: Konrad Fyner (age 28, heading to Esslingen), Johannes Mentelin (age 31, heading to Strasbourg), Günther Zainer (age 26, heading to Augsburg), Arnold Therhoernen (age 29, heading to Cologne), Heinrich Eggestein (age 24, staying in Mainz), and Georg Husner (age 27, heading to Strasbourg).

"Ten percent of gross revenues for seven years," Fyner said slowly, reading the licensing terms. "In exchange for equipment, training, type molds, and ongoing technical support."

"Below market rate," Fust confirmed. "A commercial printer's guild would charge twenty to thirty percent. We're offering better terms because we want rapid expansion, not maximum profit extraction."

"What's the catch?" Mentelin asked suspiciously.

"No catch," Peter Schöffer said. He'd been designated the technical liaison—the one who'd train these men and maintain relationships going forward. "Herr Fust wants a printing network, not a monopoly. Your success benefits everyone by normalizing printing, increasing demand, and making the technology indispensable."

"But why give us favorable terms?" Husner pressed. "Why not charge full market rate and maximize your return?"

"Because," Fust said carefully, "I'm not interested in personal wealth. I'm interested in distributing printing technology as widely and quickly as possible. The ten percent revenue share covers our equipment costs and provides modest return. More important, it keeps you solvent—able to invest in your own expansion, train your own apprentices, open additional workshops."

He gestured at the contracts. "Think of this as... coordinated capitalism. We're all building the same industry. Competition happens on quality and service, not on crushing each other financially. The real competition is against manuscript production—proving that printing is superior in speed, cost, and reliability."

"What about the Church?" Therhoernen asked. As the designated religious printer, he'd be most exposed to ecclesiastical oversight. "The licensing requirements? The censorship concerns?"

"Short term, cooperate fully," Fust said. "Print what they approve. Submit proofs for review. Maintain impeccable theological correctness. Make them depend on printing for their own operations—Bibles, missals, papal bulls, theological treatises. Become indispensable."

"And long term?"

"Long term, the infrastructure becomes too mature to restrict," Fust said. "But that's twenty to thirty years away. For now, focus on building quality, reputation, and market share. The censorship battles come later, fought by the next generation."

Eggestein, who'd been silent until now, spoke up. "You keep talking about generations. About long-term strategy. About making printing indispensable before revealing its full potential. That's not how businessmen think."

"I'm not a businessman," Fust said simply. "I'm a custodian. The defensive network preserves knowledge across civilizations. Printing is just our latest tool."

He saw their confused expressions and shifted tactics. "Let me put it differently. You six men are founding a new craft guild—the printers' guild. In twenty years, that guild will have hundreds of members. In fifty years, thousands. You're not just starting businesses—you're establishing an entirely new economic sector."

"The Guild of Saint John," Mentelin said suddenly. "That's what we should call it. Patron saint of writers."

"Perfect," Fust agreed. "The Guild of Saint John. Organized not to restrict membership like traditional guilds, but to spread knowledge. To maintain technical standards, share innovations, protect each other from political pressure."

"A defensive guild," Fyner said thoughtfully. "Protecting printing from those who'd suppress it."

"Exactly." Fust smiled at the quick understanding. "Traditional guilds protect markets by limiting competition. The Guild of Saint John protects markets by expanding them—making printing so common, so valuable, so embedded in society that restriction becomes impossible."

Zainer leaned back in his chair. "When do we start?"

"Equipment ships next week," Peter said. "Each of you receives two presses, complete type-casting equipment, six months of paper and vellum, technical manuals, and my personal assistance for the first three months of operations. After that, you're autonomous—but we maintain regular communication, share technical innovations, coordinate on major projects."

"First projects?" Therhoernen asked.

"Religious texts," Fust said firmly. "Psalters, prayer books, biblical commentaries. Prove to the Church that printing serves their mission. Build that relationship. Earn their trust. Later—much later—we print other content."

"What other content?" Husner asked.

Fust exchanged glances with Peter. How much to reveal?

"Classical texts," Peter said carefully. "Greek and Roman works. Mathematical treatises. Scientific observations. Historical chronicles. Eventually, contemporary scholarship that challenges received wisdom."

"Heresy?" Mentelin's voice carried alarm.

"Truth," Fust corrected. "There's a difference. Heresy is deliberate theological deviation. Truth is accurate reporting of reality, even when that reality contradicts official doctrine. Over the next fifty years, printing will force the Church to choose: adapt to truth, or fight technology. We're betting they'll adapt."

"And if they don't?"

"Then they lose authority," Fust said bluntly. "Because printing makes truth unstoppable. You can burn books, but you can't unprint ideas. The Reformation will happen whether Church authorities like it or not. Our job is making sure the infrastructure exists to support it."

The six master printers processed this—the audacity of planning sixty years ahead, the confidence that printing would prevail, the assumption that ecclesiastical power structures would eventually bend to technology.

"I'm in," Fyner said, signing his contract. "Esslingen needs a printer."

"Strasbourg," Mentelin agreed, signing.

One by one, they committed: Zainer to Augsburg, Therhoernen to Cologne, Eggestein to Mainz, Husner to Strasbourg.

Six workshops. Six master printers. Six nodes in a network that would grow to hundreds within a generation.

"The Guild of Saint John," Fust said formally, "is hereby established. May your presses never stop, your type never wear out, and your ink never dry."

"Until knowledge is free," Peter added—a motto he'd composed the night before.

"Until knowledge is free," they repeated.

The defensive network had just recruited its printing arm.

## The Experimental Press, December 1455

Late at night, after the apprentices had left and the workshop had fallen silent, Johannes Gutenberg worked alone at the small experimental press he'd retained as personal property.

Fust found him there, setting type by candlelight.

"What are you printing?" Fust asked quietly.

Gutenberg didn't look up. "Something that's mine. Not contracted, not licensed, not owned by anyone but me."

Fust stepped closer, studied the type forms. Latin text—not scripture, not prayer book, not approved religious content. Something else.

"Is that..." he squinted at the text "...Cicero? *De Officiis*?"

"On Duties," Gutenberg confirmed. "A philosophical treatise about moral obligation, about choosing between competing goods, about doing what's necessary rather than what's comfortable." His voice carried bitter irony. "Seemed appropriate."

Fust watched him work—hands moving with practiced precision, each letter placed exactly, the compositor's skill unchanged despite the workshop seizure.

"You hate me," Fust said. Not a question.

"I understand you," Gutenberg corrected. "Which might be worse. If you were just a greedy banker, I could hate cleanly. But you're right about distribution strategy. Right about the printing network. Right that my perfectionism would have limited the technology's spread."

He finished setting a line, moved to the next. "That doesn't make the theft less painful. Just makes it more rational."

"It wasn't theft."

"It was legal theft," Gutenberg said. "Which is worse—theft that pretends to be justice. You engineered impossible loan terms, foreclosed on schedule, and took everything I built. The fact that courts approved it doesn't make it ethical."

"No," Fust agreed. "It doesn't. But ethics and effectiveness aren't always aligned. The defensive network learned three thousand years ago that sometimes you have to choose between being good and being effective. We chose effectiveness."

"How noble." Gutenberg's sarcasm was sharp. "Sacrificing your ethics for the greater good."

"Sacrificing personal relationships for long-term mission," Fust corrected. "There's a difference. I'm not claiming moral superiority. I'm claiming strategic necessity. What I did to you was wrong on a personal level and right on a strategic level. Both things are true simultaneously."

Gutenberg finally looked up. "Why are you here, Johann? To gloat? To justify yourself? To make sure I'm not sabotaging equipment?"

"To understand what you're printing," Fust said honestly. "And to make sure you're all right."

"I'm printing Cicero because it reminds me that people have been wrestling with moral compromise for two thousand years." Gutenberg returned to the type. "And I'm fine. Employed, housed, fed, respected in my technical authority. Everything except free."

"You're more free now than before," Fust said quietly. "Before, you were imprisoned by debt, by production pressure, by the need to make commercial success happen. Now you're paid regardless of sales, supported regardless of profit, free to perfect technique without worrying about revenue."

"Free to be your employee."

"Free to be the greatest printer in history," Fust corrected. "Without the burden of being a successful businessman. Which you're terrible at, incidentally."

Despite everything, Gutenberg almost smiled. "I know. If you hadn't seized the workshop, I'd have bankrupted it within three years."

"Within two," Fust said. "You'd have poured everything into a second Bible edition with marginal quality improvements that no customer could detect. Beautiful work, financial disaster."

Gutenberg pulled the completed form from the type bed, studied it critically. "Do you ever feel guilty? About the seizure, the foreclosure, taking away someone's life work?"

"Every day," Fust said honestly. "But guilt is the price of effectiveness. The defensive network taught me that you pay for strategic success with personal cost. I destroyed your ownership to save printing's future. That was the correct choice. It was also cruel. Both truths coexist."

He paused. "In five hundred years, historians will study this moment. They'll debate whether I was a villain or a visionary. Whether you were a victim or a collaborator. And they'll miss the point entirely—which is that printing mattered more than either of our feelings about it."

Gutenberg began inking the form. "What are you printing next? After the master printers leave?"

"Peter wants to do Virgil's *Aeneid*," Fust said. "I'm pushing for Ptolemy's *Geography*—more strategically useful for navigation. We'll probably compromise with both."

"Navigation," Gutenberg said thoughtfully. "Planning for exploration? For the voyage west that everyone's discussing?"

"Planning for knowledge that enables exploration," Fust corrected. "Columbus will sail whether we print Ptolemy or not. But if we distribute geographic knowledge widely, more people can verify his findings, challenge his assumptions, build on his discoveries. That's the real revolution—not the voyage itself, but democratizing the knowledge that enables many voyages."

Gutenberg pulled a proof print—the first page of *De Officiis*, perfectly formed, exactly as Cicero had written it two thousand years earlier. "How long do you think printing survives? Before something replaces it?"

"Four hundred years minimum," Fust said. "Maybe five hundred. Eventually, some new technology will emerge—I don't know what. Something faster, cheaper, more distributable than printing. But that's twenty generations away. Our job is perfecting printing for the next five centuries."

"And then?"

"And then that generation's defensive network carriers figure out how to preserve knowledge using whatever technology comes next," Fust said. "That's the chain. Tausret preserved knowledge during the Bronze Age Collapse. Maryam encoded it in architecture. We're distributing it through printing. Someone in the twenty-first century will use technologies we can't imagine. The method changes. The mission continues."

Gutenberg hung the proof to dry. "I'm printing fifty copies of *De Officiis*. Small editions, given to people I choose. Not for sale. Not licensed. Mine."

"Good," Fust said. "Everyone should have something that's purely theirs. Legacy beyond commerce."

He moved toward the door, then paused. "Johannes? Thank you. For inventing movable type. For perfecting the press. For teaching the next generation. History will remember you as the innovator. I'm content being remembered as the banker who recognized innovation's value."

"You're more than that," Gutenberg said quietly. "But you'll let people think you're less. Because being underestimated serves your purposes."

"Exactly," Fust agreed. "The defensive network's oldest trick—hide in plain sight while changing the world."

He left Gutenberg to his private printing, to his personal project, to the small space of autonomy that remained after the seizure.

Outside, December cold settled over Mainz. The workshop stood silent, its three presses temporarily idle, waiting for morning and the return of apprentices and the continuation of work that didn't care about ownership disputes or personal feelings or strategic necessity.

Printing just was. Just distributed information. Just made knowledge unstoppable.

And that was enough.

## Historical Notes

**The Mainz Court Ruling (November 6, 1455):**
- Historically documented: Fust sued Gutenberg for unpaid loans
- Court ruled in Fust's favor, transferring workshop ownership
- Gutenberg retained personal equipment including experimental press
- Terms closely match historical record

**Johannes Gutenberg (1398-1468):**
- Did lose control of his workshop to Fust in 1455
- Continued working as a printer, but never regained financial independence
- Retained the experimental press used for initial type development
- Novel's framing (defensive network strategy) is speculative fiction
- His personal feelings about the seizure are historically undocumented but logically inferred

**Johann Fust (1400-1466):**
- Did finance Gutenberg with two loans (800 gulden each)
- Did foreclose and take ownership of the workshop
- Historical reputation: ruthless financier who exploited Gutenberg's genius
- Novel's motivation (defensive network mission) is speculative fiction
- Whether foreclosure was planned from the beginning is unknown but plausible

**Peter Schöffer (1425-1503):**
- Real historical figure who became Fust's son-in-law
- Did manage the workshop after Gutenberg's displacement
- Became one of Europe's leading printers
- Known for technical innovations and business acumen
- Novel's blood-memory carrier status is speculative fiction

**The Master Printers:**
- All real historical figures who established early printing workshops
- Konrad Fyner (Esslingen), Johannes Mentelin (Strasbourg), Günther Zainer (Augsburg), Arnold Therhoernen (Cologne), Heinrich Eggestein (Mainz), Georg Husner (Strasbourg)
- Whether they trained under Gutenberg/Fust is documented for some, inferred for others
- "Guild of Saint John" is a fictional creation—early printers did not form unified guilds immediately

**Printing Expansion Timeline:**
- 1455-1460: Printing spreads to major German cities
- 1460-1470: Expansion across Holy Roman Empire
- 1470-1480: Rapid proliferation throughout Europe
- By 1500: Over 200 printing centers operating
- Novel's coordinated expansion strategy is speculative overlay on historical pattern

**Church and Censorship:**
- Church authorities did initially welcome printing for religious texts
- Censorship efforts began early (1470s-1480s) but proved increasingly ineffective
- *Index Librorum Prohibitorum* (Index of Forbidden Books) established 1559
- Irony: Technology approved for religious purposes enabled Reformation critique
- Novel's "sixty-two years to 1517" calculation is speculative but historically accurate

**Cicero's *De Officiis*:**
- "On Duties" - philosophical treatise on moral obligations
- Was among the first classical texts printed (1460s-1470s)
- Whether Gutenberg personally printed it on his experimental press is fictional
- Thematically appropriate: deals with moral compromise and competing obligations

**Strategic Necessity vs. Personal Ethics:**
- The novel's framing examines how strategic goals sometimes require personal costs
- Defensive network carriers choosing mission over relationships
- Historical pattern: technological revolutions often involve disputed ownership (Edison/Tesla, Facebook co-founders, Apple/Xerox, etc.)
- Whether Fust felt guilt is undocumented—novel presents psychologically plausible interpretation

**Generation Tracking:**
- Johann Fust: Generation ~114 (born ~1400 CE)
- Peter Schöffer: Generation 115 (born ~1425 CE)
