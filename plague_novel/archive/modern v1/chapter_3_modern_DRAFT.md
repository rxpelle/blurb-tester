# CHAPTER 3 - THE FACILITY (Modern Timeline - Present Day)

## Scene 1: The Facility

### Unknown Location - Time Unknown

Sarah woke to white.

White ceiling. White walls. White sheets on a bed that was too comfortable to be a prison but too institutional to be a hotel.

Her head felt thick, stuffed with cotton. Drugged. They'd drugged her during transport.

She sat up slowly, fighting vertigo. The room swam, then steadied.

Small. Maybe ten by twelve feet. A bed, a desk, a chair, a door. A bathroom visible through an open doorway—sink, toilet, shower. Everything clean, modern, sterile.

No windows.

Sarah's pulse spiked. She stumbled to the door, tried the handle.

Locked from the outside.

She was in a cell. A comfortable cell, but a cell nonetheless.

*How long have I been unconscious?*

She checked herself. Still wearing the clothes from the cabin—jeans, hoodie. No watch. No phone. Her hands were clean, unmarked. They hadn't hurt her. Just transported her somewhere while she was unconscious and locked her in a comfortable room.

Sarah's mind raced through possibilities. What time was it? Had midnight passed? Had her document released? Was David alive?

She went to the bathroom, splashed cold water on her face. Looked at herself in the mirror.

Same face. Same dark circles under her eyes. Same woman who'd spent four days writing an exposé to save the world.

But was the world even saveable?

Back in the main room, she examined every surface for cameras. Found them immediately—not hidden, installed openly in the corners. They wanted her to know she was being watched.

Fine. Let them watch.

Sarah sat at the desk. On it: fresh clothes in her size (how did they know?), toiletries, a tray with food covered by a metal dome.

And a folder.

Manila folder, unlabeled. Thick.

She shouldn't open it. It was obviously bait. They wanted her to look.

She looked anyway.

Inside: her complete family tree.

Twelve generations, printed on connected pages that unfolded like an accordion. Names, dates, locations. Her great-great-great-grandmother born in Guangdong, 1847. Her great-great-grandfather immigrating to California, 1869. Each generation carefully documented.

But that wasn't the disturbing part.

The disturbing part was the annotations.

Next to each name, in small precise handwriting, were notes:

*Chen Wei-Lin (1847-1893) - Pattern recognition: High. Spatial memory: Exceptional. Selected for breeding program.*

*Chen Huang (1869-1941) - Analytical processing: Superior. Mathematical aptitude: 98th percentile. Paired with Zhang Li-Mei (compatible genetic markers).*

*Chen Robert (1923-1987) - All target cognitive traits present. Optimal pairing: Chen-Liu bloodline merger.*

On and on, generation after generation. Every marriage carefully noted. Every child's traits evaluated. Branches of the family tree that "failed to produce desired markers" marked with red lines—terminated.

Sarah's hands shook as she followed the tree forward to her own generation.

*Chen Sarah (1991-) - Pattern recognition: Exceptional. Spatial reasoning: 99th percentile. Eidetic memory markers: Present. Analytical processing: Superior.*

*OPTIMAL OUTCOME. All target traits successfully concentrated after 12 generations of guided breeding.*

She was going to be sick.

Sarah dropped the folder, stumbled to the bathroom, bent over the toilet. Nothing came up but bile—she hadn't eaten in who knew how long.

She was a product. A designed outcome. Twelve generations of controlled breeding had produced her the way you'd breed dogs for specific traits.

Her mother hadn't chosen her father because she loved him—or maybe she had, but the love itself had been guided. Put in the same graduate program, the same lab, given opportunities to meet and collaborate until attraction became inevitable.

Even Sarah's own choices—MIT, genetics, the specific PhD program—had they been her choices at all? Or had she been subtly guided her entire life toward exactly this field, this knowledge, this moment?

*Is anything about me real?*

A knock at the door.

Sarah wiped her mouth, stood on shaking legs.

The door opened.

Dr. Catherine Wells entered, looking exactly as polished as she had at the Vermont cabin. Expensive suit, perfect hair, calm expression.

"Good morning, Dr. Chen. I see you've been reading your file."

## Scene 2: Wells's Offer - The Interrogation

Sarah wanted to attack her. Wanted to scream. Wanted to do something, anything violent.

Instead she said, "What time is it?"

Wells smiled. "Eleven AM. You've been unconscious for approximately eighteen hours."

Past midnight. The document had released. Or should have.

"Where's David?"

"Your cousin is at home. Probably sleeping off the mild sedative we administered. He's fine, Dr. Chen. We're not monsters."

"You're eugenicists. You've been breeding humans like livestock for generations."

"We've been carefully guiding human evolution, yes." Wells sat in the chair, gestured for Sarah to sit on the bed. Sarah remained standing. "But isn't that what we've always done? Farmers select the best crops. Breeders select the best animals. Why should humans be different?"

"Because humans aren't crops."

"Aren't we?" Wells pulled out a tablet. "Let me show you something."

She brought up a graph. Global temperature over time. "Climate change. Accelerating beyond our ability to control it." Another graph. "Antibiotic resistance. Pandemic potential increasing yearly." Another. "Resource depletion. Water scarcity. Topsoil degradation."

Graph after graph, each one showing a world approaching multiple catastrophic tipping points.

"We're heading toward collapse, Dr. Chen. Not in centuries. In years. Maybe months." Wells set the tablet down. "Do you know what happens when civilizations collapse?"

"People die," Sarah said flatly.

"Ninety percent or more. That's the historical average. Bronze Age collapse: 90% population reduction. Rome: 80-90%. Mayan civilization: nearly total." Wells's voice was calm, clinical. "But some survive. The question is: which ones?"

Sarah said nothing.

"Left to chance, the survivors are random. Luck, location, resources. But guided..." Wells leaned forward. "Guided, we can ensure the survivors are the best of us. The smartest, strongest, most adaptable. The ones who can rebuild civilization instead of descending into barbarism."

"That's genocide."

"That's pragmatism." Wells stood, walked to where Sarah was standing. "Your document released last night. At midnight, exactly as planned."

Sarah's breath caught. "It did?"

"Yes. Would you like to see the results?" Wells offered the tablet.

Sarah took it with trembling hands. Searched for her exposé.

Found it immediately. Dozens of articles.

"Disturbed Scientist's Conspiracy Theory Goes Viral"

"GenVault Addresses Baseless Accusations"

"Former Employee's Mental Health Crisis Raises Questions"

Her carefully sourced, meticulously documented exposé was being treated as the ravings of a paranoid conspiracy theorist.

Major outlets: dismissing it outright or treating it as entertainment.

Independent journalists: some tried to verify, but within hours they'd posted retractions, apologies, statements that they'd been misled.

Her evidence, her proof, everything Marsh had died for—dismissed, discredited, buried.

"How?" Sarah whispered.

"Seventy years of preparation, Dr. Chen. We have board members at every major publication. Legal teams ready to bury anyone who publishes in litigation. Relationships with fact-checkers and verification services. And most importantly, we control enough of the media landscape that we can shape the narrative."

Wells took the tablet back. "But let's say, hypothetically, that you'd succeeded. Let's say the public believed you. What then?"

"They'd stop you."

"How? Shut down seven major corporations employing millions? Arrest everyone involved? Undo generations of genetic guidance?" Wells shook her head. "The work is done, Dr. Chen. Has been for decades. The bloodlines are already positioned. The genetic markers are already distributed through target populations. Even if you exposed us completely, you couldn't stop what's coming."

"The collapse," Sarah said.

"The collapse." Wells confirmed. "Within six months, a series of cascading failures will begin. Climate disaster triggers food shortage triggers economic collapse triggers pandemic triggers war. The dominoes are already falling. We didn't start this—we're just ensuring controlled outcomes."

Sarah's mind was reeling. "You're going to let billions die."

"We're going to ensure millions survive. The right millions." Wells's voice was gentle, almost kind. "Dr. Chen—Sarah. You're angry. I understand. You feel manipulated, violated, robbed of free will. Those feelings are valid."

"But?"

"But consider what you are. Not just your genes—your mind. Your capacity for pattern recognition. Your drive to find truth. Your unwillingness to accept comfortable lies." Wells smiled. "Those traits aren't weaknesses. They're gifts. Gifts from thirty generations of careful selection. You exist because we helped ensure the right genetic combinations would occur."

"I exist because my ancestors were bred like horses."

"You exist because we valued intelligence over chance. Would you prefer to not exist? To not have your mind, your capabilities?"

It was a trap question. Sarah knew it was a trap.

But she couldn't answer.

"I'm offering you a choice," Wells said. "Join us. Help complete the work. Use your gifts to help select and position the next generation. When the collapse comes—and it is coming—you and your family will be protected. You'll survive. You'll help rebuild."

"And if I refuse?"

"Then you're a disturbed former employee who stole corporate data and descended into paranoid delusions. We'll keep you here—comfortable, safe, but contained—until the collapse renders your resistance irrelevant." Wells walked to the door. "You have twenty-four hours to decide, Dr. Chen. I suggest you use them wisely."

The door closed. Locks engaged.

Sarah stood alone in her comfortable cell, staring at the folder full of her designed existence.

## Scene 3: The Cell - Research and Doubt

Sarah didn't sleep.

Couldn't sleep.

She paced the small room, mind racing through everything Wells had said.

The collapse was coming. That much seemed true—the data Wells had shown matched projections Sarah had seen in academic journals. Multiple systems approaching failure simultaneously.

But the breeding program. The controlled evolution. The planned culling of "inferior" genetic lines.

That was monstrous.

Wasn't it?

Sarah sat at the desk, opened the folder again. Studied her family tree more carefully this time.

Her great-grandmother had died at thirty-two. Childbirth complications. But the notation said: *Failed to produce desired cognitive markers in offspring. Terminated from program.*

Terminated.

Had she really died of complications? Or had the network... helped?

Her grandfather—her mother's father—had died in a car accident when Sarah was five. She remembered him vaguely. Kind. Told stories. The notation: *Optimal breeding outcome achieved. No further genetic value. Removed from program.*

Removed.

The car accident. Had it been an accident?

Sarah felt sick again.

How many of her family members had died not from chance but from being deemed genetically redundant?

On the desk, she noticed the tablet Wells had left behind. Probably intentional. Probably monitored. But it had internet access.

Sarah picked it up, started searching.

Her exposé: everywhere, but dismissed. Comments sections full of people calling her crazy, pointing out "obvious fabrications," sharing memes mocking her claims.

The narrative was set. She was a conspiracy theorist. Nothing she said would be believed.

She searched for David's social media.

Found his accounts. Recent posts from this morning—photos of coffee, a joke about work, response to a friend's message. Normal. Casual.

Like nothing had happened.

Either he was fine and pretending everything was normal, or... they'd done something to him. Memory alteration? Threats? Deals?

Sarah didn't know which was worse.

She went back to Marsh's research, stored in her encrypted email—of course they had access to it. They had access to everything.

Opened the files. Read through Marsh's decades of work again.

And this time, noticed something she'd missed before.

Contradictions.

In the older manuscripts—the ones from the 14th and 15th centuries—the cipher marks followed one system. Consistent. Mathematical. Precise.

But in the later manuscripts—17th, 18th, 19th centuries—the system had changed. Subtle variations. Errors that wouldn't be noticeable unless you were looking for them.

Like the knowledge had been corrupted over time.

Or deliberately altered.

Marsh had noted this: *WARNING: The cipher-makers may have misunderstood their own protocol. Evidence suggests original intent was DIFFERENT from current implementation.*

What if the original network—the one that started after Rome fell—had truly been about preserving knowledge?

But over centuries, as the knowledge passed through generations, the purpose had shifted. From preservation to control. From saving civilization to selecting it.

What if the modern corporations weren't continuing the original mission?

What if they'd corrupted it entirely?

Sarah pulled out every reference she could find to the "Genesis Protocol"—the master plan that supposedly explained everything.

Marsh's notes: *Protocol split across seven keys. Complete version never publicly read since medieval period. Current network interpretation may be fundamentally flawed.*

Seven keys.

The Iron Key, last seen in Mainz, 1347.

The Silver Key, Strasbourg, 1349.

The Living Key, Prague, 1355.

And four others, locations unknown.

What if the complete Protocol said something different than what GenVault believed?

What if the collapse they were planning wasn't supposed to happen at all?

A knock at the door. Different pattern than before.

Sarah looked up.

The door opened slightly. Not Wells. A young woman in a lab coat—Asian, maybe thirty, nervous.

"Dr. Chen?" she whispered. "I don't have much time. Five minutes, maybe less."

## Scene 4: The Contact - Unexpected Ally

The woman slipped inside, closed the door most of the way. Left it cracked—listening for approaching footsteps.

"Who are you?" Sarah asked.

"Dr. Yuki Tanaka. I work in GenVault's genetics division. East Asian bloodline program." She spoke quickly, words tumbling over each other. "I've been watching you since they brought you in. Read your exposé. Started doing my own research."

"Why are you here?"

"Because I found something. Something they don't want anyone to see." Yuki pulled out a flash drive from her pocket. "I can't give this to you—they'll know I was here. But I needed to tell someone. You're the only person who might understand."

Sarah's heart was racing. "Tell me what?"

"The collapse they're planning. The pandemic that'll kill 70-80% of global population. It's not theoretical. It's not a natural disaster they're preparing for." Yuki's voice shook. "They're going to cause it. Deliberately. They've already developed the bioweapon."

The room seemed to tilt.

"What?"

"Project THRESHOLD. I found the files. It's a genetic targeting virus—kills anyone who doesn't carry specific protective markers. The markers they've been breeding into target populations for seventy years." Yuki was almost hyperventilating. "They're waiting to hit 15% global coverage of the protected population. Then they release it. Kill everyone else. Reset civilization with only the 'optimal' genetic stock."

"That's genocide. Mass murder. They can't—"

"They can. They will. They're at 14.7% coverage now. Maybe six months until threshold. Maybe less if the latest fertility treatments take hold faster than projected." Yuki looked at the door. "I have maybe two minutes before shift change ends. I'm telling you this because you tried to stop them. Because you're still trying, even though they captured you."

"What can I do? I'm locked in here. They control the media, they have David, they—"

"I can get you out."

Sarah stared. "What?"

"Tonight. Shift change at 2 AM. Minimal security. I have access codes." Yuki pulled out a piece of paper, pressed it into Sarah's hand. "This is an address in New Hampshire. Safe house. The original network."

"The original—what?"

"Not everyone in the network is corrupt, Dr. Chen. Some of us still remember what it was supposed to be. Preservation, not genocide. Knowledge, not control." Yuki glanced at the door again. "My grandmother worked with Dr. Marsh in the '80s. They tried to expose the corruption then. The car accident that killed Marsh's research partner? My grandmother was in that car. She died. Marsh barely survived."

Yuki pulled a photograph from her pocket. Black and white, faded. Two women—young Evelyn Marsh and an Asian woman, standing in front of a university building.

"That's her. Tanaka Keiko. My grandmother." Yuki's eyes were bright with unshed tears. "She died trying to expose what they'd become. I'm finishing what she started."

"If you help me escape, they'll know it was you."

"I know."

"They'll kill you. Or worse."

"I know that too." Yuki straightened. "But some things are worth the risk. Seventy years of genocide disguised as evolution. Billions about to die. If I can help stop that, even a little..." She shrugged. "My grandmother would have done it. I can too."

Sarah looked at the address. New Hampshire. Remote location. Could be real. Could be another trap.

But what choice did she have?

"How?" Sarah asked.

"Shift change at 2 AM. I'll come for you. Have access codes for this wing. We go through the research levels—I work there, I'm supposed to be there. Down to the parking garage. I'll give you keys to my car. You drive north." Yuki touched the paper. "That address. They'll help you. They have pieces of the original Protocol. The real one, before it was corrupted."

"Who are 'they'?"

"Network members who split off fifty years ago. Went underground. Preserved the old texts, the uncorrupted cipher marks. They've been trying to piece together what the Genesis Protocol actually says." Yuki checked her watch. "One minute. I have to go."

"Yuki—"

"Don't thank me. Just stop them." Yuki opened the door, checked the corridor. "2 AM. Be ready."

She slipped out.

Sarah stood alone, holding an address in New Hampshire and the possibility of hope.

## Scene 5: The Escape - Hunted Again

Sarah didn't sleep. Spent the hours until 2 AM preparing mentally.

No plan. No backup. Just trust that this young woman she'd never met would actually come back. Would actually help her escape.

Trust had killed Marsh. Had gotten David captured. Had failed everyone who'd tried to expose the network.

But Sarah was out of other options.

At 1:58 AM, she heard footsteps in the corridor.

The door opened.

Yuki, in lab coat, carrying a tablet like she belonged there. "Now. We go now."

Sarah followed her into a pristine white corridor. Fluorescent lights. Multiple doors, all closed. A facility, but where? What floor? How many guards?

"Walk like you belong here," Yuki muttered. "We're just researchers working late."

They passed a security checkpoint. Guard barely glanced up as Yuki scanned her badge. Door clicked open.

Into another corridor—this one with windows into laboratories. Sarah glimpsed genetics equipment, breeding tanks, computer stations analyzing data.

"This is the bloodline tracking center," Yuki whispered. "East Asian program. We monitor forty thousand individuals across six countries. Make sure breeding patterns follow optimal trajectories."

Forty thousand people. Controlled like livestock. Guided toward genetic outcomes they'd never know about.

Another checkpoint. Yuki's badge worked again.

Down stairs—industrial, concrete, harsh lighting. The parking garage.

"My car is section C, spot 47. Gray Honda." Yuki pressed keys into Sarah's hand. "Drive north. Follow the address. Don't stop, don't call anyone, don't—"

Alarms.

Loud, harsh, echoing through the concrete space.

"Shit," Yuki breathed. "They know. Run!"

Sarah ran.

Behind her, shouts. Footsteps. Multiple people.

She found section C, spotted the gray Honda. Fumbled with the keys, fingers shaking.

Unlocked it. Got in. Started the engine.

In the rearview mirror, security personnel converging on Yuki. She didn't fight. Just stood there, hands raised.

Their eyes met across the parking garage.

Yuki nodded once. *Go.*

Sarah went.

Floored the accelerator, tires squealing on concrete. Security personnel diving out of the way. A barrier at the exit—closed, but Sarah didn't slow. Crashed through it in a shower of splintering wood and plastic.

Out into darkness. No idea where she was.

Looked like an industrial park. Buildings, warehouses, no people at this hour.

Headlights behind her. Two vehicles, fast and professional.

Sarah had never been good at driving. Adequate for getting to work, terrible at anything requiring skill.

She drove like her life depended on it. Which it did.

Through the industrial park, onto a main road. Signs: Route 3 North. New Hampshire.

The vehicles stayed with her. Professional drivers, coordinated pursuit.

Sarah took a turn too fast. Nearly rolled. Regained control, hands white-knuckled on the wheel.

Another turn. Into a residential area—quieter, fewer lights. Killed her headlights, navigated by streetlamps and memory.

The pursuit vehicles overshot the turn. Gave her maybe thirty seconds.

Sarah pulled into a driveway—random house, dark—cut the engine. Sat in absolute silence, heart hammering so loud she was sure they'd hear it.

The pursuit vehicles circled back. Drove past slowly, searching.

Sarah didn't breathe.

They continued down the street. Turned. Disappeared.

She waited. Five minutes. Ten. Fifteen.

Nothing.

At 3:30 AM, Sarah started the car again. Drove slowly, headlights on now, just another early morning commuter.

Found Route 3 North. Followed it.

Behind her, the facility—wherever it had been—faded into the darkness.

Ahead, New Hampshire and an address that might be salvation or another trap.

Sarah drove.

At 4 AM, radio news came on: "...GenVault Corporation reports security breach at research facility. Authorities searching for Dr. Sarah Chen, former employee considered dangerous and mentally unstable. Public advised not to approach..."

She was a fugitive again.

But this time, she had something she didn't have before.

The possibility that the network wasn't entirely corrupt. That some people still remembered what it was supposed to be.

And an address in New Hampshire where the real Genesis Protocol might still exist.

Sarah drove north through the predawn darkness, toward answers or annihilation.

Either way, she was done running.

Now she was hunting.
