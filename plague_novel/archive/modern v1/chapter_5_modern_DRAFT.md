# CHAPTER 5 - THE STORM (Modern Timeline - Present Day)

## Scene 1: Going Viral - The First 24 Hours

### Martin's Safe House, Boston Suburbs, Day One

Sarah woke to the sound of her phone exploding with notifications.

Not literally exploding, though the constant buzzing and chirping made it feel that way. She'd silenced it before sleeping, but somehow it had turned itself back on. Or maybe she'd dreamed the silencing.

She grabbed it. 147 missed calls. 823 text messages. 2,441 Twitter notifications. 1,893 emails.

All in the eight hours she'd been asleep.

"You're trending," Martin said from the doorway. He looked like he hadn't slept at all. "Number one on Twitter globally. Your name. 'Genesis Protocol.' 'GenVault Conspiracy.' All of it."

Sarah sat up, her body protesting. Every muscle ached from the night spent running through forests and hiding from pursuit teams.

"Show me."

Martin handed her a tablet. News coverage from around the world, all focused on her story.

**CNN**: "GenVault Accused of 70-Year Genetic Manipulation Program - Former Employee Releases Damning Evidence"

**BBC**: "Genetic Conspiracy Claims Spark Global Investigation - Seven Corporations Named"

**The New York Times**: "The Aethelred Cipher: Medieval Manuscript Links to Modern Genetics Controversy"

**Al Jazeera**: "Is GenVault Preparing for Civilizational Collapse? Scientists Debate Evidence"

The coverage was massive. Not relegated to fringe sites or conspiracy forums. Mainstream media. Serious journalists. Academic debates.

"They're believing it," Sarah said, almost afraid to hope.

"Some of it. The genetic manipulation is documented, the bloodline tracking is provable, the corporate connections are real. That part they can't dismiss." Martin pulled up more coverage. "But the medieval cipher connection, the 700-year conspiracy, the cyclical collapse theory—that's where they're getting skeptical."

He showed her a segment from a morning news show. Two scientists debating the evidence.

"The genetic markers are real," one was saying. "I've independently verified samples from the leaked data. These patterns shouldn't exist if breeding was random. But extrapolating that to a 700-year conspiracy requires extraordinary evidence."

"Which Dr. Chen has provided," the other scientist countered. "Medieval manuscripts with cipher marks that correlate to modern genetic data. Corporate records showing coordination between companies that supposedly compete. Financial transfers suggesting a unified network operating across apparent market divisions."

"Or it's an elaborate hoax. A former employee with access to data fabricating connections that aren't really there."

Sarah watched the debate with growing frustration. They were so close to believing. But the truth was too big. Too historical. Too impossible-sounding.

"What about the virus?" she asked. "Project THRESHOLD?"

Martin's expression darkened. "That's where we're losing them. No one wants to believe a corporation would develop a genocidal bioweapon. It sounds too movie-villain. Too deliberately evil."

He pulled up more coverage. GenVault's response, playing on every news channel.

Catherine Wells stood at a podium, calm and professional, flanked by lawyers and corporate spokespeople.

"GenVault categorically denies developing any weaponized virus," Wells was saying. "The documents Dr. Chen leaked refer to theoretical pandemic preparedness research. Models of how a natural outbreak might spread and how we could protect vulnerable populations. This is responsible corporate planning, not conspiracy."

"Fuck," Sarah breathed. "She's spinning it perfectly."

"She's had seventy years to prepare for exposure," Martin said. "Multiple contingency plans. Legal teams ready. Media strategies tested. They knew this day might come."

Sarah scrolled through social media. The response was divided.

#BelieveSarah was trending. So was #GenVaultHoax. People arguing, debating, some convinced, some skeptical, most just confused.

"We need more," Sarah said. "Better evidence. Something they can't spin."

"Like what?"

"Like the virus itself. Samples. Lab documentation. Evidence it's not theoretical but real." Sarah stood, started pacing. "Martin, you said the original network has people inside GenVault. Can they get us proof?"

"Maybe. But after your exposure, security will be maximum. Anyone suspected of disloyalty will be monitored, possibly removed."

"So we need someone they won't suspect." Sarah's mind was racing, pattern recognition kicking into overdrive. "Someone who benefits from the program. Someone on the protected list."

Martin stared at her. "You want to infiltrate GenVault. Actually walk in there and steal evidence from inside."

"It's the only way to prove THRESHOLD is real."

"It's suicide. They'll be waiting for exactly that. Expecting you to try something desperate."

"Which is why they won't expect me to succeed." Sarah grabbed her laptop, started pulling up GenVault's public information. "What if I don't sneak in? What if I walk in the front door?"

"Explain."

"Wells gave me forty-eight hours to decide. To join them or refuse. That deadline hasn't passed yet." Sarah found what she was looking for—GenVault's headquarters address. "What if I call her? Say I've reconsidered. That the media circus made me realize I'd rather be on the winning side. That I want to join the program properly."

Martin processed this. "You think she'll believe you?"

"She wants to believe I'm recruitable. That's why she let me record her. She's betting I'll see the logic of their position, accept that the collapse is inevitable, choose survival over principle." Sarah met his eyes. "And she's almost right. I am genetically optimized for pattern recognition and pragmatic thinking. If I tell her I've calculated the odds and decided to join the survivors, it fits her worldview perfectly."

"And if she sees through it?"

"Then I die. But we're running out of options. The story is out but incomplete. People are debating instead of acting. We need proof that forces action. Proof of the virus. Proof of the 15% threshold. Proof of planned genocide."

Martin was quiet for a long moment. "The original network can help. Get you in, provide support, extract you if possible. But Sarah... if this goes wrong, you won't just die. They'll disappear you. Use you for research. Study your genetics. Keep you alive as a breeding specimen."

"I know."

"And you're still willing to try?"

Sarah thought about six billion people who would die if THRESHOLD launched. About Marsh, who'd died trying to expose this. About Yuki, captured helping her escape. About twelve generations of her family bred without consent.

"Yes. I'm willing."

"Then we start planning. But first—" Martin gestured to the news coverage still playing on multiple screens. "You should watch this. Understand what you've created."

## Scene 2: Media Firestorm - The World Reacts

### Safe House, Throughout the Day

The coverage intensified as the day progressed.

By noon, GenVault's stock had crashed 73%. Trading was halted. The other six corporations—names Sarah recognized from the cipher research—were also tanking. Billions in market value evaporating.

Government agencies announced investigations. FBI. CDC. International health organizations. Academic institutions calling for independent verification.

But also: conspiracy theorists latching onto the story. Mixing truth with wild speculation. Making it harder to separate real evidence from fantasy.

Sarah watched it all with growing frustration.

"They're muddying the waters," she said, watching a YouTube personality claiming GenVault was run by alien reptilians. "Mixing legitimate evidence with insanity so people can't tell what's real."

"Classic disinformation tactic," Martin agreed. "You can't suppress information in the internet age. But you can drown it in noise. Make people give up trying to find truth because separating signal from noise is too exhausting."

By afternoon, the narrative had shifted. News coverage was focused less on the evidence and more on Sarah herself.

"Who Is Dr. Sarah Chen?" - Profile pieces digging into her background.

"Troubled Genius or Conspiracy Theorist?" - Psychological analysis by people who'd never met her.

"GenVault Whistleblower's Mental Health History Raises Questions" - Hit pieces suggesting she'd had a nervous breakdown.

"They're attacking you personally," Martin said. "Can't refute the evidence, so they discredit the source."

"Let them." Sarah was reading academic forums where actual scientists were debating the genetic data. "The evidence speaks for itself. I don't matter."

But it did matter. She could see public opinion shifting. The story was moving from "massive conspiracy exposed" to "troubled scientist makes wild claims." The twenty-four hour news cycle was already getting bored, looking for the next crisis.

By evening, a counter-narrative had emerged. GenVault wasn't denying the genetic research. They were justifying it.

Wells gave another press conference, this one more detailed. Showed graphs of civilizational collapse. Data on approaching tipping points. Models of what happens when multiple systems fail simultaneously.

"We're not engineering catastrophe," Wells said calmly, reasonably. "We're preparing for it. The evidence is clear—climate change, pandemic potential, resource depletion, political instability. Multiple existential risks approaching simultaneously. And when collapse comes, who survives shouldn't be left to chance."

She pulled up genetic data. Showed how certain traits correlated with survival in historical collapses.

"Intelligence. Disease resistance. Adaptability. These aren't privileges. They're necessities for rebuilding civilization. GenVault has been working for seven decades to ensure these traits are distributed globally. Not to create a master race, but to maximize humanity's chances of recovery."

It was compelling. Frighteningly so.

Sarah watched as public opinion started to shift again. Less "evil conspiracy" and more "controversial but maybe necessary program."

"She's winning," Sarah said. "She's turning it into a debate about ethics versus survival. And in that debate, ethics usually loses."

"Not if we prove THRESHOLD is real," Martin said. "If we can show they're planning active genocide, not passive selection, the debate changes. Murder is murder, regardless of justification."

Sarah's burner phone rang. Unknown number. She almost didn't answer.

But something made her pick up.

"Dr. Chen." Catherine Wells's voice, calm as ever. "I've been watching the news. Quite a spectacle you've created."

"You let me create it."

"I did. And now you're learning the lesson I wanted you to learn. The truth is too big. Too complex. Too frightening. People can't process it. So they retreat into simpler narratives. Heroic whistleblower versus evil corporation. The reality—that we're trying to save a fraction of humanity from total extinction—doesn't fit the story structure they need."

"I'm not done," Sarah said.

"I know. That's why I'm calling. Your forty-eight hours aren't up. The offer stands. Join us. Help us refine the selection criteria. Use your genetics and intelligence for something meaningful instead of this futile resistance."

"I recorded our conversation. Released it. Everyone knows what you're planning."

"Everyone knows one interpretation of what we're planning. Not the full picture. Not the complete Genesis Protocol." Wells's voice shifted, became almost gentle. "Sarah, you're smart enough to see the pattern. Civilizations collapse every 600-800 years. Bronze Age. Rome. Medieval period. We're overdue. And unlike our ancestors, we can choose who survives the reset. That's not evil. It's pragmatic mercy."

"It's genocide dressed up as preservation."

"It's survival dressed up as choice. The collapse is coming whether we act or not. We're just ensuring it doesn't mean extinction." Wells paused. "I'm extending the deadline. One week. Think about it. Really think about what you want your legacy to be. Died fighting the inevitable? Or helped shape what comes after?"

"I'm not joining you."

"We'll see. One week, Dr. Chen. The invitation remains open."

The line went dead.

Sarah stared at the phone.

"What did she say?" Martin asked.

"She's confident. Too confident." Sarah turned back to the news coverage. "She thinks she's already won. That the story is out but neutered. That I've shot my bolt and missed."

"Have you?"

"Maybe." Sarah pulled up data on her laptop. "But I'm not done yet. We need proof of THRESHOLD. Real proof. Irrefutable evidence that it's not theoretical but weaponized and ready to deploy."

"And you're still planning to infiltrate GenVault to get it?"

"Not planning. Doing." Sarah started researching GenVault's facility layouts, security protocols, everything available in public records. "We have maybe a week before they hit 15% and feel secure enough to deploy. I need to be inside before then."

Martin sat down heavily. "This is insane."

"This whole situation is insane. I'm just trying to match the level of crazy required to stop it."

She watched the news coverage continue. The story slowly being buried under analysis and debate and counter-narrative.

But not completely buried. Not yet.

Scientists were still demanding access to the data. Journalists were still investigating. Government agencies were still probing.

The truth was out there. Distributed. Impossible to fully suppress.

Now Sarah just needed to prove the worst part of it before anyone could act.

## Scene 3: The Plan - Becoming the Mole

### Safe House, Day Two

Martin's "original network" turned out to be larger than Sarah expected.

Over the next day, people arrived at the safe house. Scientists who'd worked at GenVault and left in disgust. Former security personnel who'd seen too much. Researchers who'd been recruited but couldn't stomach the endgame.

Fifteen people total. All carrying pieces of the puzzle.

"We've been trying to expose GenVault for twenty years," said Dr. Elena Vasquez, a geneticist who'd been on the THRESHOLD development team before leaving. "But we never had enough. Pieces of evidence. Fragments of documents. Nothing complete enough to prove the full picture."

"Until now," another person added. "Your exposure gave us credibility. Connected pieces we couldn't connect ourselves."

They gathered around the dining table, covered in maps and documents and security protocols.

"You're serious about infiltrating?" Elena asked Sarah. "Going inside GenVault?"

"I am."

"Then you need to understand the facility. THRESHOLD is developed in a high-security biolab. Level 4 containment. You can't just walk in."

"But I can be recruited into a position with access," Sarah said. "Wells wants me. Think I'm valuable enough to give access to sensitive areas."

"Not immediately. They'll test you first. Verify your loyalty. Could take months." Elena pulled up a facility schematic on her laptop. "But there's another way. If you can get yourself assigned to the genetic analysis division, you'd have legitimate access to the viral samples. They're stored in the same facility, shared containment protocols."

"How do I get assigned there?"

"Volunteer for it during your recruitment intake. Say you want to understand the full program before committing. That you need to see the science firsthand to accept it." Elena highlighted sections of the facility. "Once you're in genetic analysis, you're inside the security perimeter. You'll have badge access to contamination areas. And if we can get you a modified badge..."

"I can do that," said Marcus, former security personnel. "Worked there for twelve years. Still have contacts. Can program a badge with elevated access."

"How elevated?" Sarah asked.

"Enough to get you into THRESHOLD storage. But only for minutes before the discrepancy triggers alarms. You'd need to get samples, document evidence, and get out before security responds."

Sarah studied the schematics. "How many minutes?"

"Five. Maybe seven if you're lucky and the system is slow to flag the anomaly."

"That's not much time."

"It's what you get. The question is whether it's enough."

Sarah thought about it. She needed virus samples—proof it existed. Documentation showing it was weaponized, not theoretical. Deployment plans showing the 15% threshold.

Five minutes to find all that, copy it, get it out.

"It's enough," she said. "If I know exactly what I'm looking for and where it's stored."

"I can help with that," Elena said. "I worked on THRESHOLD for three years. I know the documentation system. Where samples are stored. What you need to look for." She pulled up more detailed information. "But Sarah, if you're caught... they won't just fire you. They'll kill you. Or worse, keep you alive for research. You're too valuable genetically."

"I know."

"And you're still willing to risk it?"

Sarah thought about the news coverage she'd watched all day. The truth being diluted, spun, debated into meaninglessness. The six billion people who would die if she failed.

"Yes."

The team spent the rest of the day planning. Entry strategy. Cover story. What to say to Wells. How to act recruitable without seeming desperate. What to steal. How to document it. Extraction plans if things went wrong.

By evening, they had a workable plan. Not perfect. Too many variables. Too many ways it could fail.

But workable.

"When do you make contact?" Marcus asked.

"Tomorrow. Give them one more day to feel confident. Then I call Wells, say I've thought it over, that I want to tour the facility and understand the program before making a final decision."

"And if she says no?"

"She won't. She wants me too badly." Sarah felt the weight of what she was about to do. "This is what I was bred for, right? Pattern recognition. Analytical thinking. Infiltration isn't that different from research. Just higher stakes."

Elena laughed, dark and humorless. "That's one way to frame it."

Sarah called the meeting to a close. People dispersed to various safe locations, each carrying parts of the plan, none knowing all of it. Compartmentalization. If one was captured, the rest could continue.

Martin stayed behind.

"You don't have to do this," he said. "We can find another way."

"What other way? The story is already losing momentum. In a week, it'll be yesterday's news. A curiosity. Something people debated once before moving on." Sarah shook her head. "This is our chance. Maybe our only chance."

"Then let me come with you. As backup."

"You can't. They'll recognize you as outside network. I need to go in alone. Looking recruitable. Looking like I've calculated the odds and chosen survival."

"And after you get the evidence?"

"Then we release it everywhere. Make it impossible to ignore. Force governments to act before GenVault can deploy THRESHOLD."

Martin was quiet for a moment. "You know there's a good chance you don't make it out."

"I know."

"And you're okay with that?"

Sarah thought about it. About her life before this. Quiet research. Academic papers. Comfortable existence. All of it built on twelve generations of manipulation she'd never known about.

"I used to think my purpose was to understand genetics. To make small contributions to human knowledge. Publish papers. Get tenure. Live comfortably."

"And now?"

"Now I know my purpose is to stop six billion people from dying. Even if it kills me. Especially since I'm only alive because forty generations were manipulated to produce me." She met Martin's eyes. "This is the most free choice I've ever made. Paradoxically."

Martin nodded slowly. "Then we make it work. Whatever it takes."

They spent the rest of the evening preparing. Sarah reviewed facility layouts until she could navigate them from memory. Studied security protocols. Memorized the documentation system for viral samples.

Tomorrow, she would contact Wells.

Tomorrow, she would walk into GenVault's headquarters and become a mole.

Tomorrow, the endgame would begin.

## Scene 4: The Call - Making Contact

### Safe House, Day Three, Morning

Sarah made the call at 9 AM. Professional hour. Shows she's serious, thoughtful, not panicked.

Wells answered immediately. Like she'd been waiting.

"Dr. Chen. I hoped you'd call."

"I've been thinking about what you said. About the collapse. About choosing to survive versus fighting inevitably." Sarah kept her voice steady, analytical. "And I've been watching the news coverage. How quickly people move from shock to debate to dismissal."

"It's predictable human behavior. We don't want to believe scary things. So we find reasons not to."

"Exactly." Sarah paused, as if making a difficult decision. "I want to see it. The full program. The research. The evidence that supports your conclusions about civilizational collapse. I need to understand the complete picture before I commit."

"Of course. That's reasonable. When?"

"Today. Now. Before I lose my nerve or the media finds me again."

Wells was quiet for a moment. Calculating. Assessing whether this was genuine or a trap.

"All right. I'll arrange a tour. Our main research facility. You'll see everything—the genetic research, the population modeling, the preparation protocols. And yes, you'll see THRESHOLD. Because you're right—you need to understand the complete picture."

Sarah's heart hammered. That was easier than expected. Which meant Wells was either confident in her security or planning something.

"Where and when?"

"Our facility in Waltham. Where we held you before. You know the location. Be there at 2 PM. Come alone. Any indication you're recording or have backup, and we're done. This is a one-time offer."

"Understood."

"Good. Dr. Chen... Sarah. You're making the right choice. I think you'll understand that once you see what we're really building."

The line went dead.

Sarah looked at Martin. "I'm in. 2 PM. Waltham facility."

"Five hours. We need to prep."

The team assembled again. Marcus with the modified badge. Elena with detailed briefings on what to look for. Others with communication equipment, extraction plans, legal strategies if Sarah was caught.

"The badge will work," Marcus said, handing her a GenVault employee card with her photo. "But only for seven minutes maximum. After that, the system flags the elevated permissions and security responds. So you need to be fast."

"Seven minutes. What's the quickest route from the tour area to THRESHOLD storage?"

Elena pulled up schematics. "If they take you through genetic analysis—which they will, it's the showcase area—then you're two corridors and one secure door from viral storage. Three minutes of walking if you move quickly. That leaves four minutes to find documentation, copy it, and get out."

"What am I looking for specifically?"

"Samples first—proof the virus exists in weaponized form. They'll be in cryogenic storage, labeled THRESH-01 through THRESH-15. Take photos of the labels and storage containers. Then documentation—deployment protocols, population threshold calculations, release timelines. Those will be in digital files on any secured terminal."

"How do I copy files in four minutes?"

"You don't. You photograph them. Phone camera, rapid fire, as many pages as possible. We'll compile and verify later." Elena handed Sarah a phone—modified, high-res camera, massive storage. "This can take 500 photos in under a minute. Point and shoot. Don't worry about perfect framing. Just get the data."

Sarah practiced the route on the schematics. Memorized turns. Counted steps. Visualized the whole sequence like choreography.

By 1 PM, she was ready. Or as ready as possible for infiltrating a bioterrorism facility.

"Last chance to back out," Martin said.

"I'm not backing out."

"Then good luck. We'll be monitoring from outside. Any signal you're in trouble, we'll... well, we'll try something."

"Reassuring."

"It's honest." Martin gripped her shoulder. "You're brave. Braver than anyone I've worked with. If this works, you'll save billions of lives."

"And if it doesn't?"

"Then you'll die trying. Which is more than most people can say."

Sarah drove to the facility alone. Same building where she'd been held captive days ago. Different purpose now.

She was walking in voluntarily. With a plan to steal evidence and expose genocide.

All she had to do was convince Wells she was recruitable. Get the tour. Wait for the right moment. Use the modified badge. Infiltrate THRESHOLD storage. Document everything. Get out.

Seven minutes.

Billions of lives.

Sarah parked, checked her appearance in the mirror. Looked professional. Competent. Like someone who'd done the calculations and chosen survival.

She walked into GenVault's headquarters at exactly 2 PM.

Time to become a spy.

## Scene 5: Inside GenVault - The Tour and the Truth

### GenVault Research Facility, Waltham

Wells met her in the lobby. Same professional demeanor. Same confidence.

"Dr. Chen. Thank you for coming. I think you'll find this enlightening."

Security screening was thorough. Metal detector. ID check. Biometric scan. They took her phone—the obvious one, not the modified one hidden in her jacket lining.

"Can't allow outside devices in the secure areas. I'm sure you understand."

Sarah nodded, acting reluctant but understanding.

They entered the facility proper. Clean, modern, exactly like any biotech company. Wells narrated as they walked.

"GenVault employs 1,200 researchers globally. We're the largest, but the other six corporations in our network have similar operations. Together, we've been working for seventy years to understand and guide human genetics."

They passed labs where scientists worked on genetic sequencing, population modeling, trait analysis.

"This is our bloodline tracking center. We monitor forty thousand individuals globally. Not controlling them—just observing. Ensuring natural genetic drift doesn't undo seventy years of careful selection."

Sarah saw databases tracking families across continents. Marriage patterns. Birth rates. Genetic markers distributed through populations.

"And people don't know they're being tracked?"

"Why would they? We're not harming them. Just ensuring optimal genetic combinations occur naturally. A nudge here, a suggestion there, fertility treatments that happen to benefit carriers of specific markers." Wells spoke like it was reasonable. Normal.

They continued deeper. More labs. More evidence of the scope.

"This is our crisis modeling division. We analyze collapse scenarios. Climate change, pandemic, war, economic failure, all the ways our current civilization could fragment."

Scientists worked on complex models showing cascading failures. Graphs of approaching tipping points. Simulations of what happens when multiple systems fail simultaneously.

It was compelling. Frightening. The data didn't lie—civilization was approaching multiple catastrophic thresholds.

"And you think you can manage the collapse? Control who survives?"

"Not control. Optimize. Ensure the survivors have traits that make recovery possible. Intelligence, disease resistance, adaptability. It's not about creating a master race. It's about giving humanity its best chance."

They reached the genetic analysis division. Elena had been right—this was the showcase area. Where they brought potential recruits.

"This is where we verify genetic markers in target populations. Confirm that desired traits are manifesting as predicted."

Rows of equipment analyzing DNA samples. Scientists at workstations reviewing data.

And two corridors away—THRESHOLD storage.

Sarah needed to separate from Wells. Create an opportunity.

"Could I see the raw data? On my own genetic profile? I'd like to understand specifically what twelve generations of selection produced."

Wells smiled. "Of course. Dr. Reeves can show you." She gestured to a nearby scientist. "Dr. Chen wants to see her detailed genetic profile. Give her the full workup."

Dr. Reeves led Sarah to a workstation. Pulled up her data. Started explaining markers, traits, inheritance patterns.

Wells stepped away to take a phone call.

This was the chance.

Sarah waited until Reeves was focused on the screen. Then stood. "Bathroom?"

"Down the hall. Third door on the left."

Sarah walked calmly. Not hurried. Just someone using the restroom.

Turned the corner. Out of sight.

Pulled out the modified badge. Swiped it at the secure door leading toward viral storage.

The light turned green.

Seven minutes starting now.

Sarah moved quickly. Down the corridor. Past laboratories. Following the route memorized from schematics.

Found THRESHOLD storage. Swiped the badge again. Door opened.

Inside: cryogenic units. Biohazard warnings. And there—samples labeled THRESH-01 through THRESH-15.

Proof. The virus was real.

Sarah pulled out the hidden phone. Started photographing. Sample labels. Storage logs. Everything.

Two minutes gone.

She moved to the computer terminal. Found the deployment protocols. Started photographing pages as fast as possible. Point, shoot, advance, repeat.

Population threshold: 15.0%. Current coverage: 14.9%. Estimated time to threshold: 8-12 weeks.

Release protocol: Airborne distribution at major transportation hubs. Global coverage within 72 hours.

Expected mortality: 85-90% of unprotected populations.

Expected survival: 95%+ of protected populations.

Four minutes gone.

Alarms. Loud, harsh.

They'd detected the badge anomaly.

Sarah kept photographing. Racing through pages. Getting every piece of evidence she could.

Five minutes.

Running feet in the corridor. Shouts.

Sarah grabbed one physical sample vial—small, could fit in her pocket. Evidence they couldn't claim was fabricated.

Six minutes.

The door burst open. Security guards. Weapons drawn.

"Hands up! Now!"

Sarah raised her hands. Phone still in one hand, obvious.

"I have evidence. Of genocide. Of a plan to kill billions." She spoke clearly, calmly. "I'm documenting it. And if you kill me, it's already uploading to servers you can't access."

It was a bluff. The phone wasn't connected. But they didn't know that.

"Restrain her," Wells's voice. Cold now. No more pretense of recruitment.

Guards grabbed Sarah. Took the phone. Found the sample vial.

Wells examined both. "Clever. Coming in under pretense of recruitment. Did you really think we wouldn't expect this?"

"Did you really think I came here without backup?" Sarah countered. Another bluff.

Wells smiled. "There's no backup. We've been monitoring the original network for years. We know about Martin's safe house. About Elena Vasquez. About all of them. You're alone, Dr. Chen. And now you're ours."

She gestured to the guards. "Take her to research containment. And someone call Dr. Morrison. Tell him we have a prime specimen for the breeding program. Twelve generations of optimization. Too valuable to waste."

Sarah struggled, but the guards were professional. Efficient.

She'd gotten the evidence. Photographed the deployment protocols. Taken a sample.

But she'd been caught.

And now she was about to disappear into GenVault's research program. Become a breeding specimen. A genetic resource to be used and studied.

As they dragged her away, Sarah hoped the evidence she'd gathered would be enough.

Hoped the original network would find a way to retrieve it.

Hoped someone would stop THRESHOLD before it launched.

But she wouldn't know.

Because she was gone.

Disappeared into the facility she'd tried to expose.

A cautionary tale about fighting conspiracies too powerful to defeat.

Or a martyr who'd gotten just enough evidence to make a difference.

She'd find out which.

Eventually.
