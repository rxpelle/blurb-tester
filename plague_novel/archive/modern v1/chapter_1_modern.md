# CHAPTER 1 - THE PATTERN (Modern Timeline - Present Day, Boston)

## Scene 1: Sarah's Discovery

### GenVault Corporation, Genomics Research Division - 11:47 PM

Sarah Chen rubbed her eyes and stared at the dual monitors again. The left screen showed a 14th-century manuscript page from the Bodleian Library's digital archive—marginal decorations, tiny colored dots around Latin text about plague mortality. The right screen displayed her own genomic dataset: population variants in CCR5-Delta32, the mutation that conferred resistance to certain pathogens.

She'd been chasing a hunch for three weeks. Now, at nearly midnight in an empty lab, the hunch had become something else entirely.

The dots weren't random.

She overlaid the medieval pattern onto her genomic map, adjusting for scale. Her breath caught.

Perfect alignment.

The colored dots in the manuscript margins—dismissed by historians as decorative flourishes—mapped exactly onto modern genetic variation hotspots. Red dots corresponded to high-frequency protective alleles. Blue dots to populations that had been decimated. Yellow dots to intermediary zones where the plague had killed selectively.

"That's impossible," she whispered to the empty lab.

She pulled up another manuscript page. Different monastery, different century, same marginal notation system. Ran the overlay.

Same result.

Her hands trembled as she opened a third document—this one from a private collection, a merchant's ledger from Mainz, 1347. The "decorative" dots appeared again, now alongside what looked like trade routes.

She mapped it.

The dots predicted genetic drift patterns with ninety-four percent accuracy.

"They knew." Sarah pushed back from her desk, heart hammering. "Somehow they knew which populations would survive."

But how? Medieval monks didn't have PCR machines or gene sequencers. They didn't understand inheritance beyond "like begets like." They certainly couldn't predict which specific genetic variations would protect against Yersinia pestis.

Unless.

Sarah pulled up GenVault's restricted database—the one she technically shouldn't have access to, but Dr. Aris had given her his credentials "for emergencies." She typed in the query she'd been avoiding for weeks:

**SEARCH: Population genetic outliers / CCR5-Delta32 / Non-European distribution**

The results loaded.

And stopped making sense.

CCR5-Delta32 was supposed to be a European mutation, selected for by plague exposure. But GenVault's proprietary dataset showed unexpected high-frequency populations in North Africa, Central Asia, and...

Sarah's blood went cold.

The same regions marked with red dots in the medieval manuscripts.

Before the plague had even reached them.

Her phone buzzed. Dr. Otto Aris. Her mentor, the only person at GenVault who still seemed to care about science over stock prices.

She answered. "Dr. Aris, I know it's late, but I found—"

"Sarah." His voice was wrong. Tight. Afraid. "Don't answer this call."

She froze. "What?"

"They're listening. They're always listening." Background noise—he was outside, wind across the phone. "I saw your query logs. You accessed the restricted genome data. The population variants that don't fit the model. They flagged it."

"I was just researching—"

"I know what you were doing. I know what you found." His voice dropped to barely a whisper. "The medieval cipher. The mortality predictions. You think you're the first?"

Sarah's office suddenly felt very small. Very exposed. The security camera in the corner—had it always had that little red light?

"Dr. Aris, what are you talking about?"

"Twenty years ago, I found the same pattern. The same impossible correlation between medieval annotations and modern genetic data." A car door slammed in the background. "I published a preliminary paper. GenVault acquired my lab six months later. Buried my research. Made me head of a department that doesn't do anything real."

"That doesn't make sense. Why would they—"

"Because it's not a discovery, Sarah. It's a map." His breathing was ragged now, like he was running. "They've been using it. The founders of GenVault, the private equity backers—they have the original manuscripts. The complete cipher. They've been selecting for specific genetic traits for decades."

Sarah's mind raced. "That's insane. You can't... genetic selection takes generations. You'd need—"

"Seventy years. That's how long GenVault's parent companies have existed under different names. Seventy years of targeted interventions. Gene therapy trials in specific populations. Fertility treatments with 'improved' embryo selection. Vaccine programs that carried more than vaccines."

"Jesus Christ."

"They're building something, Sarah. A population optimized for... I don't even know what. Plague resistance is just the beginning. There are other markers in the cipher. Other traits being selected." His voice cracked. "And now they know you've seen it."

Sarah stood, legs unsteady. "What do I do?"

"Run. Tonight. Don't go home. Don't access your email. Don't—"

The call cut off.

Sarah stared at her phone. Then at her monitors. The medieval dots, glowing accusingly.

She had maybe ten minutes before building security checked the late-night lab access logs. Maybe less if Dr. Aris was right about active monitoring.

Her research. She couldn't leave it.

Sarah grabbed a flash drive, started copying files. The manuscript overlays. Her genomic analysis. The restricted database queries. Everything that proved—

The lab door opened.

Sarah spun. Her heart stopped.

Marcus Webb stood in the doorway. VP of Research Operations. Expensive suit at midnight. Two security guards behind him.

"Dr. Chen." His smile didn't reach his eyes. "Working late?"

"Just finishing up some data analysis." Her hand closed around the flash drive, still transferring. Progress bar: 67%.

"On medieval manuscripts?" Marcus walked into the lab, hands in pockets. Casual. Threatening. "Interesting pivot from your assigned project. The influenza vaccine study, wasn't it?"

"Following a hunch. Probably nothing."

"Probably." He glanced at her monitors. His expression didn't change, but something flickered. "Still, we should chat about it. Conference room. Now."

Not a request.

Progress bar: 81%.

"Of course." Sarah's voice was steadier than she felt. "Let me just save my work—"

"The guards will handle your station. Security protocol for after-hours access." Marcus gestured to the door. "Please."

Progress bar: 89%.

Sarah's mind raced. If she refused, they'd know she was hiding something. If she went, they'd delete everything before the transfer finished.

She looked at Marcus Webb. At his patient, predatory smile.

Then at her monitors, where seven centuries of genetic manipulation glowed in red and blue and yellow dots.

"Actually," Sarah said, "I need to use the restroom first."

She grabbed her bag, leaving the flash drive hidden under a stack of papers. Walked past Marcus toward the door.

"Dr. Chen."

She stopped.

"Your phone. Company property. You'll need to leave it here."

Her stomach dropped. But she pulled out her phone, set it on the desk.

Marcus nodded to a guard. "Escort her."

---

## Scene 2: The Bathroom Gambit

The restroom was down one floor. Sarah walked ahead of the guard—young, bored, probably didn't even know why he was babysitting a geneticist at midnight.

"Wait here," Sarah said at the bathroom door.

The guard shrugged, leaned against the wall.

Sarah went inside. Checked the stalls—empty. No windows. No fire exit. Just industrial tile and the smell of antiseptic.

She was trapped.

No phone. No research. No proof. And in about five minutes, Marcus Webb would realize she'd figured out exactly what GenVault really was.

Sarah leaned against the sink, breathing hard.

*Think. What would someone who wasn't about to disappear do?*

She looked at herself in the mirror. Thirty-two years old. MIT PhD. Published researcher. Scientist.

And apparently, the latest person to stumble onto a conspiracy that had been running since before she was born.

Her reflection looked terrified.

Then Sarah noticed something. The bathroom vent. Large. Old building, old ventilation system. The grate was held by just four screws.

She didn't have a screwdriver.

But she had a lab badge. Rigid plastic. Thin edge.

Sarah climbed onto the sink, started working the screws. The first one came loose after thirty seconds of prying. The second stuck, then gave way.

The guard knocked. "You okay in there?"

"Yeah, just—stomach trouble. The Thai food from lunch."

Silence. Then: "Take your time."

Third screw. Fourth screw.

The grate came free.

Sarah pulled herself up into the vent. It was tighter than movies made it look—she had to army-crawl, her bag scraping metal. But she fit.

Behind her, she heard the bathroom door open.

"Dr. Chen?"

She crawled faster. The vent branched—she went right, toward what she hoped was the building's east side. Away from the lab. Away from Marcus Webb.

Voices behind her, echoing: "—the vent, she's in the fucking vent—"

Sarah's hands found another grate. She kicked it. Once. Twice. It fell away, clattering onto tile.

She dropped through.

Different bathroom. Different floor. Sarah ran.

The emergency exit stairs. Down four flights. Her lungs burned. Her mind raced. She had no phone, no research, no proof.

But she was alive.

She hit the ground floor, burst through the exit into the parking lot. Cold November air hit her face. She ran toward the street.

A car screeched to a stop in front of her.

Sarah stumbled back, heart hammering—

The passenger window rolled down. Dr. Otto Aris looked out, his normally composed face haggard.

"Get in."

Sarah didn't hesitate. She threw herself into the passenger seat.

Aris accelerated before she'd even closed the door.

---

## Scene 3: Aris's Confession

They drove in silence for ten minutes. Aris checked the mirrors obsessively, took random turns, doubled back twice.

Finally, he pulled into a 24-hour diner parking lot. Killed the engine.

"Are we safe?" Sarah asked.

"For about twenty minutes. Maybe less." Aris looked at her. "Did you copy the data?"

"Started to. Didn't finish."

He nodded slowly. "Doesn't matter. They'll purge it anyway. Probably already have."

"Then what's the point?" Sarah's voice cracked. "If they control everything, if they've been doing this for seventy years—"

"The point is that you're alive. And awake." Aris pulled a worn leather satchel from the back seat. "And now you need to decide what kind of scientist you are."

"What does that mean?"

"The kind who looks at impossible data and walks away? Or the kind who asks why it's impossible?" He opened the satchel. Inside were photocopies—old, faded manuscripts. The same marginal dot patterns Sarah had found.

"I've been collecting these for twenty years," Aris said quietly. "Every manuscript I could find with the cipher markings. Cross-referencing them with modern genetic data, population movements, disease resistance patterns." He pulled out a map—Europe, North Africa, Asia, marked with colored pins. "The medieval cipher-makers weren't predicting plague survival. They were recording it. In real-time. With precision that shouldn't have been possible."

Sarah stared at the map. "How?"

"That's the question, isn't it?" Aris smiled bitterly. "My theory? They had access to knowledge that's been lost. Or hidden. Mathematical tools for tracking inheritance and variation that we're only rediscovering now. Maybe even..." He hesitated. "Maybe even experimental methods."

"You think they were doing genetics? In the Middle Ages?"

"I think the people who created the Aethelred Cipher were doing *something* that let them map human genetic variation centuries before Mendel. And I think GenVault's founders found their records. Their methods. And have been using them."

Sarah's hands shook. "For what?"

"I don't know. That's the terrifying part." Aris leaned back. "But I know the pattern accelerated in the last decade. More fertility clinics offering 'enhanced' embryo selection. More targeted vaccine trials in specific populations. GenVault just acquired three pharmaceutical companies and a major health insurance provider." He met her eyes. "They're not just tracking genetic drift anymore. They're steering it."

Sarah felt sick. "We have to expose them."

"With what evidence? The data you didn't finish copying? My twenty-year-old research that everyone thinks is crackpot theory?" Aris shook his head. "They own the labs. The journals. The regulatory agencies. You can't fight them with science—they are science now."

"Then what do we do?"

Aris pulled out a piece of paper. An address in New Haven. "There's someone who can help. Dr. Evelyn Marsh. She was part of the original team that translated the Aethelred manuscripts in the '70s, before GenVault buried the project. She's old, paranoid, and probably half-crazy by now."

"But?"

"But she has the source documents. The original cipher key." Aris handed Sarah the address. "If anyone can prove what GenVault is doing—and more importantly, *how* the medieval cipher-makers did it first—it's her."

Sarah took the paper. "Why don't you go?"

"Because they know I'm compromised. They've been watching me for years, waiting for me to slip." He smiled sadly. "But you're new. Unknown. You have maybe a twelve-hour head start before they escalate from corporate security to something worse."

"Something worse?"

Aris didn't answer. Instead, he pulled an envelope from the satchel. Cash. Maybe two thousand dollars. "No credit cards. No phone calls to anyone you know. Get to New Haven, find Marsh, and for God's sake, don't trust anyone who seems helpful."

Sarah stared at the money. At the address. At the impossible choice.

"I'm a scientist," she said. "Not a spy."

"You're a scientist who just discovered that someone's been engineering the human genome for three generations." Aris started the car. "That makes you whatever you need to be to survive."

He drove her to the bus station. Sarah got out, clutching the envelope and the address.

"Dr. Aris?"

He looked at her through the window.

"What happened to the other people who found the pattern? Before me?"

His expression answered before his words did. "Go, Sarah. And don't look back."

The car pulled away.

Sarah stood in the fluorescent glare of the bus station, holding everything she had left: two thousand dollars, an address in New Haven, and the terrible knowledge that somewhere in medieval manuscripts, seven centuries ago, someone had figured out how to read—and rewrite—the future of the human race.

And now, so had she.

The next bus to New Haven left in forty minutes.

Sarah bought a ticket.

---

## Scene 4: The Road to New Haven

### Greyhound Bus - 3:15 AM

Sarah sat in the back of the bus, watching Boston disappear through the smeared window. Her reflection stared back—pale, frightened, wearing the same lab coat she'd forgotten to take off.

She shrugged it off, balled it up, shoved it under the seat. One less thing identifying her.

The bus was mostly empty. An old woman slept across the aisle. Two rows up, a college kid with headphones. Nobody paying attention.

Sarah pulled out the photocopied manuscripts Aris had given her. Studied the dots again by the overhead light.

Red. Blue. Yellow. Arranged in geometric patterns that looked decorative but encoded something else. Something that predicted genetic variation with impossible accuracy.

She'd spent her whole career believing in the steady march of scientific progress. That knowledge built on knowledge. That the past was ignorant, superstitious, wrong about everything that mattered.

But what if the past had known things the present had forgotten?

What if the Black Death hadn't just killed a third of Europe—what if it had killed everyone who understood what was really happening? Everyone except the cipher-makers. The ones who'd hidden their knowledge in plain sight, disguised as marginal decorations in religious manuscripts.

And what if GenVault had found that knowledge?

Sarah's hands tightened on the papers.

The college kid got up, walked past her toward the bathroom. Sarah tensed, watching.

He went in. Came out. Sat back down.

She was being paranoid.

Or not paranoid enough.

Her mind kept returning to Dr. Aris's last question: *What happened to the other people who found the pattern?*

She pulled out her phone—then remembered. She'd left it at GenVault. Smart. They could track phones.

But she'd also left something else.

The flash drive.

Still hidden under papers on her desk. Still containing the incomplete file transfer. Maybe sixty percent of her research. The manuscript overlays. The genetic correlations.

Gone now. Deleted. Wiped. As if she'd never discovered anything.

Sarah closed her eyes.

*No. Not wiped.*

She'd been a grad student once. She knew about backup systems. GenVault had redundant servers, automatic backups, archival protocols. Her files would exist somewhere in the system for at least seventy-two hours before the purge cycle.

If someone knew where to look.

If someone could access the system remotely.

Sarah didn't know anyone who—

Wait.

Marcus Reeves.

Her ex-boyfriend from MIT. Cybersecurity researcher. They'd dated for six months, ended amicably when he took a job in New York. They still texted occasionally. Birthday wishes. Memes about academic publishing.

He could access GenVault's servers. Maybe.

But contacting him meant risk. If GenVault was monitoring her contacts—and they probably were—Marcus would be flagged immediately.

Unless.

Sarah looked at the college kid with headphones. Then at the sleeping woman.

She walked up to the kid, tapped his shoulder. He pulled out one earbud, annoyed.

"Sorry," Sarah said. "My phone died. Could I borrow yours for one quick call? It's an emergency."

The kid hesitated.

Sarah pulled out a twenty-dollar bill from Aris's envelope. "Please. Just five minutes."

The kid took the money, handed over his phone.

Sarah walked to the back of the bus. Dialed Marcus's number from memory.

Three rings. Four. Then a groggy voice: "Hello?"

"Marcus, it's Sarah. Don't say my name."

Pause. "...okay?"

"I need help. I can't explain everything, but I need you to access a server remotely. Pull files before they get deleted."

"Sarah, what—"

"Please. I'm not joking. This is life or death." Her voice cracked. "I found something at work. Something bad. People are after me."

Silence. Then: "Where are you?"

"Can't say. Can you do it?"

"...maybe. Whose server?"

"GenVault Corporation. Genomics division. My user directory." She gave him the details—server addresses she'd memorized, folder structures, file names.

Marcus was quiet for a long moment. "This is insane."

"I know."

"You could get arrested. I could get arrested."

"I know."

Another pause. "What am I looking for?"

"Genetic data. Manuscript overlays. Population variant analysis. Anything time-stamped in the last six hours."

"Sarah, they'll have security—"

"You wrote your thesis on bypassing corporate firewalls."

"For research purposes!"

"This is research." Sarah looked out the window. Dark highway, heading north. "It's the most important research either of us will ever do. Please."

She heard him sigh. Then: "I'll try. No promises. How do I reach you?"

"You don't. I'll contact you in forty-eight hours. Same number, different phone."

"This is insane," Marcus said again.

"I know. Thank you."

She hung up. Walked back to the college kid, handed him his phone.

"Thanks. Appreciate it."

The kid nodded, already back in his music.

Sarah returned to her seat. Stared at the photocopied manuscripts.

In forty-eight hours, she'd either have proof of a seventy-year conspiracy to manipulate human genetics.

Or she'd be a paranoid ex-scientist on the run from her own delusions.

The bus rolled on through the darkness.

Sarah closed her eyes and tried not to think about Dr. Aris's final warning.

Or about the fact that somewhere behind her, in Boston, people who had been playing genetic chess for three generations were realizing she'd just stolen one of their pieces.

The road to New Haven stretched ahead.

Sarah hoped Dr. Evelyn Marsh had answers.

Because the questions were multiplying faster than she could think.
