# CHAPTER 4 - THE CHOICE (Modern Timeline - Present Day)

## Scene 1: The Recording - Sarah's Gambit

### New Hampshire Safe House, Morning

The address Yuki had given Sarah led to a farmhouse forty miles north of the Massachusetts border. Old, weathered, surrounded by forest. No visible neighbors. The kind of place where someone could disappear.

Sarah had arrived at dawn, exhausted from the drive, paranoid that every car behind her was GenVault pursuit. But the farmhouse was empty. Unlocked. A note on the kitchen table: *Supplies in the basement. Wait. Trust no one. -Original Network*

Now, twelve hours later, Sarah sat in that basement surrounded by everything she needed to complete her work.

Three laptops, all air-gapped from the internet. External hard drives. Encrypted cloud storage credentials written on a slip of paper. A satellite internet connection that couldn't be traced to her. And Marsh's complete archive, organized and digitized.

Everything she needed to finish the exposé.

Everything except the decision.

Her phone—a new burner, bought with cash—rang.

Unknown number.

Sarah answered.

"Dr. Chen." Catherine Wells's voice, smooth and controlled. "I hope you've had time to consider our conversation."

"I have."

"And?"

Sarah looked at the evidence spread across the makeshift desk. Seventy years of genetic manipulation. Twelve generations of her own family bred like show dogs. The Genesis Protocol fragments suggesting something even worse coming.

"I want to meet," Sarah said. "In person. To discuss terms."

Silence on the line. Then: "Terms?"

"If I'm going to join you, I need to understand the complete picture. Not fragments. Not carefully edited presentations. Everything. The full Genesis Protocol. What you're actually building toward."

"That information is classified at the highest—"

"I'm a product of thirty generations of genetic selection, right? You said so yourself. I'm exactly the kind of person you want in the program. So treat me like it. Show me the complete picture. Let me make an informed decision."

More silence. Sarah could almost hear Wells thinking, calculating, weighing risk against potential gain.

"There's a restaurant," Wells said finally. "Burlington, Vermont. The Farmhouse Tap & Grill. Public, neutral. I can be there in three hours."

"I'll be there," Sarah said.

"Come alone. No recording devices. No weapons. Just a conversation between colleagues."

"Agreed," Sarah lied.

She hung up.

Then immediately opened her laptop and began setting up the recording system.

## Scene 2: The Restaurant - Wells Reveals Too Much

### Burlington, Vermont, Evening

Sarah arrived early. Scoped out the restaurant. Busy dinner crowd, multiple exits, large windows facing the street. Public enough that Wells wouldn't try anything violent. Private enough that their conversation wouldn't be overheard if they kept voices low.

The recording device was hidden in her watch—expensive tech David had procured from his hacktivist contacts. Audio and video, broadcasting to an encrypted cloud server in real-time. Even if they searched her and found it, the conversation would already be uploaded.

Wells arrived exactly on time. Same expensive suit, same perfect hair, same aura of absolute control. She slid into the booth across from Sarah like they were old friends meeting for dinner.

"Dr. Chen. Thank you for agreeing to meet."

"Dr. Wells."

A waiter appeared. Wells ordered wine—something French and probably expensive. Sarah ordered water. She needed to stay sharp.

"Let's dispense with the pleasantries," Wells said once the waiter left. "You want information. I want your cooperation. Let's see if we can find mutually beneficial terms."

"Start with the Genesis Protocol," Sarah said. "What is it actually trying to accomplish?"

Wells smiled. Like she'd been waiting for this question. "What do you know about cyclical civilizational collapse?"

"Broad strokes. Bronze Age collapse around 1200 BCE. Rome's fall. Theories about 600-800 year cycles of rise and decline."

"Not theories. Patterns. Documented, repeatable patterns." Wells pulled out a tablet, brought up a graph. "Major civilizations collapse like clockwork. And it's not random. It's systems failure. Too much complexity, too little resilience. Societies optimize for efficiency until a single shock—climate change, pandemic, resource depletion—brings everything down."

The graph showed collapse events going back four thousand years. Each one clustered around the same intervals. 800 years. 600 years. 750 years.

"We're due," Sarah said.

"Overdue, actually. Should have happened already. Would have, except..." Wells zoomed in on the modern section of the graph. "Except the network has been carefully managing the variables. Preventing the worst shocks. Extending the current cycle."

"How?"

"Targeted interventions. World Wars One and Two? Not accidents. Controlled population reductions that prevented worse conflicts. The Green Revolution in agriculture? Network-backed research that prevented mass starvation. AIDS epidemic? Could have been far worse—the network ensured vaccine development was adequately funded."

Sarah felt sick. "You're taking credit for preventing disasters you probably caused?"

"We didn't cause them. We managed them. There's a difference." Wells took a sip of her wine. "But we can't prevent the next collapse. It's already started. Climate change, pandemic potential, resource wars—all the markers are present. Within a decade, probably less, the current global system will fragment. Billions will die. That's not speculation. It's mathematical certainty."

"So the Genesis Protocol is... what? A survival plan?"

"An optimization protocol." Wells pulled up another screen. Genetic markers, bloodline charts, population distributions. "We can't stop the collapse. But we can control who survives. Ensure that the rebuilt civilization is better than what came before."

"Better meaning what?"

"Higher average intelligence. Better disease resistance. Improved neuroplasticity. Reduced violence and tribalism." Wells scrolled through the markers. "We've been breeding these traits into target populations for seventy years. Before that, the network did it for nine hundred years using cruder methods. Arranged marriages. Strategic migrations. Selective culling disguised as plague or war."

"That's eugenics. That's genocide."

"That's evolution guided by intelligence instead of randomness." Wells met Sarah's eyes. "Do you know how many people carry the protected genetic markers now? Globally?"

"I don't."

"Fourteen point three percent. We need fifteen percent for critical mass. Once we hit that threshold, we release the trigger."

Sarah's blood went cold. "The trigger?"

"A genetic targeting virus. Highly contagious. Fatal to anyone who doesn't carry specific protective markers. The markers we've been breeding into protected populations."

"You're going to kill six billion people."

"We're going to save one billion." Wells's voice was calm, factual. "The alternative is chaotic collapse where nine billion die slowly from starvation, disease, and war. Our way is faster. Cleaner. Ensures the survivors can actually rebuild."

Sarah couldn't breathe. Couldn't think. This was worse than she'd imagined.

"You're insane," she managed.

"We're pragmatic. There's a difference." Wells finished her wine. "The network has done this before. Bronze Age collapse? We guided the survivors. Ensured knowledge preservation. Same with Rome's fall. Each time, the next civilization rose faster, stronger, because we ensured the right people survived to rebuild."

"And you think I'll help you with this?"

"You're already part of it." Wells pulled up a genetic profile. Sarah's profile. "Your bloodline has been optimized for pattern recognition and analytical thinking. Exactly the traits needed to manage complex systems during reconstruction. You don't have to die in the collapse, Sarah. You can be part of what comes after."

Sarah felt her watch vibrating slightly against her wrist. The recording was transmitting. Everything Wells had said was being uploaded to the encrypted server.

She just needed to keep Wells talking. Get everything on record.

"What about consent?" Sarah asked. "The six billion people you're planning to kill—do they get a choice?"

"Did the dinosaurs consent to the asteroid?" Wells signaled for another wine. "This isn't about fairness. It's about survival. The current system is unsustainable. We can manage the transition or let it happen chaotically. Those are the only options."

"There's a third option. Stop it. Change the systems that are failing. Fix what's broken instead of burning it down."

"We tried that. For fifty years, the network tried to prevent collapse through conventional means. But the problems are too entrenched. Too many competing interests. Too much momentum toward catastrophe." Wells leaned forward. "I'm showing you this because we need people like you. Smart enough to understand the math. Willing to make hard choices. Strong enough to survive the knowledge of what we're doing."

"Why tell me all this? Why not just kill me?"

"Because killing you would be wasteful. You're valuable. Your genetics, your mind, your potential. We'd rather recruit you." Wells smiled. "And because you're recording this conversation. Your watch. Very clever. Good quality surveillance tech."

Sarah's stomach dropped.

"I let you record me," Wells continued calmly. "Because I want you to have the evidence. I want you to release it. Tell the world what we're planning."

"Why would you want that?"

"Because no one will believe you." Wells's smile widened. "A conspiracy to kill six billion people using genetic targeting viruses? Guided evolution over nine hundred years? It sounds insane. You'll be dismissed as a conspiracy theorist. Again."

"The evidence—"

"Can be explained away. Genetic markers? Natural variation. Population planning? Corporate research. The virus? Theoretical work, never implemented." Wells finished her second wine. "By the time anyone takes you seriously, we'll be at fifteen percent. And then it won't matter anymore."

Sarah's hand was shaking. Wells was right. She'd been right all along.

The truth was too big. Too horrifying. No one would believe it until it was too late.

"I'm giving you a choice, Sarah." Wells stood, preparing to leave. "Release your recording. Try to warn the world. Spend the next six months being dismissed as crazy while we complete the protocol. Or join us. Help guide what comes after. Be part of the solution instead of dying with the problem."

"That's not a choice."

"It's the only choice anyone ever gets: Accept reality or be destroyed by it." Wells placed cash on the table for the wine. "You have forty-eight hours. After that, we begin the final phase. With or without you."

She walked out of the restaurant.

Sarah sat in the booth, shaking, the recording device still transmitting.

She had everything. Wells's confession. The genetic targeting virus. The plan to kill billions.

And Wells was right—nobody would believe it.

But Sarah was going to try anyway.

## Scene 3: The Upload - Racing Against Time

### Safe House Basement, Late Night

Sarah drove back to the farmhouse with her mind racing.

Wells wanted her to release the recording. Expected her to be dismissed as crazy. Was counting on Sarah's exposure attempt to fail.

Which meant Sarah needed to do more than just release it. She needed to make it undeniable. Overwhelming. Too big to ignore.

The moment she reached the basement, she began working.

First: The recording from the restaurant. She uploaded it to five different encrypted cloud services. Sent copies to twenty journalists she'd researched—reputable ones, independent ones, people who'd investigated corporate conspiracies before.

Then: Marsh's complete archive. Everything about the medieval cipher, the bloodline tracking, the network's nine-hundred-year history. Uploaded to Wikileaks, Internet Archive, academic research repositories, anywhere that couldn't be easily deleted.

Then: The GenVault data David had stolen. Personnel files showing network members embedded in governments, corporations, media. Financial records proving the seven companies were coordinating. Research files about the genetic targeting virus.

She created packages. Customized for different audiences. Scientists got the genetic data. Journalists got the human interest stories. Historians got the medieval manuscripts. Activists got the corporate conspiracy evidence.

Everything. All of it. Released simultaneously.

At 2 AM, Sarah's finger hovered over the final upload button.

Once she pressed it, there was no taking it back. Every intelligence agency, every corporation, every person with an internet connection would be able to see what she'd found.

GenVault would know immediately. Would come for her. Would probably kill her.

But the information would be out. Scattered across hundreds of servers, thousands of computers. Impossible to completely suppress.

Sarah thought about Marsh. About Yuki. About twelve generations of her ancestors bred like livestock.

She pressed upload.

And watched as the data began distributing across the internet like a virus.

Status bars filled. Upload complete. Upload complete. Upload complete.

Eighteen different platforms. Twenty-seven journalists. Five Wikileaks mirrors. Three academic journals. Twelve independent news sites.

Done.

Sarah sat back, exhausted.

Her phone rang. The burner phone. How did anyone have this number?

She answered.

"Dr. Chen." Not Wells's voice. Male, unfamiliar, digitally distorted. "You just made a very brave and very stupid decision."

"Who is this?"

"Someone who's been watching you. Someone who's been waiting for you to do exactly what you just did."

"GenVault?"

"No. We're... call us the opposition. The original network, before it was corrupted." The voice paused. "You've just declared war on the most powerful conspiracy in human history. They will come for you. Tonight. Within the hour, probably."

"Let them come. The information is out."

"The information is out but you're not thinking clearly. You just told them exactly where you are."

Sarah froze. "What?"

"The uploads. They're being traced right now. GenVault has people inside every major tech company. Your satellite connection shows this farmhouse's location. You have maybe thirty minutes before an extraction team arrives."

"Shit."

"Run. Now. Destroy the equipment. Take only what you can carry. We'll contact you again when it's safe."

"Who ARE you?"

"The people who remember what the network was supposed to be. Before it became what you're fighting." The voice softened slightly. "You did well, Dr. Chen. Better than you know. But you need to survive the next twenty-four hours. Go. Now."

The line went dead.

Sarah didn't hesitate. Grabbed a hammer from the basement workshop. Smashed the laptops. Smashed the hard drives. Destroyed anything that could contain data or tracking devices.

Kept only her burner phone and a flash drive with encrypted copies of everything.

Upstairs, she heard it. Vehicles approaching. Multiple engines. Moving fast on the rural road.

They were here.

Sarah ran.

## Scene 4: The Pursuit - Hunted Again

### New Hampshire Forest, Night

Sarah crashed through the forest behind the farmhouse, branches whipping her face, roots trying to trip her in the darkness.

Behind her, shouts. Flashlight beams cutting through the trees. Professional pursuit.

She'd left the farmhouse through the back door thirty seconds before the extraction team breached the front. Thirty seconds. That was all that separated her from capture.

But thirty seconds in a forest at night could be enough.

Sarah ran without direction. Just away. Away from the lights, the voices, the coordinated search pattern she could hear organizing behind her.

She'd never been good at outdoors survival. Her childhood was libraries and laboratories. Her adulthood was research facilities and university offices. She didn't know how to navigate by stars or build shelter or move silently through wilderness.

She just ran.

Five minutes. Ten minutes. Fifteen minutes.

Her lungs burned. Her legs screamed. She was running on pure adrenaline and terror.

Behind her, the pursuit continued. They had night vision. Radios. Coordination. She had panic and desperation.

Sarah stumbled into a stream. Ice-cold water shocked through her shoes. She kept running, splashing upstream, remembering something from a movie about dogs losing scent in water.

The voices behind her were getting quieter. More distant.

Was she losing them? Or were they just spreading out, surrounding her?

Sarah climbed out of the stream, kept moving. Her wet shoes squelched with every step. She was freezing, soaked, exhausted.

But alive.

She found a fallen tree, crawled underneath it. Pressed herself into the dead leaves and mud and tried not to breathe too loud.

Flashlight beams swept past. Fifty feet away. Then a hundred feet. Then gone.

Sarah waited. Ten minutes. Twenty minutes. Half an hour.

Silence.

Had they given up? Assumed she'd escaped? Pulled back to search a wider area?

Her burner phone vibrated. Text message from an unknown number:

*North. Two miles. Road. Black sedan. Will wait 15 minutes.*

Sarah looked at the message. Could be the mysterious voice from earlier. Could be GenVault pretending to help, leading her into a trap.

Could be her only chance.

She checked the compass app on her phone. Found north. Started walking.

Through the forest. In darkness. Soaking wet, freezing, every muscle aching.

But she walked.

Two miles felt like twenty. Every sound was a threat. Every shadow could be someone waiting.

But finally, she saw it. A road. Narrow, rural, barely paved. And a black sedan, engine running, headlights off.

Sarah approached slowly. Ready to run if this was wrong.

The passenger window rolled down.

"Get in, Dr. Chen. Quickly."

The voice matched the one from the phone. Male, middle-aged, professional.

Sarah got in.

The car pulled away immediately, no lights until they were half a mile down the road.

The driver was in his fifties, gray hair, weathered face, professional demeanor. Dressed like he'd just left an office job.

"Who are you?" Sarah asked, still breathing hard.

"Someone who's been fighting GenVault longer than you've been alive." He glanced at her. "You can call me Martin. Not my real name, but it'll do. Are you hurt?"

"No. Just... everything hurts."

"That's the adrenaline crash. You'll feel worse in an hour." Martin handed her a bottle of water. "Your upload worked. It's spreading across the internet faster than they can suppress it. Major news outlets are picking it up. Social media is exploding. You actually did it."

"Did what?"

"Broke the story they've been suppressing for seventy years." Martin smiled grimly. "It won't stop them. But it'll slow them down. Make the final phase harder to execute quietly."

"Wells said no one would believe me."

"Wells underestimated how much evidence you had. And how many people have been waiting for proof to surface." Martin turned onto a highway. "The original network—the uncorrupted one—has been preserving evidence for decades. But we couldn't release it ourselves. We needed someone outside the conspiracy. Someone they'd underestimated. Someone like you."

Sarah was too tired to process all of this. "Where are we going?"

"Safe house. Real one this time. Not compromised. You'll rest, recover, and then we'll discuss next steps."

"Next steps?"

"You've exposed part of the conspiracy. Now we stop the rest of it." Martin looked at her. "The genetic targeting virus. Project THRESHOLD. It's real. It exists. And they're planning to release it in six months when they hit fifteen percent population coverage."

"How do we stop them?"

"That's the question, isn't it?" Martin drove in silence for a moment. "The original Genesis Protocol—the one from the 14th century—contains the answer. But it's been fragmented across seven keys. We have pieces. They have pieces. No one has the complete picture."

"The keys," Sarah said. "Like in the medieval cipher."

"Exactly like that. Because it's the same system. The medieval network created a knowledge preservation protocol. Over centuries, it was corrupted into a control mechanism. But somewhere in the original protocol is information on how to stop what they've built."

Sarah felt exhaustion pulling at her. "I don't understand. If there's a way to stop them in the protocol, why would they build the protocol in the first place?"

"Because they didn't build it to create what GenVault is doing. They built it to prevent it." Martin's voice was grim. "The original network saw civilizational collapse coming. Created a system to preserve knowledge and guide recovery. But the modern corporations misunderstood—or deliberately corrupted—the original purpose. Now we need to recover what was lost."

"How?"

"We find someone who can read the complete protocol. Someone with the genetic markers and training to access the full cipher." Martin glanced at her. "Someone like you."

Sarah stared. "What?"

"Your bloodline, Dr. Chen. Twelve generations of genetic optimization. You don't just carry markers for pattern recognition and analytical thinking. You carry markers for accessing genetic memory. Ancestral knowledge encoded in your DNA."

"That's not possible. Genetic memory is—"

"Real. Proven. Suppressed by mainstream science because the network doesn't want people knowing it exists." Martin pulled into a rest stop, parked. "You're exhausted. We'll talk more tomorrow. For now, sleep. You're safe. I promise."

Sarah wanted to argue. Wanted answers. Wanted to understand.

But exhaustion won. Her eyes closed.

And she slept, while the internet exploded with her revelations, while GenVault scrambled to contain the damage, while the world began to learn about the conspiracy that had been guiding human evolution for nine hundred years.

## Scene 5: The Response - The World Reacts

### Safe House, Boston Suburbs, Morning

Sarah woke to sunlight and the sound of news broadcasts.

She was on a couch in a modest living room. Blanket over her. Fresh clothes laid out on the coffee table. The smell of coffee and eggs from somewhere nearby.

Martin sat in an armchair, watching multiple screens simultaneously. News channels, social media feeds, scientific forums.

"You're awake," he said without looking away from the screens. "Good. The world's gone insane and you need to see it."

Sarah sat up, every muscle protesting. Checked her burner phone—dozens of missed calls from numbers she didn't recognize. Hundreds of messages.

"What's happening?"

"Your exposure is the top story on every news platform globally." Martin gestured to the screens. "GenVault's stock crashed overnight. Trading was halted. The other six corporations are in chaos. Governments are launching investigations. Scientists are confirming the bloodline patterns are real. Historians are verifying the medieval manuscripts."

"Are people believing it?"

"Some. Enough." Martin pulled up a news segment. CNN, a serious-looking anchor discussing genetic manipulation conspiracies with surprising credulity. "The mainstream media is still skeptical about the full scope. But the genetic markers are being independently verified. The bloodline tracking is documented. Corporate connections are real."

Another screen showed social media explosion. #GenesisProtocol trending globally. #GenVaultConspiracy. #GeneticManipulation.

"Public opinion is split," Martin continued. "Half think it's the biggest conspiracy in human history. Half think it's elaborate fiction. But even the skeptics can't explain away the evidence."

"What about the virus? Project THRESHOLD?"

"That's where we're losing them. It sounds too extreme. Too movie-plot." Martin switched to a scientific forum. "But geneticists are taking it seriously. Three labs have independently requested samples from people flagged as 'protected' in your data dump. They're finding the markers you described."

Sarah felt hope stirring. "So we're winning?"

"We're not losing. There's a difference." Martin turned to face her. "GenVault released a statement an hour ago. They're not denying the genetic selection program. They're justifying it."

He pulled up a video. Catherine Wells at a press conference, calm and professional.

"Yes, GenVault has been continuing research started nearly a century ago," Wells was saying. "Research into genetic optimization that could help humanity survive future catastrophes. This isn't eugenics—it's insurance. The same way we buy fire insurance or maintain seed banks, we've been preserving genetic diversity that might be crucial for human survival."

"She's spinning it," Sarah said.

"Brilliantly." Martin fast-forwarded. "She admits to the bloodline tracking. Admits to the fertility treatments and genetic therapies. Frames it all as preparedness and crisis management. Never mentions the virus. Never mentions the planned collapse. Just... insurance."

The press conference showed divided reactions. Some journalists looked horrified. Others looked thoughtful, possibly convinced.

"Is it working?" Sarah asked.

"Too early to tell. But she's changed the narrative from 'evil conspiracy' to 'controversial but possibly necessary program.'" Martin paused the video. "We need more. We need proof of the virus. Proof of the fifteen percent threshold. Proof they're planning active genocide, not just passive selection."

"How do we get that?"

"We infiltrate." Martin pulled up building schematics. "GenVault's primary research facility. Outside Boston. That's where Project THRESHOLD is being developed. We need samples. Documentation. Evidence that can't be spun."

"That's suicide. They'll be in maximum security mode after my exposure."

"They will. Which is why we're not going in alone." Martin pulled up another screen. Faces. Dozens of them. "The original network. People who've been fighting GenVault from the inside for decades. Scientists, technicians, security personnel. They've been waiting for a catalyst. You're that catalyst."

Sarah looked at the faces. Young and old. Multiple ethnicities. All with expressions suggesting they'd seen too much.

"They can get us inside?" she asked.

"They can get YOU inside. You specifically." Martin pulled up Sarah's genetic profile. "You carry the markers. You're on the protected list. If you walk into that facility and claim to have reconsidered Wells's offer, they'll bring you in. Show you everything. Recruit you properly."

"And then I steal the evidence from the inside."

"Exactly."

Sarah thought about it. About walking back into GenVault's hands. About pretending to join them while planning to expose their worst secrets.

"When?" she asked.

"Tomorrow. We need time to prepare, to coordinate with the inside team, to give you a convincing backstory for your change of heart." Martin stood. "For now, eat. Rest. Review the evidence we've already gathered. Tomorrow, you become a spy."

Sarah wanted to argue. Wanted to say this was too dangerous, too stupid, too likely to fail.

But she'd already come this far. Already exposed herself. Already declared war on the most powerful conspiracy in history.

Might as well see it through.

"All right," she said. "Tomorrow."

Martin smiled grimly. "Tomorrow, we finish what you started. Or we die trying."

"Not reassuring."

"It's not meant to be. It's meant to be honest." Martin handed her a tablet. "News coverage. Watch. Understand what you've unleashed. Then we'll discuss how to direct the explosion."

Sarah took the tablet. Began watching as the world struggled to process what she'd revealed.

Some believed. Some denied. Some didn't know what to think.

But everyone was talking about it.

The secret was out.

Now came the hard part: Proving it was real before GenVault could finish what they'd started.

Sarah watched the news and prepared for the next phase.

Infiltration. Espionage. Betrayal.

Just another day in the fight against a nine-hundred-year-old conspiracy to control human evolution.

She almost laughed. A week ago, she'd been a geneticist concerned about data theft.

Now she was planning to infiltrate a bioterrorism facility to stop a planned genocide.

How did life get this insane?

But she knew the answer. Life had always been this insane.

She'd just finally learned to see the patterns underneath.
