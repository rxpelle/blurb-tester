# CHAPTER 2 - THE NETWORK (Modern Timeline - Present Day)

## Scene 1: Arrival in New Haven

### Greyhound Station, New Haven - 6:47 AM

Sarah stepped off the bus into gray dawn light that made everything look washed out and temporary.

New Haven was supposed to feel safe. It didn't.

The bus station smelled like diesel and old coffee, harsh fluorescent lights buzzing overhead. A handful of other passengers shuffled toward exits, faces blank with exhaustion. Sarah moved with them, just another tired traveler in wrinkled clothes. Invisible.

Or so she hoped.

She'd spent the four-hour bus ride watching every car on the highway, checking if any followed too consistently. Paranoia, maybe. Or pattern recognition—her brain cataloging threats the same way it cataloged genetic sequences.

Outside, the morning was cold. November in Connecticut, trees mostly bare, frost on the parked cars. Sarah's breath came out in visible clouds. She pulled her jacket tighter—the same jacket she'd been wearing for eighteen hours, smelling like lab chemicals and fear-sweat.

First priority: blend in. Look less like a fugitive scientist.

She found a 24-hour diner two blocks from the station. *Rosie's*—vinyl booths, fluorescent lights, the smell of bacon grease and burned coffee. A waitress with tired eyes and a name tag reading *KAREN* barely glanced at her.

"Coffee. Black," Sarah said, sliding into a corner booth with a view of the door.

"Food?"

"Just coffee."

Karen walked away without comment.

Sarah pulled out the photocopied manuscripts Aris had given her. Spread them on the table, careful not to get them near where coffee might spill. The medieval cipher dots stared back at her, encoding seven centuries of secrets.

*Red dots: Safe locations, high-frequency protective alleles.*

*Blue dots: Dangerous zones, decimated populations.*

*Yellow dots: Neutral ground, intermediary genetic zones.*

Except now Sarah knew it was more than that. The cipher wasn't just predicting plague survival—it was tracking BLOODLINES. Families across generations.

Her coffee arrived. Sarah wrapped her hands around it, grateful for the warmth.

Think. Plan. What do I need?

1. Find Dr. Evelyn Marsh at the address Aris provided.
2. Get answers about the cipher's real purpose.
3. Figure out what GenVault was actually doing with seventy years of genetic data.

But first—

Sarah looked at herself reflected in the diner window. Lab coat gone, but still obviously someone who didn't belong. Scientist clothes: practical slacks, button-down shirt, sensible shoes. GenVault ID badge lanyard still around her neck.

She yanked the lanyard off, shoved it deep in her jacket pocket.

Better. But not enough.

Across the street was a Goodwill. Sarah left money on the table for the coffee—cash, like Aris had taught her—and crossed over.

The thrift store smelled like old fabric and other people's lives. She bought quickly: jeans, a dark hoodie, cheap canvas sneakers. Changed in the bathroom, stuffing her old clothes in the trash. Cut her hair shorter with the scissors from a travel sewing kit—rough, uneven, but different.

She looked at herself in the mirror.

Not Dr. Sarah Chen, MIT geneticist.

Just Sarah. Nobody. Anonymous.

It would have to do.

---

The address Aris had given her was in a residential neighborhood—middle-class houses, quiet streets, the kind of place where neighbors noticed strangers. Sarah walked past it twice, observing.

27 Maple Street. Two-story colonial, white with black shutters. Well-maintained but not ostentatious. A car in the driveway—old Honda, at least ten years. Curtains drawn despite it being morning.

And across the street, parked three houses down: a dark sedan with tinted windows.

Sarah's pulse spiked.

The sedan had been there both times she'd passed. Engine off, but someone was inside—she could see the faint glow of a phone screen through the windshield.

Watching Marsh's house.

Or waiting for someone to approach Marsh's house.

Sarah kept walking, circled the block. Her mind raced through options:

1. Approach directly. Probably get grabbed by whoever was in that sedan.
2. Leave. Give up the only lead she had.
3. Find another way in.

She chose option three.

The neighborhood backed onto a wooded area—a small greenbelt, maybe fifty yards deep, separating one development from another. Sarah entered through a gap in the fence two streets over.

The woods were quiet. Dead leaves underfoot, bare branches overhead. She moved as quietly as she could, remembering childhood camping trips with her father. *Walk heel-to-toe, test your weight, don't snap branches.*

She counted houses through the trees. Third one should be Marsh's. Yes—there was the white colonial, approached from behind.

The back door was visible from here. No sedan watching this angle.

Sarah moved to the edge of the tree line. Waited. Watched.

Nothing moved. No one watching.

She crossed the backyard quickly, expecting shouts or alarms. Reached the back door. Knocked softly.

Silence.

She knocked again.

A voice from inside, sharp and afraid: "Go away or I'll call the police."

"Dr. Marsh?" Sarah kept her voice low. "Dr. Otto Aris sent me. It's about the Aethelred manuscripts."

Silence. Then: "Otto's dead."

"No, he's alive. He helped me escape GenVault last night. He gave me your address and told me about the cipher." Sarah pressed close to the door. "Please. They're hunting me. You're the only person who can help me understand what's happening."

More silence. Sarah could feel someone on the other side of the door, listening. Deciding.

Finally, locks clicked. Three of them. Four. The door opened a crack.

A face peered out—woman, maybe seventy, with wild gray hair and eyes sharp as broken glass. She studied Sarah with an intensity that reminded Sarah of her thesis defense. Being dissected and evaluated.

"You're Chinese," the woman said.

"Chinese-American. My name is Sarah Chen."

"Chen." The woman's eyes narrowed. "Your family from Guangdong province?"

"How did you—"

"Because the cipher tracked families from Guangdong. Eleven bloodlines, marked since the 1300s for specific genetic markers." Marsh opened the door wider. "Get inside. Quickly."

Sarah stepped through.

Marsh bolted the door behind her. All four locks, plus a chain. Then she turned and studied Sarah again.

Up close, Dr. Evelyn Marsh looked like she'd been living underground for forty years. Thin, wiry, her hands stained with ink and what might have been coffee or might have been something else. She wore layers—sweater over shirt over thermal, like she was always cold. The house smelled like old books and paranoia.

"Dr. Aris really sent you?" Marsh asked.

"Last night. He picked me up outside GenVault after I escaped through the ventilation system."

"The vents." Marsh laughed, sharp and bitter. "That's new. In my day we used the loading docks. How is Otto?"

"Scared. Said they've been watching him for twenty years."

"They have been. They watch everyone who figures it out." Marsh walked deeper into the house. "Come on. But don't touch anything. And if I tell you to run, you run. Understood?"

"Understood."

Sarah followed Marsh through a hallway lined with bookshelves—medieval history, genetics textbooks, statistical analysis. Into a study that looked like a bomb had gone off in a university library.

Papers everywhere. Manuscripts—some photocopied, some original—covering every surface. A cork board with strings connecting photos and documents. Maps of Europe with colored pins. Genetic charts. Population data.

It was beautiful and terrifying.

"Welcome to forty years of obsession," Marsh said, gesturing at the chaos. "Tea?"

"I... yes?"

Marsh disappeared into the kitchen. Sarah heard a kettle being filled, the click of a gas stove.

She stood in the study, trying to process the sheer volume of information. The cork board alone was overwhelming—manuscript images connected to modern genetic data, population maps, genealogical charts going back centuries.

And in the center, a photograph of a medieval manuscript page. The same cipher dots Sarah had found in GenVault's database.

Marsh returned with two chipped mugs. The tea smelled like chamomile and something herbal Sarah couldn't identify.

"Sit," Marsh commanded, gesturing to a chair buried under papers.

Sarah moved the papers carefully, sat.

Marsh remained standing, studying Sarah over the rim of her mug. "Otto said you found the correlation. Medieval cipher predicting modern genetic patterns."

"Ninety-four percent accuracy. CCR5-Delta32 distribution mapping perfectly onto 14th-century manuscript dots."

"And you realized that's impossible."

"Unless the cipher-makers had knowledge they shouldn't have possessed." Sarah set her tea down untouched. "Dr. Marsh, what is the Aethelred Cipher?"

Marsh smiled—sharp, humorless. "The wrong question. The right question is: What were the cipher-makers really doing?"

"Then what were they doing?"

"Breeding humans." Marsh set her own tea down, pulled out a manuscript. Original vellum, carefully preserved in archival plastic. "This is from 1338. Nine years before the Black Death reached Europe. Look at the marginalia."

Sarah looked. Red and blue dots, arranged in specific patterns around Latin text about agricultural yields.

"Now look at THIS." Marsh pulled out a genetic chart—modern, printed on regular paper. Family trees with genetic markers highlighted. "This is the Chen bloodline. Your bloodline. Going back twelve generations to Guangdong province, China."

Sarah stared. Her family name was there. Dates. Genetic markers for CCR5-Delta32, for pattern recognition traits, for—

"How did you get this?"

"I stole it. In 1982, when I realized what the project was really about." Marsh overlaid the medieval manuscript on the modern chart. "Watch."

The red dots in the medieval manuscript aligned perfectly with the genetic markers in Sarah's family tree.

"That's impossible," Sarah whispered.

"Exactly. It's impossible for 14th-century monks to know about 21st-century genetics. Unless..." Marsh pulled out more documents. "Unless they were tracking the same thing. Bloodlines. Inheritance. Phenotypes."

"But they didn't have the technology—"

"They didn't need technology. They had TIME." Marsh spread more manuscripts across the desk. "The cipher network started—as far as I can trace—around 400 CE. After Rome fell. A group of scholars decided knowledge was too precious to lose during the collapse. So they created a system to preserve it."

Sarah's mind raced. "A secret network of knowledge preservation."

"Yes. But it evolved. Over centuries, they realized knowledge alone wasn't enough. You needed the RIGHT PEOPLE to understand and apply it." Marsh pointed to a genealogical chart. "So they started tracking bloodlines. Identifying families with desirable traits—intelligence, memory, pattern recognition. They arranged marriages. Guided migration. Documented births and deaths."

"Proto-eugenics."

"Exactly. In the 14th century, they refined it further. The Black Death gave them an opportunity—a massive die-off that they could GUIDE. The cipher marks families with plague resistance. Red dots for those who should survive. Blue dots for those who wouldn't."

Sarah felt sick. "They let the plague kill people. Selectively."

"Worse. I think they CAUSED IT." Marsh pulled out a document—trade route maps overlaid with plague spread patterns. "The first plague cases appeared in trading posts controlled by families in the cipher network. And it spread along routes they controlled. Too perfect to be coincidence."

"That's..." Sarah couldn't finish. Too monstrous. Too calculated.

"And it worked," Marsh continued relentlessly. "After the plague, the European population was smaller but genetically concentrated for specific traits. Intelligence, disease resistance, specific metabolic markers. The cipher network had BRED a new type of human."

Sarah stood, paced. Her tea sat forgotten, going cold. "And GenVault found this? The complete cipher?"

"Not just GenVault. Seven corporations, all descended from the original seven cipher families. They've been continuing the work for seventy years." Marsh pulled out a list of company names. Sarah recognized some: GenVault, BioMerge International, Helix Dynamics. "Using modern fertility treatments, genetic screening, targeted vaccine distribution. Same methods, new technology."

"To what end?"

"That," Marsh said quietly, "is what I've been trying to figure out for forty years. What is the GOAL? What are they building toward?"

Sarah looked at the cork board again. Saw patterns she'd missed before. Migration routes. Population shifts. Genetic marker concentrations increasing in specific regions.

"They're preparing for something," Sarah said slowly. "Another collapse event. Another plague. Something that will kill most of humanity, and they're positioning the 'right' people to survive."

Marsh nodded grimly. "That's my theory. And I think it's soon."

"How soon?"

"Six months. Maybe less." Marsh pulled out recent news clippings—disease outbreaks, climate disasters, political instabilities. "The pattern is accelerating. Just like it did before Rome fell. Before the Bronze Age collapse. The cipher network predicted every major civilization collapse for two thousand years. And they've survived every one by MANAGING who lives through it."

Sarah's hands were shaking. She gripped the edge of the desk. "We have to stop them."

"With what evidence? Photocopied manuscripts and conspiracy theories?" Marsh's laugh was bitter. "Otto tried to publish twenty years ago. They buried it. I tried to go public in 1982. Car accident, three broken ribs, and a very clear message to shut up."

"I have data from GenVault's servers. My cousin is hacking their backups—"

"Data they can claim is fabricated. Or taken out of context." Marsh shook her head. "We need the SOURCE. The original cipher keys. The complete Genesis Protocol that proves their intent."

"What's the Genesis Protocol?"

"The master plan. The complete blueprint for guiding human evolution through collapse events." Marsh pointed to a notation on one of the manuscripts. *Seven keys unlock the Genesis truth.* "It's encoded in seven separate pieces, each held by one of the original cipher families. Get all seven, and you can prove everything—not just what they did, but WHY. Their endgame."

"Where are the keys?"

"That's the problem. I've found references to three. But the locations are encoded, and I never figured out—"

A crash from the front of the house.

Both women froze.

Marsh's face went pale. "Basement. Now."

"What—"

"GO!"

Sarah ran. Marsh grabbed a stack of manuscripts—originals, precious—and shoved them into Sarah's arms. "There's a tunnel. Built in the '80s after the accident. Leads to the neighbor's property."

Footsteps upstairs. Multiple sets. Professional. Quiet.

"What about you?" Sarah whispered.

"I'm too old to run. You're not." Marsh pushed her toward the basement door. "Make this matter. Make my life's work mean something."

"Dr. Marsh—"

"GO!"

Sarah went.

## Scene 3: The Source Documents

The basement was exactly what Sarah expected from a paranoid academic: climate-controlled, organized, and utterly illegal.

Manuscripts in archival sleeves. Original vellum from the 14th century, perfectly preserved. More recent documents—GenVault corporate files that Marsh shouldn't have possessed. Genetic data stolen from classified databases.

Forty years of evidence. Forty years of obsession.

And above, footsteps. Searching the house methodically.

Sarah found the tunnel entrance—behind a false wall panel, obvious once you knew what to look for. She grabbed what she could carry: the manuscripts Marsh had shoved at her, a laptop from the desk, a camera with a memory card.

Behind her, she heard voices filtering down:

"—check the basement—"

"—Dr. Marsh, we just want to talk—"

Then Marsh's voice, loud and defiant: "You're forty years too late. The information is already out."

A lie. Buying Sarah time.

Sarah crawled into the tunnel. It was narrow, dark, smelling of earth and age. She pulled the panel closed behind her, plunging into complete darkness.

She crawled. Her hands scraped concrete. The manuscripts pressed against her chest, irreplaceable and fragile. Behind her, muffled through the walls, she heard shouting.

Then a sound that might have been a gunshot.

Sarah froze, tears burning her eyes.

*Make this matter. Make my life's work mean something.*

She crawled faster.

The tunnel sloped up, emerged in a finished basement—someone else's house. Empty. Sarah climbed out, closed the tunnel entrance. Looked around frantically for an exit.

Stairs. Ground floor. A kitchen, nobody home. She grabbed a coat from a hook—not hers, but who cared—and walked out the front door like she belonged there.

Outside, sirens. Police or ambulance, Sarah couldn't tell. She walked away from Marsh's house, forcing herself not to run. Just a woman out for a morning walk. Nobody important.

Two blocks away, she ducked into an alley and let herself shake.

Marsh was dead. Or captured. Either way, gone.

Sarah looked at the manuscripts clutched in her arms. This was all that remained of forty years of research. Of one woman's dedication to truth.

She found a quiet corner behind a dumpster and opened the laptop Marsh had grabbed. Battery at 43%. No password—Marsh had been prepared for someone to take this if she died.

The desktop was organized meticulously. Folders labeled: BLOODLINES. CORPORATIONS. CIPHER KEYS. GENESIS PROTOCOL.

Sarah opened the Genesis Protocol folder.

Inside were scans of manuscript pages. Medieval Latin, dense theological text. And in the margins, the cipher dots in their coded patterns.

But also notes—Marsh's notes, translating and analyzing.

Sarah read:

*Genesis Protocol - Master Summary (as far as I've decoded):*

*1. Civilization collapse is cyclical. Approx. every 600-800 years, major cultures undergo catastrophic failure. Bronze Age (1200 BCE), Rome (400 CE), Medieval (1347 CE). Next collapse predicted: 2025-2050.*

*2. The cipher network was created to MANAGE these collapses. Not prevent—MANAGE. Ensure specific bloodlines survive with knowledge intact.*

*3. Method: Track families carrying desirable genetic traits. Guide their reproduction through arranged marriages, migration, selective survival during crisis events.*

*4. The Black Death was ENGINEERED. Deliberate release along network-controlled trade routes. Purpose: Cull European population while concentrating beneficial alleles.*

*5. Modern phase (1950-present): Seven corporations continuing the work using biotech. Fertility treatments to guide reproduction. Vaccine programs to select survival. Genetic screening to identify and track bloodlines.*

*6. ENDGAME (partially decoded): Trigger next collapse event when sufficient population carries target markers. Estimated critical mass: 15% of global population. Current estimate: 12.8%. Approaching threshold.*

*7. After collapse: Surviving population will be genetically optimized for [UNKNOWN - this section still encoded]. Something about "pattern completion" and "activation sequence."*

*8. WARNING: The cipher-makers may have misunderstood their own protocol. Evidence suggests the original intent was DIFFERENT from current interpretation. Possible corruption of knowledge over generations.*

Sarah's hands were shaking so hard she nearly dropped the laptop.

They were approaching 15% global coverage. And then they were going to TRIGGER a collapse. Kill most of humanity to "optimize" what remained.

She opened the CIPHER KEYS folder. Marsh had found references to three:

*Key 1: Iron. Last known location: Mainz, Germany, 1347. Status: UNKNOWN.*

*Key 2: Silver. Last known location: Strasbourg, France, 1349. Status: UNKNOWN.*

*Key 3: Living Key. Description: "The flesh made cipher, memory in blood." Last known: Prague, 1355. Status: UNKNOWN.*

*Keys 4-7: No reliable references found. May be deliberately obscured in later cipher corruptions.*

Sarah photographed everything with Marsh's camera. Every document, every note, every scrap of evidence.

She had what she needed. Proof of seventy years of genetic manipulation. Proof of a planned global catastrophe.

But Marsh's warning echoed: *Data they can claim is fabricated.*

She needed the ORIGINAL cipher keys. The medieval artifacts that proved this wasn't conspiracy theory but historical fact.

And she needed help.

Sarah pulled out the phone Aris had given her—burner phone, untraceable. Dialed the number David had made her memorize.

It rang four times. Then: "Hello?"

"David, it's me. Don't say my name."

"Jesus, where ARE you? The news is saying—"

"What news?"

"GenVault announced an internal investigation into missing research data. They're claiming corporate espionage. Your name isn't public yet but—" He lowered his voice. "Sarah, they're saying you stole proprietary genetic databases worth millions. They're getting law enforcement involved."

Sarah closed her eyes. "Did you get the files from my flash drive?"

"Working on it. Their security is... it's sophisticated. I need another six hours, maybe twelve."

"You have four. After that, they'll purge everything and it won't matter." Sarah looked at Marsh's documents. "I'm sending you something. Encrypted email, dead drop account. Historical documents that prove GenVault isn't just stealing data—they're continuing a seventy-year genetic engineering program."

"That sounds insane."

"I know. But it's true. Dr. Evelyn Marsh had proof. Now she's dead and I have it."

Silence. Then: "Fuck. Sarah, this is..."

"I know. I'm sorry. You can still walk away—"

"No. I'm in. All the way." She heard typing. "Send me what you have. I'll cross-reference with the GenVault data when I pull it. If we can show a pattern—historical to modern—that's harder to dismiss as fabrication."

"Thank you." Sarah's voice cracked.

"Don't thank me until we're not in prison." More typing. "Where are you going?"

"I don't know yet. Somewhere I can work. Somewhere they won't find me." She looked at the manuscripts. "I need to write this story. All of it. The medieval cipher, the modern corporations, the planned collapse. Everything. And then we release it everywhere at once."

"That'll take days."

"I know."

"They'll be hunting you."

"I know."

David was quiet for a moment. "Aunt Lin always said you were stubborn. Guess she was right."

Sarah almost smiled. "Tell her I love her. And David? If something happens to me—if they catch me before I finish—"

"I'll make sure the story gets out. I promise."

"Thank you."

She hung up. Looked at the pile of evidence in her lap.

Forty years of research. Seven centuries of conspiracy. And now it was hers to tell.

Sarah stood, gathered everything, and started walking.

She needed to disappear. Find a place to work. Write the truth before they silenced her.

Behind her, sirens wailed. Marsh's house was probably swarming with police and GenVault cleaners.

Ahead, the morning sun was burning through clouds, making everything sharp and clear.

Sarah pulled the stolen coat tighter and disappeared into the city.

She had work to do.

## Scene 5: David's Call

Three hours later, Sarah was on another bus.

This one heading north into Vermont. Rural, remote, somewhere she could vanish. She'd paid cash for a week's rental at a cabin outside Burlington—found the listing on a pay phone, called the owner, made up a story about needing quiet to finish a novel.

The owner had been sympathetic. "Writer's retreat, huh? Good luck. Place is yours, no questions asked."

Perfect.

The bus smelled like stale coffee and someone's overpowering perfume. Sarah sat in the back, Marsh's laptop hidden in her stolen coat, manuscripts in a bag at her feet.

She'd spent the last hour reviewing Marsh's notes. The more she read, the more horrifying it became.

The cipher network had been CAUSING the collapse events. Not predicting them, not managing them—ENGINEERING them. The Black Death had been deliberately released to cull and concentrate European bloodlines. Rome's fall had been accelerated by network manipulation of barbarian migrations.

And now, GenVault and the six other corporations were preparing to do it again. Trigger a collapse that would kill billions, leaving only the "optimized" bloodlines to rebuild.

Sarah's phone buzzed. The burner phone. Unknown number.

She answered carefully. "Yes?"

"It's me." David's voice was tight, urgent. "I got the files."

Sarah's heart leaped. "Everything?"

"Everything. Your manuscript analyses, the genomic correlations, the proprietary population data. It's all here." He paused. "Sarah, this is even worse than you thought."

"How?"

"GenVault isn't just tracking bloodlines. They're CREATING them. I found fertility clinic records—thousands of them. They're using genetic screening to select specific embryos. They're literally designing the next generation."

Sarah closed her eyes. "How long?"

"Twenty years. Maybe longer. They've been guiding reproduction in target populations since at least 2005." More typing. "And there's something else. A project called THRESHOLD. It's tracking global genetic marker distribution in real-time."

"The 15% critical mass," Sarah whispered.

"What?"

"Marsh's notes said they need 15% of the global population carrying specific markers before they trigger the collapse. What's the current percentage?"

Silence. Then: "14.7%."

Sarah's blood went cold. "When will they hit 15%?"

"Based on current fertility treatment rates and migration patterns... six to eight months."

Six months.

Six months until they triggered whatever catastrophe they'd been planning for seventy years.

"We need to release everything," Sarah said. "Now."

"No." David's voice was firm. "Not yet. If we release now, it's just data dumps and conspiracy theories. We need CONTEXT. We need the story. You said you were writing it—how long?"

Sarah looked at the laptop, at Marsh's decades of research waiting to be synthesized. "Three days. Maybe four. To do it right."

"Then take four days. I'll keep the data secure, create redundancies, dead drops, failsafes." He paused. "But Sarah, they're looking for you. GenVault has hired private security. Cyber teams tracking your digital footprint. You need to be invisible."

"I know. I'm going dark. No internet, no calls except this burner."

"Good. Call me in 96 hours. If I don't hear from you by then, I'm releasing everything anyway."

"Understood." Sarah hesitated. "David, if they find you first—"

"They won't. I'm careful. And I've been training for this since I was sixteen." A hint of dark humor in his voice. "Turns out all those years hacking for fun are paying off."

"Thank you. For everything."

"Thank you for trusting me. And Sarah?"

"Yes?"

"Don't get killed. Aunt Lin would never forgive me."

Sarah almost smiled. "I'll do my best."

She hung up.

The bus rolled on through New England countryside. Trees bare and stark against gray sky. Small towns passing in a blur.

Sarah opened a new document on Marsh's laptop. Titled it: THE AETHELRED CIPHER: A History of Engineered Evolution.

She began to write.

*For seven centuries, a secret network has been guiding human evolution. They started with noble intentions—preserving knowledge during Rome's collapse. But over generations, they became something else: architects of extinction, engineers of genocide, playing God with human DNA.*

*This is the story of how they did it.*

*And how we can still stop them.*

The words flowed. Everything Marsh had discovered. Everything Sarah had found. The medieval cipher, the modern corporations, the planned collapse.

Truth, documented and undeniable.

Outside, the world continued on, oblivious. People living normal lives, unaware that in six months, a conspiracy older than any nation might end civilization as they knew it.

But Sarah knew.

And soon, everyone would.

The bus reached Burlington. Sarah got off, caught a taxi to the cabin. Paid cash. Locked herself inside.

For four days, she wrote.

And when she was finished, she would burn down seventy years of secrets.

But first, she had to survive.

**FOUR DAYS LATER**

---

The cabin smelled like instant coffee and obsession.

Sarah hadn't left in ninety-six hours. She'd written 40,000 words, cross-referencing Marsh's research with David's data dumps, building an airtight case. Medieval manuscripts linked to modern genetic databases. Historical collapse events mapped onto current crisis indicators. Fertility clinic records showing deliberate genetic selection.

Everything.

She'd also barely slept. Barely eaten. Her hands shook from caffeine and adrenaline, but the document was DONE.

Sarah read through it one final time, making sure every citation was correct, every connection clear. This wasn't just a story—it was evidence. Journalism and scholarship and whistleblowing all at once.

If they released this with David's data dumps, it would be impossible to dismiss.

She saved it. Encrypted it. Created six backup copies on separate drives.

Then she picked up the burner phone and dialed David.

It rang once. "Please tell me you're done."

"I'm done. Forty thousand words, fully sourced, ready to release." Sarah rubbed her eyes. "How are you?"

"Tired. Paranoid. Pretty sure someone tried to hack my home network yesterday but I routed them through about fifty proxies and they gave up." He sounded wired, running on the same caffeine-fueled terror Sarah felt. "I've set up distribution: WikiLeaks, ProPublica, The Guardian, New York Times, Washington Post, plus about two hundred independent journalists. When we release, it goes everywhere simultaneously."

"When?"

"Tonight. Midnight. That gives major outlets eight hours to review before morning news cycles." Typing sounds. "I'll send you the encryption keys now. Upload your document to the dead drop, and I'll package it with all the data. One massive file drop that proves everything."

Sarah's hands were shaking. "This is it."

"This is it." David's voice softened. "You did it, Sarah. You're about to expose seventy years of genetic manipulation and stop a planned genocide. You're a goddamn hero."

"I'm terrified."

"Heroes usually are." More typing. "Uploading the keys now. Get your document ready. At midnight, we change the world."

"David?"

"Yeah?"

"Thank you. For believing me. For risking everything."

"Family, right?" His voice was warm despite the fear. "Aunt Lin raised us both to do the right thing. This is the right thing."

"It is."

"Okay. Encryption keys sent. Upload your document and then—" He stopped. "Wait."

"What?"

"Someone's at my door."

Sarah's blood went cold. "Don't answer it."

"It's 10 PM. No one should be here." She heard movement through the phone. "I'm checking the security cam—"

Then David's voice changed. Tight. Afraid. "Shit."

"What? What do you see?"

"Two men. Suits. Not police—they're not announcing themselves. They're just..." Breathing. "They're picking my lock."

"Run. Now. Out the back—"

"They're already at the back door too. I'm trapped." More sounds—David moving, maybe grabbing something. "Sarah, listen. I'm uploading everything RIGHT NOW. Even if they take me, it'll still release at midnight. The dead man's switch is active."

"David, no—"

"If I don't log in every twelve hours, the files auto-release anyway. So even if they—" His voice cut off.

Then a crash. Shouting. David yelling: "I already uploaded it! You're too late!"

Then the line went dead.

Sarah sat in the silent cabin, her heart hammering so hard she thought it might break through her ribs.

David.

They had David.

She stared at the phone. At the laptop. At everything she'd built over four sleepless days.

The encryption keys were on her screen. David had sent them before they'd broken in. The document was ready.

All she had to do was upload it, and in—she checked the time—94 minutes, the truth would flood the world.

Even without David to manage it. He'd set up automatic release.

But they had David. Would torture him. Hurt him. Maybe kill him.

Because of her.

Sarah's hand hovered over the laptop keyboard.

Upload the document and doom David to whatever GenVault would do to him.

Or don't upload it and let seventy years of conspiracy continue toward global genocide.

Her cousin. Her family.

Or the world.

There was a knock at the cabin door.

Sarah froze.

Another knock. Then a woman's voice, calm and professional: "Dr. Chen. I know you're in there. I'm not here to hurt you. I just want to talk."

Sarah didn't move.

"My name is Dr. Catherine Wells. I'm on the Board of Directors at GenVault." A pause. "I'm also a descendant of one of the original cipher families. I know what you've discovered. And I know what you're about to do."

Sarah's hands moved to the laptop. Started the upload process. The progress bar appeared: 0%... 1%... 2%...

"Dr. Chen, we have your cousin. David is unharmed, but that can change. Let me in, and we can discuss your options. You release that document, and he dies. You talk to me, and he lives. Simple choice."

3%... 4%... 5%...

Sarah looked at the door. At the laptop. At the impossible choice.

And she remembered Marsh's last words: *Make this matter. Make my life's work mean something.*

She remembered Aris's warning: *The others who found the pattern are dead.*

She remembered the 14.7% of the global population carrying genetic markers. The six months until they triggered collapse.

David's voice: *You're a hero.*

The upload continued. 8%... 9%... 10%...

"Dr. Chen. Last chance. Talk to me, or your cousin dies. And the document gets released anyway—but you've already corrupted it, haven't you? Your source code has a virus I helped design. The moment it's distributed, every journalist who downloads it will get a version that makes you look like a conspiracy theorist. Crazy scientist stealing corporate data and fabricating evidence."

Sarah's hands froze over the keyboard.

No.

That wasn't possible. David had been careful, he'd checked everything—

But what if Wells was telling the truth?

15%... 16%... 17%...

"We've been planning for this scenario for seventy years, Dr. Chen. You think you're the first to try to expose us? The first to gather evidence? You're the TWENTY-THIRD. And every single one before you either joined us or was discredited." Wells's voice was patient, almost kind. "You're brilliant. You'd be an asset. Join us. Help complete the Protocol. Save your cousin. And when the collapse comes, you and your family will be among the survivors."

20%... 21%... 22%...

Sarah's mind raced. Was the document corrupted? Had they sabotaged it?

Or was Wells lying, trying to make Sarah stop the upload?

She looked at the progress bar. At the door. At the phone where David had last spoken.

*I already uploaded it! You're too late!*

Had he?

Or had they stopped him in time?

25%... 26%... 27%...

Sarah made her choice.

She grabbed the laptop, ran to the bathroom, locked the door. It wouldn't hold long, but it didn't need to.

She just needed the upload to finish.

28%... 30%... 35%...

Behind her, the cabin's front door crashed open.

"Dr. Chen!" Wells's voice, louder now. "You're making this harder than it needs to be."

40%... 45%... 50%...

Footsteps in the cabin. Searching. Getting closer.

Sarah watched the progress bar. Come on. Come on.

60%... 65%...

The bathroom door handle rattled. "Dr. Chen. It's over. Come out."

70%... 75%...

"You've won," Sarah called through the door, making her voice shake. Defeated. "Just... don't hurt David. Please."

"Then open the door."

80%... 85%...

"I'm scared."

"I understand. But we're not here to hurt you. We're here to help you understand." Wells's voice was gentle, persuasive. "The collapse is coming whether you like it or not. The only question is whether you're positioned to survive it."

90%... 95%...

"What happens to David?"

"He goes home. Returns to his life. Forgets this happened."

99%...

Sarah took a breath.

100%. Upload complete.

She opened the door.

Dr. Catherine Wells stood there—sixty-ish, professional blazer, gray hair perfectly styled. Behind her, two large men in suits. Security.

Wells smiled. "Smart choice. Now, let's talk about your future."

Sarah handed her the laptop. "The document is already uploaded. In—" she checked her watch, "—eighty-seven minutes, it releases to two hundred journalists worldwide. David set up a dead man's switch. Even if you kill both of us, the truth comes out."

Wells's smile didn't falter. She opened the laptop, checked the upload.

Her expression didn't change.

"Impressive," she said finally. "You actually did it." She closed the laptop. "But you've made one critical mistake, Dr. Chen."

"What's that?"

"You assume the journalists will publish it." Wells gestured to her security. "Take her. Carefully. She's valuable."

The men moved forward.

Sarah backed up. "What do you mean they won't publish it?"

"Because we've been preparing for this exposure for seventy years. We have friends at every major publication. Board members. Editors. Lawyers who'll tie this up in litigation for decades." Wells's smile was sad, almost sympathetic. "You're not the first to try, Dr. Chen. You won't be the last. But the Protocol continues. It always continues."

Sarah's world collapsed.

They'd accounted for everything. Even this.

The security guards grabbed her arms, not rough but firm.

Wells stepped closer. "I meant what I said. You're brilliant. You'd make an excellent addition to the program. In six months, when the collapse event begins, you could be positioned to survive. To help rebuild. To be part of the next phase of human evolution."

"And if I refuse?"

"Then you become a cautionary tale. Another conspiracy theorist who stole corporate data and went mad." Wells nodded to the guards. "Take her to the facility. We'll continue this conversation there."

They dragged Sarah toward the door.

She'd failed.

Failed Marsh. Failed David. Failed everyone.

The truth was out there, but it would never see the light of day.

Wells had won.

The cipher network had won.

They always won.

Sarah was shoved into a van outside the cabin, hands zip-tied, door slamming shut.

Through the window, she watched the Vermont woods disappear.

And somewhere, a clock ticked toward midnight.

When truth would try to emerge.

And fail.

Again.
