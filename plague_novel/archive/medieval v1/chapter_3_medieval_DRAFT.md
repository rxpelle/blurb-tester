# CHAPTER 3 - THE ROAD TO STRASBOURG (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: Leaving Mainz - The Escape

### Rhine River Docks, Dawn

The alarm bells started ringing while they were still half a mile from the river.

Thomas heard them echo across Mainz—cathedral bells, church bells, even the plague bells repurposed for a different kind of death. The Order had found Anselm's body. And now every soul in the city would be looking for them.

"Faster," Margarethe hissed, pulling him down a narrow alley that stank of tanning chemicals and human waste.

They'd been moving through the pre-dawn darkness for over an hour, avoiding the main streets, keeping to the shadows. Margarethe knew every back way, every hidden path through the industrial quarter. But the bells changed everything.

"Will he be there?" Thomas asked, breathing hard. "Your smuggler?"

"He'd better be. I paid him enough." Margarethe checked the street behind them. Clear for now. "And if he's not, we steal a boat and hope we can outrun arrows."

The Rhine came into view—a dark ribbon of water reflecting the first gray light of dawn. The docks were mostly empty at this hour. A few fishermen preparing their nets. A merchant boat being loaded with barrels. And there, at the farthest dock, a small river craft with a single figure waiting beside it.

Margarethe grabbed Thomas's arm. "That's him. Otto the Weasel. Don't let the name fool you—he's the best smuggler on the Rhine."

They crossed the open ground to the dock, exposed and vulnerable. Thomas kept expecting shouts, arrows, the sound of boots on cobblestones. But they reached the boat without incident.

Otto the Weasel was aptly named—thin, nervous, with quick eyes that never stopped moving. He looked at Margarethe and his expression went from relief to horror.

"You're late. And the whole city is—" He stopped, listening to the alarm bells. "Mother of God. What did you do?"

"Nothing that concerns you," Margarethe said, already climbing into the boat. "You're being paid to ask no questions and get us downriver. So get us downriver."

"I'm being paid to smuggle goods, not fugitives who've—" Otto's eyes went wide as he looked past them. "Robes. Gray Robes on the docks. They've seen us!"

Thomas spun. Three men in gray robes were running toward them, maybe a hundred yards away. Two had bows.

"Cast off!" Margarethe shouted. "Now!"

Otto fumbled with the mooring rope, his hands shaking. "This is madness, this is—"

"NOW!"

The rope came free. Otto shoved the boat away from the dock with a long pole. They drifted into the current, agonizingly slow.

"Down!" Margarethe yanked Thomas to the deck.

The first arrow whistled overhead, struck the mast with a solid *thunk*.

The second arrow—Thomas didn't see it coming. Just heard Margarethe's sharp intake of breath, saw her stumble.

"Margarethe!"

She was still standing, but an arrow protruded from her left shoulder, just below the collarbone. Her face had gone pale.

"I'm fine," she said through gritted teeth. "Stay down."

A third arrow hit the side of the boat. A fourth splashed into the water beside them.

But they were in the current now, picking up speed. Otto worked the sail with frantic efficiency, catching the morning wind. The distance between them and the docks grew—fifty yards, seventy-five, a hundred.

The Gray Robes had stopped shooting. Too far now. But Thomas could see more figures gathering on the docks. Someone was shouting orders. They'd pursue, of course. On horseback along the river road, or in faster boats.

"Let me see," Thomas said, reaching for Margarethe.

"I said I'm fine." But her voice was weaker now. She swayed slightly.

Thomas ignored her protest, moved closer to examine the wound. The arrow had gone deep into the muscle of her shoulder. Blood soaked her cloak, dripped onto the deck.

"You're not fine. You're bleeding."

"It's just a—" She stopped, took a breath. "Just a flesh wound."

"That's deep in the muscle. Maybe touching bone." Thomas had seen enough injuries in the monastery infirmary to recognize a serious wound. "It needs to come out. Now, before—"

"Before what?" Margarethe's eyes challenged him.

"Before it gets worse."

Otto called from the tiller: "There's a medical kit under the forward bench. Basic supplies. But if you're thinking of pulling that arrow, do it quick. We can't have her screaming when we pass the watchtowers."

Margarethe's jaw tightened. "I don't scream."

Thomas found the kit—leather satchel containing bandages, a bottle of something that smelled like strong wine, needle and thread, a knife. He brought it back to where Margarethe sat.

"This is going to hurt," he said.

"Everything hurts. Just do it."

Thomas had never done this before. Had watched the infirmarian treat injuries, had helped hold patients still, but never actually—

"Thomas." Margarethe's hand gripped his wrist. Her palm was cold, clammy. "If you don't do it, I'll do it myself. And I'll probably pass out halfway through. So stop thinking and act."

She was right. He was thinking too much.

Thomas examined the arrow. It had gone through the thick wool of her cloak, through her dress, deep into the shoulder muscle. The shaft was smooth, well-made. At least it wasn't barbed.

"On three," he said. "One—"

He pulled.

Margarethe's breath hissed between her teeth, but she didn't scream. Didn't even cry out. Just went rigid, her good hand clenching the side of the boat hard enough that her knuckles went white.

The arrow came free. Blood followed, dark and thick.

Thomas grabbed a cloth from the kit, pressed it hard against the wound. "Hold this. Press as hard as you can."

Margarethe obeyed, her face gray now, eyes slightly unfocused.

Thomas opened the bottle—definitely wine, strong and cheap. "This is going to hurt worse than the arrow."

"Just—" Margarethe managed. "Just do it."

He pulled the cloth away and poured wine directly into the wound.

This time she did cry out—a sharp, bitten-off sound that ended almost before it began. Her whole body went taut.

Thomas worked quickly, packing the wound with clean cloth, wrapping bandages tight around her shoulder and under her arm. His hands were shaking but he forced them steady. Margarethe needed him steady.

"Done," he said finally. "It's... it's not good, but it's the best I can do."

Margarethe nodded, not speaking. Her eyes were closed, her breathing shallow.

"You should lie down. Rest."

"Can't. Need to watch for—" She swayed.

Thomas caught her before she fell, eased her down onto a pile of canvas sailcloth. "I'll watch. You rest."

"Thomas—"

"I'll watch," he repeated firmly. "You saved my life yesterday. Today I save yours. That's how this works."

Margarethe looked at him for a long moment. Then, finally, she nodded and closed her eyes.

Within minutes, her breathing had evened out into the rhythm of sleep. But Thomas could see the flush starting on her cheeks, the sweat beading on her forehead despite the cool morning air.

Fever. The wound was already infected.

Otto called softly from the stern: "She's tough, that one. Tougher than most men I've smuggled."

"How long to Strasbourg?" Thomas asked.

"Three days if the wind holds. Maybe four." Otto spat over the side. "She got family there? Someone who can care for her properly?"

"A contact. An alchemist."

"An alchemist?" Otto's eyebrows rose. "Well, they know about poisons and cures, I suppose. Let's hope he's the healing kind and not the other."

Thomas looked down at Margarethe. Her face was peaceful in sleep, but he could see the pain in the lines around her eyes, the tension in her jaw even unconscious.

Three days. Maybe four.

She had to survive that long.

Behind them, Mainz faded into the morning mist. Ahead, the Rhine stretched toward Strasbourg and whatever came next.

Thomas pulled out the iron key from his pocket, looked at the eye symbol staring back at him.

They'd killed for this. Margarethe was wounded because of this. And they still didn't know what it truly unlocked.

But sitting there on a smuggler's boat with a wounded woman who trusted him to keep watch, Thomas made a silent promise.

Whatever this key opened, whatever secrets the cipher held, he would see it through.

For his grandfather Wilhelm.

For Brother Hamo.

For Margarethe, who'd given everything to protect knowledge she couldn't even read.

He would see it through to the end.

## Scene 2: The River Journey - Fever and Discovery

### Day Two on the Rhine

Margarethe's fever started that night.

Thomas had tried to keep her comfortable—made a crude shelter from canvas to protect her from the wind, got her to drink water when she was lucid enough. But as the sun set on their first day on the river, her skin grew hot to the touch and her breathing turned ragged.

By the second morning, she was delirious.

"Father," she mumbled, eyes open but unseeing. "Father, I remembered. All 217. I didn't forget a single one."

Thomas dampened a cloth with river water, pressed it to her forehead. "Shhh. Rest. Save your strength."

"Can't rest. Have to remember. If I forget, they're lost. The library burns if I forget."

Otto glanced over from the tiller. "How is she?"

"Worse," Thomas said quietly. The bandage on her shoulder was stained dark, and when he'd changed it that morning, the wound had looked angry and red, the flesh around it hot and swollen.

"There's willow bark in the kit. Might help with the fever."

Thomas had already tried that. It hadn't helped.

He stayed by Margarethe's side as the boat drifted downstream, watching the riverbanks slide past. Small villages. Terraced vineyards. The occasional watchtower where Otto would tense until they'd passed and no alarm was raised.

To keep himself from panic, Thomas turned to the manuscripts they'd taken from the cathedral library. Spread them carefully on the deck, weighted down with stones from the ballast. Used the iron key to decode what he could.

And that's when he saw it.

The pattern that changed everything.

He'd been studying a merchant's ledger from 1290—nearly sixty years old. The marginal cipher dots were faded but still visible. He aligned the key with the textual anchor, counted the dots that appeared through the aperture.

Position marker: 4-2-7.

But beside the position marker was something else. A different notation. Not colored dots but tiny scratched symbols that looked like Roman numerals.

DCCXL.

740.

A date? In what system?

Thomas pulled out another manuscript—a theological commentary from 1215. Found a similar notation in the cipher.

DCCC.

800.

His heart started beating faster. He checked manuscript after manuscript, looking for the pattern.

Every cipher entry had two components: the position marker (location) and a number (date?).

But the dates didn't make sense. The manuscript from 1290 referenced 740. The one from 1215 referenced 800.

The dates were BEFORE the manuscripts were created.

Thomas sat back, mind racing.

Unless...

Unless the cipher wasn't recording when the knowledge was hidden. It was recording when the knowledge was CREATED.

Or when events the knowledge described had occurred.

Which meant the network wasn't tracking current information. It was tracking ANCIENT information. Knowledge that predated the manuscripts by centuries.

How far back did this go?

He found the oldest manuscript they had—a fragment of text from what looked like the 9th century, barely legible. The cipher marks were primitive compared to the later ones, but they were there.

And the date notation: CCCC.

400.

Four hundred CE. When Rome was falling.

"My God," Thomas whispered.

"What?" Otto called. "Find something?"

Thomas looked up. "The cipher. It's not medieval. It's... it's ancient. The network has been operating since at least the fall of Rome. Maybe longer."

Otto shrugged. "Means nothing to me. I just smuggle things."

But it meant everything to Thomas.

Wilhelm hadn't been part of a recent conspiracy. He'd been part of something that stretched back nearly a thousand years. An organization that had survived the fall of Rome, the chaos of the dark ages, centuries of war and plague and change.

And they'd been preserving knowledge the entire time. Hiding it. Protecting it.

From what?

From the Order, obviously. But why did the Order want to destroy it?

"Thomas."

He looked down. Margarethe's eyes were open, lucid for the first time in hours.

"How long?" she asked, her voice rough.

"How long what?"

"How long have I been..." She trailed off, too weak to finish.

"Since yesterday morning. The fever started last night." He touched her forehead—still burning hot. "How do you feel?"

"Like I'm dying." But she tried to smile. "Which means I'll probably live. The dying ones don't feel anything."

Thomas wasn't sure that was true, but he didn't argue. "Can you drink?"

She nodded. He helped her sit up enough to take water from the skin. She drank greedily, then fell back, exhausted by the effort.

"Did I talk?" she asked. "In the fever?"

Thomas hesitated. "Some."

"Did I say anything..." She looked away. "Anything you shouldn't know?"

He could lie. Should lie, probably. But he was terrible at lying.

"You talked about your father. About remembering 217 locations. About being the library that can't burn."

Margarethe closed her eyes. "So you know."

"Know what?"

"That I'm a fraud. That I can't actually read. That everything I know is just... memory. Tricks." Her voice was bitter. "Your grandfather could READ the cipher. I just memorize what my father told me."

Thomas was quiet for a moment. Then: "Margarethe, do you know how many locations I can remember without the key?"

"What?"

"Maybe ten. Perhaps fifteen if I really concentrate. And I can READ, I can use the key, I have my grandfather's 'gift.'" He leaned closer so she could see his face. "You've memorized 217 locations perfectly. Kept them in your head for years. That's not fraud. That's brilliance."

"It's a party trick. A trained dog can memorize—"

"A trained dog doesn't understand what it's remembering. You do. You navigate the network, you know which locations connect to which, you understand the structure." Thomas gripped her good hand. "Your father didn't just teach you to memorize. He taught you to be a living index. To BE the network in human form. Do you understand how extraordinary that is?"

Margarethe's eyes were wet. She blinked, looked away. "You're just saying that because I'm dying."

"You're not dying. And I'm saying it because it's true."

She was quiet for a long time. Then, so softly he almost didn't hear: "Thank you."

They sat in silence as the boat drifted on. The sun was high overhead now, warm despite the autumn chill. On the banks, farmers worked their fields, children played near the water, life continued on despite plague and conspiracy and the slow turning of great, terrible wheels.

"Thomas," Margarethe said finally. "If I don't make it—"

"You'll make it."

"If I don't," she continued firmly, "the locations are in my head. All 217. I should tell you. In case—"

"You'll tell me in Strasbourg. When you're recovered. When we have time to write them all down properly." Thomas smiled. "I'm not letting you off that easily. You're going to have to actually work to teach me."

Despite everything, Margarethe laughed—a weak, breathy sound, but genuine. "You're more stubborn than you look."

"I learned from the best."

She fell asleep again after that, but more peacefully this time. The fever still burned, but something in her face had relaxed.

Thomas returned to the manuscripts, to the ancient dates hidden in cipher marks, to the revelation that the conspiracy he'd stumbled into was far older and stranger than he'd imagined.

The sun moved across the sky. The river flowed on.

And somewhere ahead, Strasbourg waited.

## Scene 3: Margarethe's Confession

### Day Three, Dawn

Margarethe woke to the smell of fish and river water.

She was alive. That was surprising.

The last coherent memory she had was Thomas pulling an arrow out of her shoulder, pain so bright it had whited out her vision. After that, fragments. Fever dreams. Her father's voice. The weight of 217 locations pressing down on her skull like stones.

Now the fever had broken. She could tell by the way her thoughts moved clearly again, the way the world had solid edges instead of bleeding into nightmare.

She opened her eyes.

Thomas was asleep beside her, slumped against the mast, a manuscript open in his lap. He looked exhausted—dark circles under his eyes, his hair matted, his robes stained with her blood and river water.

He'd stayed with her. The entire time.

Margarethe tried to sit up. Her shoulder screamed in protest—the wound was still there, still bad, but no longer infected. Someone had changed the bandages, cleaned the wound. Thomas, obviously. Weak, useless Thomas who'd never treated an injury in his life.

She managed to lever herself up to sitting. The world swayed, then steadied.

They were still on the river. Otto the Weasel was at the tiller, watching her with cautious interest.

"You're awake," he said quietly. "Good. The boy's been half mad with worry."

Margarethe looked at Thomas again. His hand was close to hers, like he'd been holding it before he fell asleep.

"How long?" she asked.

"Three days. We'll be in Strasbourg by evening if the wind holds." Otto spat over the side. "You should wake him. He needs to eat something that isn't stale bread and worry."

But Margarethe didn't wake Thomas yet. Just sat there, watching him sleep, trying to understand the strange feeling in her chest.

People didn't stay. That was the lesson life had taught her. Her mother had died before Margarethe could even remember her face. Her father had been tortured to death. Every network contact who'd tried to help her had eventually been killed or had abandoned her when the risk got too high.

People left. That was what they did.

But Thomas had stayed.

Weak, naive, useless Thomas who couldn't lie to save his life and stumbled over his own feet and had no business being in this world of knives and secrets and blood.

He'd stayed.

Finally, she reached out and touched his shoulder. "Thomas."

He jerked awake, disoriented. Saw her sitting up and his face transformed. "Margarethe! You're—the fever, is it—"

"Broken." She managed a smile. "You did well. For someone who's never played nursemaid before."

"I was terrified the entire time." He rubbed his eyes. "How do you feel?"

"Like I was shot with an arrow and spent three days dying of fever." She tested her shoulder—stiff, painful, but functional. "But alive. Thanks to you."

"You saved my life first. On the river path, remember? I was just repaying the debt."

"Is that what we are now? Keeping accounts?"

Thomas looked at her directly. "No. I don't think it is."

The moment stretched between them, something unspoken and complicated. Margarethe looked away first.

"We should talk," she said. "About what I said. In the fever."

"You don't have to—"

"I do." She took a breath. "You know I can't read. You know about the 217 locations, about being a living library. But there's more. Things you need to understand if we're going to keep working together."

Thomas settled himself more comfortably. "I'm listening."

So Margarethe told him.

About her mother, who'd been part of the network before Margarethe was born. A brilliant woman who could read Greek and Arabic as easily as Latin, who'd helped translate ancient texts that the Order wanted destroyed.

About the pregnancy that had been complicated from the start. About her mother dying in childbirth, bleeding out while the midwives tried desperately to save her.

About her father, Heinrich, raising a daughter alone in a world that didn't value daughters the way it valued sons.

"I was maybe five when he realized I couldn't learn to read the way other children did," Margarethe said. "The letters would move around on the page. Change places. I could never hold them still long enough to make sense of them."

"Some people are born that way," Thomas said. "It's not a deficiency. Your mind just works differently."

"That's what my father said. But he also knew it meant I couldn't be a scholar the way my mother had been. Couldn't translate texts or copy manuscripts." Margarethe touched the scar on her jaw unconsciously. "So he found another way. If I couldn't read, I could remember. And I was good at remembering."

She described the training. Hour after hour of her father teaching her to visualize, to create memory palaces in her mind, to link information in chains and patterns.

"He'd show me a page from a manuscript. I'd look at the dots—not the words, just the geometric patterns of colored dots. And I'd remember. Perfectly. Every position, every color, every detail."

"That's extraordinary," Thomas said.

"That's survival." Margarethe's voice was flat. "The network needed people who could carry information that couldn't be written down. People who could walk into a city, recite locations to contacts, and walk out without ever touching a piece of paper. I became that person."

"Your father must have been proud."

"He was terrified." Margarethe looked out at the river. "Because if the Order ever discovered what I could do, they wouldn't just kill me. They'd torture me for every location in my head. All 217 of them. And I'd break eventually. Everyone breaks."

"You didn't break when they killed your father," Thomas pointed out.

"I wasn't captured. If I had been..." She shook her head. "I know myself, Thomas. I'd like to think I'm strong enough to die before talking. But I don't know if that's true."

They sat in silence for a while. Otto called something about a watchtower ahead, warned them to stay low.

"Why tell me all this?" Thomas asked finally.

Margarethe considered the question. "Because you trusted me with your grandfather's key. Because you stayed with me through the fever when you could have taken the manuscripts and continued on alone. Because..." She stopped, looking for the right words. "Because I need you to understand what I am. Not some warrior woman from a song. Not some brilliant scholar. Just a woman who can't read, who's been running her entire life, who's terrified every single day that she'll forget one of the locations and betray everything her father died for."

"You won't forget," Thomas said with absolute certainty.

"How can you know that?"

"Because forgetting isn't something you do. Remembering is who you ARE." He met her eyes. "And for what it's worth, I don't see you as a warrior from a song or a scholar. I see you as Margarethe. Who's brilliant and terrifying and saved my life and lets me decode locks because I'm better at that than fighting. Who matters. Deeply."

Margarethe felt her throat tighten. "You barely know me."

"I know enough."

She wanted to argue. Wanted to push him away the way she pushed everyone away. But she was too tired, too weak, too worn down by fever and pain and three days of him caring for her without asking for anything in return.

"Thank you," she said finally. "For staying. For..." She gestured vaguely. "All of it."

"Thank you for not dying. It would have been very inconvenient."

Despite everything, Margarethe laughed. It hurt her shoulder but she didn't care.

Otto called from the stern: "Strasbourg ahead! We'll dock within the hour!"

Margarethe tried to stand. Her legs trembled but held.

Thomas was beside her instantly, offering support. She took it, leaning on him more than she'd ever leaned on anyone.

"We'll need to find Johannes the Alchemist," she said. "He's our contact here. My father's friend. If anyone can help us understand the cipher better..."

"Then we find him." Thomas adjusted his grip, taking more of her weight. "Together."

"Together," Margarethe agreed.

And as the spires of Strasbourg appeared on the horizon, she allowed herself to believe—just for a moment—that maybe she didn't have to carry everything alone anymore.

## Scene 4: Strasbourg - The Alchemist

### Evening, Strasbourg

Johannes the Alchemist was not what Thomas expected.

The address Margarethe had given him led to a narrow building in the university quarter, wedged between a bookbinder and a tavern. Three stories of timber and plaster, windows darkened with soot, and a small sign by the door showing a serpent eating its own tail—the ouroboros, ancient symbol of alchemy.

"This is it," Margarethe said. She was leaning heavily on Thomas, her face pale from the walk from the docks. The shoulder wound had reopened slightly during the journey.

Thomas knocked.

No answer.

He knocked again, louder.

From inside, a crash. Cursing. Then footsteps.

The door flew open.

The man standing there was young—maybe twenty-five at most—with wild dark hair tied back in a queue, several days' worth of beard, and bright, slightly manic eyes. He wore robes that might once have been scholarly but were now stained with what looked like ash, blood, and various colored substances. His hands were ink-stained and burned in places.

"Yes? What? I'm in the middle of a very delicate—" He stopped, staring at them. "Wait. Margarethe?"

"Hello, Johannes."

"Margarethe! By all the saints!" He grabbed her good arm, pulled her inside. "You look half dead. What happened? Who shot you? Never mind, tell me inside. You!" He pointed at Thomas. "You're the boy? Wilhelm's grandson?"

"I'm Thomas, yes, but I'm not—"

"Excellent! Come in, come in! Don't stand in the street like a target!" Johannes pulled them both through the doorway and slammed it shut behind them, throwing multiple bolts.

The interior of the building was chaos.

The ground floor was part library, part laboratory, part workshop. Bookshelves lined every wall, crammed with volumes in multiple languages. Tables were covered with alchemical apparatus—glass vessels, crucibles, distillation equipment. Strange substances bubbled over small flames. The air smelled like sulfur and roses and something unidentifiable but probably dangerous.

"Excuse the mess," Johannes said, clearing a space on a bench by shoving aside what looked like someone's partial skeleton. "I've been working on a translation of Galen's medical texts and the Arabic commentaries and somewhere in there I started an experiment with mercury amalgamation and—well, you don't care about that. Sit. Both of you. You look terrible."

Margarethe sat gratefully. Thomas remained standing, too fascinated by the surroundings to sit.

Johannes was examining Margarethe's shoulder. "Arrow wound. Three, four days old? Infected, obviously. You've been treating it with what, wine and hope?"

"Wine and clean bandages," Thomas said.

"Better than nothing. Barely." Johannes moved to a shelf, began pulling down jars. "I have yarrow, calendula, honey from bees that feed on specific flowers—don't ask how I know which flowers, it's complicated—and if you're very lucky, I still have some of the Arab physician's salve that Margarethe's father gave me five years ago."

He moved with frantic energy, mixing substances in a mortar. Thomas watched, amazed.

"You're not what I expected," Thomas admitted.

"What did you expect? Old man with a long beard muttering about turning lead to gold?" Johannes laughed. "I'm twenty-six. I studied at Paris until my experiments set fire to my dormitory and I was asked to leave. Politely. With armed escorts. Now I work here, translating ancient texts and occasionally blowing things up by accident."

"He's being modest," Margarethe said through gritted teeth as Johannes cleaned her wound. "He's one of the most brilliant natural philosophers in Europe. And completely insane."

"The two often go together." Johannes applied a thick paste to the wound. The smell was sharp, medicinal. "This will hurt. But tomorrow it will hurt less. And in three days, you'll be functional again. Probably."

Margarethe hissed in pain but didn't pull away.

Thomas watched Johannes work. Fast, efficient, clearly knowing exactly what he was doing despite the chaotic surroundings.

"You're part of the network," Thomas said. It wasn't a question.

"Was. Am. Will be." Johannes wrapped fresh bandages around Margarethe's shoulder. "My father was Wilhelm's friend. They studied together in Paris. When Wilhelm started building the network, my father joined. He taught me everything before he died—where the texts are hidden, how the cipher works, what we're really protecting."

"Which is?" Thomas asked.

Johannes looked at him with those bright, intense eyes. "Everything. The accumulated knowledge of human civilization going back two thousand years. Medical texts that could save thousands of lives. Mathematical treatises that could revolutionize commerce. Philosophical works that could change how we understand the world." He gestured at his shelves. "The Church says this knowledge is dangerous. The Order says it leads to catastrophe. They're both right, in a way. Knowledge IS dangerous. But the alternative is worse."

"The alternative is ignorance," Margarethe said.

"Exactly!" Johannes tied off the bandage. "There. Keep it clean, don't use that arm for a week, and you'll probably survive to annoy me again."

He turned to Thomas. "Now. You have the Iron Key, yes? Wilhelm's key?"

Thomas pulled it from his pocket. The eye symbol seemed to catch the lamplight.

Johannes took it with reverent hands. "My God. I've only seen this in drawings. Your grandfather guarded it with his life."

"He died for it," Thomas said quietly.

"I know. I'm sorry." Johannes set the key on his workbench like it was made of glass. "Do you understand what you're carrying?"

"A tool to decode the cipher. Margarethe showed me how."

"It's more than that." Johannes pulled out a manuscript from one of his shelves. "The cipher has layers. Surface layer—locations and basic information. Anyone with patience can decode that. Second layer—dates, connections, patterns. Requires skill to understand. Third layer—the deep structure. The real knowledge. That requires a key."

He demonstrated, showing Thomas how the Iron Key revealed patterns invisible without it.

"Your grandfather helped CREATE this system. The keys aren't just tools—they're the authorization to access the network's deepest secrets."

"How many keys are there?" Thomas asked, though he suspected he knew the answer.

"Seven." Johannes's face was serious now. "One for each of the founding families. Wilhelm's family held the Iron Key—the Eye that Sees, they call it. It reveals locations and patterns."

"What about the others?"

"Scattered across Europe. Hidden. Protected. Some have been lost over the centuries. Others..." Johannes hesitated. "Others are held by people who no longer understand what they have."

Margarethe leaned forward. "The Silver Key. You said you knew where it was."

Johannes nodded slowly. "I found it. Five years of research, following references in old texts, tracking rumors. It's here. In Strasbourg. Held by Bishop Heinrich Vogel."

"A bishop?" Thomas frowned. "Is he part of the network?"

"No. That's the problem." Johannes started pacing, his energy returning. "The Silver Key—the Hand that Guides, it's called—was held by the Strasbourg family line for generations. They were network members, protected it carefully. But the line died out thirty years ago. No heirs. The key passed to the Church as part of an estate. The bishop has no idea what it is. To him, it's just an ornate religious artifact. He keeps it in his private treasury along with other church valuables."

"Can we buy it from him?" Thomas asked.

Johannes laughed. "With what money? And even if we had money, he wouldn't sell. That treasury is his pride—he shows it off to important visitors, brags about the ancient artifacts he's 'protecting for God.'"

"So we steal it," Margarethe said flatly.

"So we steal it," Johannes agreed. "Which is why I've been planning this for two years."

He went to a desk, pulled out drawings. Detailed sketches of a cathedral—St. Thomas's, Thomas recognized it from the Strasbourg skyline. The drawings showed floor plans, guard rotations, treasury location.

"The bishop hosts a feast tomorrow night," Johannes explained. "Important clergy from across the region, wealthy merchants, nobility. It's an annual event—celebrating some saint or other, I forget which. The point is, the cathedral will be full of people, guards will be distracted, and the bishop will be too busy entertaining to pay attention to his treasury."

"You want us to steal from a cathedral full of guards during a feast?" Thomas said. "That's insane."

"It is!" Johannes grinned. "But it's also our only chance. After tomorrow, the bishop leaves for Rome. Won't be back for months. The treasury will be sealed, triple-guarded. Tomorrow night is the window."

Margarethe studied the drawings. "What's the plan?"

"You and Thomas go in as guests. Wealthy merchants from Mainz, fleeing the plague, seeking the bishop's blessing. I've already forged invitations—my handwriting is excellent when I'm not in a hurry. During the feast, you excuse yourselves, slip away, break into the treasury, take the Silver Key, return before anyone notices you're gone."

"That simple?" Thomas said skeptically.

"Of course not. There will be complications. There always are. But—" Johannes looked at both of them seriously. "This is our chance to reunite two of the seven keys. To access deeper layers of the cipher. To understand what Wilhelm and your father and all the others died to protect."

Thomas looked at Margarethe. She was pale, injured, had nearly died three days ago.

"I can do it," she said, reading his expression. "One night. I can manage one night."

"You can barely walk."

"So you'll have to be charming enough for both of us." She almost smiled. "Think you can manage that?"

Thomas thought about breaking into the Mainz cathedral. About killing a man, even indirectly. About everything he'd become since Hamo had pressed that key into his palm.

"I suppose I'll have to learn."

Johannes clapped his hands together. "Excellent! Then we begin preparation immediately. You'll need proper clothes—merchants, not monks. I'll teach you the right way to speak to bishops and nobility. And Thomas, you'll need to learn how to pick a better lock than a cathedral library door. The treasury uses a Bavarian mechanism. Very sophisticated."

"Wait," Thomas said. "Tomorrow night? We just arrived. Margarethe is wounded. I don't know how to pretend to be a wealthy merchant—"

"Which is why we start now." Johannes was already pulling clothes from a chest. "No rest for the wicked, as they say. And we, my friends, are about to become very wicked indeed."

Despite everything—the pain, the exhaustion, the insanity of what they were planning—Thomas found himself smiling.

For the first time since fleeing Mainz, he felt something other than fear.

He felt hope.

## Scene 5: The Second Key

### Johannes's Workshop, Late Night

Hours later, Thomas's head was spinning with information.

Johannes had spent the evening teaching them everything—how to walk like merchants (confident, appraising), how to speak to nobility (deferential but not servile), which wines were fashionable (he had no idea, but Johannes did), and most importantly, how the Bavarian lock mechanism on the treasury door worked.

Now, past midnight, Margarethe had finally collapsed into exhausted sleep on Johannes's guest bed. Johannes and Thomas sat in the workshop, the Silver Key drawings spread between them.

"She's stronger than she looks," Johannes said quietly, watching the stairs where Margarethe had disappeared.

"I know."

"My father told me about Heinrich. About what the Order did to him. About Margarethe memorizing everything because she couldn't read." Johannes poured wine into two cups. "When my father died—natural causes, thankfully—I thought about going into hiding. Giving up the network, just being a normal alchemist blowing things up for paying customers."

"Why didn't you?"

"Because someone has to remember. Someone has to keep trying." Johannes took a drink. "Your grandfather understood that. He knew the risks. Took them anyway."

Thomas turned the Iron Key over in his hands. "Johannes, what IS the Genesis Protocol? Everyone keeps mentioning it, but no one will explain."

Johannes was quiet for a long moment. "I don't know. Not fully. The knowledge was fragmented deliberately—split across the seven keys. But from the fragments I've translated, it seems to be... instructions."

"Instructions for what?"

"For surviving what comes next." Johannes pulled out an old manuscript, pointed to marginal notes. "The network's founders believed civilization moves in cycles. Rise, flourish, collapse. Rise again. They thought they saw a pattern—major collapses every 600 to 800 years."

Thomas thought about the dates he'd found in the cipher. The references going back to Rome's fall, possibly earlier.

"They experienced it," he realized. "When Rome fell, they were there. They watched knowledge burn, libraries destroyed, civilization fragmenting into chaos."

"Exactly. So they created a system to preserve what mattered. Not just books—those burn too easily. But knowledge encoded in ways that could survive. The cipher, the keys, the network of people who could carry information in their heads." Johannes tapped the manuscript. "And somewhere in all of this is the Genesis Protocol. The complete instructions for preserving civilization through collapse."

"Why split it? Why not just write it all down in one place?"

"Because power corrupts. Knowledge concentrated in one place, in one person's hands, becomes dangerous. It becomes control instead of preservation." Johannes looked at the seven key symbols drawn on his wall. "Split across seven keys, held by seven different families, no single person can abuse it. You need collaboration. Agreement. Trust."

"What happens if someone gets all seven keys?"

"Then they can read the complete Protocol. Understand exactly what the network's founders intended." Johannes's expression was troubled. "But here's the question that keeps me awake at night: What if the Protocol's original purpose isn't what the Order thinks it is? What if the network itself has been corrupted over centuries?"

Thomas frowned. "What do you mean?"

"The Order claims the network is trying to control humanity, to play God with knowledge. The network claims it's just preserving information for future generations. But what if both are true? What if the Protocol started as preservation and became control? What if—" He stopped. "Sorry. I theorize too much. It's a hazard of my profession."

"No, it's a good question." Thomas thought about Anselm's dying words. *The Protocol... someone has to complete it... or everything burns...*

What if Anselm had been right? What if the network really was the only thing preventing catastrophe?

Or what if the network itself was the catastrophe?

"We need all seven keys," Thomas said finally. "We need to know the truth."

"Agreed. Which is why tomorrow night is so important." Johannes rolled up his drawings. "Get some sleep. Tomorrow you need to be charming, sophisticated, and most importantly, able to pick a Bavarian lock in under two minutes."

"Two minutes?"

"That's how long it takes guards to do their rotation past the treasury corridor. Any longer and we're caught." Johannes grinned. "No pressure."

Thomas went to his own sleeping space—a pile of blankets in what Johannes called the "storage room" but was actually more like a library that had exploded. He lay down, exhausted but unable to sleep.

Tomorrow night they would steal from a bishop. Would break into a cathedral treasury during a feast. Would reunite two of the seven keys for the first time in generations.

And every step took them deeper into a conspiracy that stretched back a thousand years.

Thomas closed his eyes and tried not to think about all the ways this could go wrong.

Instead, he thought about the pattern. The beautiful, terrible pattern hidden in centuries of cipher marks.

Knowledge and catastrophe, preservation and control, truth and lies all woven together so tightly that maybe they couldn't be separated.

Maybe that was the real secret of the Genesis Protocol.

Not how to save civilization.

But how to understand that maybe civilization couldn't be saved at all.
