# CHAPTER 5 - THE RESCUE (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: The Moral Choice - To Save or Abandon

### Forest Outside Strasbourg, Two Days Later

They'd been hiding in the forest for two days, moving from one abandoned shelter to another, always staying ahead of the search parties they could hear in the distance.

Thomas sat on a fallen log, studying the manuscript by morning light filtering through bare branches. The bloodline patterns were clearer now that he understood what he was looking at. Families tracked across generations. Traits bred deliberately. His own line, engineered for forty generations.

"We should keep moving," Margarethe said. She was sharpening her knife on a whetstone, the rhythmic scraping sound oddly soothing. "East toward Prague. Every hour we stay near Strasbourg is an hour they can catch us."

"I know."

"Then why are you hesitating?"

Thomas looked up from the manuscript. "Johannes."

Margarethe's hand stopped mid-stroke. "Johannes is captured. Probably dead by now. We can't help him."

"We don't know that. Two days isn't long enough to—" Thomas stopped. "If he's alive, they're torturing him. For information about us. About the network."

"Which is why we need to leave. Put distance between us and whatever he tells them."

"Or we rescue him."

The words hung in the cold morning air.

Margarethe set down the knife and whetstone. "That's suicide. The bishop's prison is in the cathedral. The same cathedral we just stole from. They'll be on maximum alert. Guards everywhere. And even if we somehow got in, we'd never get out alive."

"Probably not," Thomas agreed.

"Then why are you suggesting it?"

Thomas thought about Brother Hamo, dying to give him the Iron Key. About Wilhelm, tortured to death by the Order. About Margarethe's parents, murdered by the network they'd served.

"Because I'm tired of leaving people behind to die," he said quietly. "Hamo died for me. Wilhelm died for this. Your parents died for refusing to be tools. And now Johannes—brilliant, eccentric Johannes who helped us despite the risk—is being tortured because we involved him in our theft."

"He chose to help us."

"And we chose to accept that help knowing it might get him killed." Thomas stood, facing Margarethe directly. "I know it's suicide. I know the smart thing is to run. But I can't... I can't keep running while people who helped me suffer and die."

Margarethe was quiet for a long moment. Then: "You've changed."

"You said that before."

"I mean it more now." She picked up her knife again, but didn't resume sharpening. "The Thomas I met a month ago would have chosen the pragmatic option. Would have prioritized the mission over the person. Now you're talking about throwing away everything—both keys, all our knowledge, your own life—for a rescue that probably won't work."

"I know."

"Good. Because I agree with you."

Thomas blinked. "What?"

"Johannes saved my life when I was sixteen. After my father died. He helped me understand the network, taught me to navigate it alone, gave me contacts and safe houses." Margarethe stood, began checking her weapons. "I've been trying to convince myself to leave him behind because it's logical. But logic isn't everything. Sometimes you have to do the stupid, dangerous thing because it's right."

Thomas felt relief wash through him. "So we try?"

"We try. And we probably die. But we try." Margarethe strapped on her knife, her small crossbow, a length of rope. "We'll need a plan. The cathedral will be heavily guarded. We can't just fight our way in."

"I have an idea," Thomas said. "But you're not going to like it."

## Scene 2: The Infiltration - Returning to the Cathedral

### Strasbourg Cathedral, Evening

Thomas walked through the cathedral's main entrance wearing monk's robes he'd stolen from a traveling friar's cart.

His heart hammered. Every guard, every priest, every person who looked at him was a potential threat. But he kept his head down, moved with the shuffling gait of someone old and tired, and headed toward the side chapel.

The disguise was thin. If anyone looked closely, if anyone recognized him from the feast two nights ago, it was over.

But people didn't look closely at monks. Especially not in plague times when holy men were everywhere, praying for the sick, administering last rites, begging for alms.

Thomas found the side chapel he'd scouted earlier—small, dark, rarely used. Knelt as if in prayer. Waited.

Margarethe should be in position now. The plan was insane, but it was all they had.

He pulled out a small vial from his sleeve. Oil of vitriol—Johannes had taught him to make it during their brief time together. Dangerous stuff. Burned through almost anything.

Thomas poured a few drops on the lock of the side door leading to the cathedral's interior passages. The acid hissed, eating through the mechanism. In thirty seconds, the lock would be destroyed.

Behind him, footsteps. A priest entering the chapel.

Thomas froze, head bowed in prayer.

"Brother," the priest said. "The chapel is closing for—"

An explosion. Distant, but loud enough to shake the cathedral stones. From the direction of the bishop's residence.

The priest ran toward the sound. Shouts echoed through the cathedral. Guards mobilizing.

Margarethe's diversion. She'd promised to make it spectacular.

Thomas stood, pushed open the now-unlocked door, slipped into the interior passages.

The cathedral was chaos. Guards running toward the explosion. Priests shouting orders. Somewhere, bells were ringing.

Thomas moved against the flow, heading down instead of up. Toward the dungeons beneath the residence where prisoners were kept.

He passed two guards running upstairs. Neither spared him a glance—just another monk, probably seeking shelter from whatever was happening.

Down stone stairs. Into darkness lit only by torches. The air grew colder, damper. The smell changed—rot and human waste and fear.

The dungeon.

Two guards stood outside the cells. Young, nervous, clearly wanting to join whatever was happening above.

Thomas approached them, keeping his head bowed. "Brothers, I've been sent to pray with the prisoner. The bishop wants his soul saved before—"

"No one's allowed down here," one guard interrupted. "Bishop's orders."

"The bishop himself sent me. To offer last rites before the execution at dawn."

The guards exchanged glances. "We weren't told about any execution."

"That's because it's secret. The prisoner is connected to the theft. The bishop wants him dealt with quietly." Thomas pulled out a forged document—more of Johannes's work, saved from his workshop. "See? The bishop's seal."

It was dark. The seal looked official enough. The guards were young and confused and wanted to believe someone else had authority.

"Fine. Five minutes. That's all."

Thomas entered the cell corridor. Three cells, all occupied. Found Johannes in the farthest one.

The alchemist was barely recognizable. Beaten, bloodied, one eye swollen shut. Chained to the wall. But alive. Breathing.

"Johannes," Thomas whispered.

The alchemist's good eye opened. Focused. Widened in recognition and horror. "Thomas? You idiot. You shouldn't be—"

"Shut up. I'm getting you out."

"You'll be captured. Both keys will—"

"I said shut up." Thomas pulled out his lock picks. Started working on the cell door. "Margarethe's creating a diversion. We have maybe ten minutes before they realize what's happening."

"You came back for me."

"Of course we came back. Did you think we'd abandon you?"

"I thought you'd do the smart thing. The logical thing."

"We tried. Turns out we're both terrible at logic." The cell lock clicked open. Thomas moved to Johannes's chains. Started picking those locks too.

"They tortured me," Johannes said quietly. "For two days. Asking about the network. About you. About where you'd go."

"What did you tell them?"

"Nothing useful. Lies. Misdirection. But..." Johannes's voice broke. "Everyone breaks eventually. Another day, maybe two, and I would have—"

"But you didn't. That's what matters." The chains fell away. Thomas supported Johannes's weight. "Can you walk?"

"I can barely stand."

"Then lean on me. We're leaving. Now."

Outside the cell, shouts. The guards had figured out something was wrong.

Thomas and Johannes stumbled into the corridor. The guards were drawing swords.

"Sorry about this," Thomas said.

Then Margarethe dropped from a ventilation shaft above, landing between Thomas and the guards.

She was a blur of motion. Knife flashing. One guard down, clutching a bleeding arm. The other backing away, sword raised but eyes showing fear.

"Go!" Margarethe shouted. "I'll handle this!"

Thomas half-carried Johannes toward the stairs. Behind them, sounds of fighting. Margarethe was good, but she was fighting trained soldiers in an enclosed space.

They made it to the stairs. Started climbing.

More guards above. Coming down to investigate.

Thomas looked around desperately. Nowhere to go. Trapped between guards above and below.

Then a door opened to the side. A priest—old, wrinkled, with kind eyes.

"This way. Quickly."

Thomas didn't question. Pulled Johannes through the door.

The priest led them through a hidden passage, moving with surprising speed for his age. "I knew Wilhelm," he said quietly. "Your grandfather. Good man. This network... it's not what it used to be."

They emerged in a storage room. The priest handed Thomas a key. "Side exit. Through the garden. Guards are all dealing with the fire and the fight. You have minutes. Maybe less."

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Just... survive. Understand what the Protocol really means. Stop them if you can." The priest disappeared back into the passage.

Thomas and Johannes stumbled through the storage room, into the garden.

Margarethe appeared from another direction, breathing hard, blood on her knife. "Guards down. Not dead, but they won't follow for a while. We need to—"

An arrow struck the ground between them.

Gray Robes. Three of them. In the garden. Bows drawn.

"Run," Margarethe said.

They ran.

## Scene 3: The Escape - Johannes's Condition

### Safe House, Strasbourg Outskirts

They made it to the safe house—a different one, further from the city—just as Johannes collapsed.

Thomas caught him, eased him down onto a straw pallet. The alchemist's breathing was labored, his face gray.

"He's dying," Margarethe said bluntly, checking his injuries. "Internal bleeding. Broken ribs. Infection already setting in. We need a physician."

"We can't risk—"

"I know we can't. But without one, he'll be dead by morning."

Johannes coughed, blood speckling his lips. "Not... dead yet. Just... resting."

"Save your strength," Thomas said.

"For what? I'm done, Thomas. I know it. You know it." Johannes grabbed Thomas's hand with surprising strength. "So... so stop pretending and listen. I need to... to tell you about the third key."

Margarethe leaned closer. "The one in Prague? The human key?"

"Not... not in Prague. Here. Was here. In Strasbourg." Johannes coughed again, more blood. "Woman named... named Adela. Carried the third key in her blood. Genetic memory. The network bred her line for... for forty generations. Just like yours. Just like Margarethe's."

"Where is she?" Thomas asked.

"Dead. Plague took her. Three weeks ago." Johannes's breathing was getting worse. "But... but she had a daughter. Teenage girl. Maria. The key passed to her. She doesn't know. Doesn't understand what she carries."

"Where?"

"Convent. Outside the city. St. Catherine's. The nuns took her in when... when Adela died." Johannes gripped Thomas's hand tighter. "You need to find her. Bring her with you. She's the third key. Without her... you can't access the next layer of the Protocol."

"We'll find her," Thomas promised.

"Good. Good." Johannes closed his eyes. "The Protocol... it's not what they think. The network... they corrupted it. Changed it. The original purpose..."

"What was the original purpose?"

"To prevent... to prevent the collapse. Not engineer it. Not control who survives. But they... they twisted it. Turned preservation into... into selection. Turned saving humanity into... into playing God."

Johannes coughed violently. When he pulled his hand away from his mouth, it was covered in blood.

"Johannes—"

"Let me... let me finish. Please." The alchemist's good eye focused on Thomas with desperate intensity. "The seven keys. They don't just unlock information. They unlock... locations. Seven locations. Each one holds a fragment of the original Protocol. The complete truth. What it was really meant to do."

"Where are these locations?"

"Scattered. Strasbourg has one. Prague has one. Constantinople. Cairo. Somewhere in England. Two others... I never learned." Johannes was fading, his voice getting weaker. "You need all seven fragments. Read together... they show the truth. Show what the network was... before corruption."

Margarethe knelt beside the pallet. "Rest now. We'll find them. We'll complete what you started."

"No. What Wilhelm started. What... what your father started, Margarethe. What Adela started. We're all... just links in a chain. Going back... going back centuries."

Johannes's breathing was slowing. Becoming labored.

"The key to understanding... is seeing the pattern. The whole pattern. Not fragments. Not pieces. The... the whole."

His eye closed. His breathing stopped.

For a moment, Thomas hoped he was just unconscious. But when Margarethe checked for a pulse, she shook her head.

"He's gone."

Thomas sat back, numb. Another death. Another person who'd helped them, dead because of it.

"He bought us knowledge with his life," Margarethe said quietly. "The third key. The locations. The truth about the Protocol's corruption. We honor him by using what he gave us."

Thomas knew she was right. But it didn't make it hurt less.

They buried Johannes in the forest behind the safe house. No marker. No ceremony. Just two people digging in cold earth, laying to rest a brilliant man who'd died for a cause he believed in.

When it was done, Thomas stood over the grave in silence.

"We need to find Maria," Margarethe said finally. "The third key. If she's at St. Catherine's Convent, we need to get her before the Order figures out she exists."

"How do we even approach this? She's a teenage girl who just lost her mother. We can't just tell her she's a genetically engineered key to a thousand-year-old conspiracy."

"We tell her the truth. Show her the cipher. Let her see what she is." Margarethe started walking back toward the safe house. "And we give her a choice. Come with us and unlock her heritage. Or stay hidden and hope the Order never finds her."

"That's not much of a choice."

"It's more than most people in the network ever got."

## Scene 4: St. Catherine's Convent - Finding Maria

### St. Catherine's Convent, Next Morning

The convent sat on a hill overlooking Strasbourg, far enough from the city to avoid the worst of the plague but close enough to take in refugees and orphans.

Thomas and Margarethe approached as travelers seeking shelter. Not entirely a lie—they were travelers, and they did need shelter.

The abbess was a severe woman in her fifties. "We have little to spare for wayfarers. The plague has brought many to our door."

"We're seeking someone specific," Margarethe said. "A girl named Maria. Her mother was Adela."

The abbess's expression hardened. "What business do you have with Maria?"

"Her mother was... a friend of our family. We promised to look after the daughter if anything happened." Thomas pulled out a small pendant—one of the items Johannes had saved from his workshop. "Adela gave this to our father years ago. Said to return it to her daughter if she died."

It was a calculated risk. The pendant had cipher marks on it, invisible unless you knew what to look for.

The abbess examined it. "This was Adela's. I saw her wear it many times." She looked at them with sharp eyes. "You're not pilgrims, are you? You're from the network."

Thomas and Margarethe tensed, ready to run.

But the abbess continued: "Adela told me some of it before she died. About the cipher. About what Maria carries. She made me promise to protect the girl from the Order. But she also said someone from the network might come. Someone who needed what Maria has."

"We need her help," Thomas said carefully. "Not to use her. To help us understand something. Something that could be important."

The abbess studied them for a long moment. "Follow me."

She led them through the convent to a small room where a girl sat reading by candlelight. Maybe fourteen, with her mother's dark hair and sharp features.

"Maria," the abbess said gently. "These people knew your mother. They need to speak with you."

The girl looked up. Her eyes were red from crying, but intelligent and alert. "About what?"

"About what you are," Thomas said. "About what your mother was. About the knowledge you carry without knowing it."

## Scene 5: The Third Key - Awakening Memory

### Private Room, St. Catherine's Convent

Maria listened to their story with growing incredulity.

"You're saying my mother was part of a secret conspiracy? That I'm... what? Genetically engineered to remember things?"

"Not remember consciously," Thomas explained, pulling out the manuscripts. "It's deeper than that. Your bloodline carries information encoded in patterns that manifest as instinct, intuition, dreams. Have you ever known things you shouldn't know? Understood things you were never taught?"

Maria hesitated. "Sometimes I dream about places I've never been. Languages I've never heard. When I read Latin, it feels... familiar. Like remembering instead of learning."

"That's the genetic memory," Margarethe said. "Your ancestors' knowledge, passed down through generations. Your mother had it. Her mother before her. Back forty generations."

"And you want to... what? Use me somehow?"

"We want to show you what you carry," Thomas said. He placed the Iron Key and Silver Key on the table. "These unlock cipher marks in manuscripts. They reveal hidden information. But there's a third key—not metal, but human. You."

He opened a manuscript, aligned both keys with the text. The familiar pattern appeared—locations, bloodlines, connections.

"Touch the page," Thomas said. "While the keys are active. Just... touch it."

Maria reached out hesitantly. Her fingers brushed the manuscript where the cipher marks glowed.

And gasped.

Her eyes went wide. Unfocused. Seeing something beyond the room.

"What is she—" the abbess started.

"Genetic memory activating," Margarethe said. "The cipher is triggering ancestral knowledge stored in her DNA. Just watch."

Maria's hand moved across the page, tracing patterns no one else could see. She began speaking, but not in German. In Latin. Then Greek. Then a language Thomas didn't recognize.

"What's she saying?" the abbess asked.

"Location coordinates," Thomas said, recognizing the pattern. "She's reciting the third fragment of the Genesis Protocol. The part her bloodline was bred to carry."

Maria spoke for ten minutes, her voice steady and clear but her eyes still distant. She drew symbols in the air, traced patterns on the table, spoke words that sounded ancient and powerful.

Then she collapsed.

Margarethe caught her, eased her into a chair. "It's all right. You're all right. The first activation is always overwhelming."

Maria's eyes focused again. Normal. But changed somehow. Like she was seeing the world through different eyes.

"I remember," she whispered. "Not my memories. Theirs. My mother. My grandmother. Back and back. Forty generations of women carrying this... this knowledge."

"What did you see?" Thomas asked gently.

"A location. In Prague. Where the fourth fragment is hidden." Maria looked at her hands like they belonged to someone else. "And I know... I know how to read it. How to understand it. The knowledge is in me. It's always been in me."

She looked up at Thomas and Margarethe with eyes too old for her young face. "They made us into living books. Didn't they? The network. They bred us to carry information in our blood because paper burns and stone crumbles but genetic memory survives."

"Yes," Thomas said. No point lying.

"And my mother died protecting this? This... curse?"

"Your mother died of plague," the abbess said firmly. "Not because of the network."

"But the plague came because the network allowed it," Maria said with eerie certainty. "I can see it now. In the memories. The pattern. They let the plague spread because it served their purpose. Thinned the population. Removed the weak. Tested the bloodlines."

She stood, suddenly decisive despite having just experienced something that should have shattered her. "You're going to Prague. To find the fourth fragment. And you need me to read it."

"We're giving you a choice," Thomas said. "You can stay here. Safe. Hidden. The Order doesn't know you're the third key yet. You could live a normal life."

"A normal life?" Maria laughed, bitter and sharp. "I just felt forty generations of memories flow through me. I know things no fourteen-year-old should know. I dream in languages I've never learned. Normal died the moment I touched that manuscript."

She looked at the abbess. "Mother Superior, I need to go with them."

"Maria—"

"I need to understand what I am. What my mother was. What forty generations of my family died to protect." Maria's voice was steady, resolved. "And maybe... maybe I can help stop whatever the network is planning. Use this curse for something good instead of just carrying it forward to the next generation."

The abbess looked at Thomas and Margarethe. "Will you protect her?"

"With our lives," Margarethe said.

"Then go. All of you. Before the Order finds this place." The abbess pressed a small bag into Maria's hands. "Money. Food. Your mother's journal. She would want you to have it."

Maria hugged the abbess, quick and fierce. Then turned to Thomas and Margarethe. "Where's Prague?"

"East," Thomas said. "Through plague country. Past the Order's strongholds. It'll take weeks."

"Then we should start walking." Maria picked up the bag, adjusted it across her shoulder with the practicality of someone much older. "I can tell you what I saw. In the memories. About the fourth fragment. About what the Protocol really says."

They left the convent as the sun rose, three people carrying two metal keys and one human key, heading east toward Prague and whatever answers waited there.

Behind them, the abbess watched from the gate until they disappeared into the forest.

Then she went inside and began burning records, erasing any mention of Maria's name, protecting what she could of the girl's past even as her future became uncertain and dangerous.

The third key had been found.

The quest continued.

And somewhere, the network and the Order both realized that the bloodlines were gathering, the keys were uniting, and the Genesis Protocol might actually be read in its entirety for the first time in centuries.

The race was accelerating.
