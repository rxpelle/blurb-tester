# CHAPTER 6 - THE QUEST BEGINS (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: The Decision - From Running to Seeking

### Forest Road East of Strasbourg, Three Days After Leaving the Convent

They'd been walking for three days, and Thomas was starting to understand the difference between fleeing and questing.

Fleeing was reactive—running from the Order's Gray Robes, from the bishop's guards, from the network's hunters. Always looking backward, always expecting pursuit, every sound a potential threat. Living in constant fear of being caught.

Questing was active—moving toward something rather than just away from danger. They still checked for pursuit, still moved carefully, still avoided main roads where the Order might be watching. But the energy was different. Forward-focused. Purposeful.

They were no longer just trying to survive. They were trying to accomplish something.

"Tell me about the seven keys again," Thomas said as they walked through forest that was slowly giving way to rolling farmland. The landscape was changing as they moved east—fewer dense woods, more cultivated fields, though many were abandoned or poorly tended thanks to the plague reducing the population that worked them.

Maria answered without hesitation, her young voice carrying the accumulated knowledge of forty generations: "Seven keys unlock seven locations where fragments of the Genesis Protocol are hidden. The complete Protocol was too dangerous to keep in one place—too vulnerable to destruction by fire or conquest or the Order's purges. So the founders fragmented it. Divided the knowledge across Europe in places they deemed secure."

She walked with the confidence of someone much older, her fourteen-year-old body carrying memories and wisdom that spanned centuries. "The Iron Key you carry—Wilhelm's key, the Eye that Sees—unlocks the first fragment. The Silver Key you stole from the bishop—the Hand that Guides—unlocks the second fragment. I am the third key—the living memory that can access and interpret what metal keys alone cannot reveal."

"Four more keys to find," Margarethe said, scanning the horizon with the tactical awareness of someone who never fully relaxed. "Any idea where they are?"

"Two of them, yes. The fourth key is in Prague—that's why we're heading there. It's called the Voice that Speaks, and it's hidden in the Jewish quarter's scholar's library. My family has protected knowledge of its location for nineteen generations." Maria's expression grew distant, accessing genetic memory. "The fifth key is in Constantinople. The Foot that Walks. Hidden in the remains of the Great Library that survived the Fourth Crusade's destruction."

"And the other two?" Thomas asked.

"Lost. Or at least, lost to my bloodline's knowledge. The sixth and seventh keys—the Heart that Feels and the Mind that Knows—were hidden so well that even the network's records became fragmented over centuries. Johannes knew more, but..." Maria's voice faltered slightly, showing her actual age beneath the ancient wisdom. "But he died before he could tell you everything."

They walked in silence for a moment, each remembering Johannes's death in the barn outside Strasbourg. The brilliant alchemist tortured for knowledge, rescued too late, dying with information still locked in his broken body.

"So we find the keys we can," Thomas said, making the decision as he spoke. "Prague for the fourth key. Constantinople for the fifth. And we search for leads to the sixth and seventh while we're gathering the others."

"That could take years," Margarethe pointed out practically. "Years of traveling through plague country. Through territories controlled by the Order or the network or just bandits and warlords taking advantage of chaos. We could die a hundred different ways before finding even half the keys."

"We could," Thomas agreed. "Or we could keep running. Hide somewhere remote and hope the Order never finds us. Live quiet lives carrying pieces of knowledge we don't fully understand."

He stopped walking, turned to face both Margarethe and Maria directly. "But I'm done running. Done letting other people die for this while I just try to survive. Hamo died to give me the Iron Key. Wilhelm was tortured to death protecting the network's knowledge. Your parents, Margarethe—murdered for refusing to be tools. Johannes captured and killed because he helped us. All of them died for fragments of this Protocol. I won't let their deaths be meaningless by refusing to complete what they started."

Margarethe studied him with those sharp eyes that missed nothing. "You've really changed. The monk I met a month ago would have chosen survival over mission. Would have prioritized staying alive over pursuing abstract knowledge."

"The monk you met a month ago hadn't watched people die for him. Hadn't discovered he was genetically engineered for a purpose he never chose. Hadn't learned that a thousand-year conspiracy was breeding humans like livestock and would probably continue for another thousand years unless someone stopped it." Thomas met her gaze steadily. "I'm still terrified. Still have nightmares about being captured and tortured. Still want to run and hide. But I'm more terrified of what happens if we don't at least try to understand and expose what the network has become."

Maria spoke quietly: "My mother tried to run. Tried to have just one child and live a normal life away from the network's breeding programs. They killed her for it during childbirth. Made it look like complications. My father tried to investigate her death, tried to resist the network's control. The Order tortured him to death while the network looked the other way. Running didn't save them. Compliance didn't save them. The only choice that might matter is fighting back."

She adjusted her pack, squared her young shoulders with determination inherited from forty generations of women who'd faced impossible choices. "I'm fourteen years old. I should be learning to run a household, preparing for marriage, studying scripture and domestic arts. Instead I'm carrying genetic memory of a conspiracy that's been manipulating humanity for a millennium. I didn't choose this burden. But I can choose what I do with it. And I choose to help you find the keys and expose what the Protocol really says versus what the network has corrupted it into."

Margarethe was quiet for a long moment. Then she smiled—a real smile, not her usual tactical assessment or grim determination. "Well then. I suppose we're doing this. Actually questing instead of just surviving. Seeking all seven keys instead of running with the two we have. Probably dying in the process but at least dying for something that matters."

She started walking again, her hand resting casually on her knife hilt. "Prague is three weeks away if we avoid main roads and move carefully. Longer if we encounter problems—which we will, because problems are inevitable when you're fugitives carrying knowledge that multiple powerful organizations want to destroy or control."

"What kind of problems?" Maria asked.

"Gray Robes hunting us. Network agents trying to recapture their genetic resources. Plague-ravaged villages that might see strangers as disease carriers to be driven away or killed. Bandits exploiting the chaos. Nobles whose territories we'll cross who might be network members or Order supporters. Winter coming, which makes travel harder and more dangerous." Margarethe listed threats with the casual competence of someone who'd been surviving dangerous situations for years. "But we knew this would be hard when we started. At least now we're choosing to do it rather than just reacting to what's happening to us."

Thomas felt something settle in his chest. Not quite peace—that was too much to ask given their circumstances. But purpose. Direction. The difference between being a leaf blown by wind and being an arrow shot toward a target, even if hitting that target was unlikely.

"Tell me about Prague," he said to Maria as they continued walking. "What do you know from your family's memories? What should we expect when we arrive?"

Maria's eyes went distant again, accessing information encoded in her genetics. "The Jewish quarter is called Josefov. It's been home to scholars and mystics for generations. The network has maintained contacts there since before my bloodline's memory extends—at least twenty-five generations, maybe longer. The scholar's library where the fourth key is hidden survived three major fires and two pogroms. My ancestors helped protect it, ensured its rebuilding, passed down knowledge of the key's location through genetic memory because writing such information down was too dangerous."

"What is the fourth key?" Thomas asked. "You called it the Voice that Speaks. What does that mean?"

"It's a manuscript," Maria said. "Not a metal key like the Iron and Silver Keys you carry. Not a human key like me. But a specific text—the Golem of Prague manuscript, which contains kabbalistic knowledge about creating life from clay. Hidden within that text, using the cipher system the network created, is the fourth fragment of the Genesis Protocol."

Thomas frowned. "Creating life from clay? That's Jewish mysticism. How does that connect to the network's knowledge preservation system?"

"The network has always worked across religious and cultural boundaries," Maria explained, her voice carrying the patient wisdom of teachers who'd answered this question for generations. "Christian monasteries. Islamic libraries. Jewish scholarship. Greek philosophy. They preserved knowledge wherever it could be safely hidden, regardless of whose theology or culture protected it. The Golem manuscript is genuine kabbalistic teaching. But it's also a container—a hiding place for information that has nothing to do with mysticism and everything to do with understanding how human civilizations collapse in predictable cycles."

She walked in silence for a moment before continuing. "That's what the Genesis Protocol really is. Not instructions for creating some perfect civilization or engineering human evolution toward some ideal. It's a warning. An explanation of why complex societies fail every 600-800 years. Why the Bronze Age collapsed. Why Rome fell. Why this current plague is devastating Europe. The pattern. The cycle. The inevitable result of societies becoming too complex, too interconnected, too fragile."

"And the network corrupted that warning into a plan," Margarethe said darkly. "Turned 'here's why civilizations collapse' into 'here's how we engineer collapse to benefit our chosen bloodlines.'"

"Exactly," Maria confirmed. "That's what my ancestors' memories show. The original Protocol was defensive—how to recognize the pattern, how to preserve knowledge through the dark ages that follow collapse, how to help the next civilization rebuild without repeating the same mistakes. But over centuries, defensive became offensive. Recognizing the pattern became engineering the pattern. Preserving knowledge became controlling who gets to use that knowledge."

Thomas thought about the bloodline manuscripts, the casual way the network discussed optimizing human genetics and selecting survival traits. "So by the time we gather all seven fragments, we'll understand both what the Protocol originally said and how the network corrupted it?"

"That's the hope," Maria said. "Though whether that understanding will help us stop what they're planning..." She shrugged. "I don't know. The network is powerful. Entrenched across Europe. Operating for a millennium with resources and connections we can barely comprehend. Three fugitives with fragments of knowledge probably can't destroy something that large and old."

"But we can expose it," Thomas said. "Can reveal what they've become. Can show other people in the network—people like Johannes who still believed in the original purpose—that they're serving something corrupted. Maybe that creates enough internal resistance to slow them down. Maybe it doesn't. But at least we tried."

They crested a hill, and the landscape opened before them—rolling farmland stretching east toward distant mountains, forests marking river valleys, the occasional village visible in the distance marked by church spires and chimney smoke. And beyond, weeks of travel away but now their definite destination: Prague.

Margarethe pointed to smoke rising from a village about two miles ahead. "We need supplies. Food, better winter clothes if we can afford them, information about road conditions and Order activity. Should we risk entering a village or avoid it?"

"We risk it," Thomas said, surprising himself with the decisiveness. "We can't walk to Prague and beyond eating only what we forage. We need contact with civilization even if that contact is dangerous."

"Then we need a cover story," Margarethe said practically. "Three people traveling together during plague times will raise questions. Especially a woman traveling with a monk and a girl young enough to be your daughter, Thomas. We need an explanation that doesn't invite too many questions."

Maria spoke up: "Pilgrims. Heading to Prague to seek healing at the shrine of Saint Vitus. It's plausible—many people are making desperate pilgrimages during the plague, seeking divine intervention when medicine fails. And it explains why we're traveling despite the danger."

"Good," Margarethe agreed. "Thomas, you're the religious guide. I'm a widow seeking healing for my daughter who survived plague but was left weakened. Maria, you look sickly—shouldn't be hard to fake with the travel exhaustion we're all feeling. We're grateful for any charity but can pay for basic supplies. That makes us sympathetic without seeming too wealthy to rob."

Thomas nodded. The plan was simple, which made it more likely to work. Elaborate lies got complicated. Simple stories that fit people's expectations were easier to maintain.

They approached the village as the afternoon sun angled toward evening, casting long shadows across the fields.

## Scene 2: The Village - Intelligence and Danger

### Village of Sankt Peter, Eastern Road

The village was small—maybe forty houses clustered around a stone church, fields extending in all directions, a mill beside a stream that probably powered grain grinding and cloth fulling. The kind of settlement that had existed for centuries, rebuilding after wars and plagues and famines, enduring through sheer peasant stubbornness.

But the plague had hit here. Thomas could see it immediately.

Houses with doors hanging open, abandoned. Fields left half-harvested, crops rotting because there weren't enough living workers to bring them in. A mass grave at the edge of the village, fresh dirt showing where bodies had been buried recently—probably without the proper rites, just desperate disposal of infected corpses before they contaminated everyone.

The living moved through the village like ghosts themselves, hollow-eyed and exhausted. Maybe twenty people where there should have been a hundred. A third of the population, gone. The survivors traumatized and frightened and trying to maintain normal life when everything had become fundamentally abnormal.

A priest met them at the church—young man, younger than Thomas, with the haunted look of someone who'd performed too many last rites in too short a time. "Pilgrims?"

"Heading to Prague," Thomas confirmed. "To the shrine of Saint Vitus. Seeking healing for the girl. She survived the plague but was left weakened. We're hoping the saint's intercession might restore her strength."

The priest's eyes softened with sympathy. "Many are making such journeys. Faith is all we have when medicine fails and death surrounds us." He made the sign of the cross. "I'm Father Andreas. Come—I can offer you shelter in the church tonight and a meal if our stores permit. We have little, but we share what God provides."

They followed him into the church—small, cold, with wooden benches instead of stone pews. Simple altar. Painted icons showing local saints. The smell of incense couldn't quite cover the underlying odor of death and sickness that seemed to permeate everything during plague times.

"How bad was it here?" Margarethe asked gently.

Father Andreas's face crumpled slightly before he controlled himself. "Bad. The plague came five weeks ago. Started with the blacksmith's family—they'd traded with travelers from Mainz. Within a week, a quarter of the village was sick. Within three weeks, two-thirds were dead or dying. We burned the bodies when we had wood and strength. Buried them when we didn't. Prayed constantly. It made no difference."

He slumped against the altar, suddenly looking exhausted. "I performed last rites until my voice gave out. Held hands as people died screaming from the pain of buboes bursting. Blessed mass graves because we couldn't dig individual plots fast enough. And I still don't know why God allowed this. Why He punished the faithful and the wicked equally. Why children died while some sinners survived."

Thomas had no answer. The plague defied theological explanation. Good people died. Evil people lived. Faith didn't protect you. Prayer didn't heal buboes. God seemed absent or indifferent or perhaps testing humanity in ways that felt indistinguishable from cruelty.

"Have there been strangers through here recently?" Margarethe asked, shifting to practical concerns. "We heard rumors that Gray Robes were active in this region. The Order hunting heretics and burning those they deemed plague-spreaders."

Father Andreas's expression darkened. "Yes. Three days ago. Four Gray Robes passed through, asking about travelers. Specifically asking about a monk traveling with a woman and possibly others. Said they were hunting heretics who'd stolen from a bishop and were spreading false teachings about the plague's origins."

Thomas felt cold wash through him. The Order was hunting them. Following their trail. And they were only three days behind.

"Did you tell them anything?" he asked, trying to keep his voice casual.

"I told them we'd seen many travelers but no one matching that description." Father Andreas met Thomas's eyes with surprising directness. "I don't trust the Order. They burned a family in the next village for 'heresy' last month. The family's only crime was speaking German instead of Latin during prayers. The Order has become cruel during these plague times. They see heresy everywhere, use the chaos as excuse to persecute anyone who doesn't conform to their narrow interpretation of doctrine."

He stood straighter. "So even if I had seen a monk traveling with a woman, I wouldn't have told them. The Order doesn't represent God's mercy anymore. Just fear and control and the particular cruelty that comes from men convinced they're doing righteousness while committing atrocity."

Thomas felt relief mixed with gratitude. "Thank you, Father."

"Don't thank me yet. The Order is systematic. They'll question every village along the eastern road. Eventually they'll find someone who saw you. Someone who remembers three travelers matching your description. Three days behind becomes two days becomes one day becomes..." He didn't need to finish.

Margarethe asked the tactical question: "How far to Prague from here?"

"Three weeks on foot if you take the main roads and don't encounter problems. Four to five weeks if you avoid main roads and move carefully through forests and back paths. Longer if you're slowed by weather or avoiding patrols or running into plague-ravaged areas where survivors are hostile to strangers."

"And the road conditions? Any areas to avoid?"

Father Andreas pulled out a rough map sketched on parchment. "The main eastern road goes through Regensburg—large city, well defended, but also well watched. If the Order is looking for you, they'll have people there checking travelers. North route goes through smaller villages but crosses territories controlled by nobility who support the Order. South route is longer but passes through lands where local lords are too busy dealing with plague and peasant revolts to care much about religious fugitives."

He traced the southern route with his finger. "I'd recommend south if you're trying to avoid the Order. Through the forests along the Danube, then north through Bohemia toward Prague. Harder travel, more time, but less likely to encounter organized pursuit."

"Thank you," Margarethe said sincerely. "This helps more than you know."

"Then stay tonight, rest, leave before dawn. I'll give you what food I can spare—not much, but enough to get you to the next village. And I'll pray for your safety, though I'm no longer certain my prayers reach anyone who cares to listen."

That night they slept in the church, taking turns on watch. Thomas couldn't fully relax, kept expecting Gray Robes to burst through the doors. But the night passed quietly except for the sounds of a plague-ravaged village—occasional coughs from the sick, crying from those who'd lost family, the general atmosphere of grief that hung over everything like smoke.

In the morning, Father Andreas provided them with bread, cheese, dried meat—humble fare but precious given the village's depleted resources.

"One more thing," he said as they prepared to leave. "There are rumors. Probably nothing, but... people are saying the plague isn't natural. That it was sent deliberately. That there are those who engineer such catastrophes for their own purposes. I dismissed it as conspiracy thinking born from desperation to explain the unexplainable. But you—you're running from something. The Order hunts you specifically. And you carry yourself like people who know dangerous secrets."

He met Thomas's eyes. "Is there truth to the rumors? Is the plague more than just God's judgment or natural disaster?"

Thomas thought about lying, about maintaining their cover. But Father Andreas had helped them, had lied to the Order to protect them, deserved some measure of truth in return.

"I don't know if this specific plague was engineered," he said carefully. "But I know there are organizations that understand the pattern of how civilizations collapse. That have knowledge of how to guide—or engineer—such collapses to serve their purposes. Whether they caused this plague or simply allowed it to happen or are taking advantage of it... I'm still learning. Still gathering pieces of a puzzle that's been assembled over centuries."

Father Andreas nodded slowly. "Then may God guide your quest. And may whatever you learn help prevent future catastrophes rather than enabling them."

They left the village as dawn broke, following the southern route Father Andreas had recommended. Through forests that were slowly shedding autumn leaves. Along the Danube's northern banks. Into territories where the plague had struck but not yet completely devastated.

And behind them, three days back but getting closer, Gray Robes followed their trail with patient professional persistence.

The quest had truly begun. Now they just had to survive long enough to complete it.

## Scene 3: Travel and Training - Maria's Education

### Danube Forest Route, One Week Later

They fell into a traveling rhythm over the following week. Wake before dawn. Walk until midday. Rest during the hottest part of the afternoon. Walk until dusk. Make camp in hidden locations away from roads and villages. Take turns on watch through the night.

Thomas used the travel time to teach Maria about the cipher system, showing her how the Iron and Silver Keys worked together to reveal patterns invisible to normal reading.

"The cipher marks look like decorative elements," he explained, demonstrating on a manuscript they'd taken from Johannes's workshop. "Tiny dots and flourishes that scribes added to margins. Most people think they're just embellishment—aesthetic choices to make the page more beautiful. But when you apply the keys at specific positions..."

He aligned the Iron Key's eye symbol with the text. Dots appeared through the aperture, seemingly random. Then he added the Silver Key, positioning the hand symbol to overlap slightly.

The dots connected into lines. The lines formed patterns. The patterns revealed hidden text and location markers and connections between pieces of information scattered across the manuscript.

"It's beautiful," Maria breathed, her eyes wide with wonder that reminded Thomas she was still just fourteen despite the ancient knowledge she carried. "Like... like a secret language written in plain sight. Everyone can see the manuscript, but only those with the keys can read the true message."

"That's exactly what it is," Thomas confirmed. "The network created this system during the collapse of Rome, when barbarian invasions and religious purges were destroying libraries and burning books. They needed a way to preserve knowledge that couldn't be easily destroyed. So they hid it inside texts that seemed harmless—religious manuscripts, agricultural treatises, medical texts. Things that barbarians and religious authorities would preserve rather than burn."

Margarethe watched the lesson from where she sat maintaining her weapons. "And my family's role was to memorize the locations where such manuscripts were hidden. Become walking libraries that couldn't burn."

"Your family and families like yours," Maria added. "The network created multiple redundancies. Some bloodlines memorized locations. Some memorized the cipher system itself. Some were bred for pattern recognition to help decode encrypted information. Some carried genetic memory of specific knowledge fragments. All of it distributed across bloodlines so no single family held everything—no single point of failure that could destroy the network's accumulated knowledge."

Thomas continued the lesson: "The seven keys unlock seven different layers of information. The Iron Key reveals locations. The Silver Key reveals connections between locations. You, Maria, can access the genetic memory needed to interpret what the metal keys alone cannot fully explain. The fourth key—the Voice that Speaks—supposedly reveals the temporal patterns. When things happened, how they connect across centuries."

"And the remaining three keys?" Maria asked.

"The Foot that Walks shows movement patterns—how knowledge physically traveled between locations, what routes the network used to transport information. The Heart that Feels..." Thomas paused, uncertain. "Johannes said it reveals the emotional and cultural context—why knowledge was preserved, what motivated people across generations to maintain this system. And the Mind that Knows synthesizes everything—shows the complete picture when all seven fragments are brought together."

He looked at both his traveling companions. "But without all seven keys, we only see pieces. Fragments that can be misinterpreted or misunderstood. That's how the network corrupted the original Protocol—by working from incomplete information, filling in gaps with assumptions, gradually twisting preservation into control."

They practiced through the afternoon. Maria learning to read cipher marks with the keys. Thomas teaching her the techniques Wilhelm had taught him—the pattern recognition, the spatial reasoning, the ability to see connections others missed.

And gradually, Thomas realized Maria was better at it than he was.

Her genetic memory gave her access to forty generations of cipher reading experience. She could look at a manuscript and immediately sense where cipher marks were hidden, could intuitively understand patterns that took Thomas minutes of careful analysis to decipher.

"You're a natural," he said, impressed and slightly envious.

"I'm designed to be good at this," Maria corrected. "Forty generations of my ancestors were bred specifically to carry and interpret this knowledge. I'm not naturally talented—I'm genetically optimized. There's a difference."

"Is there?" Margarethe asked, joining the conversation. "You're good at cipher reading whether that skill came from natural variation or selective breeding. The source of the ability matters less than what you choose to do with it."

"Does it?" Maria's voice carried doubt beyond her years. "If I'm just a designed outcome—a tool created for a purpose I never chose—am I really making free choices? Or am I just executing my genetic programming?"

Thomas thought about that question himself. He was bred for pattern recognition, for understanding systems and connections. Was his decision to pursue the seven keys really his choice? Or was he simply doing what forty-one generations of genetic optimization had programmed him to do?

"I think," he said slowly, "that you can be both. Genetically optimized AND making free choices. The network gave you tools—enhanced abilities, genetic memory, pattern recognition. But how you use those tools, what purposes you apply them to, whether you serve the network's goals or oppose them—that's your choice. That's what makes you human rather than just a tool."

"Your mother refused to have more children," Margarethe added quietly. "That was her choice. The network wanted her to breed more genetic carriers. She said no. It cost her life, but it was HER choice, not theirs. That's what free will looks like when you're a designed outcome—not freedom from your genetics, but freedom to choose what you do with them."

Maria absorbed that in silence, processing with the seriousness of someone much older.

That night, camped in a forest clearing, Maria accessed deeper genetic memory—going back further than she'd previously attempted, reaching for knowledge from ancestors twenty, thirty, forty generations removed.

Thomas watched with fascination and concern as her eyes went distant, her consciousness clearly elsewhere, experiencing memories that weren't hers but somehow were, encoded in the very structure of her being.

She spoke in languages he didn't recognize—not Latin or Greek or Arabic, but something older. Pre-Roman perhaps. Languages that had existed before the network was founded, before the cipher system was created.

"What's she seeing?" Margarethe whispered, hand on her knife in case this was some kind of seizure or attack.

"Genetic memory," Thomas said. "But deeper than before. She's accessing knowledge from the network's earliest days. From when the Protocol was first created."

Maria spoke for nearly an hour, her young voice carrying tones and cadences that seemed to come from other people, other times, other lives lived centuries before she was born. Finally her eyes focused again, returning to the present.

"I saw them," she whispered, her voice hoarse. "The founders. The people who created the Genesis Protocol during Rome's collapse. They weren't trying to engineer catastrophe. They were trying to prevent it. Trying to recognize the pattern so humanity could break the cycle instead of endlessly repeating it."

"What pattern?" Thomas asked urgently.

"Civilizations grow complex. Interconnected. Optimized for efficiency. They become incredibly powerful but incredibly fragile—like a house of cards, magnificent but vulnerable to collapse from any significant shock." Maria's eyes were distant, still processing what she'd seen. "And every 600-800 years, the shock comes. Climate change. Pandemic. Economic collapse. War. Sometimes multiple shocks simultaneously. And the optimized civilization fragments. Descends into dark ages. Loses accumulated knowledge. The survivors rebuild, slowly, and the cycle begins again."

She looked at them with eyes too old for her face. "The Protocol was meant to show humanity how to recognize when they're approaching the fragile peak. How to build in redundancy instead of pure optimization. How to preserve essential knowledge through the dark ages so rebuilding happens faster. It was defensive. Protective. Meant to help humanity survive collapse and learn from it."

"But the network corrupted it," Margarethe said.

"Over centuries," Maria confirmed. "The original founders died. Their descendants inherited the system but not the full context. Gaps in knowledge got filled with assumptions. 'Recognize the pattern' became 'control the pattern.' 'Preserve knowledge' became 'control who accesses knowledge.' 'Help humanity survive collapse' became 'engineer collapse to benefit chosen bloodlines.' Each generation making small corruptions that accumulated into something monstrous."

Thomas felt cold certainty settle in his chest. "That's what the complete Protocol will show. Not just what the network has become, but what it was meant to be. The contrast between original purpose and current corruption. Evidence that they've betrayed everything they were founded to protect."

"If we can gather all seven fragments," Maria said. "If we can survive long enough to bring them together. If we can decode the complete message and understand it fully." She looked toward the east, toward Prague, toward the fourth key that waited there. "That's a lot of 'ifs.'"

"Then we better start working through them," Margarethe said practically. "Two weeks to Prague if we don't encounter problems. Three if we do. The Order is behind us, getting closer. We need to stay ahead of them, reach the fourth key before they catch us, and keep moving. No time for philosophy when Gray Robes are hunting us."

They slept in shifts, maintained watch, and continued east the next morning.

The quest was no longer just about survival. It was about understanding. About recovering what the network had been before corruption. About maybe—possibly—finding a way to stop what they'd become.

A lot of 'ifs.'

But Thomas was committed now. They all were.

Forward, toward Prague and the fourth key and whatever revelations waited there.

## Scene 4: The Encounter - Network Agent

### Bohemian Forest, Ten Days After Sankt Peter

They were three days from Prague when the network found them.

Thomas was gathering firewood, Margarethe scouting the area for threats, Maria resting in the camp they'd established in a sheltered hollow. The afternoon was cold, autumn deepening toward winter, the trees mostly bare and the ground crunching with frost.

He heard footsteps behind him. Too quiet to be Margarethe's purposeful stride. Too careful to be Maria's youthful carelessness.

Thomas spun, reaching for the knife he'd started carrying after Sankt Peter.

A man stood ten feet away. Middle-aged, dressed in scholar's robes, with intelligent eyes and the particular posture of someone educated and comfortable in their own authority. He held his hands up, showing he was unarmed.

"Thomas of Eltville," the man said calmly. "Wilhelm's grandson. Carrier of the Iron Key. I've been looking for you."

Thomas kept his knife raised. "Who are you?"

"Someone who knew your grandfather. Worked with him. Shared his belief that the network has strayed from its original purpose." The man took a slow step forward. "My name is Marcus the Physician. I serve the network—or served it, before I understood what it had become."

"Stay back," Thomas warned.

"I'm not here to hurt you. Or capture you. Or return you to the network's control." Marcus's voice was patient, gentle, the tone of someone used to dealing with frightened people. "I'm here to help. To give you information that might save your life. And to ask for your help in return."

Margarethe appeared from the trees, crossbow raised and aimed at Marcus's chest. "Who are you and how did you find us?"

"I told the boy—I'm Marcus. And I found you the same way the network tracks all its assets: through bloodline monitoring, reports from agents in villages you've passed through, pattern analysis of where you're likely heading given your goals." He smiled slightly. "You're going to Prague. To the Jewish quarter. To find the Voice that Speaks, the fourth key. It's what I would do in your position. What anyone trying to understand the complete Protocol would do."

"So the network knows where we're going," Thomas said, feeling dread settle in his stomach.

"The network suspects. But they don't know for certain, and they're not yet mobilized to stop you. You're still fugitives, still being pursued by the Order primarily. The network views you as assets that escaped—valuable genetic resources to be recovered but not worth revealing themselves publicly to capture." Marcus lowered his hands slowly. "But that calculation will change when you reach Prague. When you attempt to access the fourth key. Then they'll intervene directly."

"Why are you telling us this?" Margarethe asked, crossbow still aimed.

"Because I knew Wilhelm. Respected him. Believed in what he believed—that the network had corrupted its purpose, that the Protocol needed to be revealed in full rather than kept fragmented and controlled. When he was killed by the Order, I mourned. And when I heard his grandson had escaped with the Iron Key, had found the Silver Key, had even recovered a genetic memory carrier... I saw an opportunity."

Marcus pulled out a scroll, held it up so they could see the wax seal—network cipher marks worked into what looked like decorative design. "This is information about the sixth key. The Heart that Feels. I've spent twenty years researching its location, trying to understand what happened to it after the network fragmented the Protocol. I want to give this information to you. Help you find what the network itself has lost or deliberately hidden."

"Why?" Thomas asked suspiciously. "What do you get from helping us?"

"Hope," Marcus said simply. "Hope that someone will finally understand the complete Protocol. Will see both what it originally said and what the network corrupted it into. Will maybe expose that corruption enough that others in the network—good people who still believe in preservation rather than control—will rebel. Will choose the network's original purpose over what it's become."

He lowered the scroll. "I'm old, Thomas. Tired. I've spent my life serving something I thought was noble only to slowly realize it had become monstrous. I don't have the courage to rebel openly—they'd kill me, kill my family, erase me like I never existed. But I can help you. Give you information that might help you complete the quest I'm too frightened to undertake myself."

Margarethe and Thomas exchanged glances. This could be a trap. Could be the network trying to track them through a seemingly helpful agent. Could be genuine help that came with hidden costs.

"Lower the crossbow," Thomas said quietly. "Let him speak."

Margarethe lowered her weapon slightly but kept it ready. "Talk."

Marcus sat on a fallen log, the scroll still in his hands. "The Heart that Feels is in England. Hidden in a monastery called Glastonbury. It was placed there by network members during the chaos after King John's death, when English politics were unstable and the network needed to hide their most precious knowledge in secure locations. It's been there for over a century. The network knows it exists but has lost the specific location within the monastery's extensive library."

"How do you know where it is if the network doesn't?" Thomas asked.

"Because I read Wilhelm's personal journals before they were destroyed. He'd been researching the sixth key for years, had narrowed down the location. He intended to retrieve it himself but died before he could make the journey. I memorized what he'd learned, kept that knowledge secret, waited for someone who could use it." Marcus held out the scroll. "This is everything Wilhelm discovered. Exact location within Glastonbury's library. Methods to access the hidden chamber where the key is stored. Recognition phrases that will identify you to network sympathizers who might help."

Thomas took the scroll cautiously. Opened it. Read cipher marks and location details and what looked like Wilhelm's handwriting—he'd seen enough of his grandfather's manuscripts to recognize the particular style.

"This looks genuine," he said slowly.

"It is. Wilhelm trusted me. I'm trying to honor that trust by helping his grandson complete what he started." Marcus stood. "But I need to warn you—the network is watching Prague. They know the fourth key is there. They'll be monitoring the Jewish quarter, watching for anyone trying to access the scholar's library. You'll need to be careful. Clever. And quick. Get the key and leave before they can mobilize to stop you."

"Why are you helping us if it puts you at risk?" Margarethe asked.

"Because doing nothing puts me more at risk," Marcus said quietly. "Risk to my conscience. Risk of living the rest of my life knowing I served evil because I was too frightened to resist. This is my resistance—small, covert, but something. Maybe it makes a difference. Maybe it doesn't. But at least I tried."

He turned to leave, then paused. "One more thing. The seventh key—the Mind that Knows—is supposedly in Constantinople, with the fifth key. They're together, both hidden in the ruins of the Great Library. If you can reach Constantinople, you can potentially get two keys in one journey. That information isn't in Wilhelm's scroll. It's my own research. Use it as you see fit."

Then he walked into the forest and was gone, disappearing between the trees like a ghost.

Thomas stood holding the scroll, uncertain whether to trust it.

"Trap?" Margarethe asked.

"Maybe. Or genuine help from someone who still remembers what the network was supposed to be." Thomas rolled up the scroll, secured it in his pack. "Either way, we were already going to Prague. This just gives us additional information about what comes after. And the warning about network surveillance is probably accurate whether Marcus is genuinely helping or setting a trap."

Maria emerged from the camp, having heard most of the conversation. "He was telling the truth. I could sense it—something in the way he spoke, the genuine regret in his voice. My genetic memory includes training in reading people, detecting deception. He believes what he's saying."

"Believing it doesn't make it true," Margarethe pointed out. "He could be genuinely trying to help while unknowingly serving the network's purposes. They could be using him to track us without his knowledge."

"Then we stay careful. Stay alert. Use his information but don't trust it completely." Thomas looked toward the east, toward Prague that was now close enough to almost feel its presence on the horizon. "Three days to the city. Then we find the fourth key, learn what it reveals, and decide our next move. Constantinople for the fifth and seventh keys. England for the sixth. Or somewhere else entirely if we learn new information."

They broke camp, continued east, moved with even more caution now that they knew the network was actively tracking them.

Three days to Prague.

To the fourth key.

To whatever revelations and dangers waited in the Jewish quarter's scholar's library.

The quest was accelerating. The network was mobilizing. The Order was closing in.

And three fugitives carrying pieces of a thousand-year puzzle were running out of time to complete it before being captured or killed.

But they kept moving forward.

Because the alternative was giving up.

And they'd all already committed to seeing this through to whatever end waited.

## Scene 5: Prague - The Jewish Quarter

### Prague, The Jewish Quarter (Josefov), Three Days Later

Prague was larger than any city Thomas had ever seen. Mainz and Strasbourg were substantial, but Prague was a true capital—sprawling across both banks of the Vltava River, multiple districts each the size of a city unto itself, church spires and castle towers rising against gray November sky.

The Jewish quarter—Josefov—occupied a small area near the river, walled off from the rest of the city both physically and culturally. Security at the gates was tight; the plague had made people suspicious of outsiders, and Jews were often blamed for catastrophes despite lack of evidence.

"Papers?" the guard demanded in Czech-accented German.

Thomas pulled out documents they'd forged—pilgrim passes claiming they were seeking the shrine of Saint Vitus, the kind of desperate religious journey that was common during plague times. Not perfect forgeries, but good enough to pass cursory inspection by a guard who just wanted to get through his shift without problems.

The guard examined the papers, looked at their faces, compared descriptions. Seemed about to wave them through when a second guard approached—older, more experienced, with the shrewd eyes of someone who'd seen countless attempts at deception.

"Why are you entering the Jewish quarter?" he asked. "The shrine of Saint Vitus is in the Christian district."

Thomas thought fast. "We were told there's a physician here who survived the plague. Who might have knowledge of treatments that could help the girl. We're seeking any aid, even from..." He hesitated, knowing he needed to show appropriate prejudice to be believable. "Even from Jewish medicine, if it might save her life."

The older guard studied Maria, who looked genuinely exhausted from the travel. "Jewish physicians are skilled. Better than most Christian healers, though don't tell the Church I said that. But they don't typically see Christians. Religious separation, community protection. Why would they help you?"

"We can pay," Margarethe said, pulling out a small purse with enough coins visible to suggest they had resources but not enough to make robbing them worth the effort.

The guard considered, then nodded. "You have two hours. Find your physician, conduct your business, leave. Don't cause problems. Don't preach Christianity. Don't touch anything in the synagogue. Josefov tolerates Christian visitors when they behave themselves. Act inappropriately and you'll be escorted out by force."

They entered the Jewish quarter, and it felt like stepping into a different world. The architecture was different—tighter, more vertical, buildings pressed close together. The language changed—Yiddish and Hebrew mixing with German and Czech. The smell changed—different spices from the Christian quarters, different cooking, different incense.

And the people looked at them with wariness. Christians entering Josefov usually meant trouble—accusations of well poisoning, forced conversions, pogroms disguised as religious fervor. The residents watched the three strangers with the caution of people who'd learned through bitter experience that outsiders often brought violence.

Maria guided them through narrow streets with the confidence of genetic memory. "My ancestors knew this quarter well. The scholar's library is near the synagogue—the Altneuschul. We're looking for a building marked with a specific symbol—a hand holding seven keys, worked into the stonework above the door."

They found it after twenty minutes of searching—a modest building that looked like a private residence or small school, the symbol exactly where Maria said it would be, barely visible unless you knew what to look for.

"How do we get inside?" Margarethe asked. "We can't just break in during daylight with guards watching."

"We don't break in. We're expected." Maria approached the door, knocked in a specific pattern—three quick, two slow, three quick. A code.

The door opened, revealing an old man with a long white beard and penetrating dark eyes. He examined them carefully, then spoke in German: "The three from the west. The monk, the warrior woman, and the genetic memory carrier. We've been expecting you for weeks. Longer, if you count the generations my family has been preparing for this moment."

He stepped aside. "Come. Quickly. There are network agents watching the quarter. They don't know specifically that you're here yet, but they're alert for any attempt to access the fourth key. We don't have much time."

They entered, and the old man—he introduced himself as Rabbi Ezra—led them through the building to a hidden door concealed behind a bookshelf.

"The scholar's library," he said, opening the door to reveal stairs descending into darkness. "Maintained by my family for nineteen generations. Protected through fires and pogroms and the endless challenges of preserving knowledge in a world that alternates between burning it and weaponizing it."

They descended into a library that took Thomas's breath away.

Manuscripts everywhere. Thousands of them, carefully preserved in wooden cases, organized with meticulous care. Not just Jewish texts—though there were many of those—but Christian, Islamic, Greek, Roman, Persian. Knowledge from across the known world, preserved by scholars who understood that human wisdom belonged to humanity, not just to one culture or religion.

"The network created this repository during the Crusades," Rabbi Ezra explained. "When religious warfare was destroying libraries across Europe and the Middle East. Jewish scholars and Christian monks and Islamic teachers worked together to preserve what they could. The Voice that Speaks—the fourth key—is their legacy. Their warning to future generations about what happens when knowledge is controlled rather than shared."

He led them to a specific section, pulled out a manuscript that looked different from the others—older, the parchment darker, the binding more elaborate.

"The Golem of Prague manuscript," Rabbi Ezra said reverently. "Genuine kabbalistic teaching about the creation of life from clay. But also a container for the fourth fragment of the Genesis Protocol. Your keys will reveal what's hidden within the mystical teachings."

Thomas and Maria worked together, placing the Iron and Silver Keys on the manuscript in the specific alignment that revealed cipher marks. Maria touched the page, activating her genetic memory.

And gasped.

"I can see it," she whispered. "The fourth fragment. It shows... temporal patterns. Not just that civilizations collapse every 600-800 years, but WHY. The mathematical certainty of it. How complex systems become fragile. How optimization for efficiency creates vulnerability to shocks. How interconnection spreads catastrophe faster than isolated systems but also enables faster recovery if knowledge is preserved correctly."

She traced patterns on the page that only she could see, accessing information encoded for genetic memory carriers. "And I can see the corruption point. Where the network twisted this from 'understand the pattern to avoid it' into 'understand the pattern to engineer it.' It happened during the 11th century. A network leader named Benedict argued that since collapse was inevitable, they should control WHEN it happened rather than letting it occur randomly. Should engineer the collapse to benefit chosen bloodlines rather than letting it devastate everyone equally."

Thomas read over her shoulder, seeing different information through the metal keys. "It shows locations too. Where the network positioned resources for post-collapse recovery. Seed banks, knowledge repositories, strategic population concentrations. All carefully placed to ensure that when the collapse they engineered happened, the 'right people' would survive to rebuild."

"That's evidence," Margarethe said urgently. "Clear documentation of corruption. Of the network planning engineered catastrophe. If we can get this to the right people—"

"There are no 'right people,'" Rabbi Ezra interrupted sadly. "The network is too entrenched. Too powerful. They control the narrative. Even with all seven fragments, even with complete documentation of their corruption, who would you show it to? What authority could act against something that's been operating successfully for a thousand years?"

"Other network members," Thomas said. "People like Marcus, who still remember the original purpose. If we show them the contrast between what they're serving and what they think they're serving, some might rebel. Might choose preservation over control."

"Maybe," Rabbi Ezra said doubtfully. "But that's a long game. For now, you need to copy what you can from the fourth fragment, memorize what you cannot copy, and leave before network agents realize you're here. Every moment you stay increases the risk of capture."

They spent an hour copying cipher marks, taking notes, Maria committing key information to her genetic memory so it would be preserved even if the physical notes were lost or destroyed.

Then Rabbi Ezra led them back upstairs, gave them food and clean water, prepared them for departure.

"The next key is in Constantinople," Thomas said. "The Foot that Walks and possibly the Mind that Knows, according to Marcus's information. That's months of travel from here. Through plague country, through territories controlled by nobility who might be network members, through lands where war and catastrophe have made everyone suspicious of strangers."

"Then you'll need help," Rabbi Ezra said. He pulled out letters of introduction, carefully written in multiple languages. "These will identify you to network sympathizers—people like Marcus and myself who remember the original purpose. Safe houses along the route to Constantinople. Contacts who can provide food, shelter, information. Not perfect safety, but better than traveling completely alone and unknown."

"Why are you helping us?" Maria asked.

"Because my family has protected this library for nineteen generations. Waiting for someone who would use the knowledge correctly rather than weaponize it for control. You're trying to understand, not control. Trying to expose corruption, not serve it. That makes you worth helping, even at risk to myself." Rabbi Ezra smiled. "Besides, Wilhelm was my friend. We corresponded for years about the network's corruption. I promised him that if anyone came seeking the fourth key with genuine purpose, I would help them. I'm keeping that promise."

They left Josefov as the afternoon sun angled toward evening, carrying the fourth fragment's knowledge and Rabbi Ezra's letters and the growing understanding that the complete Protocol would reveal something both worse and better than they'd imagined.

Worse because it would show exactly how the network had corrupted a noble purpose into something monstrous.

Better because it would show there HAD been a noble purpose once. That preservation was possible without control. That the cycle could potentially be broken rather than endlessly repeated.

Three keys down. Four to go.

Constantinople next, for the fifth and seventh keys.

Then England for the sixth.

Then... then what? What would they do with complete understanding once they had it? How would they stop a thousand-year conspiracy even armed with full knowledge of its corruption?

Thomas didn't know. But he knew they had to try.

The quest continued.

The network was mobilizing.

And three fugitives carrying pieces of a puzzle were running out of time to complete it before being stopped.
