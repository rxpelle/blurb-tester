# CHAPTER 2 - THE NETWORK (Medieval Timeline - 1347, Mainz)

## Scene 1: Morning After - The Lesson Begins

### Warehouse, Dawn

Thomas woke to the sound of steel on stone.

He opened his eyes. The warehouse was gray with dawn light filtering through cracks in the walls, dust motes swirling in the pale shafts. The smell of damp wool and old fabric hung heavy in the cold air.

Margarethe sat cross-legged on a crate fifteen feet away, methodically sharpening her daggers. The scrape of whetstone against blade was rhythmic, almost meditative. She hadn't looked at him yet.

Thomas sat up, his back protesting. The wool blankets had been comfortable enough, but the stone floor beneath them was unforgiving. His ribs ached where he'd hit the cobblestones during last night's attack.

"You snore," Margarethe said without looking up.

"I don't—" Thomas stopped. "Do I?"

"Like a dying goat." She tested the edge of one blade against her thumb, nodded in satisfaction, started on the second. "But quietly enough. No one heard us from the street."

Thomas stood, joints popping. His mouth tasted like copper and fear. "How long have you been awake?"

"Since before dawn. Habit." She finally looked at him. Her eyes were sharp, assessing. "You slept well for someone who watched two men die yesterday."

He flinched. The memory hit him—the Gray Robe's throat opening under Margarethe's blade, blood black in the lamplight. The gurgling sound.

"I didn't have nightmares," he said quietly. "Should I have?"

"Most people do. First time they see death up close." She sheathed the first dagger, started on the third. How many did she carry? "Either you're tougher than you look, or you're very good at lying to yourself."

"Maybe both."

She almost smiled. "Maybe."

Thomas walked to the makeshift table—a plank balanced on two crates. Someone, probably Margarethe, had left bread and cheese there. Hard bread, harder cheese. He tore off a piece of each, chewed mechanically. It tasted like dust.

"We start today," Margarethe said, setting down her whetstone. She stood, walked to a stack of fabric bales against the far wall. Pushed them aside.

Behind the bales was a wooden chest, bound with iron. Old, worn, the wood scarred with use. Margarethe produced a key from somewhere—Thomas didn't see where she'd been hiding it—and unlocked it.

Inside were manuscripts.

Thomas's breath caught.

"My father's collection," Margarethe said softly. "The ones he hid before the Order came. Not the originals—those are scattered across Christendom. But copies. Careful copies." She pulled out three, laid them on the table. "Everything I know about the cipher, I learned from these."

Thomas leaned over the manuscripts. The vellum was good quality, the script careful. Latin text—psalms, biblical commentary, merchant ledgers. And in the margins, faint but deliberate, geometric patterns of colored dots.

Red. Blue. Yellow. Just like the ones he'd seen in Hamo's scriptorium.

"Sit," Margarethe commanded. "And pay attention. I'm only explaining this once."

Thomas sat.

Margarethe pulled the iron key from Thomas's pocket—he hadn't even felt her take it—and placed it on the table between them. The eye symbol caught the light, seemed to stare at both of them.

"The cipher is a language," she began. "But not one written in words. It's written in positions, colors, and counts." She pointed to the first manuscript—a psalm. "Look at this page. What do you see?"

Thomas studied it. Latin text, beautifully rendered. And in the margin, near Psalm 23, a cluster of dots. Three red, two blue, one yellow. Arranged in a triangular pattern.

"Dots," he said.

"Deeper. What PATTERN do you see?"

Thomas looked closer. The dots weren't random. The red ones formed a triangle, apex pointing up. The blue ones sat at the base of the triangle. The yellow dot was offset, to the right.

"Geometry," he said slowly. "The placement is deliberate. The red dots form a shape."

"Good." Margarethe's voice held a note of approval. "Now watch."

She took the iron key and positioned it over the page. The eye symbol aligned with a specific section of the psalm—the word *"righteous"*. Through the aperture in the key, certain dots were highlighted while others were obscured.

"The key is a filter," Margarethe explained. "Position it correctly, and it tells you which dots to count, which to ignore." She counted quickly under her breath. "Three red, skip the blues, one yellow. Three-one. Position indicator."

"Position of what?"

"The location being encoded." She pulled out a second manuscript—a merchant's ledger. Found a page with similar markings. "Now watch. Same key, different alignment."

She positioned the key differently. This time, the aperture highlighted the blue dots.

"Two blues, skip the yellow, zero reds. Two-zero. That's a danger marker."

Thomas leaned forward. His mind was racing, patterns clicking into place. "So the colors mean different things. Red is safe. Blue is dangerous. Yellow is—"

"Neutral ground. Trade routes. Places where the network operates but doesn't control." Margarethe set the key down. "The cipher encodes three things: location, status, and timing."

"Timing?"

She pointed to a cluster of yellow dots arranged in a specific pattern—almost like a clock face. "Seasonal markers. When to travel, when to hide. The network moves with the seasons, with trade fairs, with pilgrimages." She met his eyes. "Your grandfather was brilliant at this. He could read a page and memorize fifty locations in an hour."

Thomas felt the weight of that legacy. "I'm not my grandfather."

"No. But you have his eyes." She pushed the manuscripts toward him. "Your turn. Take the key. Find me a location."

Thomas picked up the iron key. It was heavier than it looked, the metal cold against his palm. He positioned it over the first manuscript, trying to mimic Margarethe's movements.

The aperture didn't align properly. The dots through it looked random.

He adjusted. Still wrong.

"Not like that," Margarethe said. "The key aligns with specific WORDS, not just anywhere on the page. Look for repeated phrases. The cipher-makers used textual anchors."

Thomas scanned the psalm. *"The Lord is my shepherd..."* Common enough. But further down: *"He restoreth my soul."*

He aligned the key with *"restoreth."*

Through the aperture, three red dots and one yellow appeared.

"Three-one," he breathed. "That's... Abbey of St. Gall?"

Margarethe's eyebrows rose. "How did you—"

"You said it yesterday. When you demonstrated with the sample page." Thomas felt a rush of excitement. "Three-one was the St. Gall location. Third shelf, first volume."

She stared at him. "You memorized that from one demonstration?"

"I... yes?" Thomas looked at the key, then at her. "Is that unusual?"

For the first time since they'd met, Margarethe smiled—a real smile, not the bitter edge she usually wore. "You really are Wilhelm's grandson."

Thomas felt warmth bloom in his chest that had nothing to do with the cipher.

"Again," Margarethe said, pulling out another manuscript. "Find me another location."

Thomas worked through it. This one was harder—a commentary on Boethius, dense theological Latin. But the pattern was there. He found the textual anchor (*"fortune's wheel"*), aligned the key, counted the dots.

"Two red, three yellow. Position two-three. That's..." He frowned. "I don't know that one."

"Cologne. Private collection, merchant district. Second house, third door." Margarethe took the key back. "You're learning fast. Too fast, maybe."

"Is that a problem?"

"It is if you start thinking you're invincible." She closed the manuscripts. "The cipher is powerful, Thomas. It can tell you where to hide, who to trust, how to survive. But it can't tell you everything. It can't warn you about betrayal. It can't predict when someone you trust will sell you to the Order for fifty gold marks."

"Oskar wouldn't—"

"Anyone would. For the right price. Or the right threat." She stood, walked to the warehouse door, checked the street through a crack. "Which is why we're going to the market. You need to see how the network really works."

Thomas stood, brushing breadcrumbs from his robe. "You're taking me to meet more people? After you just said anyone might betray us?"

"No. I'm taking you to OBSERVE people. There's a difference." She turned, and her expression was serious. "The cipher is information. But information without context is useless. You need to learn to read people the way you read manuscripts."

"I'm not good at that."

"I know. You're transparent as glass." She pulled a cloak from a hook on the wall, tossed it to him. "Which is why you're going to keep your mouth shut and watch. Understand?"

Thomas caught the cloak. It smelled like rosemary and old smoke. "Understood."

"Good." Margarethe pulled her own cloak on, checked her daggers were concealed. "And Thomas?"

"Yes?"

"If something happens—if we're separated, if the Order finds us—you run. Don't try to be brave. Don't try to save me. Just run."

He wanted to argue. Wanted to say he wouldn't leave her the way everyone else in his life had left him.

But the look in her eyes stopped him.

"I'll run," he lied.

She studied him for a long moment. "You're a terrible liar. But I appreciate the effort."

Then she opened the door, and they stepped into the plague-haunted streets of Mainz.

## Scene 2: The Market - Gathering Intelligence

The market square was a shadow of what it must have been before the plague.

Half the stalls were empty, their owners dead or fled. The remaining merchants sold food at extortionate prices, cloth stripped from plague houses, and amulets that promised protection but delivered nothing. The smell was overwhelming—rotting vegetables, unwashed bodies, and underneath it all, the sweet-sick stench of death that permeated everything in Mainz.

Thomas pulled his hood lower, trying not to breathe too deeply.

Margarethe moved through the crowd with practiced ease, a cloth merchant checking inventory. She'd wrapped a scarf around her lower face—plague caution, but also disguise. Thomas followed three steps behind, as she'd instructed.

"Third stall on the left," she murmured without turning. "Bookseller. Gray beard, green tunic. Watch him."

Thomas looked. The bookseller was old, weathered, his stall displaying religious texts and prayer books. Nothing remarkable. An old man selling salvation to the desperate.

But then Thomas noticed the way people approached the stall. They didn't browse casually. They came with purpose, leaned close, whispered. The bookseller would nod, pull a specific book, show a specific page. The customer would nod back and leave.

No money changed hands.

"Information trading," Margarethe said quietly. "Oskar doesn't sell books. He sells what's in them."

As Thomas watched, a woman in widow's black approached. Whispered something. Oskar pulled a psalter, showed her a page with marginal dots. The woman's face went pale. She nodded once and hurried away.

"What did he tell her?" Thomas asked.

"Probably that her husband's trade route is compromised. Or that the plague has reached the next town over." Margarethe angled them closer. "The network runs on information. Oskar is a nexus. He knows who's traveling, who's died, where the Order is searching. People pay him in gossip and coin. He pays them back in survival."

They stopped at a neighboring stall—fabric dyes, pungent and harsh. Margarethe made a show of examining pigments while watching Oskar from the corner of her eye.

"Why don't we just ask him what we need to know?" Thomas whispered.

"Because asking costs more than observing. And because—" She stiffened. "Don't turn around. Gray Robe. Two stalls behind us."

Thomas's heart hammered. He forced himself to stay still, examining a jar of indigo dye like it was the most fascinating thing in the world.

"Is he looking at us?" Thomas breathed.

"No. He's watching Oskar." Margarethe's voice was tight. "The Order knows he's a network contact. They're monitoring who talks to him."

"Then we can't approach—"

"We can. But we have to be smarter about it." She purchased a small vial of dye—unnecessary, but maintaining her merchant cover—and moved away from the stall. "Follow. Not directly. Circle around."

Thomas obeyed. They moved through the market like water finding a path, never stopping, never drawing attention. Margarethe paused at a vegetable stall, haggled over withered turnips. Stopped to examine a rack of used clothing. All the while, she was angling them toward Oskar's stall from a different direction.

Finally, she stopped at a stall selling candles. From here, they had a clear line of sight to Oskar, but the Gray Robe was blocked by a crowd.

"Now," Margarethe said.

They crossed to Oskar's stall. Up close, the bookseller was older than Thomas had thought—seventy, perhaps. His hands were ink-stained, his eyes sharp behind wrinkled lids.

He looked at Margarethe and went still.

"Heinrich's daughter," he said softly. "I thought you'd fled the city."

"I tried. Got pulled back." Margarethe leaned over a displayed gospel, feigning interest. "I need information, Oskar."

"Information costs." But his eyes were kind. "Though for Heinrich's memory, perhaps a discount."

"The Order is hunting someone. Wilhelm of Eltville's grandson." She turned a page, casual. "What's the bounty?"

Oskar's expression didn't change, but his voice dropped. "Fifty gold marks. Alive, preferably. They want what he's carrying."

Thomas felt cold despite his cloak.

"Who's hunting?" Margarethe asked.

"Everyone." Oskar glanced toward where the Gray Robe was still watching, though his view was obscured. "Fifty marks is a year's income for most. The Order has made it clear—turn in the boy, no questions asked. Half the city is looking."

"And the other half?"

"Hoping he escapes. Wilhelm had friends. But friends don't pay rent." Oskar turned a page himself, showed Margarethe something—Thomas couldn't see what. "There's talk of a cathedral search. Tonight. The Order believes Wilhelm hid something there before he died."

"Did he?" Margarethe's voice was carefully neutral.

"If he did, I don't know where." Oskar looked at Thomas for the first time. Really looked. "You have his eyes. The pattern-seeing gift."

Thomas swallowed. "I'm trying."

"Try harder. The world needs what you're carrying." Oskar pulled another book from his stock—a prayer book, small and worn. He handed it to Margarethe. "For your troubles. No charge."

Margarethe took it, slipped it into her cloak without examining it. "Thank you, old friend."

"Don't thank me yet. The Order is tightening the circle. If you're still in Mainz tomorrow, you'll be in chains." He looked past them. "And you're being watched. The Gray Robe is coming this way."

"Run or hide?" Margarethe asked quietly.

"Neither. Disappear." Oskar raised his voice suddenly, addressing the crowd. "These lovely merchants were just leaving! No purchase today, but perhaps next market!"

Margarethe nodded, loud enough for others to hear. "Your prices are too high, old man. I'll find better elsewhere."

They moved away, blending into the crowd. Thomas's pulse thundered in his ears. He wanted to look back, see if the Gray Robe was following, but Margarethe's earlier instruction held: *Don't draw attention.*

They wove through the market, past the plague cart loading new bodies, past a preacher shouting about God's judgment. Margarethe's hand found Thomas's elbow, guided him left, then right, then through a narrow alley that stank of piss and rot.

Finally, they stopped in a recessed doorway. Margarethe checked the street behind them.

"Did we lose him?" Thomas asked.

"Maybe. Or maybe he knows where we're going and doesn't need to follow." She pulled out the prayer book Oskar had given her. Opened it.

Inside the cover, written in tiny, cramped script, was a message:

*Cathedral. Tonight. Midnight. Library accessible through chapter house. Code: Psalm 117, verse 1. Brother Marcus is sympathetic. Will let you in. But leave before Lauds or Anselm will know. One hour. No more. —O*

Margarethe memorized it, then tore out the page, chewed it, and swallowed.

Thomas stared. "You just—"

"Information that can't be stolen is information that survives." She closed the book, dropped it in a gutter. "We have our window. Midnight. That gives us eight hours to prepare."

"Prepare how?"

Margarethe's smile was sharp. "I'm going to teach you not to be useless. Come on."

They disappeared into the warren of Mainz's back streets, leaving the market and its hunters behind.

## Scene 3: Margarethe's Training Montage

Back in the warehouse, Margarethe transformed from merchant to teacher.

"First lesson," she said, bolting the door behind them. "Silence."

Thomas blinked. "What?"

"You move like a drunken cow. Every step announces you. In the cathedral tonight, you need to be a ghost." She pointed to the far end of the warehouse, where stacks of fabric created a makeshift maze. "Walk from here to there without making a sound."

Thomas looked at the distance. Maybe thirty feet. "That's all?"

"Try it."

He walked. His sandals scuffed against the stone floor. A crate creaked as he brushed past it. Somewhere, a rat squeaked and fled.

Margarethe shook her head. "Again. Slower."

Thomas tried. Concentrated on each step, placing his feet carefully.

His robe caught on a protruding nail, ripped with a loud *tear*.

"I'm not built for this," Thomas said, frustrated.

"No one is. It's a skill." Margarethe walked the same path—silent, fluid, barely disturbing the air. She moved like water finding channels. "Watch my feet. I step heel-to-toe when I need quiet, toe-to-heel when I need speed. I test my weight before committing. I know where every creak is."

She demonstrated again. Thomas watched, trying to memorize the pattern.

"Your turn."

He tried. Failed. Tried again. Failed worse.

An hour passed. Thomas's frustration mounted. He was *terrible* at this. Weak at hauling stone, weak at digging, weak at moving silently—

"Stop," Margarethe said.

Thomas stopped.

"You're in your head. You're thinking too much." She walked over, adjusted his posture. Her hands on his shoulders were firm, impersonal. "Feel the floor. Don't think about stepping—feel where your weight wants to go."

Thomas closed his eyes. Tried to feel instead of think.

He took a step. Quieter. Not silent, but better.

"Good," Margarethe said. "Again."

By the end of the second hour, Thomas could cross the warehouse with only occasional small sounds. Not perfect, but no longer announcing himself like a herald.

Margarethe nodded approval. "Better. You learn fast when you stop fighting yourself."

"Next lesson?" Thomas asked, breathing hard.

She pulled out a simple door lock—old, iron, common to half the buildings in Mainz. Set it on the table with two thin pieces of metal. "Locks."

Thomas stared. "You want me to learn lockpicking?"

"I want you to understand how locks work. The cathedral library will be locked. I can pick it, but if something happens to me, you need to be able to get in." She demonstrated, sliding the thin metal picks into the mechanism. "Feel for the pins. Gentle pressure. Don't force it. Let the lock tell you what it wants."

She popped it open. Made it look effortless.

"Your turn."

Thomas took the picks. They were awkward in his hands, too thin, too delicate.

He slid them into the lock. Felt... nothing. Just metal on metal.

"I don't feel anything," he said.

"You're not listening." Margarethe guided his hands. "The lock is a pattern. Pins in sequence. Each one has a position it wants to reach. Your job is to find that position."

Pattern.

Thomas's mind shifted. Not force—pattern recognition. Like the cipher. Like the dots in the manuscripts.

He tried again. This time, he felt... something. A resistance. He adjusted the tension, felt the resistance give slightly.

Click.

One pin set.

"There," Margarethe said, surprised. "You felt it."

Thomas's concentration was absolute. The second pin was harder. He probed, felt the pattern, adjusted.

Click.

Third pin. Fourth pin.

The lock opened.

Thomas stared at it, stunned.

"How did you—" Margarethe took the lock back, examining it like he'd performed magic. "That took me three weeks to learn. You just did it in ten minutes."

"It's patterns," Thomas said, still a little breathless. "Like the cipher. Each pin is a position in a sequence. Once I stopped thinking of it as forcing and started thinking of it as solving..."

Margarethe sat back, studying him with new eyes. "Wilhelm really did see something in you."

Thomas felt that warmth again. Different from pride. Warmer.

"Third lesson," Margarethe said, standing abruptly. "Lying."

"What?"

"You need to be able to lie convincingly. If we're caught, if we're questioned, you need to sell a story." She crossed her arms. "Convince me you're a pilgrim on your way to Cologne. Go."

Thomas tried. Stammered through a story about visiting a shrine, praying for his sick mother, needing food.

Margarethe's expression was flat. "I'd arrest you immediately. Again."

He tried again. Worse.

Fifteen minutes later, Margarethe held up a hand. "Stop. You're hopeless at this."

"I know."

"No, I mean truly, catastrophically hopeless. Your face gives away everything you're thinking." She pinched the bridge of her nose. "Fine. New plan: you don't talk. If we're questioned, you're a mute. Took a vow of silence. I'll do the talking."

"I can try to get better—"

"No. Some people are born liars. You're born honest. It's not a flaw in normal life. But in this life..." She sighed. "Just don't talk."

"Understood."

"Last lesson." She pulled out two practice daggers—blunted blades, safe for training. "Defense."

Thomas eyed them warily. "I told you, I'm weak. I can't fight—"

"I'm not teaching you to fight. I'm teaching you not to die immediately." She handed him one blade. "Hold it like this. Blade down, close to your body. It's not a sword—you don't swing it. You thrust, you slash, you create distance."

She demonstrated. Fast, economical movements.

"Now defend."

She came at him—slow, telegraphed, giving him time.

Thomas stumbled backward, nearly dropped the blade, tripped over his own feet, and fell on his ass.

Margarethe stood over him, one eyebrow raised.

Then she laughed.

It was the first real laugh Thomas had heard from her—not bitter, not sharp, just genuine. It transformed her face, made her look younger, less haunted.

"I'm sorry," she said, extending a hand. "I know I shouldn't laugh. But you looked like a startled chicken."

Thomas took her hand, let her pull him up. He was grinning despite himself. "I warned you I was useless."

"Useless is a strong word. Entertainingly incompetent, maybe." She was still smiling. "Again. And this time, try to stay on your feet."

They trained for another hour. Thomas didn't improve much—he was simply not built for physical combat. But Margarethe taught him a few basics: how to create distance, how to protect his vitals, how to run effectively.

"You're not going to win a fight," she said finally, wiping sweat from her forehead. "But you might survive one long enough for me to kill whoever's attacking you."

"Reassuring."

"It's realistic." She sheathed the practice blades. "You're a scholar, Thomas. Not a warrior. Use your strengths—patterns, locks, thinking. Leave the violence to me."

"What if something happens to you?"

"Then you run. Like I told you." But her voice was softer now. "You asked about my scar earlier."

Thomas looked at the thin line along her jaw. He had wondered, but hadn't pressed.

"I got it trusting the wrong person," Margarethe said quietly. "A merchant who said he was part of the network. Said he could get me into a library the Order had seized. Turned out he was an informant. Led me straight into a trap."

"How did you escape?"

"My father came for me. Fought three men to get me out." Her hand drifted unconsciously to one of her daggers—her father's blades. "He took a wound that never fully healed. Died six months later. Infection from a blade that should have killed me."

Thomas's chest tightened. "I'm sorry."

"Don't be. It taught me a lesson: trust is expensive, and betrayal is cheap." She met his eyes. "I'm telling you this because tonight, in that cathedral, we're going to be trusting Oskar's information. Trusting Brother Marcus to let us in. Trusting the locks to open and the Gray Robes not to be waiting. That's a lot of trust."

"Are you saying we shouldn't go?"

"No. I'm saying be ready for it to go wrong." She pulled her cloak on, checked her real daggers. "And Thomas?"

"Yes?"

"If something goes wrong tonight—if I tell you to run—you actually do it this time. Don't try to be brave. Don't try to save me. You're carrying your grandfather's legacy. That matters more than my life."

"You matter to me," Thomas said before he could stop himself.

Margarethe froze. For a moment, something flickered across her face—surprise, maybe. Or fear.

"Don't," she said quietly. "Don't make me matter. Everyone who matters to me dies."

"I'll try not to die, then."

"You're terrible at lying, remember?" But there was something in her eyes now—not warmth, exactly. But maybe the possibility of it. "Come on. It's almost nightfall. We need to eat, rest. Midnight comes fast."

She walked past him toward the food stores.

Thomas stood there a moment longer, feeling the weight of the coming night.

Tonight, they would break into the cathedral.

Tonight, they would face the Order on their own ground.

And tonight, he would discover if he was truly Wilhelm's grandson—or just another weak boy pretending to be brave.

But for now, in this moment, with Margarethe's rare laughter still echoing in the warehouse and the iron key heavy in his pocket, Thomas allowed himself to hope.

Maybe they could do this.

Maybe they could survive.

Maybe he wasn't useless after all.

## Scene 4: The Cathedral Infiltration

Midnight found them in the shadows across from Cathedral St. Martin.

The great church loomed against the night sky, its spire a dark needle pointing toward uncaring stars. Plague bells rang somewhere in the city—another cart of bodies, another list of the dead. Thomas pulled his cloak tighter against the cold.

"Remember the plan," Margarethe whispered. "Chapter house entrance. Psalm 117, verse 1. Brother Marcus will let us in if he's sympathetic. If not..."

"We improvise," Thomas finished.

"You're learning." She checked the street—empty, silent except for distant coughing from a plague house. "Stay close. Stay quiet. And for the love of God, don't trip."

They crossed the square quickly, keeping to shadows. The cathedral's side entrance was a small wooden door, unremarkable except for its iron lock—much more complex than the practice one Margarethe had given him.

She knocked. Three soft raps.

Silence.

Thomas's heart hammered. This was it. If Oskar had been wrong, if Brother Marcus wasn't sympathetic, if the Order was waiting—

The door opened a crack.

A young monk peered out, his face pale and nervous. "Yes?"

Margarethe leaned close. "*Praise the Lord, all nations,*" she quoted softly. Psalm 117, verse 1.

The monk's eyes widened. He glanced behind him, then opened the door wider. "Quickly."

They slipped inside. The chapter house was dark, lit only by a single candle the monk carried. The smell of incense and old stone was overwhelming after the plague-stink of the streets.

"Brother Marcus?" Margarethe asked.

"Yes." The monk bolted the door behind them. He was young—maybe twenty-five—with the kind of face that looked like it wanted to be kind but had learned not to be. "Oskar sent word you'd come. You have one hour. Less, if the Gray Robes suspect anything."

"The library?" Thomas asked.

Marcus gestured down a corridor. "Through the nave, past the altar, behind the choir. The door is locked, but..." He looked at Margarethe's face, seemed to understand. "You'll manage."

"Why are you helping us?" Thomas couldn't help asking.

Marcus's smile was sad. "Because Wilhelm of Eltville was kind to me when I was a novice. Because knowledge should serve God, not power. Because the Order has forgotten what mercy means." He handed Margarethe the candle. "One hour. No more. Go."

They went.

The nave of the cathedral was vast, their footsteps echoing despite Thomas's best efforts at silence. Moonlight filtered through stained glass, painting the floor in reds and blues. Saints stared down from the walls, their faces stern and judging.

Thomas had been in cathedrals before. But never like this—never as a fugitive, never in darkness, never with his life balanced on a knife's edge.

They reached the altar. Behind it, a smaller door—the entrance to the choir and, beyond that, the library.

Locked.

Margarethe pulled out her picks, but Thomas stopped her. "Let me try."

She hesitated, then nodded. "Fast as you can."

Thomas knelt, inserted the picks. Felt for the pattern. This lock was more complex than the practice one—six pins instead of four, tighter tolerances. But the principle was the same.

Pattern recognition. Sequence. Positions.

First pin. Second pin. Third—

The third pin stuck. Thomas adjusted the tension, probed gently. Come on, show me where you want to be—

Click.

Fourth pin. Fifth pin. Sixth pin.

The lock opened.

"Forty seconds," Margarethe breathed. "You did that in forty seconds."

Thomas stood, hands shaking from adrenaline and concentration. "The pattern was clear."

"You're going to be insufferable about this, aren't you?" But she was smiling.

They slipped into the library.

And Thomas forgot how to breathe.

The room was enormous—two stories of manuscripts, chained to desks and shelves. Latin, Greek, Arabic. Theological texts, natural philosophy, astronomy, medicine. The collected knowledge of centuries, preserved and protected.

Or hoarded and controlled, depending on your perspective.

"There," Margarethe pointed to a desk near the back. "The Boethius commentaries. That's where Hamo was working before..."

Before they killed him.

Thomas crossed to the desk. Found the commentary—the same one he'd seen that terrible morning in the scriptorium. Someone had cleaned it, but he could still see faint blood stains on the vellum.

Hamo's blood.

He pushed down the grief. Later. Grieve later.

He pulled out the iron key, studied the manuscript margins. Colored dots, carefully placed. Red and blue and yellow, encoding information that had cost so many lives.

"Which page?" Margarethe asked, watching the door.

"I don't... wait." Thomas flipped through carefully. The dots appeared on multiple pages, but one section had a denser concentration. The commentary on *Consolation of Philosophy*, Book V. About fate and free will and the nature of knowledge.

How appropriate.

He found the textual anchor—the phrase *"divine foreknowledge"*—and aligned the key.

Through the aperture, dots appeared in sharp focus. Thomas counted, his mind calculating automatically.

"Five red, two blue, three yellow," he murmured. "Position five-two-three. That's..." He closed his eyes, remembering Margarethe's lessons. "I don't know that code."

"Let me see." Margarethe leaned over his shoulder. Looked at the pattern. Her face went pale. "That's not a location."

"What is it?"

"A warning." She pointed to the specific arrangement. "See how the blue dots bracket the red ones? That's a danger pattern. And the count—five-two-three—that's a date marker."

"Date of what?"

"I don't know. The cipher usually marks locations, not—" She stopped. Stared at the page. "Thomas. Look at the text itself. Not the dots. The words Hamo was copying."

Thomas read the Latin. It was a discussion of divine providence, of whether God knew the future or if free will existed. Standard theological debate.

Except.

In the margin, in tiny script barely visible in the candlelight, was a notation in different ink. Hamo's handwriting:

*They know what's coming. They always have. The network didn't predict the plague—they prepared for it. How far back does this go?*

Thomas's hands went cold.

"He figured it out," Margarethe whispered. "Hamo realized the cipher wasn't just recording plague survival. It was—"

"Expecting it." Thomas flipped back through the manuscript. More marginal notes in Hamo's hand, increasingly frantic:

*Dots placed fifty years before plague arrival. Impossible unless—*

*Check Constantinople records. Plague of Justinian. Same pattern?*

*If true, then Wilhelm was right. The network is OLD. Older than Church. Older than—*

The last entry stopped mid-sentence. That must have been when the Order came for him.

"We need to copy this," Thomas said. "All of it. If Hamo died to understand this—"

"No time." Margarethe had moved to the door, was listening. "We've been here twenty minutes. We need to find what we came for and—"

Footsteps in the corridor.

Multiple sets. Heavy boots, not sandals.

Margarethe's hand went to her dagger. "Gray Robes."

"Brother Marcus—"

"Either he betrayed us or they caught him." Margarethe looked around the library. One entrance. No windows. They were trapped. "The key. Use it. Find the next location NOW."

Thomas turned back to the manuscript, hands shaking. Forced himself to concentrate. The cipher. The pattern. If Hamo had been working on this commentary, there had to be more—

Another page. More dots, denser concentration. He aligned the key.

Seven red, seven blue, seven yellow. Position seven-seven-seven.

"What does that mean?" Thomas asked urgently.

"I don't—" Margarethe's eyes widened. "Seven. That's the sacred number. Seven days of creation, seven virtues, seven—"

"Seven keys," Thomas breathed. "This isn't encoding a location. It's encoding a REVELATION."

"What revelation?"

Thomas stared at the pattern. His grandfather's gift was pattern recognition. So he looked at the WHOLE page, not just the dots. The text, the illuminations, the structure.

And he saw it.

The dots weren't just random markers. They formed a MAP. A genealogical map.

Lines of descent. Bloodlines. Families tracked across generations.

"They weren't just recording who survived the plague," Thomas said, his voice shaking. "They were tracking BLOODLINES. Specific families. For generations. This manuscript is from 1200. The plague came in 1347. They were tracking these families for over a hundred years BEFORE the plague arrived."

The footsteps were closer now. Voices in the corridor, calm and methodical:

"—check the library—"

"—Brother Marcus confessed they entered through the chapter house—"

"—Brother Anselm wants them alive if possible—"

Margarethe grabbed Thomas's arm. "We need to go. Now."

"There's no other exit—"

"Then we make one." She looked up. The library had a second floor, a gallery with more manuscripts. And high windows, too small for a grown man but maybe—

"Up," Margarethe commanded.

They climbed the spiral stairs to the gallery. The voices were in the library now, searching systematically.

"Check the upper level."

Margarethe and Thomas reached the high windows. She pulled out a dagger, used the pommel to break the glass. The sound was deafeningly loud in the quiet library.

Shouting below. Footsteps on the stairs.

"Go!" Margarethe boosted Thomas toward the window. He was thin enough to fit, barely. He squeezed through, glass cutting his arms, emerged onto a narrow ledge running along the cathedral's exterior.

Margarethe followed, faster and more graceful.

They were two stories up. Below, the cobblestone square. Above, the cathedral roof.

"Can you climb?" Margarethe asked.

Thomas looked at the wall. Rough stone, carved decorations, gargoyles leering from the eaves. "I have no choice, do I?"

"Not really."

Behind them, Gray Robes reached the gallery. One started trying to squeeze through the window.

Thomas climbed.

His hands found holds in the carved stone. His weak arms protested, muscles burning. One foot slipped, and for a terrible moment he hung by his fingers, two stories of empty air below him.

"Don't look down," Margarethe called, climbing beside him with ease. "Focus on the next handhold."

Thomas climbed. His world narrowed to stone and breath and terror.

He reached the roof. Pulled himself over the edge, lay gasping on the tiles.

Margarethe landed beside him, barely winded. "We need to keep moving. They'll have archers if they think to—"

An arrow struck the tiles near Thomas's head.

"Climb faster next time," Margarethe said, and they ran.

The cathedral roof was a landscape of slopes and spires, gargoyles and gutters. They ran along the spine, tiles cracking under their feet. Below, shouts. The Gray Robes were organizing, surrounding the building.

"There!" Margarethe pointed to an adjacent building—a wealthy merchant's house, close enough to the cathedral that their roofs nearly touched.

She jumped the gap.

Thomas looked at the distance. Maybe six feet. Could have been a mile.

"Thomas!" Margarethe called. "Jump!"

Arrows whistled past. Behind him, a Gray Robe was climbing onto the roof.

Thomas ran. Leaped.

For a moment, he was flying, suspended over empty space. The ground was very far away. The wind was very cold.

Then Margarethe's hand caught his arm, yanked him onto the merchant's roof.

"I hate you," Thomas gasped.

"You love me," she shot back, grinning wildly.

They ran across the merchant's roof, down to a lower building, through a clothesline, finally dropping into an alley. Margarethe landed in a crouch. Thomas landed in a heap.

"I really need to teach you how to fall," Margarethe said, pulling him up.

They ran through the night-maze of Mainz, the alarm bells ringing behind them, the Order mobilizing to hunt them down.

But they were alive.

And they had the knowledge Hamo had died to protect.

The cipher wasn't just a map of hidden texts.

It was a map of human bloodlines, tracked for generations before the plague.

The network had known. Had prepared. Had guided.

The question was why.

And Thomas suspected the answer was going to be worse than he could imagine.

## Scene 5: The Confrontation

They didn't make it back to the warehouse.

The streets were too watched, the Order too mobilized. Margarethe led them instead to a different safe house—a tanner's shop in the industrial quarter, abandoned since the plague killed its owner. The smell was horrific, but it was shelter.

They'd been there maybe an hour, catching their breath, when the door opened.

Brother Anselm walked in alone.

He moved like water, calm and inevitable. The lamplight caught his gray robes, the silver key pendant at his throat. His face was gentle, sad, almost grandfatherly.

Thomas had never been more terrified.

Margarethe's dagger was out instantly. "Don't move."

"I'm not here to fight, Heinrich's daughter." Anselm raised his hands, showing they were empty. "I'm here to talk."

"The Order doesn't talk. It tortures and kills."

"Sometimes. When necessary." Anselm's voice was soft, reasonable. "Your father, for instance. I took no pleasure in his death. But he wouldn't listen. Wouldn't understand what was at stake."

Margarethe's hand shook with rage. "You murdered him."

"I gave him a chance to join us. To help preserve what matters. He chose ideology over survival." Anselm looked at Thomas. "Wilhelm made the same choice. Brilliant men, both of them. Wasted on principle."

"Don't speak about my grandfather," Thomas said, finding his voice.

"Why not? I knew him well. We studied together, once. Before he decided knowledge should belong to everyone instead of those wise enough to protect it." Anselm moved further into the room, slow and non-threatening. "You found the Boethius manuscript. You saw what Hamo discovered."

"The bloodlines," Thomas said. "You're tracking families."

"We're PRESERVING families." Anselm's voice carried genuine conviction. "Do you know what happened before Rome fell? Before the Bronze Age collapsed? Before every great civilization died? Knowledge was shared freely. Every fool with a question could access power they didn't understand. And every time—EVERY time—they destroyed themselves."

"So you hoard knowledge to prevent catastrophe," Margarethe said bitterly.

"We PRESERVE knowledge to ensure survival." Anselm looked at her directly. "Your father's research, Wilhelm's cipher network—you think it was about spreading information. It wasn't. It was about SELECTING it. Ensuring the right knowledge reached the right people at the right time."

"Who decides what's right?" Thomas demanded.

"Those who understand the patterns. Those who can see the long arc of history." Anselm gestured to the iron key in Thomas's pocket. "That key is one of seven. Each one unlocks a piece of the Genesis Protocol—the complete knowledge of how civilizations die and how they can be saved. The network created it after Rome fell, to ensure the next age would be better."

"By controlling who survives?" Thomas felt sick.

"By ensuring the people who CAN survive are positioned correctly." Anselm pulled out a manuscript—Thomas recognized it as another copy of the Boethius commentary. "The plague currently killing Europe isn't random. It follows trade routes, population centers, specific bloodlines. The cipher tracks who has natural resistance, who doesn't. We guide the resistant ones to safe locations. We... thin the others."

Margarethe made a strangled sound. "You're HELPING the plague kill people."

"We're helping the RIGHT people survive." Anselm's voice was patient, like explaining to a child. "Without intervention, the plague kills indiscriminately. With guidance, it becomes selection. Painful, yes. Tragic, certainly. But necessary."

"That's monstrous," Thomas whispered.

"That's survival." Anselm looked at him with something almost like pity. "Thomas, you have your grandfather's gift. Pattern recognition. Memory. The very traits the network has been cultivating for forty generations. You're a PRODUCT of the cipher. Your family was selected, guided, preserved specifically to produce someone like you."

Thomas's world tilted. "No."

"Yes. Wilhelm understood this. He tried to rebel against it, spread the knowledge freely, dismantle the system. And it killed him." Anselm took a step closer. "You can make a different choice. Join us. Use your gift to help select the next generation. Help humanity survive what's coming."

"What's coming?" Margarethe asked.

"Worse than plague. Worse than Rome's fall. The cycles are accelerating." Anselm's face was grave. "In six hundred years, maybe less, everything will burn again. The Genesis Protocol is designed to ensure when it does, the right people are positioned to rebuild. But only if we complete it."

"And if we refuse?" Thomas asked.

"Then you die. Margarethe dies. And the knowledge you're carrying dies with you." Anselm's voice hardened slightly. "I'm offering you a choice, Thomas. Your grandfather didn't get one. Your father is teaching you to hate me, but hate doesn't change the truth: The Order preserves humanity. The network claims to free it, but freedom without wisdom is just chaos waiting to happen."

Thomas looked at Margarethe. She was rigid with fury, her dagger still pointed at Anselm. But her eyes met Thomas's, and in them he saw a question:

*What do we do?*

Thomas thought about Hamo, dying in the scriptorium. About his grandfather, dead for knowledge. About all the people the Order had killed in the name of preservation.

And he thought about the bloodlines in the manuscript. The families tracked for generations. The possibility that his entire existence was designed, cultivated, controlled.

"No," he said quietly.

Anselm's expression didn't change. "You're certain?"

"Knowledge should be free. Even dangerous knowledge. Especially dangerous knowledge." Thomas pulled out the iron key. "My grandfather believed that. So do I."

"Then you're a fool, like he was." Anselm's hand moved, and suddenly there was a dagger in it—fast, practiced, deadly. "And fools don't survive."

He lunged at Thomas.

Margarethe intercepted, her blade meeting his. The sound of steel on steel rang in the small space.

They fought—Anselm with brutal efficiency, Margarethe with desperate skill. Thomas scrambled back, useless, weak, unable to help.

Anselm was better. Older, more experienced, utterly calm. He drove Margarethe back, step by step, toward the corner.

"You fight like your father," Anselm said, almost conversationally. "Brave but predictable."

Margarethe's response was a vicious slash that Anselm barely dodged.

Thomas looked around frantically. He couldn't fight. Couldn't help directly. But patterns—there had to be a pattern—

The room. Anselm's positioning. He was driving Margarethe toward the corner deliberately. But that meant his back was to Thomas. Exposed.

There was a leather-working tool on the table. Heavy, sharp.

Thomas grabbed it.

Margarethe saw him, understood instantly. She feinted left, then dove right, clearing the path.

Thomas threw the tool with all his weak, useless strength.

It struck Anselm in the shoulder, not lethal but surprising.

The Brother spun, dagger ready—

Margarethe's blade found his exposed side, between the ribs.

Anselm made a small, surprised sound. Looked down at the blood spreading across his gray robes.

"Fools sometimes survive," Margarethe said coldly. "Because they're unpredictable."

Anselm collapsed, breathing shallow. He looked at Thomas, and his expression was... sad.

"You don't understand what you've done," he whispered. "The Protocol... someone has to complete it... or everything burns..."

"Then we'll rebuild without controlling who gets to survive," Thomas said.

Anselm smiled, blood on his teeth. "Wilhelm... would be proud... and wrong..."

He died.

Thomas stood there, shaking. He'd killed someone. Not directly, but his actions had led to this death. Two nights ago, he'd been a scribe. Now he was a killer.

"We need to go," Margarethe said, cleaning her blade. Her voice was steady, but Thomas saw her hands shaking. "The Order will find his body. They'll come for us with everything they have."

"Where?" Thomas asked numbly.

"Out of Mainz. Tonight. The river." She looked at Anselm's body, and for a moment something flickered in her eyes—grief, maybe, or regret. "He was wrong. But he believed he was right. That's almost worse."

Thomas nodded. Couldn't speak.

They gathered what little they had, slipped out into the plague-haunted streets, made their way to the river docks.

Behind them, Mainz burned with alarm bells and the Order's fury.

Ahead, the Rhine flowed dark and cold, carrying them away from the only home Thomas had known.

He clutched the iron key in his pocket and tried not to think about Anselm's dying words:

*Someone has to complete the Protocol, or everything burns.*

What if the Order was right?

What if spreading knowledge freely really did lead to catastrophe?

And what if he was wrong to fight them?

The questions followed him onto the boat, into the darkness, toward whatever came next.

But one thing was certain:

He was committed now. For better or worse.

The path forward was set.

And there would be blood before this was finished.
