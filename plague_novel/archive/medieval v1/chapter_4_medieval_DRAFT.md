# CHAPTER 4 - THE BISHOP'S FEAST (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: Preparation - Becoming Merchants

### Johannes's Workshop, Morning

Thomas had never worn clothes this fine in his life.

The merchant's outfit Johannes had procured fit surprisingly well—deep blue wool doublet with silver buttons, linen shirt that actually felt clean against his skin, hose that weren't scratchy, leather boots that didn't pinch. He looked at himself in Johannes's cracked mirror and barely recognized the person staring back.

Not a monk. Not a fugitive. A merchant. Wealthy, confident, the kind of man who'd been invited to dine with bishops.

"Stop fidgeting," Johannes said, adjusting Thomas's collar. "Merchants don't fidget. They appraise. They calculate value. They know their worth and expect others to recognize it."

"I look ridiculous."

"You look prosperous. There's a difference." Johannes stepped back, examining his work. "The key is in the posture. Shoulders back. Chin up slightly—not arrogant, just... confident. Like you've closed three profitable deals this week and expect to close three more tomorrow."

Thomas tried. Failed. Slouched back into his natural posture.

"This is hopeless," he muttered.

"It's not hopeless. It's just unfamiliar." Margarethe's voice came from the stairs. "You'll adapt. You always do."

Thomas turned—and stopped breathing.

Margarethe descended the stairs in a merchant's wife costume that transformed her completely. Deep green dress with gold embroidery, her hair actually styled instead of roughly tied back, a delicate veil that suggested wealth without ostentation. Her wounded shoulder was carefully concealed under the dress's construction. She moved differently—not the fighter's prowl Thomas was used to, but something more measured, graceful, deliberate.

She looked beautiful. Dangerous and beautiful.

"Close your mouth," she said with the ghost of a smile. "Merchants don't gape at their own wives."

"Wives?"

"You didn't think we'd attend separately?" Johannes was grinning. "A wealthy merchant and his wife, fleeing the plague in Mainz, seeking the bishop's blessing for safe passage to their holdings in Italy. It's a perfect cover."

Thomas forced himself to think practically. "What if someone questions our story?"

"Then you improvise. Use details you actually know—you grew up near Mainz, you understand the wool trade from monastery dealings, you're educated enough to discuss theology if the bishop tests you." Johannes handed him a leather purse heavy with coins. "Don't flash this around, but let it show occasionally. Wealthy people are casual about their wealth. Poor people pretending to be wealthy are too careful with money."

Margarethe took the purse, secured it to her belt with practiced efficiency. "I'll handle the money. Wives often do. And I can read people better than Thomas can."

"I can read people," Thomas protested.

"You can read manuscripts. You're terrible with people. You think everyone is honest because you're honest." She touched his arm, softening the criticism. "It's not a weakness. It's just not useful tonight."

Johannes cleared the table, spread out his drawings of the cathedral one final time. "Review the plan. One more time. Everything must be perfect."

Thomas studied the layout. St. Thomas Cathedral dominated central Strasbourg—massive Gothic structure, twin towers, side chapels, and the bishop's private residence attached to the north transept. The treasury was in the residence basement, protected by guards and a Bavarian lock mechanism.

"The feast begins at sunset," Johannes said, tracing the route with his finger. "You'll arrive fashionably late—wealthy people always do. Enter through the main doors, present your invitations to the bishop's steward. He'll show you to the great hall where the feast is being held."

"How many guests?" Margarethe asked.

"Forty, maybe fifty. Clergy from across the region, wealthy merchants, minor nobility. Enough that two strangers won't stand out, but not so many that you'll get lost in the crowd." Johannes moved his finger across the drawing. "The feast will last several hours. Multiple courses, entertainment, the bishop's speech about surviving the plague through faith and generosity to the Church."

"How generous?" Thomas asked.

"Very. He'll be soliciting donations. This is actually useful—everyone will be focused on looking pious and wealthy. Perfect distraction." Johannes tapped the treasury location. "You'll need to slip away during the third course. That's when the entertainment begins—musicians, possibly acrobats. Maximum distraction, minimum attention on individual guests."

Margarethe leaned closer, studying the route. "The treasury is here. How do we get to it without being seen?"

"Servants' corridors." Johannes traced a path. "Exit the great hall through the side door—excuse yourselves to visit the privy, perfectly reasonable. Turn left instead of right. Down this corridor, through the kitchen annex—it'll be chaos, no one will notice you. Down the stairs to the basement level. The treasury is the third door on the left."

"Guards?" Thomas asked.

"Two. Stationed outside the treasury door during the feast—the bishop doesn't trust even his own household when there are strangers about. But—" Johannes smiled. "They rotate every hour. Three minutes during rotation when the hallway is empty. You'll need to be fast."

Thomas looked at the lock drawing Johannes had made. Bavarian mechanism. Complex, expensive, supposedly unpickable. Johannes had spent all yesterday teaching him how it worked, making him practice on a replica until his fingers ached.

"Two minutes to pick the lock," Thomas said. "Two minutes inside to find the Silver Key. One minute to lock it again and disappear before the new guards arrive."

"Five minutes total," Johannes confirmed. "Can you do it?"

Thomas's hands were already sweating. "I don't know."

"Yes, you do. You picked my practice lock in ninety seconds this morning." Johannes gripped Thomas's shoulder. "You're better at this than you think. Trust your training."

Margarethe caught Thomas's eye. "I'll watch the corridor. Keep you aware of timing. You focus on the lock."

"What about you?" Thomas asked Johannes. "What's your role?"

"I'll be at the feast. Separate from you—we can't appear to know each other. I'll be the eccentric scholar invited to discuss Greek texts with the bishop's visiting theologian. It gives me an excuse to wander, to create diversions if needed, and to signal if anything goes wrong."

"What's the signal?"

"If I spill wine on myself, abort immediately. Something's wrong, guards are moving, get back to the feast." Johannes rolled up the drawings. "But it won't come to that. This plan is solid."

Thomas wasn't so sure. Plans sounded solid until they collided with reality. Then everything went wrong.

But he didn't say that. Just nodded and tried to project the confidence Johannes and Margarethe seemed to expect.

"One more thing," Johannes said, his voice serious now. "If this goes wrong, if you're caught, you don't know me. You're just thieves who heard about the treasury and tried your luck. You don't mention the cipher, the network, the keys. Nothing that puts the rest of us at risk."

"And if they torture us?" Margarethe asked bluntly.

"Then you resist as long as you can. But eventually, everyone breaks. So we make sure it doesn't come to that." Johannes looked at both of them. "Are you ready?"

Thomas looked at Margarethe. She looked at him.

Neither of them were ready. But they were out of time.

"Yes," Thomas lied.

"Good," Johannes said. "Then let's go steal from a bishop."

## Scene 2: The Feast - Playing the Part

### St. Thomas Cathedral, Evening

The great hall was overwhelming.

Thomas had attended monastery feasts before—simple affairs with long tables and plain food served on wooden trenchers. This was different. This was wealth and power made visible.

The hall soared three stories high, ribbed vaulting overhead painted with gold leaf that caught the light from hundreds of candles. Tapestries covered the walls—hunting scenes, biblical tableaux, heraldic devices of noble families. The tables were covered in white linen, set with silver plates and crystal goblets. Servants in livery moved between guests with practiced efficiency.

And the food. God above, the food.

Roasted swan. Venison in wine sauce. Pike in aspic. Peacock re-dressed in its own feathers. Fruits Thomas had never seen. Breads sculpted into elaborate shapes. Wine that tasted like liquid gold.

All this while plague ravaged the countryside.

"Stop staring," Margarethe murmured beside him. "Merchants are used to wealth. You look like a peasant at a king's table."

Thomas forced himself to relax. Took a drink of wine. Tried to appear bored by the opulence.

They'd been seated near the middle of the hall—not close enough to the bishop's high table to draw intense scrutiny, but not so far away as to seem unimportant. Perfect positioning. Johannes had clearly bribed someone to arrange the seating.

Speaking of Johannes—Thomas spotted him three tables away, deep in conversation with an elderly priest. The alchemist looked completely at ease, gesturing enthusiastically about something theological, playing his role perfectly.

The bishop himself sat at the high table, resplendent in purple robes. Heinrich Vogel was in his fifties, with a face that suggested he enjoyed feasts like this regularly. He was currently listening to a nobleman's story, laughing at appropriate moments, performing the role of generous host.

Somewhere in his private treasury, beneath this very building, the Silver Key waited.

"Third course soon," Margarethe said quietly. She'd been tracking the meal progression with the focus of a general planning a campaign. "The entertainment will begin. That's when we move."

"I know the plan," Thomas said.

"I'm reminding you because you're nervous. When you're nervous, you forget things."

She was right. He was nervous. His hands were shaking slightly. He hid them under the table.

A merchant across from them—portly man named Dietrich, owned vineyards south of Strasbourg—was telling a story about escaping Mainz just before the city gates closed. "The plague bells were ringing," he said, voice dropping to dramatic whisper. "Bodies in the streets. The Order's Gray Robes burning houses where the sick were found. It was the judgment of God made manifest."

"Terrible times," Thomas agreed, because that seemed safe.

"And you escaped when?" Dietrich asked, suddenly focused on Thomas.

Thomas's mind went blank. When had they supposedly escaped? What date had Johannes told them to use?

"Three weeks ago," Margarethe said smoothly. "Just before the river crossings closed. We lost half our goods in the rush, but we escaped with our lives. My husband's warehouse in the Mühlenstadt quarter—burned by the Order as a plague precaution. Everything gone."

She was lying perfectly. Details. Emotion. Conviction.

Dietrich nodded sympathetically. "I heard the Mühlenstadt quarter was hit hard. You were fortunate to escape."

"Fortune favors those who don't hesitate," Margarethe said. "We saw the signs, we acted. Those who waited..." She shrugged eloquently.

The conversation moved on. Thomas breathed again.

"That was close," he murmured when Dietrich's attention shifted.

"You froze. Don't freeze again." But Margarethe's hand found his under the table, squeezed once. Reassurance, not criticism.

The third course arrived—the swan, re-feathered and gilded. Servants carried it in with ceremony. And with it, the entertainment.

Musicians entered the hall. A juggler. Two acrobats who began performing impossible feats of balance and strength.

Everyone's attention shifted to the entertainment.

"Now," Margarethe breathed.

They stood. Casual, unhurried. Thomas caught Johannes's eye across the hall. The alchemist gave the smallest nod.

Go.

They walked toward the side door. Slow, relaxed, just a couple excusing themselves during the entertainment. No one paid attention.

Through the door. Into the corridor beyond.

Left instead of right.

And suddenly they were out of the great hall, in the servants' passages, and the real work began.

## Scene 3: The Heist - The Bavarian Lock

### Cathedral Basement, Treasury Corridor

The kitchen annex was chaos exactly as Johannes had predicted.

Cooks shouting orders. Servants rushing between the kitchens and the great hall with platters and ewers. Steam and heat and the smell of roasting meat. No one gave Thomas and Margarethe a second glance as they moved through, just two more bodies in the controlled madness.

Down the stairs. Stone steps worn smooth by centuries of use. Temperature dropping. Sound of the feast fading behind them.

The basement corridor was quiet. Empty. Torches in wall brackets providing flickering light.

And at the end of the corridor, two guards stationed outside a heavy oak door.

Thomas and Margarethe pressed themselves into a side alcove, watching.

"Guard rotation?" Thomas whispered.

Margarethe checked—she'd been counting time in her head, the way she counted everything. "Four minutes. Maybe five."

They waited. Thomas's heart hammered so loud he was sure the guards would hear it.

Three minutes.

Four minutes.

At five minutes, footsteps on the stairs. The new guards arriving.

Brief conversation. "Anything?"

"Nothing. Dead quiet."

"The bishop's feast always is. Everyone's too busy eating to steal."

Laughter. Then the original guards headed up the stairs. The new guards settled into position.

"Damn," Thomas breathed. "They're already here. We missed the window."

"Wait," Margarethe said.

The new guards pulled out a wineskin. Shared it between them. One said something Thomas couldn't hear. Both laughed.

Then both guards walked down the corridor. Away from the treasury. Toward what looked like a servants' privy.

"They're leaving their post?" Thomas whispered.

"Drinking on duty. Can't be seen drinking near the treasury door. They'll be back in two, three minutes." Margarethe pushed him forward. "Go. Now."

Thomas ran. Light-footed, quiet, everything Margarethe had taught him about moving without sound. Reached the treasury door.

Heavy oak, iron reinforcements, and in the center: the lock.

Bavarian mechanism. Beautiful and deadly.

Thomas pulled his picks from his sleeve—thin metal tools Johannes had made for him. Knelt before the lock. Inserted the first pick, feeling for the pins.

The Bavarian lock used multiple rotating cylinders, each with different pin configurations. You had to set them in sequence, maintaining tension on each while rotating to the next. One mistake and everything reset.

Thomas closed his eyes. Focused. This was just like the manuscripts. Patterns. Sequences. Each click was a letter in a word he was learning to read.

First cylinder. He felt the pins align, applied tension, rotated.

Click.

Second cylinder. More complex. Five pins instead of three. He worked carefully, patient, feeling each subtle shift.

Click.

Behind him, Margarethe whispered: "One minute."

Third cylinder. This one was tricky—the pins were different sizes, required different amounts of pressure. Thomas's hands were sweating. He wiped them on his doublet, started again.

One pin. Two pins. Three pins—

"Thirty seconds."

Fourth pin. Fifth pin. Come on, come on—

Click.

The lock opened.

Thomas pulled the door. It swung inward, silent on well-oiled hinges.

"I'm in."

"Twenty seconds. Hurry."

Thomas slipped inside. The treasury was small—maybe ten feet square. Shelves lined the walls, covered with church valuables. Gold chalices. Jeweled crosses. Illuminated manuscripts in protective cases. Silver reliquaries holding saints' bones.

And there, on the center shelf, displayed prominently: a key.

Not like the Iron Key Thomas carried. This one was silver, ornately carved, with a different symbol on the head—a hand reaching upward, fingers spread.

The Hand that Guides.

Thomas grabbed it. Felt the weight—heavier than the Iron Key, denser metal. The craftsmanship was extraordinary. Each finger on the carved hand was articulated, the palm inscribed with tiny symbols Thomas couldn't read in this light.

Outside, Margarethe's urgent whisper: "Guards coming back. Thirty seconds. Get out now."

Thomas ran to the door. Pulled it closed. His picks were still inside—no time to retrieve them.

The lock. He had to lock it again from the outside or they'd know immediately it had been breached.

But Bavarian locks couldn't be re-locked without the key or special tools. Tools Johannes hadn't been able to make.

"Thomas!" Margarethe's voice was sharp with panic.

"I can't lock it!"

"Leave it. Move!"

Thomas dove into the alcove beside Margarethe just as the guards rounded the corner. They were laughing about something, wineskin passing between them again.

They settled back into position outside the treasury.

Without checking the door.

Thank God. Thank every saint. They hadn't noticed.

But eventually someone would. The next rotation, or when the feast ended, or when the bishop's steward made his nightly rounds. The unlocked door would be discovered. The missing key would be noticed.

They had hours at most. Maybe less.

Margarethe pulled Thomas deeper into the shadows, back toward the stairs. They moved in silence, every nerve screaming.

Up the stairs. Through the kitchen—still chaotic, still no one caring about two more bodies in merchant clothes.

Into the corridor. Toward the great hall.

Almost there. Almost safe.

A hand grabbed Thomas's shoulder.

He spun, heart stopping.

Johannes. The alchemist's face was pale, urgent.

"We have a problem," Johannes whispered. "Don't go back to the feast. There are Gray Robes in the hall. Three of them. Asking about strangers from Mainz."

Thomas felt cold. "How did they find us?"

"I don't know. But we need to leave. Now. Before they start questioning guests individually."

"The Silver Key—" Thomas started.

"Do you have it?"

Thomas nodded.

"Then we go. Follow me. Servants' exit. Quickly."

They followed Johannes through a maze of corridors. Behind them, shouts. Running feet.

Someone had discovered the treasury.

"Run," Johannes said. And they ran.

## Scene 4: The Escape - Johannes's Sacrifice

### Strasbourg Streets, Night

They burst out of the cathedral through a servants' entrance into a narrow alley that stank of kitchen refuse. Rain was falling—cold autumn rain that soaked through Thomas's fine merchant clothes in seconds.

"This way!" Johannes led them through the maze of streets surrounding the cathedral. Behind them, bells started ringing. Alarm. Search. The entire cathedral guard would be mobilizing.

"Where are we going?" Thomas gasped, lungs burning.

"Safe house. Tanner's quarter. Not far."

They ran through rain-slicked streets. Around corners. Through passages so narrow Thomas's shoulders brushed both walls. Margarethe kept pace despite her wounded shoulder, her face set in grim determination.

Behind them, torches. Shouts. The pursuit was organized, coordinated.

"They're too close," Margarethe said. "We need to split up."

"No," Thomas and Johannes said simultaneously.

"Yes." Margarethe grabbed Thomas's arm, thrust him toward Johannes. "You have both keys. You're what matters. I'll draw them off, lose them in the tanners' quarter. I know this city."

"Margarethe—"

"Don't argue. Go." She was already moving, shouting in German, drawing attention.

The Gray Robes turned toward her voice. Three of them, closing in.

"No!" Thomas tried to go back.

Johannes grabbed him, pulled him the opposite direction. "She gave us a chance. Don't waste it."

They ran. Thomas looked back once, saw Margarethe disappearing into shadows, the Gray Robes pursuing.

Then Johannes pulled him around a corner and she was gone.

They ran for ten more minutes. Through the tanners' quarter—the smell was overwhelming, leather and chemicals and death. Across a small plaza. Down an alley barely wide enough for one person.

Finally, Johannes stopped at a nondescript door. Knocked a pattern. The door opened. They tumbled inside.

A woman—middle-aged, stern-looking—locked the door behind them. "Trouble?"

"Cathedral guards. Gray Robes. They'll search the quarter but they won't find us here." Johannes was breathing hard, soaked with rain and sweat. "Are you hurt?" he asked Thomas.

"No. But Margarethe—"

"Margarethe knows how to disappear. She's better at this than either of us." Johannes sounded confident. But Thomas heard the worry underneath.

They waited in the safe house. Thomas paced. Johannes sat still, conserving energy, thinking.

An hour passed. Two hours.

No sign of Margarethe.

Thomas pulled out the Silver Key, turned it over in his hands. The Hand that Guides. Was this worth it? Was any of this worth it?

At three hours, there was a knock at the door. The pattern Johannes had used.

The stern woman opened it.

Margarethe stumbled in, soaked, bleeding from a cut on her forehead, but alive.

"Lost them," she gasped. "Doubled back. Four times. They gave up."

Thomas caught her before she fell. "You're hurt."

"I'm alive. That's what counts." She looked at Johannes. "Did we get it?"

Thomas held up the Silver Key.

Margarethe smiled despite the blood and exhaustion. "Then it was worth it."

But before anyone could celebrate, there was another knock.

Different pattern. Urgent.

The stern woman opened the door a crack. A young man stood outside, rain-soaked, terrified.

"Message for Johannes the Alchemist. The Gray Robes raided your workshop. They're looking for you. They know you were at the feast. They're connecting you to the theft."

Johannes's face went pale. "My research. Everything—"

"Burned," the young man said. "I'm sorry. They torched it all."

For a moment, Johannes looked lost. His life's work. Gone.

Then he straightened. "It doesn't matter. The knowledge is in here." He tapped his head. "They can burn buildings but they can't burn what I've already learned."

"There's more," the young man said. "They have the guest lists from the feast. They're going through it, looking for the couple from Mainz. The ones who disappeared during the third course."

Thomas felt sick. "They'll figure it out."

"Not immediately. I used false names for your invitations. But eventually..." Johannes trailed off.

"We need to leave Strasbourg," Margarethe said. "Tonight."

"Agreed." Johannes looked at the Silver Key Thomas still held. "But first, we use it. Both keys together. See what they reveal that one alone couldn't."

He spread a manuscript on the table—one of the texts from his workshop, saved because he'd brought it to the safe house earlier. Marginal cipher dots covered the pages.

"The Iron Key alone showed us locations," Johannes said. "Single points of information. Let's see what two keys reveal."

Thomas placed the Iron Key on the manuscript. The eye symbol aligned with the text. Cipher dots appeared through the aperture, exactly as before.

Then Johannes placed the Silver Key beside it.

And the pattern changed.

The dots didn't just show locations anymore. They showed CONNECTIONS. Lines between locations, visible only when both keys were present. A web spreading across the page.

"My God," Johannes breathed. "It's not just marking where knowledge is hidden. It's showing how it's connected. How information flows through the network."

Thomas saw it. Each location was a node. Each connection a pathway. The entire manuscript became a map of knowledge distribution across medieval Europe.

"And look here." Johannes pointed to a cluster of connections. "These aren't just knowledge repositories. These are FAMILIES. Bloodlines. See—this mark indicates a family. This one indicates a marriage. This one indicates offspring."

Margarethe leaned closer. "They're tracking genealogies?"

"Not just tracking. Guiding." Johannes traced the connections. "Look—this family in Cologne produces children with the pattern recognition trait. That family in Vienna has eidetic memory. The network arranges a marriage between them. Three generations later, their descendants have both traits."

Thomas felt cold understanding. "They're not just preserving knowledge. They're breeding people to carry it."

"For forty generations," Johannes confirmed. "Creating living libraries. People who can hold information that can't be written down. Bloodlines optimized to preserve civilization through collapse."

The room was silent except for rain drumming on the roof.

"That's what Wilhelm was part of," Thomas said quietly. "Not just hiding manuscripts. Creating people who could BE manuscripts."

"And the Order knows," Margarethe said. "That's why they hunt us so viciously. We're not just carrying forbidden knowledge. We ARE forbidden knowledge."

Before anyone could respond, there was pounding on the door. Not a polite knock. A demand.

"Open! In the bishop's name! Open or we break this door!"

The stern woman looked at Johannes, terrified. "The Gray Robes found us."

"Back exit," Johannes said. He grabbed the manuscripts, thrust them at Thomas. "Take these. Both keys. Run. Don't stop until you're out of the city."

"What about you?" Thomas asked.

"I'll delay them. Give you time." Johannes was already moving to the front of the safe house. "Go. NOW."

Margarethe pulled Thomas toward the back. "He's right. We have to—"

"I'm not leaving him!"

"You don't have a choice!"

The front door shattered. Gray Robes poured in. Thomas caught a glimpse of Johannes facing them, unarmed, buying seconds with his words.

Then Margarethe dragged Thomas out the back exit into the rain-soaked night, and they were running again, and Johannes was behind them, captured or dead, and all Thomas could think was: This is my fault. All of this is my fault.

They ran until dawn.

## Scene 5: The Revelation - Bloodlines and Patterns

### Safe House Outside Strasbourg, Morning

They found refuge in a woodcutter's abandoned cottage five miles from the city. Collapsed on the dirt floor. Too exhausted to speak.

Thomas stared at the ceiling beams, watching spiders build webs in the corners. Intricate patterns. Everything was patterns.

"Johannes is captured," he said finally. His voice sounded dead even to himself.

"Or dead," Margarethe said. She was checking her shoulder wound—reopened during the run, bleeding again. "The Order doesn't take prisoners unless they want information."

"He knows everything. The network. The keys. Us."

"He'll resist as long as he can."

"And then he'll break. Everyone breaks." Thomas was using Margarethe's own words against the situation. "We have days at most before they know everything he knows."

Margarethe didn't argue. Just bandaged her shoulder with strips torn from her ruined dress. The fine merchant costume was destroyed—torn, mud-stained, bloodied. They looked like what they were now: fugitives.

Thomas pulled out the two keys. Iron and Silver. The Eye that Sees and the Hand that Guides.

And the manuscript Johannes had given him—the one showing the bloodline connections.

"He said forty generations," Thomas said. "The network has been shepherding specific families for forty generations. That's... that's almost a thousand years."

"Since Rome fell," Margarethe confirmed. "Since the network was founded. They haven't just been preserving knowledge. They've been creating people who could survive the next collapse. People with the right traits."

Thomas studied the manuscript more carefully. The bloodline patterns were extraordinarily complex. Families in Cologne married to families in Paris. Their children married to families in Rome. Traits tracked. Combinations tested. Successes marked. Failures... terminated.

"My grandfather," Thomas said slowly. "Wilhelm. His family is in here."

He found the line. Wilhelm von Eltville. Father: Friedrich. Grandfather: Conrad. Great-grandfather: Hermann. Back and back, generation after generation, all the way to someone named Adalbert in the 9th century.

And beside each name, notations. Spatial reasoning: Superior. Pattern recognition: Exceptional. Eidetic memory: Present.

"They bred him," Thomas whispered. "Bred his entire line to produce someone who could read the cipher naturally."

"And you," Margarethe said quietly. "You have his gift. Which means—"

"Which means I'm not an accident. I'm a designed outcome." The realization hit him like a physical blow. "Everything about me—my ability with patterns, my memory, the way I can see connections others miss—it's not natural talent. It's engineered."

Margarethe put a hand on his arm. "It's still you. What you can do is real, even if how you got it was guided."

"Is it? How do I know what parts of me are actually me, and what parts are just... genetic inheritance that someone planned thirty generations ago?"

He was spiraling. He knew he was spiraling. But he couldn't stop.

Margarethe gripped his shoulders, forced him to look at her. "Listen to me. I can't read. Never could, never will. My father turned that limitation into an ability. Made me a living library because the bloodlines needed one. I spent my entire life wondering if I was a person or a tool."

"And what did you decide?"

"That I'm both. And that's fine." Her eyes were fierce. "You can be a designed outcome AND make your own choices. The two aren't mutually exclusive. What you DO with your gifts matters more than how you got them."

Thomas wanted to believe that. Needed to believe it.

"Show me your family," he said. "In the bloodlines."

Margarethe hesitated. Then took the manuscript. Found her line.

Margarethe of Mainz. Father: Heinrich the Scholar. Mother: Anna the Translator. Grandmother: Elisabeth. Back and back.

And beside her mother's name: *Died in childbirth. Complication: Deliberate. Subject terminated for refusing network directive.*

Margarethe went very still.

"They killed her," she said. Voice flat, emotionless in the way that meant she was feeling too much. "My mother. She refused some network order and they killed her. Made it look like natural complications."

Thomas saw it in the notations. Anna had been brilliant—spoke five languages, translated ancient texts, carried multiple valuable genetic markers. But she'd refused to accept a marriage arrangement. Had fallen in love with Heinrich on her own. The network had allowed the pregnancy to proceed—the genetic combination was acceptable—but had terminated Anna during childbirth as punishment for disobedience.

"I'm sorry," Thomas said. Useless words.

"Don't be. I'm angry. Anger is useful." Margarethe stared at the manuscript. "My whole life, I thought my mother died from bad luck. From the risks of childbirth. But it was murder. Calculated murder."

"And your father?"

Margarethe found that notation. Heinrich's death. *Subject became security risk after spouse termination. Displayed increased resistance to network directives. Terminated via Order proxy.*

"They used the Order to kill him," Margarethe said. "Let the Gray Robes torture him to death. Cleaned up their own mess by making it look like the enemy did it."

She was shaking now. With rage or grief or both.

Thomas didn't know what to say. Everything felt inadequate.

Finally, Margarethe rolled up the manuscript. "We need to find the other keys. All seven. Understand the complete Genesis Protocol. Because if we don't..." She trailed off.

"If we don't?"

"Then my parents died for nothing. Johannes was captured for nothing. Your grandfather's murder was meaningless." She met Thomas's eyes. "But if we complete it. If we understand what the network is actually trying to do, what the Protocol truly says... then maybe we can stop them. Or expose them. Or at least make their deaths mean something."

Thomas thought about it. About the choice in front of them.

They could run. Disappear. Live quiet lives and hope the network never found them.

Or they could continue. Seek the remaining five keys. Unlock the full truth of what the network had been doing for a thousand years.

"Johannes said the third key is a person," Thomas said. "A living descendant who carries cipher knowledge in their bloodline memory. He mentioned Prague."

"That's weeks of travel. Through plague country. With the Order hunting us." Margarethe didn't sound opposed. Just realistic.

"I know."

"We could die. Probably will die."

"I know that too."

Margarethe looked at him. Really looked at him. "You've changed. You know that? A month ago you were a monk who'd never left the monastery. Now you're talking about traveling across Europe chasing keys to unlock a thousand-year-old conspiracy."

"I killed a man," Thomas said quietly. "Stole from a bishop. Ran from the Order. Discovered I'm genetically engineered. I'm already damned. Might as well finish what we started."

Despite everything, Margarethe laughed. It was a dark sound, but genuine. "All right then. We go to Prague. Find the human key. Unlock the next layer of this nightmare."

"Together?"

"Together."

Thomas felt something settle in his chest. Not peace—that was too much to ask. But purpose. Direction.

They had two of seven keys. A manuscript showing bloodline patterns going back forty generations. And the knowledge that the network had been playing God with humanity for a thousand years.

Outside the cottage, dawn was breaking. A new day. A new chapter in a quest that would probably kill them.

Thomas packed the keys and manuscript carefully. Margarethe checked her weapons.

And together, they left the cottage and headed east.

Toward Prague. Toward the third key. Toward answers.

Or toward death.

Probably both.
