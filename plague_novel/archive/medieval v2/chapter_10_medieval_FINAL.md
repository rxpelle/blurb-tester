# CHAPTER 10 - SMALL REBELLIONS (Medieval Timeline - 1348-1349)

## Scene 1: Sanctuary - The Welsh Monastery

### Welsh Mountains, Summer 1348

The monastery had no name that Thomas ever learned—just "the old place" in the Welsh the villagers spoke, or "Llan Hen" when they wanted to be specific. Ancient beyond reckoning, built on foundations that predated Christian conversion, maintained by a handful of elderly monks who cared more about preserving knowledge than enforcing orthodoxy.

Thomas stood in the scriptorium on his second morning there, looking out at mountains that disappeared into perpetual mist. The room smelled of old parchment and ink and the particular mustiness that came from centuries of accumulated manuscripts slowly deteriorating despite careful preservation efforts.

"You're the pattern-reader," a voice said behind him.

Thomas turned to find the abbot—a man so old his face had collapsed into a map of wrinkles, but his eyes remained sharp and knowing. Brother Cadfael, they called him, though Thomas suspected that was a name assumed long ago rather than one given at birth.

"I read patterns in manuscripts," Thomas said carefully. "Cipher systems. Hidden knowledge encoded in decorative elements."

"You were bred for it," Brother Cadfael said, not a question. "Forty-one generations of careful pairing to make a man who can see marks ordinary people walk past. The network's work. And from what the messenger told me, you've turned that gift against them."

Thomas felt his hand move instinctively toward the concealed Iron and Silver Keys he still carried. "How do you know about the network? About bloodline breeding?"

"I'm ninety-three years old," Brother Cadfael said, lowering himself onto a bench with the careful movements of someone whose joints no longer cooperated reliably. "I've preserved manuscripts for seventy years. Read things the network thought safely hidden in obscure Welsh monasteries where no bishop or lord would ever look. Learned enough of their breeding work—and the ruin they sometimes set loose—to know what they've become."

He gestured at the shelves surrounding them. "These manuscripts hold scraps of knowledge the network has been trying to bury for centuries. Not the complete Genesis Protocol—I never had access to all seven keys here. But enough that a patient man could begin to scent the trail. I've been waiting decades for someone who might use it honestly."

"And you think I will?" Thomas asked.

"I think you already have. The messenger said you distributed the complete Protocol. Created copies spreading across Europe. Forced network members to choose between continued service and rebellion. That's more than anyone has accomplished in centuries of resistance." Brother Cadfael's ancient face showed something like satisfaction. "Now you need to survive long enough to see whether your distribution matters."

"By hiding here," Thomas said, feeling the weight of retreat even as he recognized its necessity.

"By teaching here," Brother Cadfael corrected. "The village has forty children. None of them can read. Most of their lives are spent scratching bread from cold soil. They're exactly the kind of people the network dismisses as irrelevant—too poor, too far off, too untaught to matter in bloodline work or calamity-making."

The old monk stood with effort, moved to the window beside Thomas. "Teach them anyway. Give them letters and sums. A man who can read a charter is harder to cheat. A child who can count is harder to rob. That's how you break a hand that grips too tightly—one finger at a time."

Thomas thought about Maria's blood memory of forty generations of women who'd done exactly that—taught and preserved and witnessed, keeping truth alive by putting it into mouths that couldn't be burned.

"How long can we stay?" he asked.

"As long as you choose," Brother Cadfael said. "The Order's reach doesn't extend effectively this far into the Welsh mountains. The network has better targets than fugitives who've already distributed the evidence they were trying to suppress. You're probably safer here than anywhere else in Europe, at least until the political situation changes."

"And when it changes?"

"Then you adapt. Move if you must. Teach somewhere else. That's how this kind of work survives—by carrying it in your hands, not in one set of walls." Brother Cadfael smiled slightly. "Your ancestors understood that. Forty-one generations of it. Now you add your own small piece."

## Scene 2: Teaching Begins - The First Students

### Three Weeks Later

The children arrived on a bright August morning, shepherded by parents who looked simultaneously hopeful and skeptical about the strange monk who'd offered to teach their offspring without charging the coin that normally made learning impossible for poor families.

Eight children ranging from six to fourteen. Thomas recognized the type immediately—bright eyes, quick movements, the barely contained energy of young minds desperate for stimulation beyond the routine of farm labor and survival.

"This is Master Thomas," Brother Cadfael introduced him to the assembled families. "He'll teach your children to read Latin and do arithmetic and understand basic natural philosophy. No fees. No requirements except that they attend regularly and try to learn."

The parents exchanged glances—the offer was too generous, which meant there must be a catch. Thomas understood their suspicion. In their experience, outsiders offering free services usually wanted something in return.

"I'm a fugitive," he said bluntly, deciding honesty was better than maintaining elaborate cover stories. "Hunted by church authorities for exposing corruption. I can't go to cities or use my real name in places where the Order might find me. But I can teach. And teaching your children costs me nothing except time I have in abundance while hiding."

The honesty seemed to help more than any elaborate reassurance would have. They understood fugitives—plague and war and failed harvests had created plenty of refugees in recent years. A monk hiding from church authority while teaching children was unusual but not incomprehensible.

They left their children and returned to their farms.

Thomas looked at his eight new students—five boys, three girls, ages six to fourteen, all staring at him with the particular mix of curiosity and wariness that came from children unsure what to expect from adult strangers.

"Who can write their own name?" he asked in simple English.

None of them raised their hands.

"Who can count to one hundred?"

Two hands went up—the two oldest boys, probably taught basic arithmetic for market transactions.

"Who wants to learn things nobody in your village currently knows?"

All eight hands shot up immediately.

Thomas smiled. "Then we'll get along fine. Sit down. We'll start with the alphabet."

He'd prepared materials—scraped vellum too damaged for church use but serviceable for practice, charcoal for writing, simple primers he'd created from memory. Had arranged the scriptorium's least valuable benches into student seating.

Had not anticipated how difficult teaching would actually be.

The youngest children—ages six and seven—couldn't sit still for more than a few minutes before fidgeting. The oldest boy, Owain, clearly resented being grouped with younger students. The three girls were quiet to the point of silence, apparently trained to believe learning wasn't meant for them. And none of them had the background knowledge Thomas had unconsciously assumed—they didn't know Latin existed as a different language, didn't understand what "reading" meant beyond vague awareness that monks did it, couldn't grasp why anyone would want to learn letters when farming didn't require literacy.

By midday, Thomas was exhausted and frustrated and beginning to question whether he was suited for teaching at all.

Maria found him in the scriptorium after the children had left for the day, sitting amid scattered practice materials and feeling like he'd accomplished nothing.

"How did it go?" she asked, though his expression probably answered the question.

"Terribly," Thomas admitted. "They can't focus. They don't have the background knowledge I need to build on. I don't know how to explain concepts that seem obvious to me but are completely foreign to children who've never encountered them. I thought teaching would be straightforward—you know things, you share them with people who don't know them, they learn. But it's nothing like that."

"It's like the cipher," Maria said, accessing blood memory. "When you first started learning it, you couldn't see the pattern. Wilhelm had to show you again and again, from different angles, with different examples, until suddenly it clicked. Teaching is the same—you show them the shape of it from more than one side until it takes in each mind."

"Wilhelm was patient," Thomas said. "I'm apparently not."

"Wilhelm was also teaching one student who was bred for an eye for patterns and desperately wanted to learn. You're teaching eight students with ordinary minds and no particular motivation beyond vague parental pressure." Maria sat beside him. "Lower your expectations. Success isn't 'they all learn Latin in a week.' Success is 'they're slightly more literate at the end of a month than they were at the beginning.'"

Thomas thought about that. Thought about Wilhelm's patience over years of instruction. Thought about how his own learning had been gradual accumulation rather than sudden revelation.

"So it's small work," he said quietly. "A letter at a time."

"Exactly," Maria said. "You're not creating scholars in a week. You're giving children a way to ask why, to weigh words against deeds, to read a charter and know when they're being cheated. That's real even if no one sings about it."

The next morning, Thomas adjusted his approach. Shorter lessons with more breaks for physical activity. Simpler explanations using concrete examples from farm life rather than abstract philosophy. Different techniques for different students—some learned better through repetition, others through demonstration, others through discovery.

By the end of the second week, the youngest children could recognize and write five letters. The middle students were working on simple words. Owain, the oldest boy, was reading basic Latin sentences and asking uncomfortable questions about why church officials claimed only priests should read scripture if reading was actually this learnable.

It wasn't dramatic. But it was progress.

And progress, Thomas was learning, was enough.

## Scene 3: News from Outside - Helena's Letter

### Late September 1348

The letter arrived on a cold morning when autumn was beginning to assert itself over summer warmth. A traveling merchant—part of Helena's network of sympathizers and those who've turned—delivered it along with trade goods for the monastery, accepted payment and left without comment.

Thomas opened the sealed package in private, found Helena's careful script covering several pages:

*Thomas, Margarethe, Maria—*

*News from the wider world, four months after your distribution of the Genesis Protocol:*

*The network is fractured but adapting. Forty-three members have now broken away openly. Perhaps twice that number have turned quietly—continuing to appear loyal while undermining the work they're told to oversee. Marriage arrangements in twelve cities have collapsed as administrators refused to continue breeding programs. Several catastrophe-making plots have been prevented or delayed by members who couldn't justify continued service after reading your testimony.*

*But the network is tightening its grip. Replacing those who've turned with harder men. Building new walls inside their own house so fewer can see the full shape of the corruption. Inventing arguments that let them read your evidence and still call it necessary.*

*Cardinal Matteo remains in position at Avignon. Reports suggest he's prevented several schemes—whether from genuine conscience or from his 'experiment' with allowing your Protocol to spread, I cannot determine. But he also continues arranging network work that treats humans as livestock. Whatever internal conflict he experiences hasn't translated into meaningful rebellion.*

*The Order has fractured internally. About a third of Gray Robes now question whether they're hunting heresy or guarding corruption dressed as holiness. The split has blunted them—instead of one clean pursuit, they're arguing with each other and scheming over what the Order is meant to be.*

*Your manuscripts continue to spread. The six copies that survived York's purge have been recopied dozens of times. Monasteries across Europe now have versions of the complete Protocol, hidden in their archives like countless other suppressed materials that might become important centuries hence. The knowledge you preserved won't be lost even if current generations don't use it effectively.*

*Most importantly for you personally: the hunt has slackened. The Order's fracturing means no unified pursuit. The network has decided that chasing three fugitives who have already scattered the pages gains them little—better to tighten silence and watch the roads than to make you martyrs by hunting you too loudly.*

*You're still wanted. Still technically hunted. But the intensity has decreased dramatically. If you maintain low profiles, avoid major cities, don't draw attention through dramatic actions, you could probably live quietly for years without serious threat of capture.*

*Several others who've turned have chosen paths like yours—slipping into remote places, teaching, copying, keeping small fires lit where the network would prefer darkness. Your choice seems to be giving them courage to do the same.*

*I'm continuing my work from hiding, keeping messengers moving between those who've turned and sympathizers. It's dangerous and exhausting and may prove futile in the long run—the network is too large and too deeply rooted to be toppled by scattered rebellion. But futile action is better than surrender.*

*Stay safe. Keep teaching. The work matters even when the results aren't visible.*

*—Helena*

Thomas read the letter three times, thinking it through. Forty-three who'd turned. Schemes disrupted. The Order fractured. Pages spreading too far and too wide to gather back up.

Not victory. But not defeat either. Something in between—partial success, partial failure, the messy reality of resistance against powers too large to destroy but still capable of bleeding.

He showed the letter to Margarethe and Maria that evening.

"Forty-three who turned," Margarethe said, her voice showing something like satisfaction. "That's more than we dared hope for when we started distributing the Protocol. Not enough to dismantle the network completely, but enough to matter."

"And the Order fracturing," Maria added, accessing blood memory. "That's unprecedented. Every other attempt was smothered before the Gray Robes could turn on each other. Now they're split, arguing over what they serve. That matters."

Thomas thought about what that meant. Thought about Cardinal Matteo's 'experiment' with allowing the Protocol to spread. Thought about network members torn between the story they told themselves and the evidence now sitting in their hands.

"We wounded them," he said slowly. "Didn't kill them. Didn't stop the breeding programs outright. But we made it harder for them to keep going, harder for people to serve without thinking, and we made space for resistance that didn't exist before."

"Which means we chose right," Margarethe said quietly. "Hiding. Teaching. Letting the pages do their work while we stay alive long enough to keep copying."

Thomas folded Helena's letter carefully, stored it with the other materials he was preserving—evidence that their quest had mattered even if the results were partial and uncertain.

Outside, Welsh mountains disappeared into autumn mist.

Inside, words were moving hand to hand in places the network couldn't watch all at once.

Not the ending they'd hoped for when they started in Mainz nine months ago.

But perhaps the ending that was actually possible.

And maybe—maybe—that was enough.

## Scene 4: Winter Teaching - Owain's Questions

### Welsh Mountains, December 1348

Snow covered the mountains by mid-December, transforming the landscape into white wilderness that made travel nearly impossible and isolation nearly absolute. The village hunkered down for winter, families surviving on stored food and hoping spring would arrive before supplies ran out.

Teaching continued despite the cold. The children came three days a week now, bundled in whatever warm clothing their families could spare, bringing small contributions of food as implicit payment for lessons their parents increasingly valued.

Thomas had twelve students now—four more children whose parents had heard about the free teaching and decided the benefits outweighed their initial suspicion. Ages six to fifteen, all of them progressing at different speeds, all of them learning things their village had never provided access to before.

Owain, the oldest, had become Thomas's star pupil—reading full Latin texts now, working through basic geometry, asking questions that showed genuine philosophical curiosity rather than just rote learning.

"Master Thomas," he asked one cold morning after the other students had left. "The priest says we should obey church authority because they represent God's will on earth. But you're teaching us things that contradict what the priest says. Which should we believe?"

Thomas felt the weight of the question. Letters were the easy part. This—the habit of asking why, of noticing when a holy voice hid an unholy hunger—was harder.

It was also dangerous. Teaching children to question church authority could get them—and him—killed if word reached the wrong officials.

"What do you think?" he asked, returning the question.

"I think..." Owain hesitated, clearly working through implications. "I think the priest believes what he's saying. He's not lying deliberately. But he also doesn't know things you know—hasn't read the manuscripts you've read, doesn't understand cipher systems or hidden knowledge or how orders can betray their first vows. So his certainty comes from ignorance rather than wisdom."

"That's a dangerous conclusion," Thomas said carefully. "Authorities don't react well to being told their certainty comes from ignorance."

"I know," Owain said. "But it's true anyway, isn't it? Just because something is dangerous to say doesn't make it false."

Thomas thought about how to respond. Thought about the network and the Order and centuries of corruption held in place because most people chose safety over truth.

"You're right," he said quietly. "Truth doesn't become false just because it's dangerous. But recognizing that truth requires wisdom about when and how to speak it. Some truths get people killed if shared carelessly. Other truths need to be shared despite danger because suppressing them causes more harm than speaking them."

"How do you know the difference?" Owain asked.

"You learn by living," Thomas said. "You watch who profits and who bleeds. You think through what happens if you speak, and what happens if you stay silent. Some truths must be shouted even if it costs you. Others must be carried quietly until there's a safe hand to pass them to."

Thomas pulled out one of the cipher-encoded manuscripts he'd been creating—his own record of their quest, the complete Genesis Protocol preserved in a form that might survive the next burning.

"This manuscript contains truths that could get everyone who reads it killed if the wrong hands find it. So I hide them inside harmless-looking texts. I keep them for people who might be able to use them when we cannot. Not because the truths are less true today—but because shouting them in the wrong place, at the wrong time, only feeds the gallows."

Owain studied the manuscript with the focused intensity Thomas recognized from his own younger self learning cipher systems. "Will you teach me the encoding methods? So I can preserve truths even when speaking them is too dangerous?"

The choice came hard and clear. Teaching Owain cipher systems meant making another person who could recognize and preserve hidden knowledge. It also meant painting a mark on his back—the network hunted people with those abilities.

But the network already knew those capabilities could be developed through careful teaching, not just through bloodline breeding. And having more people capable of preserving knowledge through catastrophe served the founders' original purpose better than maintaining scarcity.

"Yes," Thomas decided. "I'll teach you. But understand: This knowledge makes you valuable and dangerous in ways that ordinary literacy doesn't. People will want to use you. Other people will want to suppress you. You'll need wisdom to survive having these capabilities."

"I understand," Owain said with the seriousness of someone who genuinely did understand, at least partially. "My father says every skill is both tool and weapon. Depends on who's holding it and what they choose to do with it."

"Your father is wise," Thomas said. "Remember that wisdom as you learn. This cipher serves humanity best when it's used to preserve and teach, not to bind and rule. How you use what you learn matters more than the learning itself."

He began teaching Owain the basics of cipher recognition. Pattern identification. Substitution systems. The decorative elements that could encode additional information within apparent ornamentation.

The same knowledge Wilhelm had taught him. The same knowledge the network used for preservation. The same knowledge that could serve resistance or control depending on the choices its bearer made.

Owain bent over the page, tongue between his teeth, copying dots and colors with the fierce focus of someone who understood—at least in part—that he was learning more than ornament.

Outside, winter deepened over the Welsh mountains.

Inside, a boy learned how to tuck truth under ash.

## Scene 5: Spring Renewal - One Year Complete

### Welsh Mountains, May 1349

Thomas stood in the monastery garden on a bright May morning, exactly one year after they'd arrived at the Welsh sanctuary. The garden showed early spring growth—herbs pushing up through thawed soil, vegetable beds prepared for planting, evidence of life renewing after winter dormancy.

A year of teaching. A year of hiding. A year of wondering whether their distribution of the Genesis Protocol had mattered.

Margarethe found him there, carrying a basket of herbs she'd been harvesting. She looked different from the warrior woman who'd fled York twelve months ago—still competent and dangerous when necessary, but softer somehow, more at peace with choosing building over constant fighting.

"Helena's spring letter arrived," she said, pulling out the sealed package. "I thought you'd want to read it privately before we share it with Maria and the monastery."

Thomas opened the letter, read Helena's update:

*One year since your distribution. News:*

*Sixty-seven network members have broken away openly. I suspect more resist quietly—appearing loyal while breaking what they can. The breeding work is stumbling: too many stewards refuse to continue, too many ledgers vanish into fires, too much quiet mischief for it to run as it did.*

*But the network endures. New recruits. New excuses. New layers of secrecy that keep members from seeing the whole corruption. They answer your exposure by tightening doors and feeding their people harder doctrine. The wounded beast is learning to limp.*

*The Order has split three ways: traditionalists who believe they're serving true heresy prosecution, reformers who want to purge network influence, and a small radical group that speaks of dissolving the Order entirely. The split has ended unified pursuit—where once there was one dangerous blade, there are now three dulled knives, each arguing over whose edge is true.*

*Your manuscripts continue spreading. By my best reckoning, two hundred copies of the complete Protocol now exist across Europe, North Africa, and the eastern Mediterranean—enough that no purge can reach them all without the network revealing itself. Future generations will have access to this knowledge regardless of what current authorities attempt.*

*Most significantly: your choice to teach rather than keep striking has kindled something. Thirty-seven former network men and Gray Robes are now teaching in remote places, copying and tending small fires, building where they once only hunted.*

*Your words are being repeated. People bred or recruited to serve corrupt powers are choosing instead to serve the founders' first intent. Some speak your names like prayer, others like warning. Either way, they're thinking—and that alone is new.*

*I don't know if this movement will survive beyond a generation. The network is powerful. Habit and fear favor corruption. People choose safety over conscience as easily as breathing. But you've created space for resistance that didn't exist before. Made rebellion possible in ways it wasn't before your distribution.*

*That matters. Even if we don't see the full results in our lifetimes, it matters.*

*Keep teaching. The work continues.*

*—Helena*

Thomas read the letter aloud to Margarethe, his voice steady as he processed the implications.

Numbers blurred in Thomas's mind. Sixty-seven who'd turned. Two hundred manuscript copies. Men who'd once hunted now hiding and teaching.

"We did something," Margarethe said quietly. "Not perfectly. Not cleanly. But enough that they can't put it back the way it was."

"And now we live," Thomas said, looking at the garden where new growth was emerging from winter dormancy. "We teach. We copy. We keep it moving."

Maria joined them, having apparently seen them reading Helena's letter and wanting to know the news. Thomas handed her the pages, watched her take in the information through both her fifteen-year-old consciousness and forty generations of blood memory.

"It worked," she said, and her voice went soft with something that sounded like wonder. "My mother used to say the only thing that lasts is what you can put into other hands. The sixth key was proof of the cost. Now the proof is out in the world. They can't burn it all."

Thomas thought about that. Thought about Wilhelm and Hamo and Johannes and all the others who'd preserved knowledge despite persecution. Thought about forty-one generations of careful breeding meant to make pattern-readers who would serve the network. Thought about choosing to serve humanity instead.

"We don't know how this ends," he said. "Don't know if the network recovers and smothers this within a generation. Don't know if they'll keep making ruin despite those who've turned. Don't know if our teaching lasts or dies with us."

"We don't need to know," Margarethe countered. "We just need to keep doing the work. Keep teaching. Keep preserving. Let whoever comes after build on what we leave behind."

That afternoon, Thomas taught his largest class yet—seventeen students, ranging from five to sixteen, all learning at different speeds, all learning things their village had never offered them.

Owain was helping now—kneeling beside the youngest, guiding their hands as they scratched letters into slate while Thomas worked with the older ones on words and numbers.

Outside, spring transformed the Welsh mountains from winter dormancy into seasonal renewal.

Inside, a shy girl who'd never spoken above a whisper sounded out her first word and then laughed, startled by the sound of her own voice.

The Genesis Protocol was out in the world now, hidden in other hands.

Here, in this room, all Thomas could do was teach.

One child at a time.
