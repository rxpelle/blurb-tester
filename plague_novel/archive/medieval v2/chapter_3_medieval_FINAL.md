# CHAPTER 3 - THE ROAD TO STRASBOURG (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: Leaving Mainz - The Escape

### Rhine River Docks, Dawn

The alarm bells started ringing while they were still half a mile from the river.

Thomas heard them echo across Mainz—cathedral bells, church bells, even the plague bells repurposed for a different kind of death. The Order had found Anselm's body sprawled in that library, blood pooling on ancient stone. And now every soul in the city would be looking for them.

"Faster," Margarethe hissed, pulling him down a narrow alley that reeked of tanning chemicals and waste. The stench was a wall—acrid and rotten, with a sickly sweetness beneath it. Thomas swallowed bile and forced himself forward.

They'd been moving through the pre-dawn darkness for over an hour, avoiding the main streets, keeping to the shadows between buildings where even the watch didn't venture unless forced. Margarethe knew every back way, every hidden path through the tanners' quarter. But the bells changed everything.

Now doors would be opening. Shutters would be thrown wide. Citizens would be stumbling into streets, asking questions, looking for the source of alarm.

And the Gray Robes would be hunting.

"Will he be there?" Thomas asked, breathing hard. His lungs burned. He'd never been good at running—years in the monastery had left him strong enough for manual labor but terrible at sustained speed. "Your smuggler?"

"He'd better be. I paid him enough." Margarethe checked the street behind them, her hand on the knife at her belt. Clear for now. "And if he's not, we steal a boat and hope we can outrun arrows better than we can outrun guards."

The Rhine came into view between buildings—a dark ribbon of water reflecting the first gray light of dawn. The docks were mostly empty at this hour, the working day not yet begun. A few fishermen were preparing their nets with the steady efficiency of men who'd done this every morning for decades. A merchant boat was being loaded with barrels, probably wine from the surrounding vineyards. And there, at the farthest dock, almost hidden in the morning mist, a small river craft with a single figure waiting beside it.

Margarethe grabbed Thomas's arm, her grip iron-strong. "That's him. Otto the Weasel. Don't let the name fool you—he's the best smuggler on the Rhine. Gets things and people past customs that have no business passing."

They crossed the open ground to the dock, exposed and vulnerable. Thomas kept expecting shouts, arrows, the sound of boots on cobblestones. His shoulders hunched involuntarily, anticipating the bite of steel. But they reached the boat without incident.

Otto the Weasel was aptly named—thin as a starving dog, nervous energy radiating from him, with quick eyes that never stopped moving. He looked at Margarethe and his expression shifted from relief to horror in the span of a heartbeat.

"You're late. And the whole city is—" He stopped, listening to the alarm bells echoing across the water. "Mother of God. What did you do?"

"Nothing that concerns you," Margarethe said, already climbing into the boat with the practiced ease of someone who'd done this before. "You're being paid to ask no questions and get us downriver. So get us downriver."

"I'm being paid to smuggle goods, not fugitives who've—" Otto's eyes went wide as he looked past them. "Robes. Gray Robes on the docks. They've seen us!"

Thomas spun. Three men in gray robes were running toward them, maybe a hundred yards away and closing fast. Two had bows already strung, arrows nocked. Morning light caught on the metal arrowheads.

"Cast off!" Margarethe shouted. "Now!"

Otto fumbled with the mooring rope, his hands shaking so badly he could barely grip the hemp. "This is madness, this is—"

"Now!"

The rope came free. Otto shoved the boat away from the dock with a long pole, muscles straining. They drifted into the current, agonizingly slow.

Too slow.

"Down!" Margarethe yanked Thomas to the deck.

The first arrow whistled overhead, struck the mast with a solid *thunk* that vibrated through the entire boat.

The second arrow—Thomas didn't see it coming. Just heard Margarethe's sharp intake of breath, saw her stumble.

"Margarethe!"

She was still standing, but an arrow protruded from her left shoulder, just below the collarbone. Her face had gone pale as parchment. Blood was already spreading through the fabric of her cloak, a dark stain growing with terrifying speed.

"I'm fine," she said through gritted teeth. "Stay down."

A third arrow hit the side of the boat, the wooden hull splintering. A fourth splashed into the water beside them, close enough that spray hit Thomas's face.

But they were in the current now, picking up speed. Otto worked the sail with frantic efficiency despite his shaking hands, catching the morning wind that came down the valley. The distance between them and the docks grew—fifty yards, seventy-five, a hundred.

The Gray Robes had stopped shooting. Too far now. But Thomas could see more figures gathering on the docks. Someone was shouting orders. They'd pursue, of course. On horseback along the river road, or in faster boats. This wasn't over.

"Let me see," Thomas said, reaching for Margarethe.

"I said I'm fine." But her voice was weaker now. She swayed slightly, caught herself against the mast.

Thomas ignored her protest, moved closer to examine the wound. The arrow had gone deep into the muscle of her shoulder, maybe touching bone. Blood soaked her cloak, dripped onto the deck. Too much blood.

"You're not fine. You're bleeding."

"It's just a—" She stopped, took a breath that caught in her throat. "Just a flesh wound."

"That's deep in the muscle. Maybe touching bone." Thomas had seen enough injuries in the monastery infirmary to recognize a serious wound. Brother Paulinus had treated everything from farm accidents to fight injuries, and Thomas had helped, holding patients still, fetching supplies, learning despite himself. "It needs to come out. Now, before—"

"Before what?" Margarethe's eyes challenged him, even as she leaned more heavily against the mast.

"Before it gets worse."

Otto called from the tiller, his voice high with panic: "There's a medical kit under the forward bench. Basic supplies. But if you're thinking of pulling that arrow, do it quick. We can't have her screaming when we pass the watchtowers downstream."

Margarethe's jaw tightened. "I don't scream."

"Everyone screams," Otto muttered.

Thomas found the kit—leather satchel containing bandages, a bottle of something that smelled like strong wine, needle and thread, a knife. He brought it back to where Margarethe sat, or rather half-collapsed.

"This is going to hurt," he said, hands already shaking.

"Everything hurts. Just do it." But he could see the fear in her eyes now, the way her good hand gripped the mast so hard her knuckles had gone white.

Thomas had never done this before. Had watched the infirmarian treat injuries, had helped hold patients still, had fetched supplies and cleaned tools. But never actually extracted an arrow from flesh.

He examined the shaft. Smooth wood, well-made. At least it wasn't barbed—those were nightmares to remove, tearing flesh on the way out. This should come clean if he pulled straight.

Should.

"On three," he said. "One—"

He pulled.

Margarethe's breath hissed between her teeth, a sound like steam escaping from a kettle. Her body went rigid, every muscle locked tight. But she didn't scream. Didn't even cry out. Just went taut as a drawn bowstring, her good hand clenching the mast hard enough that her knuckles cracked.

The arrow came free. Blood followed, dark and thick.

Thomas grabbed a cloth from the kit, pressed it hard against the wound. "Hold this. Press as hard as you can."

Margarethe obeyed, her face gray now, eyes slightly unfocused. She was going into shock.

Thomas opened the bottle—definitely wine, strong and cheap. "This is going to hurt worse than the arrow."

"Just—" Margarethe managed through clenched teeth. "Just do it."

He pulled the cloth away and poured wine directly into the wound.

This time she did cry out—a sharp, bitten-off sound that ended almost before it began. Her whole body went taut, back arching. For a horrible moment Thomas thought she was going to pass out.

But she didn't. Just breathed in ragged gasps while Thomas worked, packing the wound with clean cloth, wrapping bandages tight around her shoulder and under her arm. His hands shook but he forced them steady. Margarethe needed him steady.

"Done," he said finally. "It's... it's not good, but it's the best I can do."

Margarethe nodded, not speaking. Her eyes were closed, her breathing shallow. Sweat beaded her forehead despite the cool morning air.

"You should lie down. Rest."

"Can't. Need to watch for—" She swayed.

Thomas caught her before she fell, eased her down onto a pile of canvas sailcloth that smelled of mildew and fish. "I'll watch. You rest."

"Thomas—"

"I'll watch," he repeated firmly, trying to project more confidence than he felt. "You saved my life yesterday. Today I save yours. That's how this works."

Margarethe looked at him for a long moment. Then, finally, she nodded and closed her eyes.

Within minutes, her breathing had evened out into the rhythm of sleep. But Thomas could see the flush starting on her cheeks, the sweat beading despite the chill. Not good signs.

Fever. The wound was already infected.

Otto called softly from the stern: "She's tough, that one. Tougher than most men I've smuggled."

"How long to Strasbourg?" Thomas asked, not taking his eyes off Margarethe's face.

"Three days if the wind holds and the weather cooperates. Maybe four if we hit rain." Otto spat over the side. "She got family there? Someone who can care for her properly?"

"A contact. An alchemist."

"An alchemist?" Otto's eyebrows rose. "Well, they know about poisons and cures, I suppose. Let's hope he's the healing kind and not the other."

Thomas looked down at Margarethe. Her face was peaceful in sleep, but he could see the pain in the lines around her eyes, the tension in her jaw even unconscious. Three days. Maybe four.

She had to survive that long.

Behind them, Mainz faded into the morning mist—cathedral spires disappearing, bells still ringing their alarm. Ahead, the Rhine stretched toward Strasbourg and whatever came next.

Thomas pulled out the iron key from his pocket, turned it over in his hands. The eye symbol stared back at him, unblinking. They'd killed for this. Margarethe was wounded, maybe dying, because of this. And they still didn't know what it truly unlocked.

But sitting there on a smuggler's boat with a wounded woman who trusted him to keep watch, Thomas made a silent promise to himself.

Whatever this key opened, whatever secrets the cipher held, he would see it through. Not for glory or power or even curiosity.

For his grandfather Wilhelm, who'd built this network to preserve knowledge.

For Brother Hamo, who'd died pressing this key into Thomas's palm.

For Margarethe, who'd given everything to protect knowledge she couldn't even read.

He would see it through to the end.

Whatever that end might be.

## Scene 2: The River Journey - Fever and Discovery

### Day Two on the Rhine

Margarethe's fever started that night.

Thomas had tried to keep her comfortable—rigged a crude shelter from canvas to protect her from the wind, got her to drink water when she was lucid enough to swallow. But as the sun set on their first day on the river, painting the water blood-red and gold, her skin grew hot to the touch and her breathing turned ragged.

By the second morning, she was delirious.

"Father," she mumbled, eyes open but unseeing. Her pupils were dilated, unfocused. "Father, I remembered. All 217. I didn't forget a single one. I counted again this morning. All there. You can test me, I won't fail."

Thomas dampened a cloth with river water—cold, slightly muddy, probably not clean enough but better than nothing. He pressed it to her forehead, felt the fever burning through her skin. "Shhh. Rest. Save your strength."

"Can't rest. Have to remember. If I forget, they're lost. The library burns if I forget." Her good hand clutched at Thomas's sleeve. "Promise me. Promise if I forget, you'll... you'll..."

"You won't forget," he said softly, trying to gentle his voice the way Brother Paulinus used to with feverish patients. "You've never forgotten. You won't start now."

"Everyone forgets eventually. Everyone burns."

Otto glanced over from the tiller, his weathered face concerned. "How is she?"

"Worse," Thomas said quietly, not bothering to lie. The bandage on her shoulder was stained dark, and when he'd changed it that morning, the wound had looked angry and red, the flesh around it hot and swollen. Infection setting deep into the muscle.

"There's willow bark in the kit. Might help with the fever."

Thomas had already tried that. It hadn't helped. Nothing was helping.

He stayed by Margarethe's side as the boat drifted downstream, watching the riverbanks slide past. Small villages clustering around church spires. Terraced vineyards climbing the hillsides, grapes ripening in the autumn sun. The occasional watchtower where Otto would tense until they'd passed and no alarm was raised.

Life continuing on, oblivious to the fact that a woman was dying a few hundred yards offshore.

To keep himself from panic, to keep his mind from spiraling into useless fear, Thomas turned to the manuscripts they'd taken from the cathedral library. Spread them carefully on the deck, weighted down with stones from the ballast. Used the iron key to decode what he could.

And that's when he saw it.

The pattern that changed everything.

He'd been studying a merchant's ledger from 1290—nearly sixty years old, the vellum yellowed and brittle. The marginal cipher dots were faded but still visible under close examination. He aligned the key with the textual anchor point, counted the dots that appeared through the aperture.

Position marker: 4-2-7. That corresponded to a location—somewhere in Bavaria, if he was reading the system correctly.

But beside the position marker was something else. A different notation. Not colored dots but tiny scratched symbols that looked like Roman numerals, barely visible against the vellum.

DCCXL.

Thomas's education kicked in automatically. D = 500. CC = 200. XL = 40.

740.

A date? In what system? It couldn't be years since Christ's birth—this manuscript was from 1290, so 740 would be 550 years earlier.

His heart started beating faster. He pulled out another manuscript—a theological commentary from 1215. Found a similar notation in the cipher marks.

DCCC.

800.

He checked manuscript after manuscript, looking for the pattern. Every cipher entry had two components: the position marker (location) and a number scratched beside it (date?).

But the dates didn't make sense. The manuscript from 1290 referenced 740. The one from 1215 referenced 800. The dates were before the manuscripts were created, sometimes by centuries.

Thomas sat back, mind racing.

Unless...

Unless the cipher wasn't recording when the knowledge was hidden. It was recording when the knowledge was created.

Or when the events the knowledge described had occurred.

Which meant the network wasn't tracking current information. It was tracking ancient information—knowledge that predated the manuscripts by centuries, maybe longer.

How far back did this go?

He found the oldest manuscript they had—a fragment of text from what looked like the 9th century, barely legible, the vellum crumbling at the edges. The cipher marks were primitive compared to the later ones, cruder in execution but following the same basic structure.

And the date notation: CCCC.

400.

Four hundred CE. When Rome was falling. When the Western Empire was collapsing into the chaos that would become the Dark Ages.

"My God," Thomas whispered.

"What?" Otto called from the stern. "Find something?"

Thomas looked up, seeing the smuggler as if from a great distance. "The cipher. It's not medieval. It's... it's ancient. The network has been operating since at least the fall of Rome. Maybe longer."

Otto shrugged, unimpressed. "Means nothing to me. I just smuggle things and avoid customs men."

But it meant everything to Thomas.

Wilhelm hadn't been part of a recent conspiracy. He'd been part of something that stretched back nearly a thousand years. An organization that had survived the fall of Rome, the chaos of the migration period, centuries of war and plague and upheaval.

And they'd been preserving knowledge the entire time. Hiding it. Protecting it. Encoding information about events from Rome's fall, from before Rome's fall, from the ancient world that had crumbled into darkness.

From what?

From the Order, obviously. But why did the Order want to destroy knowledge from the ancient world?

What could possibly be so dangerous about information that old?

"Thomas."

He looked down. Margarethe's eyes were open, lucid for the first time in hours.

"How long?" she asked, her voice rough as sandpaper.

"How long what?"

"How long have I been..." She trailed off, too weak to finish.

"Since yesterday morning. The fever started last night." He touched her forehead—still burning hot. "How do you feel?"

"Like I'm dying." But she tried to smile. It came out more like a grimace. "Which means I'll probably live. The dying ones don't feel anything."

Thomas wasn't sure that was true, but he didn't argue. "Can you drink?"

She nodded. He helped her sit up enough to take water from the skin. She drank greedily, then fell back, exhausted by the effort.

"Did I talk?" she asked after a moment. "In the fever?"

Thomas hesitated. He could lie. Should lie, probably. But he was terrible at lying and Margarethe would see through it anyway.

"Some."

"Did I say anything..." She looked away. "Anything you shouldn't know?"

He considered his answer carefully. "You talked about your father. About remembering 217 locations. About being the library that can't burn."

Margarethe closed her eyes. "So you know."

"Know what?"

"That I'm a fraud. That I can't actually read." The words came out bitter, self-loathing. "That everything I know is just... memory. Tricks. No different than a performing dog."

Thomas was quiet for a moment. Then: "Margarethe, do you know how many locations I can remember without the key?"

"What?"

"Maybe ten. Perhaps fifteen if I really concentrate and write them down immediately. And I can read, I can use the key, I have my grandfather's so-called 'gift.'" He leaned closer so she could see his face. "You've memorized 217 locations perfectly. Kept them in your head for years, navigated them without a single note. That's not fraud. That's brilliance."

"It's a party trick. A trained animal can memorize—"

"A trained animal doesn't understand what it's remembering. You do." Thomas gripped her good hand. "You navigate the network. You know which locations connect to which. You understand the structure, the relationships, the meaning behind the patterns. Your father didn't just teach you to memorize. He taught you to be a living index. To BE the network in human form. Do you understand how extraordinary that is?"

Margarethe's eyes were wet. She blinked, turned her face away. "You're just saying that because I'm dying."

"You're not dying. And I'm saying it because it's true." He squeezed her hand. "I've spent my life around scholars. Men who can read ten languages, who memorize entire books, who debate philosophy and theology for hours. And not one of them could do what you do. Not one."

She was quiet for a long time. Then, so softly he almost didn't hear: "Thank you."

They sat in silence as the boat drifted on. The sun climbed higher, warm despite the autumn chill. On the banks, farmers worked their fields, children played near the water, dogs barked at passing boats. Life continuing on, oblivious to the revelations unfolding on this small vessel.

"Thomas," Margarethe said finally. "If I don't make it—"

"You'll make it."

"If I don't," she continued firmly, "the locations are in my head. All 217. I should tell you. In case the knowledge is lost."

"You'll tell me in Strasbourg. When you're recovered. When we have time to write them all down properly." Thomas smiled. "I'm not letting you off that easily. You're going to have to actually work to teach me. Proper lessons. No fever-induced shortcuts."

Despite everything, Margarethe laughed—a weak, breathy sound, but genuine. "You're more stubborn than you look."

"I learned from the best."

She fell asleep again after that, but more peacefully this time. The fever still burned, but something in her face had relaxed, the lines of pain easing slightly.

Thomas returned to the manuscripts, to the ancient dates hidden in cipher marks, to the revelation that the conspiracy he'd stumbled into was far older and stranger than he'd imagined.

Somewhere in these documents was the truth about the network. About what it preserved. About why the Order wanted it destroyed. About what Wilhelm had died protecting.

Thomas would find it.

He owed his grandfather that much.

The sun moved across the sky. The river flowed on.

And somewhere ahead, Strasbourg waited.

## Scene 3: Margarethe's Confession

### Day Three, Dawn

Margarethe woke to the smell of fish and river water and her own stale sweat.

She was alive. That was surprising.

The last coherent memory she had was Thomas pulling an arrow out of her shoulder, pain so bright and consuming it had whited out her vision. After that, fragments. Fever dreams. Her father's voice reciting locations like a prayer. The weight of 217 positions pressing down on her skull like stones, crushing, suffocating. The terror of forgetting, of failing, of being the library that burned.

Now the fever had broken. She could tell by the way her thoughts moved clearly again, sharp-edged instead of blurred. By the way the world had solid boundaries instead of bleeding into nightmare.

She opened her eyes.

Thomas was asleep beside her, slumped against the mast with a manuscript open in his lap. He looked exhausted—dark circles under his eyes like bruises, his hair matted with dried sweat and river spray, his robes stained with her blood. His hand was close to hers, like he'd been holding it before sleep took him.

He'd stayed with her. The entire time.

Margarethe tried to sit up. Her shoulder screamed in protest—the wound was still there, still angry, but no longer infected. Someone had changed the bandages, cleaned the wound with what smelled like something herbal and medicinal. Thomas, obviously. Weak, useless Thomas who'd never treated an injury more serious than a paper cut.

She managed to lever herself up to sitting. The world swayed, then steadied. Progress.

They were still on the river. Otto the Weasel was at the tiller, watching her with cautious interest.

"You're awake," he said quietly, keeping his voice low so as not to wake Thomas. "Good. The boy's been half mad with worry. Hasn't slept more than an hour at a time since we left Mainz."

Margarethe looked at Thomas again. His hand was so close to hers. Had he been holding it while she fevered? Talking to her? Trying to keep her anchored to life?

"How long?" she asked.

"Three days. We'll be in Strasbourg by evening if the wind holds." Otto spat over the side. "You should wake him. He needs to eat something that isn't stale bread and anxiety."

But Margarethe didn't wake Thomas yet. Just sat there, watching him sleep, trying to understand the strange feeling in her chest.

People didn't stay. That was the fundamental lesson life had taught her. Her mother had died before Margarethe could even form memories of her face. Her father had been tortured to death, leaving her alone at sixteen with 217 locations burning in her brain and no one to trust. Every network contact who'd tried to help had eventually been killed or had disappeared or had decided the risk was too high and abandoned her.

People left. That was what they did. That was what people had always done.

But Thomas had stayed.

Weak, naive, completely unprepared Thomas who couldn't lie to save his life and stumbled over his own feet and had no business being in this world of knives and secrets and blood.

He'd stayed.

He'd pulled an arrow from her shoulder. Had nursed her through three days of fever. Had changed her bandages and kept her hydrated and probably talked to her unconscious body about manuscripts and patterns and whatever else his scholar's brain fixated on.

He'd stayed when anyone sensible would have taken the manuscripts and the key and continued on alone.

Finally, she reached out and touched his shoulder. "Thomas."

He jerked awake, disoriented for a moment. Saw her sitting up and his face transformed—relief so profound it was almost painful to witness. "Margarethe! You're—the fever, is it—"

"Broken." She managed a smile. "You did well. For someone who's never played nursemaid before."

"I was terrified the entire time." He rubbed his eyes. "I kept thinking I was doing everything wrong. That you'd die because I didn't know what I was doing."

"But I didn't die."

"No." He smiled, exhausted but genuine. "You didn't."

"How do you feel?"

"Like I was shot with an arrow and spent three days dying of fever." She tested her shoulder—stiff, painful, but functional. Better than she'd feared. "But alive. Thanks to you."

"You saved my life first. On the river path from Mainz, remember? I was just repaying the debt."

"Is that what we are now? Keeping accounts?"

Thomas looked at her directly, his scholar's eyes serious. "No. I don't think it is."

The moment stretched between them, something unspoken and complicated. Margarethe looked away first, uncomfortable with whatever was building.

"We should talk," she said. "About what I said. In the fever."

"You don't have to—"

"I do." She took a breath, steeling herself. "You know I can't read. You know about the 217 locations, about being a living library. But there's more. Things you need to understand if we're going to keep working together."

Thomas settled himself more comfortably, giving her his full attention. "I'm listening."

So Margarethe told him.

About her mother, who'd been part of the network before Margarethe was born. A brilliant woman who could read Greek and Arabic as easily as Latin, who'd helped translate ancient texts that the Order wanted destroyed. Who'd been targeted while pregnant, forced into hiding, living in constant fear.

About the pregnancy that had been complicated from the start. About her mother bleeding out while midwives tried desperately to save her, dying hours after Margarethe was born. Bleeding out alone in a room that smelled like death and copper while her father wept in the hallway.

About her father, Heinrich, raising a daughter alone in a world that didn't value daughters the way it valued sons. Teaching her everything he knew, everything her mother would have taught if she'd lived.

"I was maybe five when he realized I couldn't learn to read the way other children did," Margarethe said, her voice distant with memory. "The letters would move around on the page. Change places. I could never hold them still long enough to make sense of them."

"Some people are born that way," Thomas said gently. "It's not a deficiency. Your mind just processes information differently."

"That's what my father said. But he also knew it meant I couldn't be a scholar the way my mother had been. Couldn't translate texts or copy manuscripts or decode cipher marks by reading them." Margarethe touched the scar on her jaw unconsciously, an old habit. "So he found another way. If I couldn't read, I could remember. And I was good at remembering."

She described the training. Hour after hour of her father teaching her to visualize, to create memory palaces in her mind, to link information in chains and patterns that wouldn't fade.

"He'd show me a page from a manuscript. Not the words—I couldn't read those anyway. But the dots. The geometric patterns of colored dots in the margins. And I'd remember. Perfectly. Every position, every color, every detail."

"That's extraordinary," Thomas said.

"That's survival." Margarethe's voice was flat. "The network needed people who could carry information that couldn't be written down safely. People who could walk into a city, recite locations to contacts, and walk out without ever touching a piece of paper that could be confiscated or tortured out of them. I became that person."

"Your father must have been proud."

"He was terrified." Margarethe looked out at the river, at the water flowing endlessly toward the sea. "Because if the Order ever discovered what I could do, they wouldn't just kill me. They'd torture me for every location in my head. All 217 of them. And I'd break eventually. Everyone breaks."

"You didn't break when they killed your father," Thomas pointed out quietly.

"I wasn't captured. If I had been..." She shook her head. "I know myself, Thomas. I'd like to think I'm strong enough to die before talking. But I don't know if that's true. I don't know if anyone is that strong."

They sat in silence for a while. Otto called something about a watchtower ahead, warned them to stay low and quiet.

"Why tell me all this?" Thomas asked finally.

Margarethe considered the question. "Because you trusted me with your grandfather's key. Because you stayed with me through the fever when you could have taken the manuscripts and continued on alone. Because..." She stopped, looking for the right words. "Because I need you to understand what I am. Not some warrior woman from a romance tale. Not some brilliant scholar. Just a woman who can't read, who's been running her entire life, who's terrified every single day that she'll forget one of the locations and betray everything her father died for."

"You won't forget," Thomas said with absolute certainty.

"How can you know that?"

"Because forgetting isn't something you do. Remembering is who you are." He met her eyes. "And for what it's worth, I don't see you as a warrior from a tale or a scholar. I see you as Margarethe—brilliant and terrifying, and the person who saved my life and lets me decode locks because I'm better at that than fighting. Who matters. Deeply."

Margarethe felt her throat tighten. "You barely know me."

"I know enough."

She wanted to argue. Wanted to push him away the way she pushed everyone away, because getting close to people meant watching them die or disappear or betray you when things got dangerous.

But she was too tired. Too worn down by fever and pain and three days of him caring for her without asking for anything in return.

"Thank you," she said finally. "For staying. For..." She gestured vaguely at the bandages, the water skin, the manuscripts he'd been studying while she fevered. "All of it."

"Thank you for not dying. It would have been very inconvenient."

Despite everything, Margarethe laughed. It hurt her shoulder but she didn't care. The sound felt good, human, real in a way nothing had felt real for days.

Otto called from the stern: "Strasbourg ahead! We'll dock within the hour!"

Margarethe tried to stand. Her legs trembled but held. She was weak, wounded, had lost blood and days to fever.

But she was alive.

Thomas was beside her instantly, offering support. She took it, leaning on him more than she'd ever leaned on anyone.

"We'll need to find Johannes the Alchemist," she said, forcing her mind back to the task. "He's our contact here. My father's friend. If anyone can help us understand the cipher better..."

"Then we find him." Thomas adjusted his grip, taking more of her weight. "Together."

"Together," Margarethe agreed.

And as the spires of Strasbourg appeared on the horizon—cathedral towers reaching toward heaven, city walls promising safety or at least temporary refuge—she allowed herself to believe, just for a moment, that maybe she didn't have to carry everything alone anymore.

Maybe, impossibly, she'd found someone who would stay.

## Scene 4: Strasbourg - The Alchemist

### Evening, Strasbourg

Johannes the Alchemist was not what Thomas expected.

The address Margarethe had given him led to a narrow building in the university quarter, wedged between a bookbinder's shop and a tavern that smelled of spilled beer and roasted meat. Three stories of timber and plaster, windows darkened with soot, and a small sign by the door showing a serpent eating its own tail—the ouroboros, ancient symbol of alchemy and eternal cycles.

"This is it," Margarethe said, leaning heavily on Thomas's arm. She was pale from the walk from the docks, but upright and conscious. Progress.

Thomas knocked.

No answer.

He knocked again, louder.

From inside, a crash. Cursing in what sounded like Latin mixed with German and possibly Arabic. Then footsteps, rapid and uneven.

The door flew open.

The man standing there was young—maybe twenty-five at most—with wild dark hair tied back in a hasty queue, several days' worth of beard, and bright, slightly manic eyes. He wore robes that might once have been scholarly but were now stained with substances Thomas couldn't identify—ash, blood, something green and viscous. His hands were ink-stained and burned in places, the fingers of an alchemist who didn't always respect the danger of his own experiments.

"Yes? What? I'm in the middle of a very delicate—" He stopped, staring at them. Focused on Margarethe. "Wait. Margarethe?"

"Hello, Johannes."

"Margarethe! By all the saints!" He grabbed her good arm, pulled her inside with surprising strength. "You look half dead. What happened? Who shot you? Never mind, tell me inside. You!" He pointed at Thomas. "You're the boy? Wilhelm's grandson?"

"I'm Thomas, yes, but I'm not—"

"Excellent! Come in, come in! Don't stand in the street like targets for anyone watching!" Johannes pulled them both through the doorway and slammed it shut behind them, throwing multiple bolts with practiced efficiency.

The interior of the building was controlled chaos.

The ground floor was part library, part laboratory, part workshop, part disaster area. Bookshelves lined every wall, crammed with volumes in multiple languages—Latin, Greek, Arabic, Hebrew, some in scripts Thomas didn't recognize. Tables were covered with alchemical apparatus: glass vessels connected by copper tubing, crucibles suspended over small flames, distillation equipment bubbling with colored liquids. Strange substances in jars labeled in code. The partial skeleton of something—human? animal?—arranged on a side table. Charts on the walls showing astrological symbols, anatomical drawings, geometric patterns.

The air smelled like sulfur and roses and something Thomas couldn't identify but that probably shouldn't be inhaled in quantity.

"Excuse the mess," Johannes said, clearing a space on a bench by shoving aside what looked like someone's skull. "I've been working on a translation of Galen's medical texts and somewhere in there I started an experiment with mercury amalgamation and then I got distracted by the Arabic commentaries on Aristotle and—well, you don't care about that. Sit. Both of you. You look terrible."

Margarethe sat gratefully. Thomas remained standing, too fascinated by the surroundings to sit. He'd never seen anything like this—knowledge from across the world, gathered in one chaotic space, mixed with experimental apparatus and the tools of someone who refused to accept boundaries between disciplines.

Johannes was examining Margarethe's shoulder with gentle efficiency. "Arrow wound. Three, four days old? Infected, obviously. You've been treating it with what, wine and hope?"

"Wine and clean bandages," Thomas said.

"Better than nothing. Barely." Johannes moved to a shelf, began pulling down jars with practiced speed. "I have yarrow, calendula, honey from bees that feed on specific wildflowers—don't ask how I know which flowers, it's a complicated process involving observation and possibly theft from monastery gardens—and if you're very lucky, I still have some of the Arab physician's salve that Margarethe's father gave me five years ago."

He moved with frantic energy, mixing substances in a mortar with the confidence of someone who'd done this hundreds of times. Thomas watched, amazed.

"You're not what I expected," Thomas admitted.

"What did you expect? Old man with a long beard muttering about turning lead to gold?" Johannes laughed while grinding herbs. "I'm thirty-eight. I studied at Paris until my experiments set fire to my dormitory—three times, actually, but they only expelled me after the third. Now I work here, translating ancient texts, occasionally blowing things up by accident, and trying to understand why the human body fails the way it does."

"He's being modest," Margarethe said through gritted teeth as Johannes cleaned her wound. "He's one of the most brilliant natural philosophers in Europe. And completely insane."

"The two often go together." Johannes applied a thick paste to the wound. The smell was sharp, medicinal, oddly pleasant. "This will hurt. But tomorrow it will hurt less. And in three days, you'll be functional again. Probably."

Margarethe hissed in pain but didn't pull away.

Thomas watched Johannes work. Fast, efficient, clearly knowing exactly what he was doing despite the chaotic surroundings. Within minutes, the wound was cleaned, treated, and wrapped in fresh bandages.

"You're part of the network," Thomas said. Not a question.

"Was. Am. Will be." Johannes washed his hands in a basin. "My father was Wilhelm's friend. They studied together in Paris, back when Paris actually taught useful things instead of just endless theological debates. When Wilhelm started building the network—or rather, when he joined it and started expanding it—my father was one of his first recruits. He taught me everything before he died. Where the texts are hidden. How the cipher works. What we're really protecting."

"Which is?" Thomas asked.

Johannes looked at him with those bright, intense eyes. "Everything. The accumulated learning of mankind going back two thousand years. Medical texts that could save thousands of lives. Mathematical treatises that could transform trade and building. Philosophical works that could change how we understand the world and our place in it."

He gestured at his shelves. "The Church says this knowledge is dangerous. That it leads to pride, to questioning God, to catastrophe. The Order says it must be controlled or destroyed before it corrupts humanity. They're both right, in a way. Knowledge IS dangerous. But the alternative is worse."

"The alternative is ignorance," Margarethe said quietly.

"Exactly!" Johannes tied off the bandage with a final flourish. "There. Keep it clean, don't use that arm for a week, and you'll probably survive to annoy me again. Now—" He turned to Thomas. "You have the Iron Key, yes? Wilhelm's key?"

Thomas pulled it from his pocket. The eye symbol seemed to catch the lamplight, watching.

Johannes took it with reverent hands. "My God. I've only seen this in drawings. Your grandfather guarded it with his life."

"He died for it," Thomas said quietly.

"I know. I'm sorry." Johannes set the key on his workbench like it was made of glass. "Do you understand what you're carrying?"

"A tool to decode the cipher. Margarethe showed me how."

"It's more than that." Johannes pulled out a manuscript from one of his shelves, opened it to a page covered in marginal cipher dots. "The cipher has layers. Surface layer—locations and basic information. Anyone with patience can decode that. Second layer—dates, connections, patterns. Requires skill to understand. Third layer—the deep structure. The real knowledge. That requires a key."

He demonstrated, showing Thomas how the Iron Key revealed patterns invisible without it. Connections between locations. Hidden notations. Encoded knowledge that couldn't be accessed any other way.

"Your grandfather helped build this web. The keys aren't just tools—they're your leave. Proof that you're trusted with the network's deepest secrets."

"How many keys are there?" Thomas asked, though he suspected he knew the answer.

"Seven." Johannes's face was serious now, the manic energy replaced by something more focused. "One for each of the founding families. Wilhelm's family held the Iron Key—the Eye that Sees, they call it. It reveals locations and patterns."

"What about the others?"

"Scattered across Europe. Hidden. Protected. Some have been lost over the centuries. Others..." Johannes hesitated. "Others are held by people who no longer understand what they have."

Margarethe leaned forward despite her pain. "The Silver Key. You said you knew where it was."

Johannes nodded slowly. "I found it. Five years of hunting for it—following references in old texts, tracking down rumors. It's here. In Strasbourg. Held by Bishop Heinrich Vogel."

"A bishop?" Thomas frowned. "Is he part of the network?"

"No. That's the problem." Johannes started pacing, his energy returning. "The Silver Key—the Hand that Guides, it's called—was held by the Strasbourg family line for generations. Network members, protected it carefully. But the line died out thirty years ago. No heirs. The key passed to the Church as part of an estate. The bishop has no idea what it is. To him, it's just an ornate religious artifact, something decorative to show off to important visitors."

"Can we buy it from him?" Thomas asked.

Johannes laughed, harsh and humorless. "With what money? And even if we had gold, he wouldn't sell. That treasury is his pride—he shows it off constantly, brags about the ancient artifacts he's 'protecting for God.' To sell one would be admitting it has monetary value rather than spiritual significance."

"So we steal it," Margarethe said flatly.

"So we steal it," Johannes agreed. "Which is why I've been planning this for two years."

He went to a desk, pulled out drawings. Detailed sketches of a cathedral—St. Thomas's, Thomas recognized it from the Strasbourg skyline. The drawings showed floor plans, guard rotations, treasury location, entry points.

"The bishop hosts a feast tomorrow night," Johannes explained, spreading the drawings across his workbench. "Important clergy from across the region, wealthy merchants, nobility. It's an annual event—celebrating Saint somebody, I forget which. The point is, the cathedral will be full of people, guards will be distracted by crowd control, and the bishop will be too busy entertaining to pay attention to his treasury."

"You want us to steal from a cathedral full of guards during a feast?" Thomas said slowly. "That's insane."

"It is!" Johannes grinned. "But it's also our only chance. After tomorrow, the bishop leaves for Rome. Won't be back for months. The treasury will be sealed, triple-guarded. Tomorrow night is the window."

Margarethe studied the drawings, her quick mind clearly working despite the pain and exhaustion. "What's the plan?"

"You and Thomas go in as guests. Wealthy merchants from Mainz, fleeing the plague, seeking the bishop's blessing. I've already forged invitations—my handwriting is excellent when I take my time. During the feast, you excuse yourselves, slip away, break into the treasury, take the Silver Key, return before anyone notices you're gone."

"That simple?" Thomas said skeptically.

"Of course not. There will be complications. There always are. But—" Johannes looked at both of them seriously. "This is our chance to reunite two of the seven keys. To access deeper layers of the cipher. To understand what Wilhelm and Heinrich and all the others died to protect."

Thomas looked at Margarethe. She was pale, injured, had nearly died three days ago. Still recovering from fever and blood loss.

"I can do it," she said, reading his expression. "One night. I can manage one night."

"You can barely walk."

"So you'll have to be charming enough for both of us." She almost smiled. "Think you can manage that?"

Thomas thought about breaking into the Mainz cathedral. About Brother Hamo dying. About killing Anselm—not directly, but the result was the same. About everything he'd become since that key had been pressed into his palm.

"I suppose I'll have to learn."

Johannes clapped his hands together. "Excellent! Then we begin preparation immediately. You'll need proper clothes—merchants, not monks. I'll teach you the right way to speak to bishops and nobility. And Thomas, you'll need to learn how to pick a better lock than a cathedral library door. The treasury uses a Bavarian mechanism. Very sophisticated. Very expensive. Very difficult to pick if you don't know the trick."

"Wait," Thomas said. "Tomorrow night? We just arrived. Margarethe is wounded. I don't know how to pretend to be a wealthy merchant—"

"Which is why we start now." Johannes was already pulling clothes from a chest—fine wool, expensive dyes, the kind of garments Thomas had never worn in his life. "No rest for the wicked, as they say. And we, my friends, are about to become very wicked indeed."

Despite everything—the pain, the exhaustion, the sheer insanity of what they were planning—Thomas found himself smiling.

For the first time since fleeing Mainz, since watching Hamo die, since pulling an arrow from Margarethe's shoulder and watching her fever for three days, he felt something other than fear.

He felt hope.

And maybe, just maybe, the chance to actually succeed.

## Scene 5: The Second Key

### Johannes's Workshop, Late Night

Hours later, Thomas's head was spinning with information.

Johannes had spent the evening teaching them everything—how to walk like merchants (confident, appraising everything in terms of value), how to speak to nobility (deferential but not servile, aware of your own worth), which wines were fashionable this year (he had extensive notes), how to navigate a formal feast without revealing you'd spent your life eating monastery food.

And most importantly, how the Bavarian lock mechanism on the treasury door worked.

Now, past midnight, Margarethe had finally collapsed into exhausted sleep on Johannes's guest bed. Johannes and Thomas sat in the workshop, the Silver Key drawings spread between them, a single lamp burning low.

"She's stronger than she looks," Johannes said quietly, watching the stairs where Margarethe had disappeared.

"I know."

"My father told me about Heinrich. About what the Order did to him. About Margarethe memorizing everything because she couldn't read." Johannes poured wine into two cups—better quality than the stuff in Otto's medical kit. "When my father died—natural causes, thankfully, though you never know with our profession—I thought about giving up the network. Going into hiding. Just being a normal alchemist blowing things up for paying customers."

"Why didn't you?"

"Because someone has to remember. Someone has to keep trying." Johannes took a drink. "Your grandfather understood that. He knew the risks. Took them anyway because the alternative—letting the knowledge die, letting ignorance and fear win—was worse than death."

Thomas turned the Iron Key over in his hands. "Johannes, what IS the Genesis Protocol? Everyone keeps mentioning it, but no one will explain."

Johannes was quiet for a long moment. "I don't know. Not fully. The knowledge was fragmented deliberately—split across the seven keys. Safety measure. But from the fragments I've translated, it seems to be... instructions."

"Instructions for what?"

"For surviving what comes next." Johannes pulled out an old manuscript, pointed to marginal notes in faded ink. "The network's founders believed civilization moves in cycles. Rise, flourish, peak, collapse. Rise again. They thought they saw a pattern—major collapses coming in waves, separated by centuries. Bronze Age collapse around 1200 BCE. Rome's fall around 400 CE. If the pattern holds..."

Thomas felt cold. "We're due for another collapse."

"Within the next century or two, yes. Maybe sooner if the plague spreads the way some fear." Johannes tapped the manuscript. "The founders who shaped the Protocol's seven-key form experienced Rome's fall firsthand. They watched knowledge burn, libraries destroyed, civilization fragmenting into chaos. Centuries of accumulated wisdom lost in decades."

"So they created a system to preserve it."

"Not just preserve. To transmit. To ensure that when the next collapse comes, the knowledge survives. Not just in books—those burn too easily. But in people. In networks. In systems that can adapt and survive."

Thomas thought about the dates he'd found in the cipher. The references going back to 400 CE, to Rome's fall, possibly earlier. "The network—at least in its present form—has been operating for nine hundred years. Waiting for the next collapse."

"Or trying to prevent it. The texts aren't clear on that point." Johannes looked troubled. "Which brings me to my question: Why split it? Why not just write down the complete Protocol in one place?"

"Because power corrupts," Thomas said slowly, understanding. "Knowledge concentrated in one place, in one person's hands, becomes dangerous. It becomes control instead of preservation."

"Exactly. Split across seven keys, held by seven different families, no single person can abuse it. You need collaboration. Agreement. Trust."

"What happens if someone gets all seven keys?"

"Then they can read the complete Protocol. Understand exactly what the network's founders intended. See the full pattern, the complete knowledge." Johannes's expression was troubled. "But here's the question that keeps me awake at night: What if the Protocol's original purpose isn't what the Order thinks it is? What if the network itself has been corrupted over centuries?"

Thomas frowned. "What do you mean?"

"The Order claims the network is trying to control humanity, to play God with knowledge and power. The network claims it's just preserving information for future generations. But what if both are true? What if the Protocol started as preservation and became something else over nine centuries?"

He stood, began pacing. "Think about it. Nine hundred years. Dozens of generations. Knowledge passed from parent to child, mentor to student. How much could change in that time? How much could be misunderstood, misinterpreted, corrupted?"

Thomas thought about Anselm's dying words. *The Protocol... someone has to complete it... or everything burns...*

"Anselm believed the network was essential. That without it, civilization would collapse."

"Or maybe he believed a corrupted version of the truth." Johannes stopped pacing, looked directly at Thomas. "What if the complete Protocol says something different than what either the Order or the network currently believes?"

"Then we need all seven keys," Thomas said. "We need to know the truth."

"Agreed. Which is why tomorrow night is so important." Johannes rolled up his drawings. "Get some sleep. Tomorrow you need to be charming, sophisticated, and most importantly, able to pick a Bavarian lock in under two minutes."

"Two minutes?"

"That's how long it takes guards to do their rotation past the treasury corridor. Any longer and we're caught." Johannes grinned, manic energy returning. "No pressure."

Thomas went to his own sleeping space—a pile of blankets in what Johannes called the "storage room" but was actually more like a library that had achieved critical mass and exploded. He lay down, exhausted but unable to sleep.

Tomorrow night they would steal from a bishop. Would break into a cathedral treasury during a feast. Would attempt to reunite two of the seven keys for the first time in generations.

And every step took them deeper into a conspiracy that stretched back a thousand years.

Thomas closed his eyes and tried not to think about all the ways this could go wrong.

Instead, he thought about the pattern. The beautiful, terrible pattern hidden in centuries of cipher marks.

Knowledge and catastrophe. Preservation and control. Truth and corruption all woven together so tightly that maybe they couldn't be separated anymore.

Maybe that was the real secret of the Genesis Protocol.

Not how to save civilization.

But how to understand that civilization might not be saveable. That the cycles would continue regardless. That the only choice was whether knowledge survived the burning or was lost forever.

Thomas opened his eyes in the darkness, staring at Johannes's ceiling covered in astronomical charts and alchemical symbols.

Wilhelm had understood this. Had spent his life building a network to preserve knowledge through the coming darkness. Had died to protect a key that might unlock the truth.

The least Thomas could do was see it through.

For Wilhelm. For Hamo. For Margarethe.

For the possibility that maybe, just maybe, the next collapse could be different if enough knowledge survived.

He closed his eyes again and this time, sleep came.

Tomorrow, they would steal a key.

Tomorrow, the real work would begin.
