# CHAPTER 6 - THE QUEST BEGINS (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: The Decision - From Running to Seeking

### Forest Road East of Strasbourg, Three Days After Leaving the Convent

Thomas's feet hurt in ways he hadn't known feet could hurt.

Not the sharp pain of blisters—though he had those too, burning reminders of how monastery life hadn't prepared him for walking fifteen miles a day through November cold. This was deeper ache, bone-deep exhaustion that radiated up through his calves and thighs and settled into his lower back like stones. Three days of continuous walking on roads that alternated between frozen mud and sharp stones, carrying a pack that seemed to get heavier with each mile despite containing only manuscripts and keys and the minimum supplies needed for survival.

But the physical pain was bearable. Almost welcome, actually—something concrete and immediate to focus on instead of the abstract terror of being hunted by the Order or the nameless dread of being a tool bred for a purpose he never chose, pursuing a quest that would probably kill them all.

"Tell me about the seven keys again," Thomas said, partly to distract himself from his aching feet, partly because the repetition helped him set in order what his mind for patterns was still struggling to fit together.

They were walking through forest that was slowly surrendering to rolling farmland—the landscape changing as they moved east, fewer dense beech and oak woods, more cleared fields that should have been brown with autumn plowing but instead showed the irregular patterns of abandonment. Crops left half-harvested, their brown stalks rotting where they stood. Fields gone to weed and wild growth. Stone walls crumbling from lack of maintenance. The visible scars of plague reducing populations faster than the land could adjust.

Maria answered without hesitation, her young voice carrying knowledge that predated her birth by centuries: "Seven keys unlock seven locations where fragments of the Genesis Protocol are hidden. The complete Protocol was too dangerous to keep in one place—too vulnerable to destruction by fire or conquest or the Order's systematic purges of heretical knowledge. So the founders fragmented it deliberately. Divided the knowledge across Europe in places they deemed secure enough to survive dark ages and catastrophe."

She walked with confidence that seemed impossible for a fourteen-year-old girl who'd left the only home she'd known just days ago. But Thomas was learning that Maria's youth was deceptive—her blood carried forty generations of memory and experience, making her simultaneously the youngest and oldest person in their small company.

"The Iron Key you carry," Maria continued, touching the pack where Thomas kept Wilhelm's inheritance carefully wrapped in oiled cloth, "unlocks the first fragment. The Eye that Sees. It reveals locations, shows where knowledge is hidden across Europe in patterns only those who understand the cipher can read. The Silver Key you stole from the bishop's treasury—the Hand that Guides—unlocks the second fragment. It shows connections between locations, reveals how information flows through the network, demonstrates the relationships between scattered pieces of knowledge."

The cold November wind picked up, carrying the smell of distant wood smoke and the particular scent of approaching winter—frost on dying vegetation, earth settling toward dormancy, the sharp clean smell of air too cold for decay. Thomas pulled his stolen monk's robe tighter, grateful for the thick wool even if it itched constantly and smelled of the friar he'd taken it from.

"And you're the third key," Margarethe added from where she walked slightly ahead, her eyes constantly scanning the horizon for threats with the vigilance of someone who'd learned through bitter experience that relaxing meant dying.

"Exactly." Maria's expression grew distant, accessing blood memory in the way that still made Thomas deeply uncomfortable despite understanding it intellectually. Her eyes unfocused, her gait becoming slightly stiff, like someone sleepwalking through landscapes only she could see. "I carry interpretation. Context. The human understanding needed to translate cipher marks and location marks into actual comprehension of what the Protocol says. Metal keys show you where and how. I show you why and what it means."

"Four more keys to find," Margarethe said, her voice carrying the hard-eyed assessment of someone counting odds and supplies. "Any idea where they are? Or are we just wandering Europe hoping to stumble across ancient conspiracies that might or might not want to kill us?"

"Two of them, yes—I have definite knowledge from blood memory passed down through my mother's line." Maria's eyes focused again, returning from whatever ancestral landscape she'd been accessing. "The fourth key is in Prague. That's our immediate destination. It's called the Voice that Speaks, and it's hidden in the Jewish quarter's scholar's library. My family—my mother's family, stretching back nineteen generations—has protected knowledge of its location. Memorized the specific building, the exact shelf, the countersigns needed to identify ourselves to the guardians who protect it."

"And after Prague?" Thomas asked.

"Constantinople. The fifth key—the Foot that Walks—is hidden somewhere in the ruins of the Great Library. The library that supposedly burned during the Fourth Crusade but actually survived partially, with network members preserving its most valuable holdings in hidden chambers beneath the destroyed sections." Maria frowned, accessing information that came with gaps and uncertainties. "My memories of Constantinople are less clear. The knowledge of that key's location was held by different bloodlines, different families. I have general information but not specific details like I have for Prague."

"So we might get to Constantinople and have to search blindly," Margarethe said.

"Possibly. Or we might find network contacts there who can guide us. Or we might access the fifth key and it will reveal the sixth and seventh." Maria shrugged with the fatalism of someone carrying burdens too heavy for her age. "Blood memory isn't perfect. It's like a tale passed from mouth to mouth through forty generations—some details stay sharp, others blur, and what matters most can shift with each telling."

Thomas thought about that as they walked. Forty generations of women carrying this knowledge in their blood, passing it mother to daughter through inheritance rather than written records. The network's solution to the problem of knowledge being too easily destroyed—make humans themselves into libraries that couldn't burn, archives that couldn't be torn down, repositories that survived dark ages and catastrophe through simple continuation of bloodlines.

Brilliant. And absolutely monstrous.

"And the other two keys?" he asked, though he already suspected the answer.

"Lost. Or at least, lost to my bloodline's knowledge." Maria's voice carried genuine regret, showing the fourteen-year-old beneath the ancient wisdom. "The sixth and seventh keys—the Heart that Feels and the Mind that Knows—were hidden so well that even the network's internal records became fragmented over centuries of wars and plagues and the natural entropy of secret organizations trying to maintain perfect security. Johannes knew more about them. He'd spent twenty years researching the sixth key specifically, had narrowed down its location to somewhere in England. But..."

Her voice faltered, cracking slightly with genuine emotion rather than blood memory. "But he died before he could tell you everything. Died in that barn outside Strasbourg with information still locked in his tortured body. I keep thinking—if we'd rescued him sooner, if we'd gotten there hours earlier, if the torture hadn't broken him so completely—maybe he'd still be alive. Maybe we'd have that information now."

They walked in silence for a moment, each carrying grief in a different way. Thomas remembered Johannes's death—the brilliant, eccentric alchemist reduced to barely breathing wreckage, coughing blood and broken pieces of information, dying despite their rescue because torture had done damage that couldn't be healed with any physic a man knew.

"He told us about you," Thomas said quietly to Maria. "Used his last conscious moments to ensure we knew about the third key, knew where to find you, knew you were essential to understanding the Protocol. He could have saved his strength, tried to survive, focused on his own life. But he chose to do his duty instead. That matters, Maria. His death wasn't meaningless."

"Doesn't make it hurt less," Maria said, her voice very young and very tired.

Margarethe stopped walking abruptly, her hand moving to her knife with the reflexive speed of someone who'd survived multiple ambushes. "Decision point. We can keep heading east toward Prague—three weeks of travel through increasingly dangerous territory, with winter coming and the Order hunting us. Or we can find somewhere to hide for the winter, wait for spring when travel is easier and pursuit might have cooled off. What's our choice?"

Thomas looked at both his companions. Margarethe, who'd lost both parents to the network's cruelty but kept fighting because the alternative was surrender. Maria, fourteen years old and orphaned and carrying blood memory of forty generations of oppression but choosing to help them rather than running. And himself—a monk who'd been bred across forty-one generations to read patterns, who'd killed and stolen and fled, who'd discovered his entire existence was someone else's design.

"We keep going," he said, making the decision as he spoke the words. "Prague for the fourth key. Constantinople for the fifth. And we search for the sixth and seventh while we're traveling. We don't stop. Don't pause. Don't give the Order time to catch us or the network time to recapture us."

"That could take years," Margarethe pointed out with brutal practicality. "Years of traveling through plague country where a third of Europe is dying or dead. Through territories controlled by the Order or the network or just warlords and bandits taking advantage of kingdoms breaking apart. Through winter cold that kills as surely as any Gray Robe's sword. We could die a hundred different ways before finding even half the keys."

"We could," Thomas agreed. He stopped walking, turned to face both of them directly. The late afternoon sun painted everything in shades of gold and shadow, making the abandoned farmland look almost beautiful despite the decay. "Or we could run. Find somewhere remote—a mountain village or isolated monastery or forest deep enough that no one would find us. Hide with the two keys we have and the knowledge Maria carries. Live quiet lives, maybe survive to old age, die peacefully in our beds having accomplished nothing except personal survival."

He met Margarethe's eyes first, then Maria's. "But I'm done running. Done letting other people die for this while I just try to stay alive. Brother Hamo died to give me the Iron Key—bled out in the monastery scriptorium to protect me and buy me time to escape. Wilhelm was tortured to death by the Order for protecting the network's knowledge and refusing to break under interrogation. Your parents, Margarethe—your mother murdered by the network during childbirth for refusing to have more children, your father tortured to death by the Order in a network-sanctioned purge. Johannes captured and killed because he helped us steal the Silver Key and taught us what it meant. All of them died for fragments of this Protocol. For pieces of knowledge they believed was worth dying to preserve and transmit."

His voice grew stronger, more certain, surprising him with its conviction. "I won't let their deaths be meaningless by refusing to complete what they started. Won't let Wilhelm's torture and Hamo's sacrifice and Johannes's last words amount to nothing because I was too frightened or too tired or too pragmatic to continue the quest. They died believing this mattered. I owe them the effort to find out if they were right."

Margarethe studied him with those sharp eyes that missed absolutely nothing—not the tremor in his hands, not the fear he was trying very hard to hide behind determination, not the fact that he was terrified and exhausted and maybe twenty percent convinced this was suicidal stupidity. But she also saw, he thought, that he meant it. That he'd made a choice and intended to follow through regardless of cost.

"You've really changed," she said finally. "The monk I met three weeks ago—that frightened scholar who'd never left the monastery, who thought copying manuscripts was the most dangerous thing he'd ever do—would have chosen survival over the quest without hesitation. Would have prioritized staying alive over pursuing abstract knowledge about conspiracies he barely understood. That Thomas would be running east, yes, but running to somewhere safe, not toward something dangerous."

She smiled slightly, approval mixed with resignation. "Now you're talking about throwing yourself at a quest that will probably kill you. About prioritizing dead people's purposes over your own survival. About choosing meaning over safety. When did you become someone who makes decisions like that?"

"When we killed Brother Anselm," Thomas said quietly, remembering the weight of the leather-working tool in his hand, the distraction he created, Margarethe's blade finding its mark. "That was the moment I stopped being someone who could just walk away. Murder changes you. Makes you responsible for consequences in ways you can never step back from. If I'm going to carry that sin, if I'm going to die with blood on my hands, at least let it be for something more than just personal survival."

Maria spoke up, her young voice carrying the accumulated weight of forty generations: "My mother tried to run. After I was born, after she understood what the network had done—breeding her specifically to produce blood memory carriers, arranging her marriage to shape inheritance, planning her entire life like she was livestock instead of human—she tried to create distance. Tried to have just one child and live a normal life away from the network's breeding programs. She wanted to focus on raising me properly rather than producing more children to serve purposes she'd never consented to."

Her voice tightened with emotion that was both personally felt and inherited through blood memory. "The network killed her for it. During childbirth. Made it look like natural complications—hemorrhage, fever, the kind of thing that kills women in childbirth with tragic regularity. But my blood memory includes medical knowledge from healers in my bloodline, and I can see now what I couldn't understand as a one-year-old: The complications weren't natural. The midwives did something. Subtle. Undetectable without knowledge they didn't expect me to carry. They murdered her and made it look like tragedy."

She took a shaky breath, continuing with difficulty. "And my father—when he started investigating, started asking questions about why a healthy woman died from routine childbirth, started looking into network connections that he was supposed to ignore—the Order found him. Tortured him for information about my mother's death and about what he knew of the network's work. And the network let it happen. Provided information that helped the Order find him. Arranged for his torture to look like standard heretic prosecution while actually being a purge of someone who'd become inconvenient."

Maria squared her young shoulders, forcing strength into her posture. "Running didn't save them. Trying to comply while secretly resisting didn't save them. The only choice that might matter is fighting back openly. Gathering all seven keys. Understanding the complete Protocol. Exposing what the network has become so publicly that they can't easily kill or silence everyone who knows. It might not work—probably won't work, if I'm honest about odds and resources. But it's the only rebellion available to people like us who were bred to be tools but insist on being human instead."

Margarethe was quiet for a long moment, her hand resting on her knife hilt more from habit than perceived threat. The wind picked up again, carrying early evening cold that suggested they'd need to find shelter soon. Finally she spoke:

"All right then. We're doing this. Seeking instead of just surviving. Hunting all seven keys instead of running with the two we have and the one we've become." She started walking again, her pace determined and steady. "Prague is three weeks away if we avoid main roads and move carefully. Maybe four weeks with winter conditions and the need to stay ahead of pursuit. Longer if we encounter serious problems—which we will, because problems are inevitable when you're fugitives carrying knowledge that multiple powerful organizations want to destroy or control or reclaim."

"What kind of problems?" Maria asked with the practical curiosity of someone too young to fully understand danger but intelligent enough to want details.

"Gray Robes hunting us—they're probably three or four days behind, moving faster than we can because they travel openly on main roads and have horses and resources we lack. Network hunters trying to drag back what they bred—that's you and Maria, Thomas; you're both too valuable for them to simply write off as losses. Plague-ravaged villages where survivors might see strangers as plague-bearers to be driven away or killed on suspicion. Bandits and deserters exploiting the chaos of a world coming undone. Nobles whose territories we'll cross who might be network members or Order supporters or just paranoid enough to arrest anyone who can't prove they belong. Winter cold that makes sleeping rough dangerous, that makes rivers harder to cross, that reduces available food and increases the chances of sickness. Supply problems—we need food and water and warmer clothes and probably better boots for Thomas whose feet I can hear blistering from here."

Margarethe listed threats with the casual competence of someone who'd been surviving dangerous situations since her teenage years, her mind automatically counting risks and supplies and odds. "But we knew this would be hard when we started. When we chose quest over hiding. At least now we're making active choices rather than just reacting to what's being done to us. That's worth something, even if it gets us killed."

Thomas felt the knot in his chest tighten into something harder. Not peace—that was too much to ask given their circumstances and the certainty of danger ahead. But resolve. Direction. The difference between being a leaf blown by autumn wind and an arrow loosed toward a mark, even if the mark was far and the odds were bad.

They were no longer fugitives just trying to survive. They were seekers now—trying to finish something that mattered.

It felt good. Terrifying, but good.

"Tell me about Prague," Thomas said to Maria as they continued walking. "What do you know from your family's memories? What should we expect when we arrive?"

Maria's eyes went distant again, accessing inherited memory most people would never touch or even know existed. When she spoke, her voice carried the strange dual quality of being both entirely hers and somehow channeling voices from women long dead:

"The Jewish quarter—the Jewish Town by the river—has been home to scholars and mystics and merchants for generations beyond counting—my blood memory extends back twenty-five generations of knowledge about Prague, but the community itself is older. The network maintained contacts there long before my bloodline's memory begins, back to the network's earliest days when it was still primarily about knowledge preservation rather than breeding people."

She walked automatically, her body moving while her mind accessed inherited information. "The Jewish quarter has survived fires and floods and persecutions—waves of violence against Jewish communities that have happened regularly for centuries. Each time the quarter burned or was destroyed, it was rebuilt. Each time documents were destroyed, they were recreated from memory. The scholar's library where the fourth key is hidden has been destroyed and rebuilt three times that my blood memory can verify. But it always comes back. The network ensured it—provided funds for rebuilding, sent scholars to help recreate lost texts, maintained the library as essential ground for knowledge preservation."

"Why put a key there?" Thomas asked. "Why hide Christian network knowledge in a Jewish library?"

"Because the network has always worked across religious and cultural boundaries," Maria explained, her voice carrying patient wisdom from teachers who'd answered this same question for generations. "Christian monasteries. Islamic libraries in Baghdad and Córdoba. Jewish scholarship in Prague and Cairo. Greek philosophy preserved in Constantinople. Chinese texts that reached Europe through trade routes. The network didn't care about theology or cultural ownership—just about preserving knowledge wherever it could be safely hidden, maintained by communities that valued scholarship regardless of whose religion or empire claimed authority."

She paused, reaching for deeper memory. "The golem manuscript is the fourth key. Genuine kabbalistic teaching about creating life from clay using sacred names and mystical processes. But it's also a container. The cipher marks are hidden within the Hebrew text, invisible unless you know to look for them and have the metal keys to reveal them. The manuscript teaches mysticism on the surface, but underneath it contains the fourth fragment of the Genesis Protocol—information that has nothing to do with creating life and everything to do with understanding how kingdoms fall."

Thomas thought about the elegant simplicity of it. Hide hard knowledge of how kingdoms fall inside mystical teachings about divine creation. Put Christian network secrets inside Jewish texts that Christians wouldn't normally read carefully. Create layers of protection—cultural boundaries, language barriers, religious separation—all serving to preserve information through the very prejudices and divisions that might otherwise destroy it.

"What does the fourth fragment say?" he asked. "What information is hidden in the golem manuscript?"

"Temporal patterns," Maria said, her voice distant. "The fourth key is called the Voice that Speaks because it reveals when things happen. Not just what collapses or how knowledge is preserved, but the timing. The centuries-long cycle of kingdoms failing. The certainty of it—how kingdoms grow mighty, then brittle, and fall in patterns you can learn to recognize. The warning signs that collapse is approaching: the poor crushed, the powerful grasping, roads and bridges failing, cities grown too tangled to endure a hard blow."

She focused again, meeting Thomas's eyes with disturbing intensity. "And it shows the corruption point. The exact moment—or as exact as can be determined from fragmentary records—when the network stopped trying to prevent collapse and started trying to make it. Eleventh century. A network leader named Benedict argued that since collapse seemed inevitable based on the pattern, the network should control when it happened rather than letting it occur randomly. Should choose the timing to benefit chosen bloodlines who'd be positioned to survive and rebuild."

"So the corruption is documented in the Protocol itself?" Margarethe asked. "The fourth fragment shows both the original purpose and the betrayal of that purpose?"

"Yes. That's what makes it potentially powerful. If we can access all seven fragments, we'll see not just what the Protocol originally said but where and how and when it was corrupted. A complete record of the network's betrayal of its own founding principles. That's a blade, if we can figure out how to turn it against them."

They walked in silence for a while, turning over what it meant and what it might make possible. The sun was getting lower, shadows lengthening across the abandoned farmland. They'd need to find shelter soon—sleeping rough in November was possible but dangerously cold, increasing the risk of sickness that could slow or stop their quest entirely.

Margarethe pointed ahead to smoke rising from a village maybe two miles distant. "We need supplies before we can continue to Prague. Food, warmer clothes if we can afford them, information about road conditions and Order activity in the region. Should we risk entering a village or bypass it and try to survive on foraging?"

Thomas thought about their remaining resources—limited money, even more limited food, clothes that were adequate for autumn but wouldn't survive deep winter. "We risk it. We can't walk to Prague and beyond eating only what we can forage. Winter is coming. We need a city's stores even if cities are dangerous."

"Then we need a cover story that explains why three people are traveling together during plague times," Margarethe said with hard practicality. "A monk traveling with a woman and a young girl will raise questions—especially since women don't usually travel with unrelated men, and children don't usually travel at all unless in family groups. We need an explanation that doesn't invite too much scrutiny."

Maria spoke up immediately: "Pilgrims. We're heading to Prague to seek healing at the shrine of Saint Vitus. I survived plague but was left weakened, and my mother"—she gestured to Margarethe—"is seeking miraculous healing since medicine failed. Brother Thomas is our religious guide, hired to ensure our spiritual safety during the dangerous journey."

"Good," Margarethe said with approval. "That's plausible. Desperate plague survivors make pilgrimages all the time, seeking divine intervention when human medicine proves useless. And it explains why we're traveling despite obvious dangers. I'm a widow—recently bereaved, which is sadly common enough to be completely believable. You're my only surviving child. Thomas is earning modest payment for spiritual guidance, which explains why a monk is traveling with women. Simple story. Fits expectations. Easy to maintain."

Thomas nodded agreement. Simple lies worked better than complex ones. Stories that matched what people expected to hear were easier to remember and harder to catch in contradictions.

They approached the village as afternoon transitioned toward evening, long shadows painting the landscape in dramatic contrasts of gold light and deep shadow.

And as they walked, Thomas felt the weight of their choice settling more firmly onto his shoulders.

They were committed now. No more running blindly. No more just reacting.

They were actively seeking all seven keys. Deliberately pursuing a quest that would probably kill them.

But at least they'd chosen it. At least they were dying—if they died—for something they'd decided mattered rather than something that had been decided for them by people dead for centuries.

It wasn't much agency. But it was more than most people bred as tools ever got.

And Thomas intended to make it count.

## Scene 2: The Village - Intelligence and Danger

### Village of Sankt Peter, Eastern Road

The village looked like every other village in plague-ravaged Europe—smaller than it should be, quieter than it should be, haunted by the absence of people who should have been there but weren't.

Sankt Peter had probably been home to a hundred people before the plague. Maybe hundred-twenty during good harvest years when prosperity allowed slightly larger families. The kind of agricultural settlement that had existed for centuries, enduring through wars and famines through sheer peasant stubbornness and the fundamental necessity of farming to feed cities and nobility.

Now maybe twenty people remained.

Thomas could see the evidence everywhere as they approached. Houses with doors hanging open to the elements, their interiors looted or simply abandoned when entire families died and no one remained to claim possessions. Fields left half-harvested, grain rotting in brown stalks because there weren't enough living hands to bring in crops that had been planted when the population was three or four times larger. A mass grave at the eastern edge of the village, fresh-turned earth showing where bodies had been buried recently—probably without proper rites, just desperate disposal of infected corpses before pestilence could spread further.

The smell hit Thomas from fifty yards out—wood smoke and human waste and the sweet-rot odor of decay that seemed to permeate everything during plague times. Not the sharp stench of fresh death, thank God, but the background miasma of a community where normal sanitation had broken down under the strain of mass mortality and too few survivors to maintain basic hygiene.

The living moved through Sankt Peter like ghosts themselves. Hollow-eyed survivors going through the motions of daily life—hauling water, tending animals, repairing buildings—with the numb look of people who'd watched too much death come too fast and didn't yet know how to live beside it. A woman hanging laundry with tears streaming silently down her face. A man mending a fence with such fierce focus it felt like a charm—if he just kept working, kept the old routines, maybe the horror would stop pressing so close.

Children were notably absent. Either dead from plague, or sent away to relatives in safer regions, or kept hidden indoors where strangers couldn't see them and possibly carry infection.

A priest met them at the village entrance—young man, maybe mid-twenties but looking forty from exhaustion and grief. His robes were stained and rumpled, his face unshaven, his eyes gone distant from too many last rites in too short a time.

"Pilgrims?" he asked in German accented with the local Alemannic dialect.

"Yes, Father," Thomas confirmed, falling easily into the religious role that wasn't entirely performance—he was still technically a monk, even if he'd killed and stolen and fled from church authority. "Heading to Prague. To the shrine of Saint Vitus. Seeking healing for the girl." He gestured to Maria, who looked genuinely exhausted and pale enough to seem convincingly ill. "She survived the plague but was left weakened. We're hoping the saint's intercession might restore her strength where medicine has failed."

The priest's eyes softened with sympathy that looked both genuine and bone-deep weary. "Many are making such journeys. Faith is all we have when medicine fails and death surrounds us like wolves circling a campfire." He made the sign of the cross with automatic precision. "I'm Father Andreas. Come—I can offer you shelter in the church tonight and a meal if our stores permit. We have little, but we share what God provides. Though I confess I'm no longer certain what God provides versus what we simply manage to scrape together through desperate effort while God watches silently."

The bitter theology in that last sentence spoke to deep crisis of faith. Thomas recognized the tone—he'd felt it himself when watching plague victims die screaming despite prayers and last rites and desperate appeals to divine mercy that went unanswered.

They followed Father Andreas into the church—small stone building that probably predated the village itself, built in an earlier century when religious architecture was simpler and Christian communities were still establishing themselves in formerly pagan territories. Plain wooden benches instead of ornate pews. Simple altar with a painted wooden crucifix showing more wear than artistry. The smell of incense couldn't quite cover the underlying odor of death and sickness that permeated everything, that seemed to have soaked into the very stones.

"How bad was it here?" Margarethe asked gently, her mind immediately working to learn plague patterns and survival rates.

Father Andreas's face crumpled slightly before he controlled himself with visible effort. "Bad. The plague came five weeks ago. Started with the blacksmith's family—they'd hosted travelers from Mainz who seemed healthy but apparently carried pestilence invisibly. Within three days, the blacksmith's wife showed symptoms. Within a week, a quarter of the village was sick. Within three weeks, two-thirds were dead or dying or fled in panic."

He slumped against the altar, suddenly looking unable to support his own weight. "We burned the bodies when we had wood and strength for cremation. Buried them in mass graves when we didn't. I performed last rites until my voice gave out from constant prayers. Held hands as people died screaming from the pain of buboes bursting inside their groins and armpits. Blessed mass graves because we couldn't dig individual plots fast enough and the ground was freezing and the living were too exhausted to provide proper Christian burial for everyone."

His voice dropped to barely a whisper. "And I still don't understand why God allowed this. Why He punished the faithful and the wicked equally. Why children who'd never sinned died in agony while some notorious sinners survived apparently untouched. Why prayer and faith and righteousness made no difference whatsoever to who died and who lived. It feels random. Arbitrary. Like God threw dice with human lives and didn't care about the results."

Thomas had no answer to offer. Theology provided various explanations—divine testing, punishment for collective sin, incomprehensible plans beyond human understanding. But none of them felt adequate when confronted with the reality of mass death that killed indiscriminately and left survivors hollowed out and questioning everything they'd been taught about divine justice and mercy.

"We carry what faith we can," he said finally, which was both inadequate and honest. "And try to help those still living, since we can't help those already dead."

Father Andreas nodded wearily, accepting the non-answer because what else could he do? "Have there been strangers through here recently?" Margarethe asked, shifting to practical concerns. "We heard rumors that Gray Robes were active in this region. The Order hunting heretics and burning those they deemed plague-spreaders or carriers of false doctrine."

Father Andreas's expression darkened with an anger that cut through his exhaustion like light through cracks. "Yes. Three days ago. Four Gray Robes on horseback, moving with the kind of purpose that suggested specific quarry rather than random patrols. They were asking about travelers. Specifically asking about a monk traveling with a woman and possibly others. Said they were hunting heretics who'd stolen from a bishop and were spreading false teachings about the plague's origins and God's purposes."

Thomas felt cold wash through him despite the church's relative warmth from a small brazier burning in the corner. The Order was hunting them. Had their descriptions. Was questioning every village along the eastern roads. And they were only three days behind—close enough to be a real threat, far enough that there was still time to stay ahead if they moved quickly and carefully.

"Did you tell them anything?" he asked, trying to keep his voice casually curious rather than desperately worried.

Father Andreas met Thomas's eyes with surprising directness, intelligence showing through his grief-exhaustion. "I told them we'd seen many travelers but no one matching that specific description. I explained that the plague has driven hundreds of pilgrims and refugees through this region, that distinguishing one monk and woman from another would be impossible given how many desperate people are fleeing cities and seeking divine intervention."

He stood straighter, anger giving him temporary strength. "I don't trust the Order anymore. They've become cruel during these plague times. They see heresy everywhere, use the chaos as excuse to persecute anyone who doesn't conform to their increasingly narrow interpretation of doctrine. Last month they burned an entire family in the next village for what they called 'heresy.' The family's only crime was speaking German instead of Latin during prayers because they couldn't afford Latin schooling. Five people burned alive for linguistic poverty disguised as religious error."

Father Andreas's voice filled with rare certainty. "The Order doesn't represent God's mercy. They represent fear and control and the particular cruelty that comes from men who are convinced they're doing righteousness while committing atrocity. So even if I had seen a monk traveling with a woman, I wouldn't have told them. Whoever they're hunting probably deserves protection more than persecution."

Thomas felt relief wash through him, mixed with profound gratitude for this exhausted priest who'd chosen conscience over authority despite obvious personal risk. "Thank you, Father Andreas. That... that means more than you know."

"Don't thank me yet. The Order is systematic and patient. They'll question every village along every eastern road. Eventually they'll find someone who saw you clearly, who remembers three travelers matching your description—monk, woman, young girl. Three days behind becomes two days becomes one day becomes..." He didn't need to finish the sentence.

Margarethe asked the crucial question: "How far to Prague from here? And what route would you recommend for travelers who need to avoid... official attention?"

Father Andreas pulled out a rough map sketched on parchment, the kind of informal navigation aid that local priests maintained to help pilgrims and refugees. "Three weeks on foot if you take the main roads and don't encounter serious problems—flooding, bandits, military patrols. Four to five weeks if you avoid main roads and move carefully through forests and back paths. Longer if you're slowed by weather or need to avoid plague-ravaged areas where survivors are hostile to strangers who might carry infection."

He traced routes with his finger, showing alternatives. "The main eastern road goes through Regensburg—large city, well-defended, plenty of resources. But also well-watched. If the Order is looking for you specifically, they'll have men there checking pilgrims and refugees. Hard to pass through a city that size without being observed and recorded by someone."

"Alternative routes?" Margarethe asked.

"North route goes through smaller villages but crosses territories controlled by nobility who actively support the Order. Barons and counts who provide resources and shelter to Gray Robes, who view heretic-hunting as holy duty. Dangerous if you're trying to avoid official notice."

Father Andreas traced a different path. "South route is longer—maybe adds a week to your journey—but passes through lands where local lords are too busy dealing with plague and peasant revolts to care much about religious fugitives. Forests along the Danube, then north through Bohemia toward Prague. Harder travel, more time, less comfortable conditions. But also less likely to encounter organized pursuit or communities that might report strangers to Order authorities."

"That's the route we'll take," Margarethe decided immediately, her instincts automatically choosing the option that prioritized survival over comfort. "Thank you, Father. This information is invaluable."

"Then stay tonight. Rest. Eat what little we can provide. Leave before dawn while it's still dark, before anyone in the village can get a clear enough look at you to provide detailed descriptions if the Order returns with questions." Father Andreas pressed a small bundle into Margarethe's hands—bread, cheese, dried meat wrapped in cloth. "Not much, but enough to get you to the next village. And I'll pray for your safety, though I'm no longer certain my prayers reach anyone who cares to listen."

That night they slept in the church, taking turns on watch even though Father Andreas had offered that they were safe here. Thomas couldn't fully relax despite exhaustion, kept expecting Gray Robes to burst through the doors with swords drawn and accusations ready. But the night passed quietly except for the ambient sounds of a plague-ravaged village—occasional coughs from the sick, crying from those who'd lost family, the general atmosphere of grief that hung over everything like smoke.

In the morning, before dawn, Father Andreas provided them with the promised supplies and one more piece of advice:

"One more thing," he said as they prepared to leave, his voice dropping to a whisper despite no one else being present. "There are rumors. Probably nothing, conspiracy thinking born from desperation to explain the unexplainable. But people are saying the plague isn't entirely natural. That it was sent deliberately or at least allowed to spread by those who benefit when kingdoms fall. That there are organizations—secret societies older than crowns—who set such calamities loose for their own purposes."

He met Thomas's eyes with uncomfortable penetration. "I dismissed it as the kind of fantasy people create when reality is too horrible to accept without explanation. But you—you're running from something. The Order hunts you specifically, not just as random heretics but as particular targets they've been told to find. You carry yourselves like people who know dangerous secrets. And that girl"—he glanced at Maria—"looks at the world like someone much older than fourteen, like she sees patterns others miss."

Father Andreas's voice grew even quieter. "Is there truth to the rumors? Is the plague more than just God's judgment or natural disaster? Is there something darker happening that I should know about?"

Thomas thought about lying, about maintaining their cover, about protecting Father Andreas from knowledge that might get him killed. But this priest had helped them, had lied to the Order to protect them, deserved some measure of truth in return for the risk he'd taken.

"I don't know if this specific plague was deliberately set loose," he said carefully, choosing each word with precision. "But I know there are organizations that understand patterns of how civilizations collapse. That have knowledge of how to guide—or steer—such falls to serve their purposes. Whether they caused this plague or simply allowed it to happen or are just taking advantage of it... I'm still learning. Still gathering pieces of a puzzle that's been assembled over centuries by people who didn't want the complete picture to be visible."

Father Andreas nodded slowly, his exhausted face showing resignation rather than surprise. "Then may God guide your quest. And may whatever you learn help prevent future catastrophes rather than enabling them. The world has enough suffering without human hands deliberately adding more."

They left Sankt Peter as dawn broke, following the southern route Father Andreas had recommended. Through forests that were slowly shedding their last autumn leaves. Along the Danube's northern banks where the river ran cold and gray under November skies. Into territories where the plague had struck but not yet completely devastated, where villages still functioned and survivors still maintained hope.

And behind them, three days back but closing the distance with systematic, grim persistence, Gray Robes followed their trail.

The quest had truly begun. The choice had been made. The path forward was set.

Now they just had to survive long enough to complete it.

Thomas adjusted his pack, felt the weight of both keys wrapped carefully in oiled cloth, and walked east toward Prague and whatever revelations waited in the Jewish quarter's hidden library.

Behind him, Margarethe scanned constantly for threats.

Beside him, Maria reached into blood memory and provided guidance.

And ahead—weeks of travel through winter-approaching landscape, through plague country, through territories that might kill them before they ever reached their destination.

But they walked on.

Because stopping meant being taken.

And they'd all chosen the quest instead.

## Scene 3: Travel and Training - Maria's Education

### Danube Forest Route, One Week Later

The rhythm of travel settled into Thomas's bones over the following week—wake before dawn when frost still painted the ground silver, walk until midday when his legs began to scream, rest during the warmest part of afternoon, walk again until dusk made the forest paths too dangerous to navigate safely, make camp in hidden locations away from roads.

Thomas used the walking hours to teach Maria about the cipher system, showing her how the Iron and Silver Keys worked together to reveal patterns invisible to normal reading. They practiced on manuscripts they'd taken from Johannes's workshop before it burned—texts that seemed innocuous on the surface but contained hidden knowledge when properly decoded.

"The cipher marks look like decorative flourishes," Thomas explained one afternoon, holding a manuscript open while they walked slowly behind Margarethe. "Tiny dots and geometric patterns that scribes added to margins. Most people think they're just embellishment—aesthetic choices to make pages more beautiful. But when you apply the keys at specific positions and angles..."

He demonstrated, placing the Iron Key's eye symbol against the text with precise alignment. Dots appeared through the aperture, seemingly random until he added the Silver Key, positioning its hand symbol to overlap slightly with the eye.

The dots connected into lines. The lines formed patterns. The patterns revealed hidden locations and connections between pieces of information scattered across the manuscript like seeds in a field.

"It's beautiful," Maria breathed, her fourteen-year-old wonder showing through despite the ancient knowledge she carried. "Like... like a secret language written in plain sight. Everyone can see the manuscript, but only those with the keys can read the true message."

"Exactly. The network created this system during Rome's collapse, when barbarian invasions were burning libraries and Christian zealots were destroying 'pagan' knowledge. They needed a way to preserve information that couldn't be easily destroyed. So they hid it inside texts that seemed harmless—agricultural treatises, medical texts, religious manuscripts. Things that barbarians and religious authorities would preserve rather than burn."

Over the following days, they practiced constantly. Maria learning to see where cipher marks were hidden, to sense patterns before Thomas could point them out, to intuitively understand connections that took him minutes of careful analysis to decipher.

And gradually, Thomas realized Maria was better at this than he was—better even than Wilhelm had been, according to the descriptions in his grandfather's journals.

"You're a natural," he said one evening, watching her decode a particularly complex passage in under thirty seconds that had taken him nearly ten minutes.

"I was bred to be good at this," Maria corrected, her voice carrying the philosophical weight of someone wrestling with bloodline fate. "Forty generations of my ancestors were bred specifically to carry and interpret this knowledge. My talent isn't chance—it's bred. There's a profound difference."

"Is there?" Margarethe asked from where she sat maintaining her weapons. "You're good at cipher reading whether that skill came from random inheritance or deliberate breeding. The source of ability matters less than what you choose to do with it."

"Does it?" Maria's eyes were troubled, too old for her face. "If every choice I make is something I was bred to make—am I really making free choices? Or am I just a tool following its design?"

Thomas thought about that question deeply, because it was his question too. He'd been bred for an eye for patterns, for seeing connections others missed. Was his decision to pursue all seven keys really his choice? Or was he simply doing what forty-one generations of breeding had set him up to do?

"I think," he said slowly, working through philosophy he barely understood, "that you can be both. Bred for purpose and still making free choices. The network gave you tools—unusual gifts, blood memory, an eye for patterns most people don't have. But how you use those tools, what purposes you apply them to, whether you serve the network's goals or actively oppose them—that's your choice. That's what separates humans from mere tools."

"Your mother chose resistance," Margarethe added quietly. "The network wanted her to bear more carriers after you were born. She refused. Said no to powerful people who don't accept refusal. It cost her life, but it was her choice, not theirs. Free will when you're their handiwork doesn't mean freedom from your blood—it means freedom to choose what you do with it."

Maria absorbed that in silence, grave beyond her years.

That night, they camped in a forest hollow sheltered from wind. Maria sat by the fire and deliberately accessed deeper blood memory than she'd previously attempted—reaching back through twenty, thirty, forty generations of ancestral knowledge carried in her bloodline.

Thomas watched with fascination tinged with fear as her eyes went distant, unfocused, seeing things that existed only in inherited memory. She began speaking in languages he didn't recognize—not Latin or Greek or Arabic, but something older. Pre-Roman perhaps, languages that had existed before the network was founded, before the cipher system was created.

"What's she accessing?" Margarethe whispered, hand on knife hilt in case this was seizure or attack.

"The network's earliest days," Thomas said, recognizing patterns even in languages he couldn't understand. "When the Protocol was first created. The founding purpose before centuries of corruption."

Maria spoke for nearly an hour, her young voice carrying tones and cadences from other people, other times, other lives lived centuries before she was born. Thomas couldn't understand the words, but he could hear the emotional weight—urgency, hope, determination, fear all woven together in the voices of people trying to preserve knowledge through approaching catastrophe.

Finally her eyes focused again, returning to the present. She looked exhausted, shaken, transformed by what she'd seen.

"I saw them," she whispered hoarsely. "The founders. Roman scholars and Greek philosophers and Christian monks and Jewish teachers all working together as their civilization collapsed around them. They weren't trying to make calamity or decide who survived. They were trying to prevent future calamities by understanding the pattern of why civilizations fail."

"What pattern?" Thomas asked urgently.

"Civilizations grow," Maria said. Her voice carried the weight of forty generations of understanding. "They become powerful and tangled—and that very tangling makes them brittle, like a great house built too high on narrow beams. And every few centuries, the hard times come: failed harvests, sickness, war, trade and coin breaking, lands stripped bare. Sometimes more than one at once. And the proud structure shatters. Dark ages follow. Knowledge is lost. The survivors rebuild slowly, and eventually the cycle repeats."

She met Thomas's eyes with disturbing intensity. "The Protocol was meant to show humanity how to recognize when they're nearing that brittle peak. How to build strength instead of chasing ease. How to preserve essential knowledge so rebuilding happens faster with fewer mistakes repeated. It was defensive. Protective. Meant to help all humanity survive and learn from collapse, not to make ruin to benefit chosen bloodlines."

"But the network corrupted it," Margarethe said.

"Over centuries," Maria confirmed. "The founders died. Their immediate disciples maintained the original purpose, but their children inherited the system without complete context. Gaps in knowledge got filled with assumptions. 'Recognize the pattern to prevent it' became 'recognize the pattern to control it.' 'Preserve knowledge for everyone' became 'control who accesses knowledge.' Small corruptions each generation, gradually accumulating into something monstrous."

Thomas felt the weight of that understanding settling onto his shoulders. They weren't just fighting a conspiracy—they were fighting the accumulated weight of centuries of slow corruption, of good intentions twisted imperceptibly until they became evil without anyone realizing exactly when the transformation happened.

"The complete Protocol will show that," he said. "All seven fragments together will demonstrate both original purpose and the corruption that transformed it. That's our blade—showing the network they've betrayed everything they were founded to protect."

"If we survive to gather all seven fragments," Maria said. "That's still a very large 'if.'"

"Then we better keep working through the 'ifs,'" Margarethe said practically. "Because philosophical understanding doesn't help if we're dead. Two days to Prague. Then we access the fourth key and continue this impossible quest."

They slept in shifts that night, maintained watch against threats both human and natural, and continued east the next morning.

The quest had become clearer. The stakes had become higher. The purpose had become sharper.

They weren't just running anymore. They were actively seeking to understand and expose corruption that had accumulated across centuries.

It probably wouldn't work. They'd probably die before completing the quest.

But at least they'd be dying for something that mattered.

## Scene 4: The Encounter - Network Agent

### Bohemian Forest, Three Days From Prague

Marcus the Physician found them on a gray afternoon when frost painted the bare trees silver and the cold bit through even Margarethe's practical traveling clothes.

Thomas was gathering firewood, his hands numb despite gloves, when he heard footsteps behind him—too quiet to be Margarethe's purposeful stride, too careful to be Maria's youthful carelessness.

He spun, hand reaching for the knife he'd started carrying after Sankt Peter, his heart hammering with immediate terror of Gray Robes or bandits or network hunters.

A man stood ten feet away. Middle-aged, scholar's robes of good quality but not ostentatious wealth, intelligent eyes studying Thomas with the kind of analytical intensity he recognized in himself. Hands raised, showing he carried no visible weapon.

"Thomas of Eltville," the man said calmly. "Wilhelm's grandson. Carrier of the Iron Key. I've been looking for you."

Thomas kept his knife raised despite trembling hands. "Who are you and how did you find us?"

"Someone who knew your grandfather. Worked with him. Shared his belief that the network has strayed catastrophically from its original purpose." The man took one slow step forward, movements deliberately non-threatening. "My name is Marcus the Physician. I serve the network—or served it, before I understood fully what it had become."

"Stay back," Thomas warned, hearing Margarethe approaching silently through the forest behind him.

"I'm not here to capture you or return you to network control," Marcus said patiently. "I'm here to help. To give you information that might save your life. And to ask for your help in return to save something I still believe is worth preserving from the corruption that's consumed it."

Margarethe appeared with crossbow raised and aimed at Marcus's chest. "Speak quickly and convincingly. I've killed network hunters before."

Marcus smiled slightly. "I believe you. You're Heinrich's daughter. The living library trained by your father before the Order tortured him to death in what everyone thought was standard heretic prosecution but was actually a network-sanctioned purge of someone who'd become inconvenient."

Margarethe's expression didn't change, but Thomas saw her finger tighten slightly on the crossbow trigger. "You know things you shouldn't."

"I know many things the network wishes I didn't. Including how to find you—reports from sympathetic men in villages you've passed through, watchers who track bloodlines, and the pattern that says you're heading to Prague for the fourth key. It's what I'd do in your position. What anyone trying to understand the complete Protocol would do."

"So the network knows where we're going," Thomas said, feeling dread settle into his stomach like stones.

"The network suspects. But they haven't yet set the full hunt on you—you're still something they want to reclaim quietly, without showing their hand. That will change when you reach Prague. When you attempt to access the fourth key in the Jewish quarter. Then they'll intervene directly rather than relying on the Order to recapture you."

Marcus lowered his hands slowly. "Which is why I'm here. To warn you. And to give you information Wilhelm died before he could pass to you."

He pulled out a scroll sealed with wax bearing cipher marks worked into decorative patterns. "This is everything Wilhelm discovered about the sixth key—the Heart that Feels. Its location, access methods, countersigns. He spent twenty years researching it, died before he could retrieve it himself. I memorized his findings before his journals were destroyed, waited for someone who could use this knowledge properly."

Thomas felt suspicious hope warring with trained paranoia. "Why help us? What do you gain?"

"Hope," Marcus said simply. "Hope that someone will finally understand the complete Protocol. Will see both what it originally said and what the network corrupted it into. Will expose that corruption publicly enough that network members who still believe in the original purpose will rebel rather than continue serving something monstrous disguised as preservation."

He held out the scroll. "I'm old, Thomas. Tired. I've spent my life serving something I thought was noble, only to slowly realize it had become evil without me noticing exactly when the transformation happened. I don't have courage to rebel openly—they'd kill me and my family. But I can help you. Give you tools to complete what I'm too frightened to attempt myself."

Thomas took the scroll cautiously, examined the seal, opened it carefully. Read cipher marks and location details written in handwriting he recognized from Wilhelm's manuscripts.

"This looks genuine," he said slowly.

"It is. Wilhelm trusted me with his notes. I'm trying to honor that trust by helping his grandson complete what he started." Marcus stood, prepared to leave. "But I need to warn you—the network is watching Prague. They know the fourth key exists in the Jewish quarter, even if they've lost exact location details over centuries. They'll be watching for anyone attempting to access the scholar's library. Be careful. Be clever. Get the key and leave before they can move to stop you."

"Why are you helping if it puts you at risk?" Margarethe asked, crossbow still aimed but finger relaxing slightly on the trigger.

"Because doing nothing puts me more at risk," Marcus said quietly. "Risk to my conscience. Risk of dying knowing I served evil when I could have chosen differently. This is my small refusal. Maybe it matters. Maybe it doesn't. But at least I tried."

He turned to leave, then paused. "One more thing. The seventh key—the Mind that Knows—is in Constantinople with the fifth key. Both hidden in the Great Library's surviving sections. If you can reach Constantinople, you can potentially get two keys in one journey. That's my own work, not Wilhelm's. Use it as you see fit."

Then Marcus walked into the forest and disappeared between frost-silvered trees like smoke dissipating.

Thomas stood holding the scroll, uncertain whether to trust it despite his mind for patterns insisting that everything Marcus said fit what he already knew about the network's fragmentation and internal conflicts.

"Trap?" Margarethe asked.

"Maybe. Or genuine help from someone who still remembers what the network was supposed to be." Thomas secured the scroll carefully in his pack. "Either way, we were already going to Prague. This just gives us information about what comes after. And the warning about network eyes is probably accurate regardless of Marcus's true intentions."

Maria emerged from camp, having heard most of the conversation. "He was telling the truth. I could sense it—blood memory teaches you how to read people, how to hear the lie in a voice. He genuinely believes he's helping, genuinely regrets what the network has become. Whether that makes his help safe or dangerous..." She shrugged. "Unknown."

"Then we stay careful," Thomas decided. "Three days to Prague. We access the fourth key, learn what it reveals, and decide next steps. Constantinople for two more keys. England for the sixth. We keep moving forward because stopping means capture or death."

They broke camp and continued east with even greater caution.

The quest was accelerating. The network was stirring. Time was running out.

So they walked east.

## Scene 5: Prague - The Jewish Quarter

### Prague, The Jewish Quarter, Three Days Later

Prague was magnificent and terrifying in equal measure—sprawling across both banks of the Vltava River under gray November sky, church spires and castle towers rising like stone prayers, multiple districts each large enough to be cities themselves.

The Jewish quarter occupied a walled area near the river, physically and culturally separated from the Christian majority. Guards at the gates examined their forged pilgrim papers with suspicious thoroughness that plague times had intensified.

After tense questioning and small bribes, they entered the Jewish quarter just as the afternoon sun angled toward evening.

The quarter felt like a different world—buildings pressed close together, streets narrow and winding, languages mixing in ways Thomas had never heard, smells of unfamiliar spices and different cooking. The residents watched them with the wariness of people who'd learned through bitter experience that Christian visitors often brought trouble disguised as piety.

Maria guided them through the maze of streets with blood memory's confidence, leading them to a modest building marked with a nearly invisible symbol worked into the stonework—a hand holding seven keys.

She knocked in a specific pattern. Three quick, two slow, three quick.

The door opened, revealing an old man with a long white beard and penetrating dark eyes that seemed to see through their pilgrim disguise immediately.

"The three from the west," he said in German. "Monk, warrior woman, and blood memory carrier. We've been expecting you for weeks. Longer, if you count the generations my family has been preparing for this moment. I'm Rabbi Ezra. Come inside quickly. The network is watching."

He led them down hidden stairs to a library that took Thomas's breath away—thousands of manuscripts carefully preserved, knowledge from across the known world protected by scholars who understood that human wisdom belonged to humanity, not just one culture or religion.

"The golem manuscript," Rabbi Ezra said, pulling out an ancient text with reverence. "Kabbalistic teachings on creating life from clay. But also a container for the fourth fragment of the Genesis Protocol. Your keys will reveal what's hidden within the mystical text."

Thomas and Maria worked together, placing both metal keys on the manuscript in precise alignment. Maria touched the page, activating blood memory.

Her gasp echoed in the quiet library.

"I can see it," she whispered. "The fourth fragment. Temporal patterns—not just that civilizations collapse in recurring waves, but why. The certainty of it. How kingdoms grow mighty, then brittle. How chasing ease breeds weakness. How ties between cities can spread ruin faster—and how those same ties can spread recovery, if knowledge is preserved correctly."

She traced invisible patterns on the page. "And I see the corruption point. Eleventh century. A network leader named Benedict arguing that since collapse was inevitable, they should control when it happened rather than letting it occur randomly. The moment preservation became control. The betrayal documented in the Protocol itself."

They spent an hour copying information, Maria committing crucial details to blood memory for preservation even if physical notes were lost.

Finally, Rabbi Ezra provided them with letters of introduction to network sympathizers along routes to Constantinople, and they prepared to leave before network eyes could gather.

"Thank you," Thomas said. "This is dangerous for you."

"My family has protected this library for nineteen generations," Rabbi Ezra said. "Waiting for someone who would use the knowledge correctly rather than turn it into a weapon for control. You're trying to understand, not control. That makes you worth helping, even at risk."

They left the Jewish quarter as dusk fell, carrying the fourth fragment's knowledge and understanding that the complete Protocol would reveal both the network's original nobility and its centuries of corruption.

Three keys down. Four to go.

Constantinople next, for the fifth and possibly seventh keys.

Then England for the sixth.

Then synthesis—if they survived long enough to gather all seven fragments.

The quest continued. The network roused itself. The Order pursued.

And three fugitives carrying pieces of a thousand-year puzzle kept moving forward because stopping meant failure and failure meant the network's corruption would continue unchallenged for another thousand years.

Thomas felt the weight of both metal keys in his pack, felt Maria's living memory walking beside him, felt the scroll from Marcus containing Wilhelm's notes.

They probably wouldn't survive to Constantinople. Probably wouldn't gather all seven keys. Probably wouldn't stop the fall the network was steering.

But they would try.

And trying—choosing active quest over passive surrender—was the most human choice people bred for a purpose could make.
