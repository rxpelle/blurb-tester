# CHAPTER 7 - THE THIRD KEY REVEALED (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: The Journey Begins - Toward Constantinople

### Four Weeks East of Prague, Late December 1347

The cold had teeth.

Not the clean cold of autumn frost or even the sharp bite of first winter. This was December cold in the Carpathian foothills, the kind that found every gap in clothing and settled into bones like an unwelcome guest planning a long stay. Thomas's fingers had gone numb inside his wool-wrapped gloves two hours ago. His feet existed somewhere at the ends of his legs, theoretical rather than felt.

Margarethe walked ahead with the steady endurance of someone who'd learned to ignore physical discomfort through sheer will, her breath steaming in the pre-dawn darkness. She rolled her left shoulder occasionally—the old wound from Strasbourg still stiff in December cold, though she never complained about it. Maria followed between them, fourteen years old and carrying forty generations of blood memory like a pack she couldn't set down.

They'd been traveling for three weeks since leaving Prague with the fourth key's knowledge. Three weeks of avoiding main roads where the Order might be watching. Three weeks of sleeping rough in forests and abandoned barns and occasionally—when they had coin and the village seemed safe—paying for a corner of someone's floor near their fire.

Winter was making everything harder.

"How much farther?" Maria asked, her young voice tight with cold and exhaustion.

"Constantinople is still eight weeks away if we maintain current pace and don't encounter serious problems," Margarethe said without turning. "Twelve weeks if we're realistic about winter conditions and the need to avoid pursuit. Four months if we're being honest about how plague has disrupted everything and how few safe paths remain through territories we'll need to cross."

Thomas thought about four more months of this cold, this fear, this constant movement with the Order somewhere behind them like wolves following wounded prey. Thought about whether they'd survive that long, whether the quest would kill them before they could complete it.

But he also thought about what they'd learned in Prague. About the fourth fragment of the Genesis Protocol showing temporal patterns of collapse. About the network's corruption from defensive purpose to offensive plotting. About having evidence—finally—that the conspiracy could be exposed and challenged.

"Tell me again what Maria saw in Prague," he said to Margarethe, partly to distract from the cold, partly because repetition helped his mind for patterns settle things into place. "When she accessed the fourth key's knowledge. What exactly did the fragment reveal?"

Margarethe slowed her pace slightly, letting Thomas come alongside. "Temporal patterns. The centuries-long rhythm of kingdoms rising and falling. Bronze Age collapse around 1200 BCE. Roman collapse around 400 CE. And the current collapse—what they're calling the 'Great Mortality'—showing the same warning signs the Protocol describes."

"But also the corruption point," Maria added, her breath coming in visible puffs. "Eleventh century. A network leader named Benedict arguing that since collapse seemed inevitable from the pattern, the network should control when it happened rather than letting it occur randomly."

Thomas felt anger kindle in his chest despite the cold trying to extinguish all feeling. "So they went from trying to prevent collapse to steering it. From helping humanity survive to selecting who deserves survival."

"Over generations," Maria said. "Not a sudden shift from good to evil, but slow corruption across centuries. Each generation making small changes that seemed reasonable at the time but accumulated into something monstrous."

The path they followed was barely a track—goat trail probably, winding through hills covered in bare trees and early snow. Somewhere to their left, Thomas could hear water running, a stream not yet frozen despite December cold. To the right, forest pressed close, dark pines and skeletal deciduous trees creating shadows in the pre-dawn gloom.

"Why Constantinople?" Thomas asked. "Why hide two keys there instead of spreading them further apart?"

"Because the Great Library survived," Maria said, accessing blood memory with the distant-eyed expression Thomas had learned to recognize. "Everyone thinks it burned during the Fourth Crusade in 1204. The Latin crusaders did destroy the visible sections—looted and burned what they could see. But network members had been preparing for that possibility for decades. They created hidden chambers beneath the destroyed sections. Preserved the most valuable materials underground where fire and conquest couldn't reach."

"And the fifth and seventh keys are both there?" Margarethe asked.

"The fifth key—the Foot that Walks, showing movement patterns and how populations migrate during collapse—is definitely there. My blood memory is clear about that location." Maria frowned, sorting through inherited memory that came with gaps and uncertainties. "The seventh key—the Mind that Knows, which enables complete synthesis of all fragments—is probably there. At least, several bloodlines believed it was hidden in Constantinople when that information was committed to blood memory. But the seventh key's location was more carefully obscured than the others. Multiple conflicting memories. Deliberate misdirection to protect the most important piece."

Thomas thought about gathering six of seven keys, about having most of the Genesis Protocol but not quite complete understanding. Would six fragments be enough to expose the network? Or would they need all seven to prove the full scope of corruption?

The sky was lightening now, darkness giving way to that particular gray that came before winter sunrise. Thomas could see the landscape more clearly—rolling hills covered in snow and ice, scattered villages sending thin smoke columns into cold air, roads that had once connected human settlements but now served mostly to spread plague between dying communities.

"We need to discuss what happens when we have all seven keys," Margarethe said practically. "Assuming we survive to Constantinople and then to England for the sixth key—assuming we actually gather all the fragments. What do we DO with complete understanding of the Genesis Protocol?"

"Expose it," Thomas said immediately. "Show the network's corruption. Prove they betrayed their founding purpose. Force internal rebellion by demonstrating to current members that they're serving evil disguised as preservation."

"To whom?" Margarethe challenged. "The Church? The Church is bought—we know Order men serve the network while pretending to hunt heretics. Secular authorities? Kings and nobles who benefit from the network's arrangements and breeding? The common people who can't read and wouldn't understand a bloodline plot this twisted even if we could somehow tell it plainly?"

Thomas felt frustration war with his determination. She was right—they were gathering evidence of a conspiracy so old and so careful that simply shouting it wouldn't stop it. Truth needed legs. It needed scribes and priests and merchants—people others trusted enough to listen. It needed ears willing to hear.

"Then what?" he asked. "We gather all seven keys, understand the complete Protocol, and do nothing because exposure is too difficult?"

"I'm not saying do nothing," Margarethe said. "I'm saying we need a plan beyond 'gather knowledge and hope truth naturally defeats evil.' The network has been operating for nine hundred years. They've survived times when realms broke apart, dozens of attempts to expose them, centuries of people discovering pieces of their conspiracy. They've learned how to survive simple exposure—by buying monasteries and courts and smothering the story before it can spread."

Maria spoke up quietly: "My blood memory includes failed exposures. People who discovered pieces of the network's work and tried to reveal them publicly. Sometimes they were killed. Sometimes they were discredited. Sometimes their revelations were turned—the network admitted to a sliver of truth while hiding deeper corruption, then claimed the reformers were satisfied and the matter was settled."

"So we need something more than just knowing the truth," Thomas said, trying to think as plainly as he could. "We need... what? A way to turn the knowledge against them?"

"Or a way to make suppression impossible," Margarethe suggested. "Distribute the knowledge so widely, through so many different channels, that killing us or discrediting us doesn't matter because the information continues spreading independently."

Thomas thought about manuscripts. About the network's own cipher system designed to preserve knowledge through catastrophe. About how information could survive if properly encoded and distributed across enough locations that destroying all copies became impossible.

"We use their own methods against them," he said, the idea forming as he spoke. "The network created the cipher system to preserve knowledge across generations and disasters. We use the same system to preserve evidence of their corruption. Hide it in manuscripts, in locations across Europe, encoded so that even if the network destroys most copies, enough will survive for future generations to discover."

"That's long-term thinking," Margarethe said. "Planting seeds for people centuries from now. What about immediate action?"

"Both," Thomas decided. "We gather all seven keys. We understand the complete Genesis Protocol. We document the network's corruption as thoroughly as possible. Then we do two things simultaneously: Expose it to current authorities—Church, nobility, anyone who might act—and preserve the evidence for future discovery using the network's own preservation methods. One prong might fail, but maybe both together create enough damage to matter."

They walked in silence for a while, each feeling the weight of what they were proposing. Fighting a nine-hundred-year conspiracy with scattered evidence and desperate hope.

The sun broke over the eastern horizon, pale winter light painting the snow-covered landscape in shades of white and gray and gold. Beautiful, if you could ignore the cold trying to kill you and the knowledge that somewhere behind them, the Order was still hunting.

"Constantinople is defended by both Byzantine authorities and network members," Maria said, accessing more inherited information. "Getting into the city will be relatively easy—they need travelers and merchants to keep trade alive. But accessing the hidden library chambers beneath the burned sections will be much harder. The network guards those locations carefully. We'll need local contacts. Recognition phrases. Probably bribery and possibly violence."

"Do you have names?" Thomas asked. "Names of network members in Constantinople who might help?"

"I have countersigns and locations where contacts should be. Whether those people are still alive after plague and unrest..." Maria shrugged. "Unknown. The blood memory is decades old—passed from my grandmother to my mother to me. Constantinople might have changed significantly in that time."

"Then we adapt," Margarethe said. "We've adapted this far. Survived Order pursuit, stolen from bishops, slipped into plague cities, accessed restricted libraries. We can handle Byzantine complications."

Something in Thomas steadied—not quite confidence, because that seemed too presumptuous given their circumstances. But purpose. Direction. The sense that they weren't just running blindly but moving toward something meaningful.

They were three people against a nine-hundred-year conspiracy. Three fugitives carrying stolen keys and forbidden knowledge. Three tools bred by others choosing to rebel anyway.

The odds were terrible. The likelihood of success was minimal. The chances of survival to complete the quest were almost none.

So they walked on.

Because stopping meant being taken.

Behind them, the sun continued rising. Ahead of them, Constantinople waited with its hidden libraries and network guardians and two more keys that might complete their understanding.

And somewhere in the winter landscape, the Order followed their trail with grim patience.

The road went on.

## Scene 2: The Network Physician - Marcus Provides More Information

### Moravian Forest, Two Days Later

Marcus found them again in a forest clearing where they'd stopped to rest and eat cold provisions. Thomas heard him approaching this time—footsteps crunching deliberately on snow rather than trying stealth. A courtesy. Announcing his presence before appearing suddenly and risk getting a crossbow bolt from Margarethe.

He emerged from trees with hands visible and empty, the same middle-aged physician they'd met weeks ago, the network physician who'd given them Wilhelm's notes on the sixth key and information about the seventh key being in Constantinople with the fifth.

"You're making good time," Marcus said calmly, stopping ten feet away. "I expected you to still be in Bohemia, but you're already into Moravian territory. Impressive pace given winter conditions and Order pursuit."

Margarethe had her crossbow raised but not aimed, finger resting near—but not on—the trigger. "What do you want? More cryptic information? More half-warnings about network eyes in Constantinople?"

"More complete information," Marcus said. "Because you impressed me by making it this far, and because I'm too old to keep waiting to see whether you'll complete this quest or die in a ditch." He pulled out a scroll sealed with wax, held it up so they could see it clearly. "This holds everything I know about the network's work in Constantinople—who controls the hidden library chambers, countersigns that still work instead of outdated ones from Maria's blood memory, and safer ways into the underground sections that avoid the main guards."

"Why help us?" Thomas asked, suspicious despite wanting the information desperately.

"Because I'm tired," Marcus said with genuine weariness in his voice. "Tired of serving a conspiracy that's betrayed everything it was supposed to protect. Tired of watching generation after generation of network members commit horrors while genuinely believing they're helping humanity survive. Tired of being too frightened to rebel openly but too conscience-stricken to keep serving silently."

He took a step forward, still keeping movements slow and non-threatening. "You three represent something I thought died centuries ago—people who understand the network's work but choose humanity over conspiracy when forced to decide. That's rare enough to be valuable. Rare enough that I'm willing to risk network retaliation by helping you more actively."

Maria studied him with that too-old expression in her young eyes. "You're gambling we'll succeed. Betting your life and your family's safety on three fugitives gathering evidence that might—against all odds—create rebellion within the network."

"Yes," Marcus said simply. "It's a terrible bet. Odds are strongly against you surviving to Constantinople, let alone gathering all seven keys and exposing the corruption effectively. But it's the only bet available that leads to meaningful change rather than continued service to monstrous purpose."

Margarethe lowered her crossbow slightly, though Thomas noticed she didn't unload it. "What's in the scroll beyond Constantinople information?"

"Details on the sixth key's location in England," Marcus said. "Wilhelm spent twenty years researching it, but he died before completing his investigation. I picked up where he left off. Spent another decade tracking the key through fragmented records and partial network documents. I believe it's hidden in a monastery in York—probably in the library's restricted sections where only senior monks are allowed access."

"York is far," Thomas said, thinking about geography. "After Constantinople, we'd need to travel back west through plague country, across territories probably worse-affected than what we've already seen. That's another four or five months of travel minimum. Maybe longer if winter extends or if conditions have degraded further."

"Yes," Marcus agreed. "The complete quest—all seven keys—is probably a year-long journey minimum. More likely eighteen months or two years given complications and the need to avoid pursuit. You'll be traveling across a continent fragmenting under plague and unrest. Many of the network contacts I'm giving you information about might be dead before you reach them. The hidden library chambers might have been discovered and looted. The keys themselves might have been moved or destroyed."

He held out the scroll. "But this is the best information available outside of network leadership's complete archives. It gives you fighting chance instead of just desperate hope."

Thomas took the scroll carefully, examined the wax seal showing cipher marks worked into decorative patterns. "If we succeed—if we gather all seven keys and expose the network's corruption—what happens to you? The network will know you helped us. Will hunt you."

"Probably," Marcus said calmly. "I've prepared as well as I can. Hidden my family. Set aside false names and roads out. Scattered my own notes to locations where they might survive my death. But yes—if you succeed publicly, the network will identify me as one of the sources who helped you. They'll retaliate."

"And you're accepting that risk?" Margarethe asked.

"I'm tired of not accepting risks," Marcus said. "I'm old, Thomas. Sixty-three this winter. Most people don't live this long even without plague. I've spent forty years serving the network because I believed their justifications about preserving humanity through collapse. But the fourth fragment's revelations about timing that you accessed in Prague? I've known that for a decade. I've known the network corrupted its purpose centuries ago. And I've done nothing except keep serving while quietly gathering evidence and waiting for someone brave enough to actually use it."

His voice grew passionate despite the cold making everyone's breath visible in steaming clouds. "You're using it. You're not just gathering knowledge and hiding. You're actively questing to understand the complete Protocol so you can expose corruption and create rebellion. That's worth supporting. Worth risking everything for. Because if I die having helped you succeed, I die having finally chosen correctly after decades of cowardice."

Thomas felt the weight of that on his shoulders. Marcus wasn't just providing information—he was betting his life and his family's safety on their quest. Was trusting that three fugitives would somehow accomplish what centuries of scattered opposition had failed to achieve.

"Thank you," Thomas said, which felt inadequate but honest. "We'll do everything we can to make your risk worthwhile."

"I know you will. That's why I'm helping." Marcus prepared to leave, pulling his heavy cloak tighter against December cold. "One more piece of advice: When you reach Constantinople, seek out a woman named Helena. She manages a boarding house near the Augustaion—the great square where old Roman statues still stand. She's network-affiliated but morally conflicted. Use the countersign 'Seven keys open seven doors but only truth opens minds.' She'll help you access the underground library if you convince her you're seeking knowledge rather than just stealing artifacts."

"How do we convince her of that?" Margarethe asked practically.

"Show her what you've learned," Marcus said. "The proof from Prague. The evidence that Benedict twisted the network's purpose from defensive to offensive. Helena knew the network's history before she joined. She believed the preserved founding purpose. If you show her that the network's work now betrays that original purpose, her conscience will do the rest."

He started to leave, then paused. "The Order is six days behind you. Maybe seven if they're being cautious about winter conditions. But they're persistent and well-funded. Don't stop moving. Don't assume any safe distance. They'll chase you across Europe if necessary because what you carry threatens everything they've built."

Then Marcus walked into the forest and disappeared like smoke dissipating into December air.

Thomas secured the scroll in his pack next to the Iron and Silver Keys, felt the accumulated weight of evidence and information they were carrying. Felt the responsibility of all the people who'd died or risked death to provide these pieces—Wilhelm and Brother Hamo and Johannes and now Marcus betting everything on their quest.

"Six days," Margarethe said, already packing up their camp with efficient speed. "We need to increase pace. Push harder. Cover more distance before they close the gap further."

"In winter conditions?" Maria asked, shivering despite her layers. "We're already walking ten to twelve hours a day. Any faster and we'll collapse from exhaustion or freeze from not having time to make proper shelters."

"Then we find villages more frequently," Margarethe decided. "Pay for indoor shelter even when it costs more coin than we'd prefer. Stay warm enough and rested enough to maintain faster pace. The Order moves faster because they sleep indoors and eat hot meals and travel on main roads. We need similar advantages without giving up our stealth."

Thomas thought about their remaining money—enough for maybe two weeks of paid shelter if they were frugal. After that, they'd be back to sleeping rough and hoping winter didn't kill them.

"We'll manage," he said with more confidence than he felt. "We've managed this far. Constantinople is still achievable. We just need to keep moving forward."

They broke camp and continued east, pushing harder through cold that seemed to intensify as they traveled deeper into winter and farther from the milder climate near the Rhine.

Behind them, six days back but closing inexorably, the Order pursued.

Ahead of them, eight weeks away if they survived, Constantinople waited with its hidden libraries and network guardians and two more keys that might complete their understanding.

The days went on. The winter deepened. The danger intensified.

And three fugitives kept walking toward a future they could barely imagine but were determined to reach anyway.

## Scene 3: Maria's Revelation - The Deeper Memory

### Four Weeks East of Prague, Deep Winter

The sickness took Maria on a gray afternoon when they were three days from a village that Marcus's scroll indicated should have network contacts willing to help.

She'd been coughing for two days—they all had, actually, the harsh dry cough that came from breathing cold air and sleeping in insufficient shelter. But Maria's cough developed a wet quality that made Thomas's medical instincts scream warnings. Then she started running a fever despite the cold.

By evening, she was barely walking. By the time they made camp in a half-collapsed barn offering minimal shelter from wind, she was shaking with fever-chills and coughing up blood.

"Plague?" Margarethe asked quietly while Maria slept fitfully on their makeshift bedding.

"Maybe," Thomas said, pressing a hand to Maria's burning forehead and feeling his stomach drop at how hot she felt. "Or just pneumonia from exposure and exhaustion. Or some other illness that's killing her just as effectively as plague would."

He'd been trained in monastery medicine—the basic herbalism and prayer-based healing that medieval monks practiced. Enough to know that fever this high in winter conditions usually meant death. Enough to know that coughing blood was a terrible sign. Enough to know that they were three days from help and Maria probably didn't have three days.

"What can we do?" Margarethe asked, and Thomas heard the grief already in her voice. They'd known Maria for weeks now. She'd become not just a tool for accessing blood memory but a person. A fourteen-year-old girl who'd lost everything and chosen to help them anyway despite knowing it would probably kill her.

"Keep her warm," Thomas said helplessly. "Give her water when she's conscious enough to drink. Pray, if you believe it helps. And prepare for the likelihood that she dies in the next day or two."

But Maria didn't die that night. Or the next day. She burned with fever, coughed blood, sometimes became delirious and spoke in languages Thomas didn't recognize. But she kept breathing through sheer stubborn will.

On the third night, the fever broke.

Thomas woke to find Maria sitting up, pale and weak but conscious and clear-eyed in a way she hadn't been for days.

"I saw something," she said hoarsely, her voice barely above a whisper. "During the fever. Accessed blood memory deeper than I've ever gone before. Saw things from before the network was founded. Saw the original conspiracy's actual beginning."

"Save your strength," Thomas said, offering water that she drank gratefully. "You can tell us when you're better recovered."

"No," Maria insisted with surprising force. "I need to tell you now while the memory is fresh. Before my conscious mind starts editing and filtering what I saw into more comfortable narratives."

Margarethe had woken at the commotion, moved closer so she could hear. "What did you see?"

Maria took another sip of water, gathered herself. "The network wasn't founded during Rome's collapse around 400 CE the way everyone believes. That's the story they tell themselves, the comfortable founding myth. But my blood memory goes deeper—back to the actual beginning. And it's older. Much older."

"How much older?" Thomas asked, feeling cold that had nothing to do with December temperatures.

"Bronze Age collapse," Maria said. "Around 1200 BCE. A great breaking, recorded even in the oldest chronicles. Kingdoms across the Mediterranean fell almost together—Mycenaean Greece, the Hittite empire, Egypt weakened, cities burned. It wasn't one war or one famine, but a chain: one power toppling another until the whole world of bronze cracked."

She coughed, winced at the pain in her chest, continued anyway. "The network was founded by survivors of that collapse. Scribes and priests and scholars who watched their world burn and decided the pattern needed to be understood and prevented from repeating. They created the first versions of what would become the Genesis Protocol—a reckoning of why kingdoms grow brittle when they grow great, how ruin leaps from city to city, what signs come before catastrophe."

Thomas felt his understanding of everything shift like ground becoming unstable beneath his feet. "So the network is over two thousand years old? Not nine hundred years like we thought?"

"Older," Maria said. "And their original purpose was genuinely defensive—figure out why collapse happens so future generations can prevent it or at least survive it with their eyes open. They tracked where knowledge was kept. They watched courts and trade, harvests and wars. Everything they could observe and write down to understand the pattern."

"When did it corrupt?" Margarethe asked.

"Gradually, across centuries," Maria said, accessing memory that clearly pained her. "The Roman collapse was a test. The network had existed for nearly eight hundred years by then. They'd successfully preserved knowledge through multiple smaller catastrophes. They thought they understood the pattern well enough to prevent Roman collapse. But they failed."

"Rome fell anyway," Thomas said.

"Rome fell anyway," Maria confirmed. "And that failure broke something in them. Before, they believed the pattern could be turned aside with wise hands. After Rome, they began to believe the fall was as sure as numbers. That's when Benedict's arguments gained traction—six centuries later, but rooted in the despair Rome planted."

She took more water, continued with difficulty. "Benedict argued: If a fall will come—if two thousand years prove it—then we should steer it, not wait for it. Put chosen families in the right places at the right times. Use disaster like a lever to shape the world, instead of trying to prevent disaster at all. And enough network members, scarred by Rome, agreed."

"How do you know this?" Thomas asked. "If this happened in the eleventh century, how does blood memory from your bloodline extend that far back?"

"Because my ancestors weren't just carriers," Maria said. "They were chroniclers. Each generation deliberately memorized the network's history and wrote it into blood for preservation. My family—going back forty generations—were the network's living archive. The library that cannot burn. The last safeguard when written records were destroyed."

Her expression grew troubled. "But I also saw something else during the fever. Saw what happens when all seven keys are used together. When someone with full blood memory accesses the complete Genesis Protocol using all seven fragments simultaneously."

"What happens?" Margarethe asked quietly.

"The joining," Maria said. "Complete understanding not just of what the network has done but why the pattern exists. Why civilizations collapse again and again. And..." She hesitated, clearly struggling with what she'd seen. "And whether collapse actually is inevitable or whether the network's interventions have been causing the collapses they claim to be preventing."

Thomas felt ice settle into his stomach. "They're causing the collapses?"

"I don't know," Maria admitted. "The vision during fever was fragmented. But I saw suggestions—implications in the deepest blood memory—that network meddling creates instability. That their attempts to shape bloodlines and 'prepare' for collapse actually make kingdoms more brittle. That the pattern they've been tracking for two thousand years is a pattern they themselves may have carved through their interference."

"So they've been making calamities for two millennia?" Margarethe asked. "Playing God with civilizations while thinking they were helping?"

"Maybe. Or maybe the collapses are genuinely inevitable and the network is just delusional about their ability to control events." Maria looked at them with eyes too ancient for her fourteen years. "That's why we need all seven keys. Why we need the complete Genesis Protocol. To understand whether we're fighting an evil conspiracy or a deluded one. Whether they're deliberately causing harm or accidentally causing harm while genuinely believing they're helping."

"Does the distinction matter?" Thomas asked.

"It might," Maria said. "If they're evil—consciously making calamities for their benefit—then exposure and rebellion might stop them. But if they've deceived themselves—truly believing they're helping while actually causing harm—then exposure might not work. They'll twist it. Tell themselves the proof is false, or that their cruelty is justified. A man can swallow any truth when he's made his lies into holy duty."

They sat in silence in the cold barn, turning over what it meant—two thousand years instead of nine hundred.

"Constantinople," Thomas said finally. "We still need the fifth and seventh keys. Whether the network is evil or deluded, we need complete understanding to have any chance of stopping them. Maria, when you're strong enough to travel, we continue the quest."

"I'm strong enough now," Maria lied, though Thomas could see she was barely able to sit upright.

"You're strong enough in three days," Margarethe said firmly. "We rest here. Build up your strength. The Order is behind us but they don't know exactly where we are. Three days won't make difference between escape and capture, but it might make difference between Maria surviving the journey to Constantinople or dying from pushing too hard too soon after serious illness."

Maria looked like she wanted to argue, but another coughing fit stopped her. When it passed, she nodded reluctantly.

They spent three days in the half-collapsed barn. Thomas foraged for food and firewood. Margarethe maintained watch and used the time to repair their equipment and plan the route to Constantinople using Marcus's information. Maria slept and slowly recovered strength.

On the morning of the fourth day, they continued east.

Maria walked on her own power now, though clearly still weak. The fever had passed but left her thinner and more fragile-looking than before.

But she'd also accessed memories deeper than anyone had reached before. Had seen the network's true age and founding purpose. Had glimpsed what might happen when all seven keys came together.

The quest had become more urgent. More complex. More uncertain in what it meant and where it might end.

Behind them, the Order pursued.

Ahead of them, Constantinople waited.

And the truth—whatever it actually was—lay hidden in seven keys scattered across a fragmenting continent.

## Scene 4: Constantinople - Arrival and Contact

### Constantinople, February 1348

Constantinople hit Thomas like a physical force after months in smaller cities and rural landscapes.

The city stretched across hills overlooking the Bosphorus, still enormous despite centuries of upheaval and recent plague devastation. Massive walls that had protected the city for a thousand years. Church domes and palace towers rising against cold February sky. Markets that still functioned despite population reduction, still traded goods from across the known world because geography made Constantinople the crossroads between Europe and Asia.

And everywhere, visible evidence of the plague's passage.

Entire neighborhoods showed signs of abandonment. Buildings stood empty with doors hanging open. Mass graves marked with crosses occupied what had been public squares. The population that remained moved through streets with the particular wariness of survivors who'd watched their neighbors die and weren't confident the dying had stopped.

"How do we find Helena?" Margarethe asked as they entered through the main gates, showing forged travel documents that claimed they were pilgrims seeking to visit holy sites before returning to their homes in the west.

"Marcus said she runs a boarding house near the Augustaion," Thomas recalled. "The great square with old Roman statues. We find that landmark first, then search the area."

Finding the Augustaion proved straightforward—the massive column and plaza were visible from multiple approach angles, ancient Roman monuments still standing after centuries. Finding Helena's boarding house required another hour of wandering through nearby streets, asking merchants and residents in Thomas's limited Greek until someone pointed them to a three-story building with a sign showing a ship and anchor.

The woman who answered their knock looked to be in her fifties, with silver-streaked dark hair and sharp eyes that assessed them with the practiced wariness of someone who'd survived plague times by trusting carefully. She spoke Greek first, switched to Latin when Thomas responded in that language, then finally settled on German when he admitted that was his native tongue.

"Pilgrims?" she asked skeptically, clearly not believing the cover story.

"Seekers," Thomas said, using the countersign Marcus had provided. "Seven keys open seven doors but only truth opens minds."

Helena's expression shifted—not to welcome exactly, but to understanding mixed with resignation. "Come inside quickly. If you're using that phrase, you're either genuine network business or an elaborate Order trick. Either way, discussing it openly on the street is stupidity."

She led them into the boarding house—clean if sparse, showing signs of recent prosperity before plague reduced the traveler population. Up stairs to a private room on the third floor where they could talk without being overheard.

"Who sent you?" Helena asked once the door was secured.

"Marcus the Physician," Thomas said. "From the western side. He gave your name and the countersign."

"Marcus is still alive? I heard he'd died during plague." Helena's expression showed genuine pleasure. "That old fox. Still providing information to both sides while pretending neutrality. What does he want?"

"He wants us to access the hidden library chambers beneath the burned sections of the Great Library," Margarethe said bluntly. "We're seeking the fifth and seventh keys of the Genesis Protocol."

Helena's face went very still. "You're seeking the complete Protocol? Do you understand what you're asking? The network guards those fragments more carefully than gold or military secrets. Trying for them without proper leave means death if you're caught. And I could be killed just for helping you attempt it."

"We understand," Maria said quietly. "But we have the first four keys already. We've seen what they reveal—the temporal patterns, the network's corruption from defensive purpose to offensive plotting, the evidence that Benedict transformed preservation into control. We need the complete Protocol to expose the full scope."

"You're network fugitives," Helena said, understanding dawning. "Not sanctioned seekers. Not here under any seal. You're rebels gathering evidence to challenge the conspiracy itself."

"Yes," Thomas confirmed. "And Marcus believed you might help because you knew the network's history before you joined. Because you believed the preserved founding purpose about helping all humanity rather than selecting survivors."

Helena was silent for a long moment, clearly weighing dangerous possibilities. Finally she spoke:

"I joined the network forty years ago because my father was a scholar who believed in their stated mission—preserve knowledge through catastrophe, help civilizations recognize collapse patterns early enough to prevent them. He died still believing that was the network's actual purpose. I didn't learn about Benedict's corruption until I'd already served for twenty years. By then I had family, position, resources all tied to network membership. Leaving would have meant abandoning everything."

She met Thomas's eyes. "So I stayed. Told myself the corruption was exaggerated or that I could work for reform from inside. Typical rationalizations people use to justify serving powers they know are wrong. But I never stopped feeling guilty about my father dying while believing noble lies."

"Will you help us?" Margarethe asked directly.

"If I do, and you're caught, the network will know I assisted. Will trace the access back to me. Will kill me and possibly my family for treason against an organization that's existed for two thousand years." Helena's voice was steady despite discussing her own potential death. "But if I don't, and you somehow succeed without my help, I'll die knowing I had a chance to honor my father's actual values and chose cowardice instead."

She stood, moved to a locked chest, pulled out a key. "I'll help. But you need to understand the reality of what you're attempting. The underground chambers are guarded. The network doesn't trust a countersign alone—they verify bloodlines, demand seals and letters, and they don't mind spilling blood when someone looks wrong. You'll be walking into defended ground with stolen keys and no official leave."

"We've managed similar situations before," Margarethe said.

"In backwater cities with thin guards," Helena countered. "This is Constantinople—the network's eastern seat. They guard the hidden archives like a fortress. You three—a German monk, a warrior woman, and a sick-looking teenage girl—will look suspicious the moment you approach the underground entrance."

"Then we need disguises," Thomas said. "Sealed papers if possible. Anything that increases our chances of reaching the chambers without immediate combat."

Helena thought for a moment. "I can provide Byzantine network papers that might pass at first glance. Claim you're western scholars granted temporary leave for approved study. But the fifth and seventh keys are in the most restricted sections. Even with papers, you'll face more gates and more guards who assume every stranger is a thief."

"How long do we have once we access the chambers before the guards realize something is wrong?" Margarethe asked.

"Minutes," Helena said. "Maybe fifteen or twenty if you're lucky and guards are complacent. Maybe less than five if you're unlucky and they're on high alert. You'll need to find the keys, take whatever knowledge they hold, and get out before the response overwhelms you."

Maria spoke up: "I can access blood memory faster now. Since the fever, since reaching deeper than I ever have before, the process is quicker. If we can get me to the keys, I can read them in under ten minutes even for complex fragments."

"Then your plan is: Use papers to access the underground chambers, move quickly to the most restricted sections, locate the fifth and seventh keys, Maria reads them as fast as possible, you get out before the guards kill you." Helena's tone made it clear she thought this plan was barely better than suicide. "And if it works, you'll have six of seven keys and need to somehow survive fleeing Constantinople while the network rouses to hunt you."

"We've fled hostile cities before," Thomas said.

"Not like this," Helena warned. "Constantinople isn't Strasbourg or Mainz or Prague. The network is strong here. If they decide to bar the gates and comb the city street by street, they will find you. Your only chance is getting out immediately after accessing the chambers—within hours, before they can organize pursuit."

"Can you arrange ship passage?" Margarethe asked practically. "Something that leaves on short notice, doesn't ask questions about passengers, heads west toward territories we need to reach for the sixth key?"

Helena considered. "Possibly. I have contacts among merchants who owe me favors. But moving you out in haste, without telling them why, will cost hard coin and spend favors I've saved for decades."

"We can pay," Thomas said, though their funds were nearly exhausted.

"It's not about money," Helena said. "It's about risk. Every person I contact, every favor I call in, creates more connections that network investigators can trace back to me if you're caught or if the conspiracy you're building goes public. I'm betting my life and my family's safety on you three fugitives somehow accomplishing what centuries of scattered opposition has failed to achieve."

"Others have bet on us before," Maria said quietly. "Marcus the Physician. Johannes the Alchemist. Brother Hamo. Wilhelm. Rabbi Ezra in Prague. All of them risked or sacrificed everything to help us continue this quest. Not because our success is likely, but because it's possible. Because giving up means the network's corruption continues unchallenged for another thousand years."

Helena looked at the fourteen-year-old girl with ancient eyes, clearly seeing the blood memory and accumulated wisdom beneath the young face.

"My father would have helped you," she said finally. "Would have risked everything to support people fighting for the network's original purpose rather than its corrupted present. I won't dishonor his memory by being less brave than he would have been."

She pulled out more papers—seals, maps, names. "I'll arrange ship passage leaving two days from now. Merchant vessel heading to Italy, willing to take passengers who pay well and don't attract official attention. You have two days to access the underground chambers, read the keys, and get to the docks. After that, you're on your own. I can't protect you once you're back in western territories."

"Two days," Margarethe said, already planning. "Enough time to scout the underground entrance, plan our approach, and strike when conditions are best."

"Or enough time to get discovered during reconnaissance and killed before you ever reach the keys," Helena countered. "But yes, two days is what you have. Use it wisely."

They spent the rest of the day reviewing maps and watch arrangements. Helena had detailed knowledge of the underground chambers' layout—her network position gave her access to information most members never saw. She showed them where the fifth and seventh keys were likely hidden, what guard rotations looked like, what countersigns might help at various gates.

By evening, they had something resembling a plan.

Tomorrow they'd scout the underground entrance, verify Helena's information was current, prepare for the actual break-in.

The day after, they'd either successfully access the fifth and seventh keys and make it to the waiting ship.

Or they'd die trying.

Constantinople had seemed like a distant goal when they started this quest months ago in Germany. Now they were here. Two days from potentially gathering six of seven keys. Two days from understanding most of the Genesis Protocol.

Two days from either the greatest success of their quest or catastrophic failure that would end everything.

Thomas lay in the boarding house room that night, listening to Margarethe and Maria sleeping, thinking about all the people who'd helped them reach this point. Thinking about whether their sacrifices would be honored by success or wasted by failure.

Thinking about two more days.

Then Constantinople's underground chambers and the network's most carefully guarded secrets.

Then westward toward England and the sixth key.

Then—if they survived—understanding complete enough to challenge a conspiracy that had existed for two thousand years.

The quest was approaching its critical phase.

Success or death waited in Constantinople's hidden libraries.

And Thomas wasn't sure which outcome was more likely.
