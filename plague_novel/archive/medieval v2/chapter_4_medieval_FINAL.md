# CHAPTER 4 - THE BISHOP'S FEAST (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: Preparation - Becoming Merchants

### Johannes's Workshop, Morning

Thomas had never worn clothes this fine in his life.

The merchant's outfit Johannes had procured fit surprisingly well—deep blue wool doublet with silver buttons that caught the morning light, linen shirt that actually felt clean and soft against his skin instead of scratchy monastery-woven wool, hose that didn't itch or sag, leather boots that didn't pinch his toes or rub blisters on his heels. He looked at himself in Johannes's cracked mirror, angling to catch the light through the workshop window, and barely recognized the person staring back.

Not a monk hunched over manuscripts. Not a fugitive running for his life. A merchant. Wealthy, confident, the kind of man who'd been personally invited to dine with bishops and discuss trade routes and profit margins.

"Stop fidgeting," Johannes said, appearing behind him to adjust Thomas's collar with practiced efficiency. "Merchants don't fidget. They appraise. They calculate value. They know their worth and expect others to recognize it."

"I look ridiculous," Thomas muttered, tugging at the doublet that felt too tight across the shoulders.

"You look prosperous. There's a difference." Johannes stepped back, examined his work with a critical eye. "The key is in the posture. Shoulders back—yes, like that. Chin up slightly. Not arrogant, just... confident. Like you've closed three profitable deals this week and expect to close three more tomorrow. Like money flows through your hands the way prayer flows through a priest's."

Thomas tried. Pulled his shoulders back. Lifted his chin. Tried to project wealth and confidence.

Failed. Slouched back into his natural posture of someone who'd spent years hunched over manuscripts in cold scriptoriums.

"This is hopeless," he muttered. "I look like a monk playing dress-up."

"It's not hopeless. It's just unfamiliar." Margarethe's voice came from the stairs, amused. "You'll adapt. You always do."

Thomas turned toward her voice—and stopped breathing.

Margarethe descended the stairs in a merchant's wife costume that transformed her so completely that for a moment Thomas thought Johannes had brought in someone else. Deep green dress with gold embroidery at the collar and cuffs, the fabric expensive enough that it caught the light with a subtle sheen. Her hair was actually styled instead of roughly tied back—braided and coiled and pinned, with a delicate veil that suggested wealth without ostentation. Her wounded shoulder was carefully concealed under the dress's construction, no sign of the bandages Thomas knew she wore beneath.

She moved differently too. Not the fighter's prowl Thomas was used to, the constant readiness for violence. Something more measured, graceful, deliberate. The walk of a woman who'd been raised with wealth and refinement.

She looked beautiful. Dangerous and beautiful and completely unlike the Margarethe who'd pulled an arrow from her own shoulder and killed men without hesitation.

"Close your mouth," she said with the ghost of a smile playing at her lips. "Merchants don't gape at their own wives."

"Wives?"

"You didn't think we'd attend separately?" Johannes was grinning, clearly pleased with his costuming work. "A wealthy merchant and his wife, fleeing the plague in Mainz, seeking the bishop's blessing for safe passage to their holdings in Italy. It's a perfect cover. Sympathetic. Believable. The kind of story everyone's hearing these days."

Thomas forced himself to think practically, to push past the distraction of Margarethe looking like she'd stepped out of a nobleman's portrait. "What if someone questions our story? Asks for details we don't know?"

"Then you improvise using details you actually know." Johannes handed him a leather purse heavy with coins. Real coins—Thomas could feel the weight, hear the clink of silver and gold. "You grew up near Mainz, you understand the wool trade from monastery dealings, you're educated enough to discuss theology if the bishop tests you. Use truth whenever possible. It's easier to remember and harder to catch in lies."

He demonstrated how to hold the purse. "Don't flash this around, but let it show occasionally. Wealthy people are casual about their wealth. Poor people pretending to be wealthy are too careful with money. They count coins. They worry about spending. You? You expect to have money. Always have. Always will."

Margarethe took the purse from Thomas, secured it to her belt with practiced efficiency. "I'll handle the money. Wives often do—it's expected that a man too busy with trade to worry about household expenses would let his wife manage coins. And I can read people better than Thomas can."

"I can read people," Thomas protested.

"You can read manuscripts. You're terrible with people. You think everyone is honest because you're honest. You miss deception because you can't imagine lying yourself." She touched his arm, softening the criticism. "It's not a weakness. It's just not useful tonight when we need to lie convincingly to a room full of people who lie professionally."

Johannes cleared the table with a sweep of his arm, sending half-finished alchemical experiments and scattered notes to the floor. He spread out his drawings of the cathedral one final time. "Review the plan. One more time. Everything must be perfect. Even small mistakes can turn into ruin."

Thomas studied the layout, committing it to memory the way he'd memorized hundreds of manuscripts. St. Thomas Cathedral dominated central Strasbourg—massive Gothic structure with twin towers visible from miles away, side chapels clustered like children around a mother, and the bishop's private residence attached to the north transept like a fortified wing. The treasury was in the residence basement, protected by guards and locks and the bishop's paranoia about theft during times of plague and chaos.

"The feast begins at sunset," Johannes said, tracing the route with his finger, nail scratching against the parchment. "You'll arrive fashionably late—wealthy people always do, shows they're not desperate for the bishop's favor. Enter through the main doors, present your invitations to the bishop's steward. He'll show you to the great hall where the feast is being held."

"How many guests?" Margarethe asked, leaning over the drawing with sharp focus.

"Forty, maybe fifty. Clergy from across the region—diocese heads, abbot from the nearby monastery, visiting theologians. Wealthy merchants grateful to be alive and wanting to show piety. Minor nobility trying to curry favor." Johannes moved his finger across the drawing. "Enough that two strangers won't stand out, but not so many that you'll get lost in the crowd or that the steward won't know who belongs."

"How generous will the bishop expect people to be?" Thomas asked, remembering monastery politics and the constant pressure for donations.

"Very. He'll be soliciting donations for cathedral repairs, plague relief, prayers for the dead. This is actually useful—everyone will be focused on looking pious and wealthy simultaneously. Perfect distraction. They'll be watching each other, not watching you."

Johannes tapped the treasury location with emphasis. "You'll need to slip away during the third course. That's when the entertainment begins—musicians, possibly acrobats or a performing bear if the bishop is feeling generous. Maximum distraction, minimum attention on individual guests. That's your window."

Margarethe leaned closer, studying the route with the focus of a general planning an assault. "The treasury is here, basement level. How do we reach it without being seen by servants or guards?"

"Servants' corridors." Johannes traced a path with his finger, showing turns and stairs. "Exit the great hall through the side door—excuse yourselves to visit the privy, perfectly reasonable, people do it constantly during long feasts. Turn left instead of right. Down this corridor, through the kitchen annex—it'll be absolute chaos during the feast, servants running everywhere, no one will notice two more people moving through. Down the stairs to the basement level. The treasury is the third door on the left."

"Guards?" Thomas asked, dreading the answer.

"Two. Stationed outside the treasury door during the feast—the bishop doesn't trust even his own household when there are strangers in the building. Can't blame him. These are plague times. Desperation makes thieves of everyone." Johannes smiled grimly. "But they rotate every hour. Three minutes during rotation when the hallway is empty. You'll need to be fast."

Thomas looked at the lock drawing Johannes had made—detailed sketch showing the internal mechanism of what Johannes called a Bavarian design. Complex, expensive, supposedly unpickable by anyone without the original key or specialized training. Johannes had spent all of yesterday teaching Thomas how it worked, making him practice on a replica until his fingers ached and his eyes blurred from concentration.

"Two minutes to pick the lock," Thomas said, reviewing the timing they'd rehearsed. "Two minutes inside to locate the Silver Key among whatever other valuables are stored there. One minute to lock it again from the outside and disappear before the new guards arrive."

"Five minutes total," Johannes confirmed. "Can you do it?"

Thomas's hands were already sweating just thinking about it. "I don't know."

"Yes, you do. You picked my practice lock in ninety seconds this morning. Seventy-five seconds on your best attempt yesterday afternoon." Johannes gripped Thomas's shoulder with surprising strength. "You're better at this than you think. You see patterns. Locks are just metal patterns that need to be understood. Trust your training. Trust your gift."

Margarethe caught Thomas's eye, held his gaze. "I'll watch the corridor. Keep you aware of timing. You focus only on the lock. Nothing else exists. Just you and the mechanism."

"What about you?" Thomas asked Johannes. "What's your role during all this?"

"I'll be at the feast. Separate from you—we can't appear to know each other, can't even acknowledge each other's presence. I'll be playing the eccentric scholar invited to discuss Greek medical texts with the bishop's visiting theologian from Paris." Johannes gestured to a pile of books. "It gives me an excuse to wander the hall looking for the bishop's library, to create diversions if needed by starting theological arguments, and to signal if anything goes wrong."

"What's the signal?"

"If I spill wine on myself—loudly, dramatically, making a scene—abort immediately. Something's wrong, guards are moving unexpectedly, get back to the feast through whatever route is safest. Don't worry about the lock. Don't worry about the key. Just survive." Johannes rolled up the drawings carefully. "But it won't come to that. This plan is solid. I've been refining it for two years."

Thomas wasn't so sure. Plans sounded solid until they collided with reality. Then everything went wrong in ways you couldn't predict.

But he didn't say that. Just nodded and tried to project the confidence Johannes and Margarethe seemed to expect from him.

"One more thing," Johannes said, his voice dropping, becoming serious in a way that made Thomas's stomach clench. "If this goes wrong, if you're caught, you don't know me. You're just thieves who heard about the treasury and tried your luck. You don't mention the cipher, the network, the keys, Wilhelm, Margarethe's father, none of it. Nothing that puts the rest of us at risk."

"And if they torture us?" Margarethe asked bluntly. Always direct. Always practical.

"Then you resist as long as you can. Give us time to disappear, to hide evidence, to warn others. But eventually, everyone breaks. Torture breaks everyone given enough time and enough pain. So we make sure it doesn't come to that by not getting caught." Johannes looked at both of them, his young face suddenly old with the weight of what they were attempting. "Are you ready?"

Thomas looked at Margarethe. She looked at him.

Neither of them were ready. But they were out of time. Out of options. Out of everywhere to run.

"Yes," Thomas lied.

"Good," Johannes said. "Then let's go steal from a bishop."

## Scene 2: The Feast - Playing the Part

### St. Thomas Cathedral, Evening

The great hall was overwhelming in a way Thomas's monastery experience had never prepared him for.

He'd attended monastery feasts before—simple affairs with long trestle tables and plain food served on wooden trenchers, weak beer and watered wine, the abbot saying grace while everyone's stomach growled with hunger. Austere. Practical. Focused on sustenance rather than display.

This was different. This was wealth and power made visible, tangible, impossible to ignore.

The hall soared three stories high, ribbed vaulting overhead painted with gold leaf that caught and multiplied the light from hundreds of candles burning in iron chandeliers. Tapestries covered the walls—hunting scenes showing nobles on horseback chasing stags through forests, biblical tableaux depicting miracles and martyrdoms, heraldic devices of noble families rendered in threads that must have cost more than a peasant family earned in a lifetime.

The tables were covered in white linen so fine and clean it looked like snow. Set with silver plates that reflected candlelight, crystal goblets from Venetian glassmakers, knives with handles of carved ivory and bone.

And the food. God above, the food.

Roasted swan served in its own feathers, gilded beak gleaming. Venison in wine sauce thick enough to stand a spoon in. Pike in aspic shaped to look like the fish was swimming. Peacock re-dressed in its own plumage and positioned as if alive. Fruits Thomas had never seen—oranges from Spain, figs from the Mediterranean, dates from the Holy Land. Breads sculpted into elaborate shapes—castles and crowns and crosses. Wine that tasted like liquid gold, sweet and strong and nothing like the vinegar the monastery served.

All this while plague ravaged the countryside outside. While bodies burned in mass graves. While the poor starved and the sick died alone in their hovels.

"Stop staring," Margarethe murmured beside him, her hand light on his arm in a gesture that looked affectionate but felt like a warning. "Merchants are used to wealth. You look like a peasant at a king's table."

Thomas forced himself to relax. Took a measured drink of wine—not gulping despite the fact that it was the best he'd ever tasted. Tried to appear bored by the opulence surrounding him, like this was normal, expected, barely worth noticing.

They'd been seated near the middle of the hall—not close enough to the bishop's high table to draw intense scrutiny, but not so far away as to seem unimportant or suspect. Perfect positioning. Johannes had clearly bribed someone to arrange the seating in exactly this position.

Speaking of Johannes—Thomas spotted him three tables away, deep in animated conversation with an elderly priest who kept nodding and laughing. The alchemist looked completely at ease, gesturing enthusiastically about something theological, playing his role of eccentric scholar perfectly. Wine glass in hand, expensive but not ostentatious clothes, the picture of a man comfortable in his own intelligence.

The bishop himself sat at the high table under a carved canopy, resplendent in purple robes that probably cost more than most people earned in a year. Heinrich Vogel was in his fifties, with a florid face that suggested he enjoyed feasts like this very regularly. Soft hands, expensive rings, the satisfied look of a man who'd never gone hungry or cold or fearful. He was currently listening to a nobleman's hunting story, laughing at appropriate moments, playing the generous host who found everyone fascinating.

Somewhere in his private treasury, beneath this very building, beneath all this wealth and luxury and carefully maintained power, the Silver Key waited.

"Third course soon," Margarethe said quietly, her voice barely audible under the noise of conversation and music. She'd been tracking the meal progression with the focused intensity of a general planning a campaign. "The entertainment will begin. Acrobats, I think—I saw them being positioned in the side chamber. That's when we move."

"I know the plan," Thomas said, trying to sound confident.

"I'm reminding you because you're nervous. When you're nervous, you forget things. You overthink. You doubt yourself." She took a delicate bite of venison, chewed, swallowed. Perfect manners. "Just follow the plan. Trust your training. We'll be fine."

She was right. He was nervous. His hands were shaking slightly under the table. He hid them in his lap, pressed them against his thighs to stop the trembling.

A merchant across from them—portly man named Dietrich who owned vineyards south of Strasbourg—was telling a story about escaping Mainz just before the plague really took hold. His voice dropped to a dramatic whisper that somehow still carried across the table. "The plague bells were ringing day and night. Bodies in the streets, piled like cordwood waiting to be burned. The Order's Gray Robes going house to house, burning buildings where the sick were found. No trials. No mercy. Just fire and death. It was the judgment of God made manifest. The end of days approaching."

"Terrible times," Thomas agreed, because that seemed safe and neutral.

"And you escaped when?" Dietrich asked, suddenly focused on Thomas with wine-sharpened intensity. "When did you leave Mainz?"

Thomas's mind went completely blank. When had they supposedly escaped? What date had Johannes told them to use? The timeline was important, needed to match what people would know about when the plague hit Mainz, when the gates closed, when—

"Three weeks ago," Margarethe said smoothly, not missing a beat. "Just before the river crossings closed. My husband had already heard rumors from his trading contacts. We saw what was coming. Packed what we could carry. Lost half our goods in the rush—looters, fires, the Order confiscating entire warehouses. My husband's warehouse in the Mühlenstadt quarter—burned by the Order as a plague precaution. Everything gone. Forty years of built-up inventory. Gone in an afternoon."

She was lying perfectly. Every detail specific. Emotion in her voice—bitterness, loss, fear—but not overdone. Just the right amount to be believable. The performance of someone who'd actually lived through it.

Dietrich nodded sympathetically, face softening. "I heard the Mühlenstadt quarter was hit particularly hard. Half the district burned. You were fortunate to escape with your lives."

"Fortune favors those who don't hesitate," Margarethe said, taking a sip of wine. "We saw the signs, we acted immediately. Didn't wait for official warnings or orders. Those who waited..." She shrugged eloquently. "Well. You can see the results. The roads are full of refugees now. But the smart ones left early."

The conversation moved on to other topics—trade routes disrupted by plague, whether it was safe to travel to Venice, speculation about whether the pope in Avignon would call for a crusade against the pestilence.

Thomas breathed again.

"That was close," he murmured when Dietrich's attention shifted to another guest.

"You froze. Don't freeze again when it matters." But Margarethe's hand found his under the table, squeezed once. Reassurance, not just criticism. She understood the fear even as she pushed him past it.

The third course arrived with ceremonial flourish—servants carrying the swan on a massive silver platter, the bird gilded and re-feathered and positioned as if taking flight. The crowd applauded. The bishop stood to say a blessing that was more about his own piety than gratitude to God.

And with the swan, the entertainment.

Musicians entered the hall—lute, viol, tambourine, creating complex interwoven melodies. A juggler in motley colored clothing began tossing knives and torches with casual skill. Two acrobats in tight leather garments began performing impossible feats of balance and strength, building human pyramids, flipping through the air, drawing gasps and applause.

Everyone's attention shifted to the entertainment. Even the guards at the hall entrances were watching, distracted by the spectacle.

"Now," Margarethe breathed, so quiet Thomas almost didn't hear it.

They stood. Casual, unhurried, no sudden movements. Thomas caught Johannes's eye across the hall. The alchemist gave the smallest nod—so subtle anyone else would have missed it.

Go. May God protect you.

They walked toward the side door. Slow, relaxed, just a couple excusing themselves during the entertainment. No one paid attention. Everyone was watching acrobats flip through the air.

Through the door. Into the corridor beyond, sudden quiet after the noise of the hall.

Left instead of right.

And suddenly they were out of the great hall, in the servants' passages, and the real work began.

## Scene 3: The Heist - The Bavarian Lock

### Cathedral Basement, Treasury Corridor

The kitchen annex was controlled chaos exactly as Johannes had predicted.

Cooks shouting orders in German and French and kitchen Latin, the universal language of culinary command. Servants rushing between the kitchens and the great hall with platters of food and ewers of wine, their faces flushed from heat and exertion. Steam and heat rolling out from ovens and cooking fires. The smell of roasting meat and bread and spices, overlaid with the sharp tang of sweat and smoke.

No one gave Thomas and Margarethe a second glance as they moved through, just two more bodies in expensive clothes navigating the controlled madness. Servants assumed they were lost guests looking for the privy. Kitchen staff was too busy to care.

Down the stairs. Stone steps worn smooth and slightly concave from centuries of use, the center of each step lower than the edges from countless feet treading the same path. Temperature dropping with each step. Sound of the feast fading behind them, replaced by silence and the drip of moisture on stone.

The basement corridor was quiet. Empty. Torches in wall brackets providing flickering yellow light that created moving shadows.

And at the end of the corridor, two guards stationed outside a heavy oak door reinforced with iron bands.

Thomas and Margarethe pressed themselves into a side alcove—storage niche or doorway to some disused chamber. Watched. Waited.

"Guard rotation?" Thomas whispered, barely moving his lips.

Margarethe was already counting in her head—she'd been tracking time since they entered the cathedral, maintaining an internal clock the way some people could sense direction. "Four minutes. Maybe five if they're being casual about the timing."

They waited. Thomas's heart hammered so loud he was sure the guards would hear it echoing down the stone corridor. His palms were sweating. His mouth was dry despite the wine he'd drunk at the feast.

Three minutes.

Four minutes.

At five minutes, footsteps on the stairs. Heavy boots on stone. The new guards arriving for their rotation.

Brief conversation, voices carrying in the quiet corridor. "Anything?"

"Nothing. Dead quiet down here."

"The bishop's feast always is. Everyone's too busy stuffing their faces to think about stealing."

Laughter. Good-natured complaining about being stuck on guard duty during a feast while others enjoyed the entertainment and food.

Then the original guards headed up the stairs. The new guards settled into position outside the treasury door, settling their weight, adjusting their sword belts, preparing for an hour of boredom.

"Damn," Thomas breathed. "They're already here. We missed the window between rotations."

"Wait," Margarethe said.

One of the new guards pulled out a wineskin—leather, probably containing something stronger than the watered wine served at the feast. Shared it with his companion. Said something Thomas couldn't quite hear. Both laughed.

Then both guards walked down the corridor. Away from the treasury. Toward what looked like a servants' privy at the far end.

"They're abandoning their post?" Thomas whispered, disbelieving.

"Drinking on duty. Can't be seen drinking right in front of the treasury door where a passing steward might notice. They'll relieve themselves, pass the wine, complain about duty. Be back in two, three minutes." Margarethe pushed him forward urgently. "Go. Now. This is the only chance we'll get."

Thomas ran. Light-footed, quiet, everything Margarethe had drilled into him about moving without sound during their training sessions. Stay on the balls of your feet. Don't let your heels strike. Roll your weight. Breathe through your nose.

Reached the treasury door.

Heavy oak, iron reinforcements in decorative patterns, and in the center at waist height: the lock.

Bavarian mechanism. Beautiful and deadly and supposedly unpickable.

Thomas pulled his picks from his sleeve—thin metal tools Johannes had forged specifically for this, hardened steel shaped to manipulate pins and cylinders. Knelt before the lock. Inserted the first pick, feeling for the pins with the sensitivity he usually reserved for reading damaged manuscripts.

The Bavarian lock used multiple rotating cylinders, each with different pin configurations that had to be set in exact sequence. You had to manipulate them one at a time, maintaining tension on each while rotating to the next. One mistake, one slip, one moment of lost concentration and everything reset to the beginning.

Thomas closed his eyes. Shut out the corridor, the guards somewhere in the distance, the fear racing through his veins. Focused entirely on the lock.

This was just like the manuscripts. Patterns. Sequences. Each click was a letter in a word he was learning to read. Each cylinder was a line of text. The entire lock was a page he had to decode.

First cylinder. He felt the pins with the pick—delicate pressure, listening with his fingers for the subtle shift when they aligned. Applied tension with his other pick. Rotated.

Click.

The sound was barely audible, but to Thomas it was as clear as a church bell.

Second cylinder. More complex. Five pins instead of three. Different sizes, requiring different amounts of pressure. He worked carefully, patient despite the adrenaline screaming at him to hurry. One pin at a time. Feel. Adjust. Set.

Click.

Behind him, Margarethe's urgent whisper: "One minute."

Third cylinder. This one was tricky—the pins were uneven sizes, one tiny pin hidden between two larger ones that could trap the pick if he wasn't careful. Thomas's hands were sweating. He wiped them quickly on his doublet, started again.

One pin. Two pins. Three pins—there was the small one, disguised between the others—

"Thirty seconds."

Fourth pin. Fifth pin. Come on, come on, align, please—

Click.

The lock opened.

Thomas pulled the heavy door. It swung inward on hinges that had been oiled recently, silent and smooth.

"I'm in."

"Twenty seconds. Hurry."

Thomas slipped inside the treasury, leaving the door ajar so Margarethe could warn him.

The treasury was smaller than he expected—maybe ten feet square. Shelves lined the walls from floor to ceiling, covered with church valuables that represented generations of donations and tithes. Gold chalices encrusted with gems. Jeweled crosses that caught the torchlight from the corridor and threw rainbow patterns. Illuminated manuscripts in protective leather cases. Silver reliquaries holding fragments of saints' bones—a finger bone of Saint Thomas, a piece of skull from some martyr, a vial supposedly containing blood from a miraculous healing.

And there, on the center shelf at eye level, displayed more prominently than anything else: a key.

Not like the Iron Key Thomas carried hidden in his doublet. This one was silver, gleaming even in the dim light. Ornately carved with a level of detail that must have taken months of work. The symbol on the head was different—a hand reaching upward, fingers spread wide, palm inscribed with tiny markings.

The Hand that Guides.

Thomas grabbed it. Felt the weight—heavier than the Iron Key, made from denser metal. The craftsmanship was extraordinary. Each finger on the carved hand was fully articulated with visible joints and knuckles. The palm was inscribed with symbols Thomas couldn't read in this light—cipher marks or something older, maybe both.

Outside, Margarethe's voice sharp with controlled panic: "Guards coming back. Thirty seconds. Get out now."

Thomas ran to the door. Pulled it closed behind him. His picks were still in the lock mechanism inside—no time to retrieve them without risk.

The lock. He had to lock it again from the outside or they'd know immediately it had been breached.

But Bavarian locks were designed to prevent exactly that. Couldn't be re-locked from the outside without the original key or specialized tools. Tools Johannes hadn't been able to make, hadn't even known existed until recently.

"Thomas!" Margarethe's voice was sharp with real fear now, not just urgency.

"I can't lock it from out here!"

"Leave it. Move now!"

Thomas dove into the alcove beside Margarethe just as voices echoed from the far end of the corridor. The guards returning, laughing about something, the wineskin audibly sloshing.

They settled back into position outside the treasury, adjusting their sword belts, complaining good-naturedly about the cold in the basement.

Without checking the door.

Thank God. Thank every saint in heaven and all the angels. They hadn't noticed.

But eventually someone would. The next rotation in an hour, or when the feast ended and the steward made his rounds, or when the bishop decided to show a visitor his treasury's treasures. The unlocked door would be discovered. The missing key would be noticed.

They had hours at most. Maybe less if the guards got suspicious or bored and actually tested the door.

Margarethe pulled Thomas deeper into the shadows, back toward the stairs, moving with absolute silence. They climbed slowly, every step careful to avoid any sound that might make the guards investigate.

Up the stairs. Through the kitchen annex—still chaotic, still no one caring about two more bodies in expensive clothes.

Into the corridor leading back toward the great hall. Toward the feast. Toward safety and cover and—

A hand grabbed Thomas's shoulder from behind.

He spun, heart stopping, reaching for a knife he didn't have.

Johannes. The alchemist's face was pale, urgent, fear in his eyes for the first time since Thomas had met him.

"We have a problem," Johannes whispered, his voice tight with controlled panic. "Don't go back to the feast. There are Gray Robes in the hall. Three of them. Asking questions about strangers from Mainz. Checking invitations against names. Looking for discrepancies."

Thomas felt cold wash through him. "How did they find us? We used false names. You said the invitations were perfect."

"They are perfect. I don't know how they traced us. Maybe someone at my workshop before it was raided. Maybe the Order has wormed into the bishop's household. It doesn't matter. We need to leave. Now. Before they start questioning guests one by one and our false names don't hold up."

"The Silver Key—" Thomas started, hand moving unconsciously to his doublet where he'd hidden it.

"Do you have it?"

Thomas nodded.

"Then we go. Forget the feast. Forget the cover story. Follow me. Servants' exit. Quickly and quietly."

They followed Johannes through a maze of corridors Thomas had never seen, servants' passages that wound through the cathedral like the intestines of some great stone beast. Behind them, shouts echoed. Running feet. The alarm had been raised.

Someone had discovered the treasury.

"Run," Johannes said, abandoning all pretense of stealth. And they ran.

## Scene 4: The Escape - Johannes's Sacrifice

### Strasbourg Streets, Night

They burst out of the cathedral through a servants' entrance into a narrow alley that stank of kitchen refuse and human waste, the smell hitting Thomas like a physical blow after the perfumed air of the feast. Rain was falling—cold autumn rain that soaked through Thomas's fine merchant clothes in seconds, the expensive fabric offering no more protection than monastery wool.

"This way!" Johannes led them through the maze of streets surrounding the cathedral, his knowledge of Strasbourg proving invaluable. Behind them, cathedral bells started ringing. Alarm bells. Search bells. The entire cathedral guard would be rousing, spreading out through the city, searching for thieves who'd violated the bishop's treasury.

"Where are we going?" Thomas gasped, lungs burning, side aching from running in clothes not designed for it.

"Safe house. Tanner's quarter. Not far if we can avoid the patrols."

They ran through rain-slicked streets, water streaming down from roofs and gurgling in gutters. Around corners sharp enough that Thomas nearly fell, catching himself on rain-wet stone walls. Through passages so narrow his shoulders brushed both sides, his expensive doublet collecting grime from centuries-old stone.

Margarethe kept pace despite her wounded shoulder, her face set in grim determination, breathing controlled despite the exertion. The merchant's wife costume was already ruined—hem soaked and muddy, veil torn away somewhere in the flight.

Behind them, torches bobbed in the darkness. Shouts echoed between buildings. The pursuit was organized, coordinated, disciplined. Cathedral guards who knew the city better than Thomas did, who had numbers and weapons and local authority.

"They're too close," Margarethe said, glancing back. "We need to split up. Draw them in different directions."

"No," Thomas and Johannes said simultaneously, both immediately rejecting the idea.

"Yes." Margarethe grabbed Thomas's arm, thrust him physically toward Johannes. "You have both keys. You're what matters. The Iron Key and the Silver Key together—that's the objective. That's what Wilhelm died for. What my father died for. I'll draw them off, lose them in the tanners' quarter. I know this city better than they do. I know every alley, every dead end, every hidden passage."

"Margarethe—" Thomas tried to pull away, to go back for her.

"Don't argue. We don't have time." She was already moving, shouting in German to draw attention, making herself visible and loud. "Hey! Over here! Follow me if you want the thieves!"

The Gray Robes turned toward her voice. Three of them in their distinctive robes, moving with coordinated efficiency. Closing in on her position.

"No!" Thomas tried to go back, to help, to not leave her alone against armed men.

Johannes grabbed him with surprising strength, pulled him in the opposite direction. "She gave us a chance. Don't waste it by getting captured trying to save her. She knows what she's doing. Trust her."

They ran. Thomas looked back once, saw Margarethe disappearing into shadows, the Gray Robes pursuing her like hounds after a fox.

Then Johannes pulled him around a corner and she was gone, swallowed by darkness and rain and the labyrinth of Strasbourg's streets.

They ran for ten more minutes. Through the tanners' quarter—the smell was overwhelming even in the rain, tanning lye and animal decay and the particular stench of worked hides. Across a small plaza, empty at this hour except for a drunk sleeping in a doorway. Down an alley barely wide enough for one person, Thomas's shoulders scraping both walls.

Finally, Johannes stopped at a nondescript door that looked like a dozen others on the street. Knocked a specific pattern—three fast, pause, two slow, pause, one fast. A code.

The door opened immediately. They tumbled inside, soaked and exhausted and breathing hard.

A woman stood in the doorway—middle-aged, stern-looking, with the practical clothing of someone who worked for a living. She locked the door behind them immediately, threw multiple bolts with practiced efficiency. "Trouble?"

"Cathedral guards. Gray Robes. They'll search the quarter methodically but they won't find us here. This location isn't in any network records they could have captured." Johannes was breathing hard, water streaming from his hair and clothes, creating puddles on the floor. "Are you hurt?" he asked Thomas.

"No. But Margarethe—"

"Margarethe knows how to disappear. She's better at this than either of us. She'll lose them and make her way here when it's safe." Johannes sounded confident, but Thomas heard the worry underneath, the fear that he was wrong.

They waited in the safe house, a modest building that felt more like a working-class home than a hiding place. The stern woman—she never gave her name—brought them dry clothes, towels, bread and cheese.

Thomas paced, unable to sit, unable to relax. Changed into dry clothes mechanically, his mind elsewhere. Imagining Margarethe cornered, captured, interrogated. Tortured. Killed.

Johannes sat still, conserving energy, thinking through next steps with visible concentration.

An hour passed. Two hours.

No sign of Margarethe.

Thomas pulled out the Silver Key, turned it over in his hands with trembling fingers. The Hand that Guides. Beautifully crafted. Heavy. Valuable.

Was this worth it? Was any of this worth Margarethe's life?

At three hours, there was a knock at the door. The same pattern Johannes had used—three fast, pause, two slow, pause, one fast.

The stern woman opened it cautiously, ready to slam it shut again if necessary.

Margarethe stumbled in, soaked to the bone, bleeding from a cut on her forehead that had already started to clot, but alive. Gloriously, impossibly alive.

"Lost them," she gasped, leaning against the doorframe. "Doubled back four times through the tanneries. They gave up after the second hour. Decided I wasn't worth catching in the rain."

Thomas caught her before she fell, supporting her weight. "You're hurt."

"I'm alive. That's what counts. Head wounds bleed more than they hurt." She looked past him to Johannes. "Did we get it? Tell me we got the damned key."

Thomas held up the Silver Key, its hand symbol catching the firelight.

Margarethe smiled despite the blood and exhaustion and ruined clothes. "Then it was worth it."

But before anyone could celebrate or even properly tend Margarethe's wound, there was another knock at the door.

Different pattern. Urgent and random, no code. Just desperate pounding.

The stern woman opened the door a crack, ready to slam it shut if needed. A young man stood outside, rain-soaked, terrified, out of breath.

"Message for Johannes the Alchemist. Urgent. The Gray Robes raided your workshop an hour ago. They're looking for you specifically. They know you were at the feast. They're connecting you to the theft."

Johannes's face went pale. "My work. Everything I've done—"

"Burned," the young man said, his voice apologetic. "I'm sorry. I tried to warn you sooner but they had the street blocked. They torched everything. Your books, your equipment, your notes. All of it. Gone."

For a moment, Johannes looked lost. Devastated. His life's work destroyed in an afternoon. Years of work, rare books, irreplaceable notes. All burned because he'd helped them steal a key.

Then he straightened, visibly gathering himself. "It doesn't matter. The knowledge is in here." He tapped his head with emphasis. "They can burn buildings and books but they can't burn what I've already learned. What I've already memorized. The network taught us that generations ago—knowledge in the mind survives when everything else burns."

"There's more," the young man said, clearly reluctant to deliver worse news. "They have the guest lists from the feast. The bishop's steward is going through them methodically, looking for discrepancies. Looking for the couple from Mainz who disappeared during the third course. They're asking questions. Checking names against city records."

Thomas felt sick. "They'll figure out our names were false."

"Not immediately. I forged those invitations perfectly. But eventually, yes. Within a day, maybe two at most. They'll realize no merchant named Thomas of Mainz exists in any guild records." Johannes looked at the Silver Key Thomas still held. "We need to leave Strasbourg. Tonight. But first, we use what we risked everything to obtain."

He spread a manuscript on the table—one of the texts from his workshop, saved because he'd brought it to the safe house days ago for safekeeping. Marginal cipher dots covered the pages, barely visible in the firelight.

"The Iron Key alone showed us locations," Johannes said, his alchemist's mind already working despite the disaster of the evening. "Single points of information scattered across Europe. Let's see what two keys together reveal that one alone couldn't."

Thomas placed the Iron Key on the manuscript first, his hands steadier now with purpose. The eye symbol aligned with the text. Cipher dots appeared through the aperture, exactly as they had countless times before. Location markers. Knowledge bearings.

Then Johannes placed the Silver Key beside it, positioning the hand symbol to overlap slightly with the eye.

And the pattern changed.

The dots didn't just show locations anymore. They showed connections—lines between locations, visible only when both keys were present, only when the Eye that Sees and the Hand that Guides worked together. A web spread across the page, transforming the manuscript from a simple text into a map of information flow.

"My God," Johannes breathed, his mind racing to grasp it. "It's not just marking where knowledge is hidden across Christendom. It's showing how it's tied together. How word and parchment pass through the network. How knowledge goes from hand to hand, generation to generation."

Thomas saw it with the clarity that came from years of reading manuscripts. Each location was a knot in a vast web. Each connection a path for knowledge. The entire manuscript became a map of how learning was scattered across Europe, showing not just what was preserved but how it was passed along.

"And look here." Johannes pointed to a cluster of connections with trembling fingers. "These aren't just knowledge repositories like monasteries or libraries. These are families. Bloodlines. See—this mark indicates a family line. This one indicates a strategic marriage. This one indicates offspring who carry specific traits."

Margarethe leaned closer despite her exhaustion, her quick mind immediately grasping the implications. "They're tracking genealogies? Like breeding horses or hunting dogs?"

"Not just tracking. Guiding. Orchestrating." Johannes traced the connections with growing horror and fascination. "Look—this family in Cologne produces children with the same gift Wilhelm had—an eye for patterns. That family in Vienna has children who remember whole pages at a glance for three generations. The network arranges a marriage between the bloodlines. Three generations later, their descendants have both gifts combined."

Thomas felt cold understanding spreading through him like winter ice. "They're not just preserving knowledge in books and hiding places. They're breeding people to carry it. Creating living libraries who can hold information that can't be safely written down."

"For forty generations," Johannes confirmed, pointing to date markers in the margins that showed the tracking extended back centuries. "Creating living vessels for knowledge. People who can remember things perfectly. People who can see patterns others miss. People who can survive with the information humanity needs to rebuild after the next collapse."

The room was silent except for rain drumming on the roof and the crackle of the fire. The magnitude of what they were seeing slowly settling over all of them.

"That's what Wilhelm was part of," Thomas said quietly, his voice hollow. "Not just hiding manuscripts in clever places. Creating people who could BE manuscripts. Who could carry the knowledge in their blood and bones and pass it to their children."

"And the Order knows," Margarethe said, her voice hard. "That's why they hunt us so viciously. We're not just carrying forbidden knowledge in bags and boxes. We are forbidden knowledge. Our existence is the crime. Our breeding is the heresy."

Before anyone could respond, before anyone could fully process the horror of what they'd discovered, there was pounding on the door. Not a polite knock. Not even an urgent knock. A demand.

"Open! In the name of the bishop and the Holy Church! Open or we break this door down!"

The stern woman looked at Johannes with terror in her eyes. "The Gray Robes found us. How? This safe house has been secure for—"

"It doesn't matter how. Back exit. Now." Johannes grabbed the manuscripts, thrust them at Thomas with desperate urgency. "Take these. Both keys. Run. Don't stop running until you're miles outside the city walls."

"What about you?" Thomas asked, though he already knew the answer.

"I'll delay them. Buy you time. Give you a chance to escape." Johannes was already moving to the front of the safe house, positioning himself between the door and Thomas. "Go. Now."

Margarethe pulled Thomas toward the back of the house, her grip iron-strong on his arm. "He's right. We have to go. The keys matter more than any of us."

"I'm not leaving him to die!"

"You don't have a choice! The knowledge dies if we're all captured!"

The front door shattered inward, wood splintering. Gray Robes poured in—six of them, armed, disciplined, moving with coordinated efficiency. Thomas caught a final glimpse of Johannes facing them, unarmed but unbowed, buying precious seconds with his words and his courage.

Then Margarethe dragged Thomas out the back exit into the rain-soaked night, and they were running again, and Johannes was behind them—captured or dead or worse—and all Thomas could think was: *This is my fault. All of this. Every death. Every sacrifice. Every horror. My fault.*

They ran until dawn, until their lungs burned and their legs gave out and they collapsed in an abandoned woodcutter's cottage five miles outside Strasbourg's walls.

And behind them, the city bells continued ringing. Searching. Hunting.

Never stopping.

## Scene 5: The Revelation - Bloodlines and Patterns

### Abandoned Cottage Outside Strasbourg, Morning

They found refuge in a woodcutter's abandoned cottage five miles from the city, deep enough in the forest that the smoke from their small fire wouldn't be visible from any road. Collapsed on the dirt floor that smelled of old leaves and mouse droppings and abandonment. Too exhausted to speak. Too shocked to process what they'd discovered.

Thomas stared at the ceiling beams, watching spiders build webs in the corners with patient, methodical precision. Intricate geometric patterns. Everything was patterns. The cipher. The bloodlines. The web of connections spreading across Europe. All patterns.

And he was one of them. A man shaped by patterns laid generations before he was born.

"Johannes is captured," he said finally, his voice sounding dead even to himself. Hollow. Empty of everything except guilt.

"Or dead," Margarethe said with brutal honesty. She was checking her shoulder wound again—reopened during the night's running, bleeding fresh through the bandages Thomas had helped her change. "The Order doesn't take prisoners unless they want information. If they think he doesn't know anything useful, they'll just kill him. If they think he does..." She didn't finish the sentence. Didn't need to.

"He knows everything. The network. The keys. Our names. Where we're going." Thomas felt panic rising in his chest, tightening his throat. "Us."

"He'll resist as long as he can. Johannes is stronger than he looks. He'll give us time."

"And then he'll break. Everyone breaks." Thomas was using Margarethe's own words, thrown back at the situation like weapons. "You said it yourself. Everyone breaks under torture eventually. We have days at most before they know everything he knows. Before they know we're heading east. Before—"

"Then we use those days," Margarethe interrupted, her voice sharp with command. "We don't waste them on guilt and fear. We keep moving. Keep learning. Keep gathering the keys. Make Johannes's sacrifice mean something."

She was right. Thomas knew she was right. But knowing didn't make it easier.

Margarethe finished bandaging her shoulder with strips torn from her ruined merchant's dress. The fine green fabric was destroyed now—torn, mud-stained, bloodied. They looked like what they were: fugitives barely surviving.

Thomas pulled out the two keys with trembling hands. Iron and Silver. The Eye that Sees and the Hand that Guides. Heavy with meaning and history and the weight of everything they'd sacrificed to obtain them.

And the manuscript Johannes had given him—the one showing the bloodline connections revealed only when both keys were used together.

"He said forty generations," Thomas said, unrolling the manuscript carefully. "The network has been shepherding specific families for forty generations. That's... that's almost a thousand years of controlled breeding."

"Since Rome fell," Margarethe confirmed, moving to sit beside him despite her pain. "Since the network was supposedly founded to preserve knowledge through the dark ages. They haven't just been preserving information in books and hiding places. They've been creating people who could survive the next collapse with the right traits already bred into their bloodlines."

Thomas studied the manuscript more carefully in the morning light streaming through gaps in the cottage walls. The bloodline patterns were extraordinarily complex—too complex to be accident, too steady to be chance. Families in Cologne wed to families in Paris for a gift of memory, or an eye for patterns, or a steadiness of hand. Their children were wed again—into Rome, into Vienna, into places marked in the margins—each match chosen like a stitch in a tapestry. Beside the names ran cold marks of worth. Lines that pleased the network were braided tighter. Lines that did not were cut short.

The same word kept appearing in the margins: *cut*. Like the network was pruning a garden, shearing away what it didn't want.

"My grandfather," Thomas said slowly, voice catching. "Wilhelm. His family line must be in here somewhere."

He searched through the manuscript, following connections and notations, using the two keys together to reveal the complete pattern. Found it after several minutes of careful work.

Wilhelm von Eltville. Father: Friedrich von Eltville. Grandfather: Conrad von Eltville. Great-grandfather: Hermann von Eltville. Back and back through generations, each name carefully noted with birth and death dates, marriage records, and the cold judgments of those who'd written the cipher.

All the way back to someone named Adalbert von Eltville in the 4th century. Forty-one generations. The bloodline had been tracked and guided for over a thousand years.

And beside each name, cold notations that made Thomas's blood run cold:

*Wilhelm von Eltville (1287-1347): Mind for space: strong. Eye for patterns: rare. Memory: keen. Wit: sharp. Marked for cipher-work.*

*Friedrich von Eltville (1261-1312): Eye for patterns: good. Mind for space: fair. Memory: steady. Gifts pass true to children.*

Back and back, generation after generation, each one evaluated like livestock at market.

"They bred him," Thomas whispered, horror and fascination warring in his voice. "Bred his entire line across forty generations to produce someone who could read the cipher naturally. Who could see patterns that others couldn't. Who had the exact combination of traits needed to be a living key to the network's knowledge."

"And you," Margarethe said quietly beside him. "You have his gift. More than just inheritance—you demonstrated it the first time you used the Iron Key. Which means—"

"Which means I'm not an accident. I'm their handiwork." The realization hit Thomas like a physical blow, stealing his breath. "Everything about me—my ability with patterns, my memory, the way I can see connections others miss and grasp intricate workings by instinct—it's not natural talent. It's not God-given ability. It's wrought. Planned. Bred into me by people who died centuries before I was born."

He was falling again. He could feel panic and a dread without name pulling him down into darkness.

Margarethe gripped his shoulders hard enough to hurt, forced him to look at her with physical intensity. "Listen to me, Thomas. I can't read. Never could, never will. The letters move around on the page no matter how hard I try to hold them still. My father turned that limitation into an ability. Made me a living library because the bloodlines needed one and I was the daughter he had. I spent my entire life wondering if I was a person or a tool. A human being with free will or an instrument made to serve someone else's purpose."

"And what did you decide?" Thomas asked, desperate for an answer that would make sense of this.

"That I'm both. And that's fine." Her eyes were fierce, demanding he understand. "You can be their handiwork and still make your own choices. The two aren't mutually exclusive. What you do with your gifts matters more than how you got them. Wilhelm was bred to read ciphers, but he chose to use that ability to preserve knowledge instead of hoarding it. That choice was his. The network gave him tools but he decided how to use them."

Thomas wanted to believe that. Needed to believe it with desperate intensity.

"Show me your family," he said, voice rough. "In the bloodlines. Let me see what they did to you."

Margarethe hesitated, her face showing reluctance and fear and the knowledge that she needed to know. Then she took the manuscript from Thomas with steady hands. Used the two keys together to reveal the full pattern. Found her line after several minutes of methodical searching.

Margarethe of Mainz. Father: Heinrich the Scholar. Mother: Anna the Translator. Grandmother: Elisabeth the Herbalist. Great-grandmother: Clara the Merchant. Back and back through her maternal line, each generation carefully tracked.

And beside her mother's name, a notation that made Margarethe go absolutely still:

*Anna the Translator (1319-1343): Tongues: Latin, Greek, Arabic, Hebrew, French. Eye for patterns: strong. Memory: keen. Wit: sharp. Fit for breeding.*

*Wed Heinrich the Scholar 1342 (match approved—bloodlines answer well).*

*With child 1343 (approved).*

*Died in childbirth 1343. Cause: made. Killed for refusing the next command to bear again. Example set to keep the line obedient.*

Margarethe went very still in the way that meant she was feeling too much to safely express.

"They killed her," she said finally, voice flat and emotionless and terrible. "My mother. She refused some network order about having more children—probably wanted to stop after one difficult birth—and they killed her. Made it look like natural complications. Left me motherless and my father devastated as punishment for her disobedience."

Thomas saw more in the detailed notations. Anna had been brilliant—spoke five languages fluently, could translate ancient texts that baffled most scholars, carried multiple valuable bloodline traits for intelligence and memory. But when the network had ordered her to have more children to further spread those traits, she'd refused. Had wanted to focus on her existing daughter, on her studies and translation work.

The network had allowed the initial pregnancy with Heinrich to proceed—the bloodline match was acceptable, even ideal. But they'd arranged for "complications" during childbirth that killed Anna while leaving Margarethe alive. Punishment for disobedience. Warning to others who might resist network directives.

"I'm sorry," Thomas said, knowing the words were useless but having nothing else to offer.

"Don't be. I'm angry. And anger is useful." Margarethe's hands were shaking as she searched for her father's entry. "Anger can be directed. Controlled. Used as a weapon against the people who deserve it."

She found Heinrich's death notation. Read it with eyes gone cold and hard:

*Heinrich the Scholar (1315-1347): Eye for patterns: strong. Tongues: many. Gift for teaching: rare. Memory: keen (proved again and again).*

*Became a risk after Anna's killing. Resisted commands. Refused a new match. Began to pry into Anna's death.*

*Killed by Order hands 1347. Put to the question; spoke little. Bloodline continues through daughter (Margarethe—living library).*

"They used the Order to kill him," Margarethe said, her voice steady but her eyes burning with rage. "Let the Gray Robes torture him to death because he'd figured out they killed my mother. Made it look like the enemy killed him when it was really them cleaning up their own mistake. Using their supposed opponents as tools for murder."

She was shaking now. With rage or grief or both, Thomas couldn't tell.

He didn't know what to say. Everything felt inadequate in the face of such calculated cruelty.

Finally, Margarethe rolled up the manuscript with careful, controlled movements. "We need to find the other keys. All seven. Understand the complete Genesis Protocol. See what it says in its entirety, not these fragments we're working from."

"Why?" Thomas asked. "What will that accomplish?"

"Because if we don't, my parents died for nothing. Johannes was captured for nothing. Your grandfather's murder was meaningless. All the people killed by this network across forty generations—all for nothing." She met Thomas's eyes with fierce determination. "But if we complete it. If we understand what the network is actually trying to do, what the Protocol truly says when all seven keys are brought together... then maybe we can stop them. Or expose them. Or at least make all those deaths mean something."

Thomas thought about it. About the choice in front of them, stark and unforgiving.

They could run. Disappear into some remote corner of Europe and hope the network never found them. Live quiet lives, have children who would carry what had been bred into them into the next generation whether they wanted to or not.

Or they could continue. Seek the remaining five keys scattered across plague-ravaged Europe. Unlock the full truth of what the network had been doing for a thousand years. Risk everything to understand everything.

"Johannes said the third key is a person," Thomas said slowly, making his decision even as he spoke. "A living descendant who carries cipher knowledge in their blood memory. Not a physical object like the Iron and Silver Keys, but a human being whose blood carries the knowledge. He mentioned Prague as the location."

"That's weeks of travel. Through plague country. Through territory controlled by nobles who might be network members or Order supporters or just bandits taking advantage of chaos." Margarethe didn't sound opposed to the idea. Just realistic about the dangers. "With the Order hunting us specifically. With Johannes likely breaking under torture and revealing where we're headed."

"I know."

"We could die. Probably will die before we reach the seventh key."

"I know that too."

Margarethe looked at him—really looked at him, studying his face like she was seeing him for the first time. "You've changed, Thomas. You know that? Three weeks ago you were a monk who'd never left the monastery. Never killed anyone. Never stolen anything. Never questioned your place in God's plan. Now you're talking about traveling across Europe through plague and war, chasing keys to unlock a thousand-year-old conspiracy. Talking about it like it's not insane."

"We killed a man," Thomas said quietly, remembering Brother Anselm's surprised face as he fell, the leather-working tool leaving his hand, Margarethe's blade finding its mark. "Stole from a bishop's treasury. Ran from the Order. Discovered I'm bred to serve a purpose I never chose. I'm already damned by every measure that matters. Might as well finish what we started and at least make the damnation meaningful."

Despite everything—the exhaustion, the pain, the revelation of their parents' murders—Margarethe laughed. It was a dark sound, edged with bitterness and resignation, but genuine. "All right then. We go to Prague. Find the human key, whatever that means. Unlock the next layer of this nightmare we've uncovered. See how much worse it can get before it kills us."

"Together?"

"Together." Margarethe held out her hand.

Thomas took it. Shook once. A pact sealed between two people bred for purposes they'd never consented to, choosing their own path despite the bloodline manipulation that had created them.

Thomas felt a hard steadiness take root in him. Not peace—that was too much to ask given everything. But purpose. Direction. A path forward that was his choice, not something planned by people who'd died centuries ago.

They had two of seven keys. A manuscript showing bloodline patterns going back forty generations. The knowledge that the network had been playing God with humanity for a thousand years, breeding people like animals, killing those who resisted.

And now they knew the truth about their own families. The calculated murders. The breeding. The casual cruelty disguised as preservation of knowledge.

Outside the abandoned cottage, dawn light was strengthening. A new day. A new chapter in a quest that would probably kill them but felt necessary anyway.

Thomas packed the keys and manuscript carefully, wrapping them in oiled cloth to protect them from rain. Margarethe checked her weapons—knife still sharp, small crossbow still functional despite the night's chaos.

They should leave. Head east immediately, put as much distance as possible between themselves and Strasbourg before the Order organized a proper search. Every hour they delayed was an hour the net could tighten around them.

But first, they needed rest. Real rest, not the terrified half-sleep of the previous night. Their bodies were reaching their limits—Margarethe's arrow wound still healing, Thomas's hands scraped raw from climbing stone walls, both of them running on fear and determination alone.

"We rest here today," Margarethe said, voicing what Thomas was thinking. "Move tonight under cover of darkness. Travel is slower but safer when the Order's patrols rely on daylight to spot fugitives."

Thomas nodded, grateful for the pragmatism that kept them alive. He spread blankets from their packs on the cleanest section of floor, tried to make something approaching comfort from the abandoned cottage's meager offerings.

They took turns sleeping, one always on watch. The autumn sun climbed higher, filtered through gaps in the walls where wattle and daub had crumbled to nothing. Birds sang in the forest beyond, oblivious to human concerns about conspiracies and keys and bloodlines bred across generations.

Thomas woke to footsteps approaching the cottage—boots on frost-brittle grass, deliberate and unhurried. Not trying to hide. Not sneaking.

He grabbed the knife Margarethe had insisted he keep within reach while sleeping, heart hammering despite the fact that Gray Robes wouldn't announce themselves so openly.

Margarethe was already awake, positioned by the window with her crossbow aimed at the door. She'd heard the approach long before Thomas had.

The footsteps stopped outside. A pause. Then a voice, young and uncertain:

"I have a message. For Thomas of Mainz. From... from someone who says you'll understand the name Wilhelm."

Thomas and Margarethe exchanged glances. A trap? Or genuine?

"Leave it by the door," Margarethe called out, voice hard. "Then step back twenty paces. We'll retrieve it when you're clear."

"I can't. It's not written down. Too dangerous." The voice was definitely young—maybe sixteen, seventeen at most. Male. Scared but determined. "The message is in my head. I was told to speak it directly to Thomas, no one else, to ensure it wasn't intercepted or altered."

Margarethe's jaw tightened. Messages carried in memory instead of on paper—classic network tradecraft. Safer. Harder to intercept. But also potentially a way to lure them into opening the door.

Thomas made the decision quickly, before he could talk himself out of it. "I'm opening the door. If you're armed, if there are others with you, we'll kill you before you can call for help. Understand?"

"I understand. I'm alone. Unarmed. Just a runner for the network. Please—I need to deliver this message and get back before I'm missed."

Thomas cracked the door open, knife ready.

A boy stood there—sixteen at most, dressed in apprentice's clothes, with the nervous energy of someone terrified but committed to a task. His hands were visible, empty, trembling slightly in the cold morning air.

"Quickly," Thomas said. "Your message."

The boy took a breath, reciting with the careful precision of someone who'd memorized every word exactly: "Johannes the Alchemist lives. Captured during the raid on the safe house two nights past. Currently held in the cathedral's prison—beneath the bishop's residence, same complex where you stole the Silver Key. The Order is interrogating him for information about the network, about you, about the theft. He's being tortured but hasn't broken yet—my contact says he's been asking for specific herbs the guards think are for pain relief but which might be something else entirely. This is day two of captivity. The Order typically executes prisoners after five days of questioning if they haven't extracted useful information. You have three days, maybe four at most, before they either kill him or transfer him to a more secure location."

The boy paused, then added in his own voice, no longer reciting: "My master—the one who sent me—says it's suicide to try a rescue. That you should run east while you still can. That Johannes would want you to complete the quest rather than die trying to save him. But the master also said you'd want to know Johannes is alive. That you'd want the choice."

Thomas felt ice and fire warring in his chest. Johannes was alive. Being tortured. Three days, maybe four.

"Who's your master?" Margarethe asked, crossbow still steady. "Who sent you with this message?"

"I can't say. Network security—you understand. But someone who knew Wilhelm. Who knew Heinrich. Who remembers when the network was about preserving knowledge instead of..." He trailed off, clearly uncomfortable.

"Instead of breeding people like livestock and killing those who resist," Margarethe finished coldly.

The boy nodded, miserable. "I should go. I've delivered the message. What you do with it is your choice." He turned to leave, then paused. "For what it's worth—I hope you run. I hope you survive. Someone needs to understand what the Protocol really says. What it was before they corrupted it."

He disappeared into the autumn forest before Thomas could respond.

Margarethe lowered the crossbow slowly, her expression unreadable. "It could be a trap."

"I know."

"The Order could have forced someone to send that message. Could be using Johannes as bait to lure us back into the cathedral where they have every advantage."

"I know that too."

"We should run. East toward Prague like we planned. Every hour we waste is an hour they have to find us. To surround us. To close the net." But her voice lacked conviction. She was arguing out of habit, out of the need to state obvious facts, not because she believed them.

Thomas looked at the manuscript spread on the floor where he'd been studying it before sleep—bloodline patterns tracked across forty generations. His family. Margarethe's family. Johannes's family. All of them shaped and guided and bred like animals to serve someone else's purpose.

And Johannes, who'd helped them understand that horror, who'd taught them what the keys meant, who'd given them everything they needed to steal the Silver Key and begin unlocking the truth—he was being tortured right now. Suffering because he'd helped them. Because they'd involved him in their theft.

Thomas took one last look at Strasbourg in the distance, smoke rising from the cathedral quarter where they'd stolen the Silver Key and where Johannes was now imprisoned.

Not east.

Not yet.

First, they had a rescue to attempt.

Two people bred for someone else's purpose, choosing their own path.

Even if it killed them.
