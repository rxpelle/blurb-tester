## Scene: The Message

### Abandoned Cottage Outside Strasbourg, Second Morning After Escape

Thomas woke to the sound of someone approaching the cottage—footsteps crunching through frost-brittle grass, deliberate and unhurried. Not trying to hide. Not sneaking.

He grabbed the knife Margarethe had insisted he keep within reach while sleeping, heart hammering despite the fact that Gray Robes wouldn't announce themselves so openly.

Margarethe was already awake, positioned by the window with her crossbow aimed at the door, finger on the trigger. She'd heard the approach long before Thomas had.

The footsteps stopped outside. A pause. Then a voice, young and uncertain:

"I have a message. For Thomas of Mainz. From... from someone who says you'll understand the name Wilhelm."

Thomas and Margarethe exchanged glances. A trap? Or genuine?

"Leave it by the door," Margarethe called out, voice hard. "Then step back twenty paces. We'll retrieve it when you're clear."

"I can't. It's not written down. Too dangerous." The voice was definitely young—maybe sixteen, seventeen at most. Male. Scared but determined. "The message is in my head. I was told to speak it directly to Thomas, no one else, to ensure it wasn't intercepted or altered."

Margarethe's jaw tightened. Messages carried in memory instead of on paper—classic network tradecraft. Safer. Harder to intercept. But also potentially a way to lure them into opening the door.

Thomas made the decision quickly, before he could talk himself out of it. "I'm opening the door. If you're armed, if there are others with you, we'll kill you before you can call for help. Understand?"

"I understand. I'm alone. Unarmed. Just a runner for the network. Please—I need to deliver this message and get back before I'm missed."

Thomas cracked the door open, knife ready.

A boy stood there—sixteen at most, dressed in apprentice's clothes, with the nervous energy of someone terrified but committed to a task. His hands were visible, empty, trembling slightly in the cold morning air.

"Quickly," Thomas said. "Your message."

The boy took a breath, reciting with the careful precision of someone who'd memorized every word exactly: "Johannes the Alchemist lives. Captured during the raid on the safe house three nights past. Currently held in the cathedral's prison—beneath the bishop's residence, same complex where you stole the Silver Key. The Order is interrogating him for information about the network, about you, about the theft. He's being tortured but hasn't broken yet—my contact says he's been asking for specific herbs the guards think are for pain relief but which might be something else entirely. This is day three of captivity. The Order typically executes prisoners after five days of questioning if they haven't extracted useful information. You have two days, maybe three at most, before they either kill him or transfer him to a more secure location."

The boy paused, then added in his own voice, no longer reciting: "My master—the one who sent me—says it's suicide to try a rescue. That you should run east while you still can. That Johannes would want you to complete the quest rather than die trying to save him. But the master also said you'd want to know Johannes is alive. That you'd want the choice."

Thomas felt ice and fire warring in his chest. Johannes was alive. Being tortured. Two days, maybe three.

"Who's your master?" Margarethe asked, still holding the crossbow steady. "Who sent you with this message?"

"I can't say. Network security—you understand. But someone who knew Wilhelm. Who knew Heinrich. Who remembers when the network was about preserving knowledge instead of..." He trailed off, clearly uncomfortable.

"Instead of breeding people like livestock and killing those who resist," Margarethe finished coldly.

The boy nodded, miserable. "I should go. I've delivered the message. What you do with it is your choice." He turned to leave, then paused. "For what it's worth—I hope you run. I hope you survive. Someone needs to understand what the Protocol really says. What it was before they corrupted it."

He disappeared into the morning mist before Thomas could respond.

Margarethe lowered the crossbow slowly, her expression unreadable. "It could be a trap."

"I know."

"The Order could have forced someone to send that message. Could be using Johannes as bait to lure us back into the cathedral where they have every advantage."

"I know that too."

"We should run. East toward Prague like we planned. Every hour we waste is an hour they have to find us. To surround us. To close the net." But her voice lacked conviction. She was arguing out of habit, out of the need to state obvious facts, not because she believed them.

Thomas looked at the manuscript spread on the floor where he'd been studying it the night before—bloodline patterns tracked across forty generations. His family. Margarethe's family. Johannes's family. All of them shaped and guided and bred like animals to serve someone else's purpose.

And Johannes, who'd helped them understand that horror, who'd taught them what the keys meant, who'd given them everything they needed to steal the Silver Key and begin unlocking the truth—he was being tortured right now. Suffering because he'd helped them. Because they'd involved him in their theft.

"We rescue him," Thomas said quietly.

It wasn't a question.

Margarethe didn't argue. Just began checking her weapons with the methodical efficiency of someone preparing for battle.

They had two days. Maybe three.

And a cathedral full of guards between them and a wounded alchemist who'd become their friend.

Thomas rolled up the manuscript, secured both keys in his pack, and tried not to think about all the ways this could end badly.
