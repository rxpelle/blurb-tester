# THE AETHELRED CIPHER
## Book 1: Medieval Timeline (1347-1350)

**By Randy Pellegrini**

---


# CHAPTER 1 - THE KEY (Medieval Timeline - 1347, Mainz)

## Scene 0: The Scriptorium

### St. Martin's Monastery, Mainz - Dawn

Blood and ink.

The smell hit Thomas first, sharp and metallic beneath the acrid bite of smoke from overturned candles. Brother Hamo lay sprawled across the copying desk, his robes dark with spreading crimson. The manuscript he'd been working on—a commentary on Boethius—was ruined, vellum soaking through with blood.

Thomas froze in the doorway. Breakfast bread forgotten in his hand.

"Hamo?"

The old monk's hand twitched. Thomas dropped the bread and knelt beside him.

"Thomas." Hamo's voice bubbled wetly. A narrow wound gaped in his chest, precise as a scribe's cut. The dagger was gone. "Took... too long..."

"The infirmarian—I'll fetch—"

"No." Hamo's grip seized Thomas's wrist. "They're coming. The Gray Robes."

"Who—"

"The Order." Blood flecked Hamo's lips as he coughed. His other hand fumbled in his robes, pulled out something small. Iron. "Your grandfather... Wilhelm... he made me swear. If anything happened... this goes to you."

He pressed the object into Thomas's palm. A key, crude iron, with an eye symbol etched into the handle.

Thomas stared. "I don't understand—"

"The cipher. The marks in the margins. Wilhelm's network." Hamo's eyes lost focus, then sharpened again with effort. "This is the only tool that can read it. Hide it. Never let the Order—"

Footsteps in the corridor. Multiple sets. Heavy boots, not sandals.

Terror transformed Hamo's face. "Go. The tunnels beneath the wine cellar. Now!"

"I won't leave you—"

"You're weak, Thomas. Everyone knows it. Weak at digging, weak at hauling, weak at everything that matters." Hamo smiled, sad and certain. "But Wilhelm saw something in you. Some gift." Blood ran from the corner of his mouth. "If he believed it... then I believe it too."

The footsteps drew closer. Calm voices, methodical, asking questions in the chapter house.

"Go," Hamo whispered. "See the patterns your grandfather saw. Make my death—"

His eyes went distant. His hand went slack.

Thomas knelt there, the iron key cutting into his palm.

The voices reached the cloister.

"—the scribe boy, Thomas of Eltville. Where is he?"

"—Brother Hamo was the last to see the manuscript—"

"—search the scriptorium first—"

Thomas looked at Hamo's body. At the ruined Boethius. At the key in his hand.

Grandfather Wilhelm had died for this. Now Hamo.

The footsteps turned toward the scriptorium.

Thomas ran.

Left at the refectory, down the stone stairs, past the chapter house—he knew these corridors, had scrubbed them often enough during penance. Shouts erupted behind him, no longer bothering with stealth.

The wine cellar was dark, cool, smelling of earth and fermentation. Squeezing between the large barrels, Thomas found the tunnel entrance Hamo had mentioned—a low arch behind the oldest cask, barely visible in shadow.

He dropped to his knees and crawled into darkness.

Behind him, the cellar door crashed open.

"—saw him come this way—"

"—check behind the barrels—"

Thomas crawled faster, hands scraping stone, the key clutched tight enough to draw blood. The tunnel sloped down and turned, opening into a larger passage—old Roman construction, forgotten when the monastery was built centuries ago.

Voices echoed behind him, but the tunnel was too narrow for a grown man in armor.

"Get the boy from the village. Someone who can fit."

Thomas didn't wait. He ran through darkness, one hand on the tunnel wall, until light appeared ahead—a grating opening onto the city streets.

He pushed through and emerged in an alley reeking of offal and tanning chemicals.

Free—for now.

Thomas opened his palm. Blood on the key—his own, from the sharp edges. Or Hamo's, from those final moments.

*Your grandfather saw something in you.*

*Hamo saw it too.*

*And they both died for it.*

But he was alive. The Gray Robes wanted this key badly enough to kill for it.

Pulling his hood up, Thomas stepped into the crowded streets of Mainz. Plague bells rang their hollow rhythm across the city. Somewhere beyond the market square, someone screamed—grief or pain, impossible to tell. The world was dying, one bell at a time.

Somewhere in this chaos, there had to be answers.

## Scene 1: Wilhelm's Lesson

Lungs burning, Thomas ran.

Through Mainz's twisted streets he fled, the key pressed into his palm, its edges cutting deeper with each stride. His breath came in ragged gasps. His sandals slapped against cobblestones still wet from morning rain.

*Grandfather.*

The memory came unbidden: eight years old, standing in his father's vineyard while his grandfather showed him a manuscript.

"Do you see the marks, Thomas?" Wilhelm's weathered hands had pointed to tiny dots in the margin, his silver ring catching sunlight. "Not decoration. Information. Each position means something. Each color carries weight."

"Why hide it, Grandfather?"

"Because power wants to stay powerful. And the most powerful thing in this world isn't gold or swords—it's knowing what others don't." Wilhelm had smiled sadly. "The Church says knowledge should serve God. I say knowledge should serve humanity. But there are those who say knowledge should serve only the worthy."

"Are we worthy?"

"That's the question, isn't it?" Wilhelm had closed the book. "Your father thinks me a fool. He wants you in the vineyards, learning useful work. But I think you have your grandmother's eyes—you see patterns. That's worth more than strong arms."

Three weeks later, Wilhelm was dead. "Fell from his horse," they said. But Thomas remembered the stranger who'd visited the day before—the silver key pendant at his throat, the same symbol now etched on the iron key in Thomas's hand.

Thomas stumbled into an alley and pressed his back against cold stone. His father's voice echoed in memory: *Weak. Useless. Can't even dig a proper hole without whining.*

Maybe he was weak.

Maybe he was useless.

But Grandfather had believed he could see patterns.

He looked at the key, at the eye symbol staring back.

*Then I'd better start seeing them.*

---

## Scene 2: First Glimpse of Margarethe

The cloth merchant's quarter sprawled like a maze—drying racks casting latticed shadows, funeral shrouds hanging limp in the still air.

Thomas moved through it like a ghost, his monk's robes stained with tunnel filth. The smell here was different from the alley: lye soap cutting through decay, wet wool drying in weak sunlight, and underneath it all, the sweet-sick odor of bodies waiting to be wrapped.

A woman worked at a sorting table, hands moving with numb precision through plague-stained linens. The clothes of the dead.

Thomas started to turn away.

"You're bleeding," she said without looking up.

Thomas examined his palm. The key had cut deeper than he'd thought. "It's nothing."

"Infection kills as surely as plague." She looked up.

Younger than he'd expected—mid-twenties, perhaps. Sharp face, intelligent eyes, a scar running along her jawline. Her hands, though working with fabric, were callused in ways that spoke of harder work than cloth.

"The Gray Robes are watching the gates," she said, returning to her work. "Whatever you stole, they want it back. Three men just came through here asking about a scribe boy. I told them I saw you heading toward the cathedral."

"Why would you—"

"Because the Order burned my father for having books they didn't approve of." Her voice was flat. "I don't help them. Ever."

Thomas stepped closer. "The books. Did they have... marks? Patterns in the margins?"

Her hands stilled. For just a moment, her mask cracked. "Who are you?"

"Thomas. Of Eltville."

"Eltville." She tested the name. "Wilhelm's grandson?"

His breath caught. "You knew him?"

"My father knew him. Worked with him." She studied Thomas with new intensity. "You look like him. Same soft hands. Same naive eyes that think the world should be fair."

"I don't—"

"Good. Because it's not." She pulled a strip of cloth from her pile and walked around the table. "Give me your hand."

Thomas hesitated, then extended his bleeding palm.

She bound the wound with efficient movements. This close, he could smell rosemary, lavender, and something acrid—chemicals used in cloth dyeing. Or erasing ink.

"The river path," she said, tying off the bandage. "Go before dark. There's a warehouse district. Third building, red mark on the door. Knock twice. If I'm not there by morning, run south and don't look back."

"I don't even know your name."

"Margarethe." She met his eyes directly. "And if you're carrying what I think you're carrying, Thomas of Eltville, knowing my name might be the most dangerous thing you ever learn."

She turned back to her work, dismissing him.

Thomas walked toward the river, his bandaged hand throbbing. He looked back once.

Margarethe was watching him. When their eyes met, she didn't look away.

For reasons he couldn't name, his chest tightened.

---

## Scene 3: Margarethe's Backstory

Margarethe's hands shook as she returned to sorting linens.

*Wilhelm's grandson.*

She closed her eyes. Memory came anyway, unwanted and uninvited:

**Seven years ago. Age nineteen.**

Her father's warehouse. The smell of imported silks and hidden manuscripts. Brother Anselm standing over Heinrich's broken body, his soft voice asking questions, his calm hands wielding instruments of precision and pain.

"Where is the index, Heinrich? Where does Wilhelm keep the master list?"

"There is no... master list..." Her father's voice, barely recognizable through blood and broken teeth.

"We know about the network. The cipher maps. The locations hidden across Christendom. Wilhelm is dead, but his knowledge remains. Tell me where."

"Knowledge... should be free..."

"Knowledge in the wrong hands is a plague worse than the Death outside these walls." Anselm had sounded genuinely sad. "I take no pleasure in this, old friend. But the Order cannot allow chaos. Tell me, and I will make it quick."

Margarethe, hidden in the loft, had bitten through her lip to keep from screaming.

Her father had looked up—directly at her hiding place. Their eyes met.

*Don't speak. Don't move. Live.*

Then: "I'll tell you. The index is in... Abbey of Fulda. Behind the altar stone. Third—"

Anselm's dagger had been quick. Merciful, even.

The lie had saved her life. There was no index at Fulda. The real locations were in her father's head—and he'd spent her entire childhood teaching her to memorize them.

*"In case something happens to me, little raven. In case the Order comes. You must be the library they cannot burn."*

She'd become exactly that. Two hundred and seventeen locations, memorized perfectly. Monasteries, private collections, merchant safe houses. The complete network of the Aethelred Cipher's hidden knowledge.

She was the index. The living catalog.

And she'd just revealed herself to Wilhelm's grandson.

But when she'd seen Thomas's eyes—that same naive belief that the world could be better, that knowledge mattered more than survival—she'd seen her father. And Wilhelm. And all the good men the Order had murdered.

Maybe one more wouldn't matter.

Or maybe this time, she'd be fast enough to save one.

She finished sorting the linens, locked her shop, and began preparing for the evening.

If Wilhelm's grandson was carrying the cipher key—and she was certain he was—the Order wouldn't stop at gates and guards. They'd hunt him properly.

And Thomas of Eltville, soft-handed scribe who probably couldn't throw a punch to save his life, would be dead by nightfall unless someone kept him alive.

Margarethe strapped her daggers beneath her cloak. The good ones, perfectly balanced. Her father's blades.

She hated getting attached.

But some debts ran deeper than survival.

---

## Scene 4: The Rescue

### Night. River Path.

Thomas followed the path along the Rhine, the water black and cold beside him. Behind him, the city bells tolled for the dead. Another plague cart rattling through the streets.

He'd made it perhaps half a mile when he heard the footsteps.

Two sets. Heavy boots on cobblestones. Not bothering to hide.

Thomas's heart hammered. He walked faster. The footsteps matched his pace.

"Scribe boy!" A voice called behind him. "Brother Anselm would speak with you. No need to run."

Thomas ran.

The river path narrowed—warehouses loomed on one side, the drop to the water on the other. Thomas's sandals slipped on wet stone, his breath coming in ragged gasps.

A hand grabbed his shoulder and yanked him back. Thomas spun, fell, hit the cobblestones hard. Stars exploded across his vision.

"Easy now," the first man said. He wore the gray robes, the silver key pendant. "We just want the little iron toy you're carrying. Give it over, and maybe you live to copy another psalm."

The second man drew a dagger. "Or don't, and save us the trouble."

Thomas's hand closed around the key in his pocket. Hamo's blood was probably still on it. Grandfather's legacy.

"No," he whispered.

"What was that?"

"I said no." Louder now. "You killed my grandfather. You killed Hamo. You're not getting this."

The first man sighed. "Brave and stupid. The worst combination." He nodded to his partner. "Make it quick."

The dagger came toward Thomas's throat.

And stopped.

The second man made a strange gurgling sound, looked down at the blade protruding from his neck, confused. Then he collapsed.

The first man spun, reaching for his own weapon—

Margarethe rose from the shadows behind him, a second dagger already in motion. She drove it under his ribs with practiced precision, twisted, withdrew. The man fell.

Thomas stared from his position on the ground, ears ringing. The world narrowed to Margarethe standing over the bodies, wiping her blades on the first man's cloak.

She looked at Thomas. Calm. Practiced.

"Breathe," she said. "Unless you want to faint and crack your skull."

Thomas breathed. The ringing subsided.

Margarethe sheathed her daggers and extended a hand. Her grip was strong, callused. She pulled him to his feet.

"You were supposed to go to the warehouse," she said.

"I was going—"

"Too slow. The Order doesn't just guard gates. They watch the paths too." She looked at the bodies without emotion. "I followed you. Figured you'd be dead within the hour if I didn't."

"You've been following me since—"

"Since you left my shop. Aye." She checked the alley behind them. "These two won't be the last. Can you run?"

"Yes."

"Then run. Toward the warehouses. Third door, red mark. I'll make sure no one else is tracking you, then meet you there."

"Wait—" Thomas found his voice. "You killed them."

"Aye."

"You just... killed two men."

"Would you prefer I let them kill you?" Her tone was matter-of-fact, but something flickered in her eyes. "This isn't the scriptorium, Thomas. Out here, you either run or you bleed. Sometimes both."

She turned to leave.

"Thank you," Thomas said.

Margarethe paused. Didn't turn around. "Don't thank me yet. You're not safe. Neither of us is." A beat. "But I owed your grandfather a debt. I pay my debts."

"Is that the only reason you helped me?"

Now she did turn. Looked at him directly. "I don't know. Ask me again when we're not standing next to corpses."

She disappeared into the shadows.

Thomas ran for the warehouse, hands shaking—from fear, adrenaline, or the memory of her touch, he couldn't say.

---

## Scene 5: The Warehouse Interrogation

Thomas found the warehouse. Third building. Red mark painted on the door. He knocked twice.

Silence.

He waited. Minutes dragged. Every shadow became a gray cloak.

The door opened. Margarethe stood there, still wearing the same bloodstained dress. She'd made no effort to clean it.

"Inside. Quick."

The warehouse was vast and cold. Bales of fabric rose like monuments in the gloom. A single lantern burned on a makeshift table.

Margarethe locked the door behind him—three separate bolts—then turned, arms crossed, and studied him.

"Sit."

Thomas sat on a crate, legs grateful for it.

Margarethe didn't sit. She circled him slowly, like a merchant appraising wares.

"You're a terrible liar," she said finally.

"I didn't lie—"

"You're afraid. Of me, of the Order, of yourself. That's honest." She stopped in front of him. "But you're hiding something. Everyone does. So here's how this works: I ask questions, you answer truthfully, and maybe we both survive the week. Lie to me, and I throw you out. Clear?"

Thomas nodded.

"Name."

"Thomas. Thomas of Eltville."

"Family."

"My father is alive. In Eltville. We... don't speak. He wanted me in the vineyards. I was too weak for it." The admission hurt. "My grandfather raised me. Wilhelm. He died when I was eight."

"How did he die?"

Thomas met her eyes. "The Order killed him. I didn't know it then. Thought it was an accident. But I saw the man who visited the day before. He wore a silver key."

Something softened in Margarethe's expression. Not much. But enough.

"Your grandfather was brilliant," she said quietly. "Reckless, but brilliant. My father said Wilhelm could hold an entire library in his head. Memorize every location, every cipher mark, every hidden text." She paused. "Did he teach you?"

"Some. He showed me how to see patterns. How to remember sequences. But I was young. I didn't understand..." Thomas pulled out the iron key. "Until Hamo gave me this."

Margarethe's breath caught. She reached for the key, then stopped herself. "May I?"

"Aye."

She took it gently, reverently, turning it over in her hands, examining the etched eye symbol.

"I thought this was lost," she whispered. "When my father died, Anselm searched for weeks. He tore apart our warehouse, our home. He never found it."

"Because Wilhelm had it?"

"Wilhelm kept it separate. Safe." She looked up at Thomas. "Do you know what this is?"

"Hamo said it reads the cipher. Shows which dots matter."

"It's more than that." Margarethe pulled a folded piece of parchment from her bodice and spread it on the table.

Thomas leaned forward. The parchment showed what looked like a manuscript page, covered in dense Latin text. But in the margins, faintly visible, were geometric dot patterns.

Margarethe placed the iron key over the page. The eye symbol aligned with a specific section of text. Through the aperture, certain dots were highlighted.

"The key is a template," she explained. "Position it correctly, and it tells you which dots to count, which to ignore. Without it, the cipher looks random. With it..." She counted quickly. "Abbey of St. Gall. Third shelf. Volume on Aristotelian comedy."

Thomas stared. "Madness. Those dots—they're tiny. Random—"

"Not random. Never random. Every mark has meaning. Position, color, depth. It's a language, Thomas. A mathematical language your grandfather helped create." She handed the key back. "And now you're holding the only decoding tool that exists."

"Why not make more?"

"Because more copies mean more chances for the Order to find one. This key is unique. The knowledge of how to make it died with the original architects." Margarethe's jaw tightened. "My father tried to recreate it. He couldn't. The mathematics were beyond him. Beyond anyone alive."

Thomas turned the key over in his hands. Such a small thing. Iron, worthless. But it held the power to unlock centuries of hidden knowledge.

"What do we do with it?" he asked.

"We?" Margarethe raised an eyebrow.

"You saved my life. You clearly care about protecting this cipher. I have the key, but I don't know how to use it properly. You know the system, but you don't have the tool." Thomas met her gaze. "Separately, we're incomplete. Together..."

"Together we're bigger targets."

"Perhaps. Or perhaps we're strong enough to actually do something with this."

Margarethe studied him for a long moment. "You're naive."

"Aye."

"Weak. Probably useless in a fight."

"Almost certainly."

"And you think because we share a dead grandfather's legacy, that makes us allies?"

"I think," Thomas said carefully, "that you could have let those men kill me. You could have taken the key off my corpse and been done with it. But you didn't. So either you're a worse pragmatist than you pretend, or..." He trailed off.

"Or?"

"Or you're just as tired of being alone as I am."

The silence stretched between them. The lantern flickered.

Finally, Margarethe sat on a crate across from him. "Here's what you need to understand, Thomas of Eltville. Everyone I protect dies. My mother. My father. The merchants who worked with him. The Order doesn't leave loose ends."

"But you're still alive."

"Because I'm careful. Because I trust no one. Because I've made myself useful enough to too many people that killing me would cause problems." She leaned forward. "You want to be allies? Very well. But don't mistake alliance for friendship. Don't expect me to save you again. And don't—" Her voice caught slightly. "Don't ask me to care whether you live or die. Because everyone I care about ends up dead."

Thomas heard what she tried to bury: grief, and something close to fear.

"I won't ask," he said softly. "I'll try not to get killed, anyway. Can't promise about the caring part."

"What?"

"I already care whether you live or die. You saved my life. That tends to create attachment."

Margarethe stared at him. Then, unexpectedly, she laughed—sharp and bitter. "You're going to be a problem, aren't you?"

"Probably."

She shook her head, but something in her expression had shifted. "We start tomorrow. I'll teach you to decode the cipher properly. You'll teach me everything your grandfather told you about the network. And we'll figure out what to do with this damned key before the Order finds us."

"Where do I sleep?"

"There." She pointed to a pile of wool blankets in the corner. "I'll be on the other side of the warehouse. If you try anything, I have more daggers."

"I wouldn't—"

"I know. You're too naive to be dangerous." But she almost smiled. "Sleep, Thomas. Tomorrow we find out if you're really Wilhelm's grandson or just another dead scribe waiting to happen."

She walked into the shadows, leaving Thomas alone with the lantern and the weight of his grandfather's legacy.

He lay on the wool blankets, the key pressed against his chest.

For the first time since Hamo's death, he felt something other than fear.

He felt purpose.

And, though he didn't quite understand it yet, he felt the first stirrings of something else entirely when he thought about the scarred woman with daggers and grief.

But that was a problem for tomorrow.

Tonight, he just needed to survive.

# CHAPTER 2 - THE NETWORK (Medieval Timeline - 1347, Mainz)

## Scene 1: Morning After - The Lesson Begins

### Warehouse, Dawn

Scrape.

Pause.

Scrape.

Thomas woke to the sound of steel on stone.

The whetstone against blade cut through his dreams—rhythmic, deliberate, patient. Gray dawn filtered through gaps in the warehouse walls, dust motes swirling in pale shafts like ash from distant fires. The air smelled of damp wool and old fabric, and underneath, faintly, the rosemary scent he'd come to associate with Margarethe.

His back ached where stone had pressed through the blankets all night, ribs protesting from yesterday's fall on the cobblestones.

When Margarethe had saved him.

She sat cross-legged on a crate fifteen feet away, methodically sharpening her daggers. The whetstone moved steadily—scrape, pause, test, repeat.

"You snore," Margarethe said without looking up.

Thomas blinked, still foggy. "I don't—" He stopped. "Do I?"

"Like a dying goat." She tested the blade's edge against her thumb. Nodded, set it aside, started on the next. "But quietly enough. No one heard us from the street."

"How long have you been awake?"

"Since before dawn. Habit." She finally looked at him, eyes sharp and assessing. "You slept well for someone who watched two men die."

The river-path images surged back: a throat opening under Margarethe's blade, lanternlight turning blood black, the wet gurgle that went on too long.

Thomas's stomach rolled.

"I didn't have nightmares," he said quietly. "Should I have?"

"Most people do. First time they see death up close." She sheathed the first dagger, started on the third. "Either you're tougher than you look, or very good at lying to yourself."

Thomas considered it. "Perhaps both."

She almost smiled.

He walked to the makeshift table—a rough plank balanced on two crates. Someone had left bread and cheese there. The bread was hard as stone, the cheese not much softer. He tore off pieces of each, chewed mechanically. It tasted like dust and salt.

"We start today," Margarethe said, setting down her whetstone. The scraping stopped, leaving the warehouse suddenly quiet. She stood and walked to a stack of fabric bales against the far wall, movements fluid and economical. "Your grandfather's gift won't save you if you don't know how to use it."

She pushed the bales aside. Behind them sat a wooden chest, old and scarred, bound with iron straps. Margarethe produced a key from somewhere—perhaps her boot—and unlocked the chest.

Inside were manuscripts.

Thomas's breath caught. Even from here, he could see the quality—good vellum, careful script, the kind of work that took months to produce. Each one worth more than his father's entire vineyard.

"My father's collection," Margarethe said softly. For the first time since they'd met, something shifted in her expression—grief, raw and unguarded. "The ones he hid before the Order came. Not the originals—those are scattered across Christendom. But copies. Careful copies." She pulled out three and carried them to the table with reverence. "Everything I know about the cipher, I learned from these. My father would show me page after page, teaching me to see the patterns. To remember them."

Thomas leaned over them. The vellum was beautiful—smooth, nearly translucent in the dawn light. Latin text: psalms, biblical commentary, what looked like a merchant's ledger. And in the margins, faint but deliberate, geometric patterns of colored dots.

Red. Blue. Yellow.

Just like the ones in Hamo's scriptorium before blood had ruined everything.

"What do the words say?" Margarethe asked, pointing to the first page. "The Latin. Which psalm is it?"

Thomas blinked at the odd question. "Psalm 23. *Dominus regit me...* The Lord is my shepherd."

She nodded, as if confirming something from memory. "Good. Sit."

Thomas sat.

She pulled the iron key from Thomas's pocket—he hadn't even felt her take it. Pickpocket's touch. She placed the key on the table between them. The eye symbol etched into the handle caught the light, seemed to stare at them both. Accusing. Demanding.

"The cipher is a language," Margarethe began, her teacher's voice different from her merchant voice or survivor voice—more patient, almost kind. "But not one written in words. It's written in positions, colors, and counts." She gestured to the dots in the margins. "What do you see in the markings? Describe them."

Thomas studied the margins near Psalm 23. A cluster of dots. Three red, two blue, one yellow. Arranged in what looked like a triangular pattern.

"Dots," he said. "Three red, two blue, one yellow."

"Deeper. What pattern do they form?"

Thomas looked closer. Squinted. The three red ones formed a perfect triangle, apex pointing upward. The two blue dots sat at the triangle's base, equidistant. The single yellow dot was offset to the right, like a waypoint.

"Geometry," he said slowly. "The placement is deliberate. The red dots form a triangle."

"Good." The faintest hint of approval crossed her face. "That's what my father taught me to see. Not the words—I can't read those. Never could. But the shapes. The patterns. The positions." She picked up the iron key. "Now watch."

She positioned it over the page with practiced precision, her movements careful and deliberate. "My father taught me that the eye symbol aligns with specific words. Common phrases that appear in many manuscripts. In Psalm 23, there's a word—six lines down from the beginning. Read it for me."

Thomas scanned down. "'Righteous.'"

"That's the anchor." She had the key positioned perfectly over that word, as if she knew exactly where it would be without reading it. "Through the aperture in the key's handle, certain dots are highlighted while others fall into shadow."

Thomas leaned closer, looking through the key's opening. She was right—the aperture created a frame that isolated specific dots.

"The key is a filter," Margarethe explained. "Position it correctly over the textual anchor, and it tells you which dots to count, which to ignore. Count what you see through the opening."

Thomas counted. "Three red, one yellow. The blue ones are hidden."

"Three-one. That's a position indicator." She set the key down. "Every page has these anchors—specific words my father taught me to find by memory. I know where they sit on the page, how many lines down, roughly where in the line. I memorized hundreds of them."

"Position of what?"

"The location being encoded." She pulled out a second manuscript—a merchant's ledger, pages stained with wine or blood. Found a page with similar marginal markings. "Same key, different page. Different anchor point." She positioned the key with the same practiced precision. "There should be a word... merchant accounts typically have repeated phrases. Look for something about payment or debt, about three fingers' width from the left edge, maybe ten lines down."

Thomas scanned the page where she'd indicated. "There. 'Debitor.' Debtor."

"That's it." She adjusted the key slightly to align with the word. "Now count what you see through the opening."

Thomas looked. "Two blue dots. The yellow and red are outside the frame."

"Two-zero. That's a danger marker. A warning." She met his eyes. "The cipher isn't just hiding information—it's protecting people. Warning them where not to go."

Thomas's mind raced, patterns clicking into place like pins in a lock finding their positions. "So the colors mean different things. Red is safe locations. Blue is dangerous. Yellow is—"

"Neutral ground. Trade routes, markets, places where the network operates but doesn't control." Margarethe set the key down between them. "The cipher encodes three types of information: location, status, and timing. Together, they create a map of the known world that tells you where to hide, when to travel, and who to trust."

"Timing?"

She turned to another page, one with a different pattern of dots. "Describe what you see here. The yellow ones."

Thomas studied them. "They're arranged in a circle. Twelve positions, like a clock face."

"Seasonal markers," she said. "When to travel, when to hide, when to wait. The network moves with the seasons, with trade fairs, with pilgrimages. Your grandfather was brilliant at this. He could look at a page and memorize fifty dot patterns in an hour. Pair them with the text you can read, and you have the complete system."

Thomas felt the weight of that legacy settle on his shoulders. "I'm not my grandfather."

"No." Margarethe's gaze was steady, unflinching. "But you have his eyes. The way you look at things—seeing patterns, seeing connections. That's his gift." She pushed the manuscripts toward him. "Your turn. Take the key. Find me a location."

Thomas picked up the iron key, heavy and cold against his palm, edges sharp enough to cut if held wrong. He positioned it over the first manuscript, trying to mimic Margarethe's smooth movements.

The aperture didn't align properly. Through it, the dots looked random, meaningless.

He adjusted, moving the key slightly left. Still wrong.

"You need to find an anchor word first," Margarethe said, patient. "My father taught me the common ones. In psalms, look for words like 'restoreth' or 'righteousness' or 'salvation.' They appear often enough to work across many manuscripts, but not so often that they're everywhere."

Thomas scanned the psalm. *"The Lord is my shepherd..."* Too common. But further down: *"He restoreth my soul."*

"There," he said. "Restoreth. Is that one?"

"It is." She sounded pleased. "Now align the eye with that word."

He positioned the key carefully over *"restoreth."*

Through the aperture, three red dots and one yellow appeared in sharp focus, the blue ones hidden outside the frame.

"Three-one," he breathed, heart hammering. "Three red, one yellow."

"And what location is that?" Margarethe asked.

Thomas frowned. "I... I don't know. How would I know?"

"You wouldn't. Not yet." But she was smiling slightly. "That's the Abbey of St. Gall. Three-one. You'd learn it the way I learned it—by having someone tell you, over and over, until the pattern and the place become linked in memory. My father spent years teaching me the 217 locations. You'll need to learn them too."

"Two hundred and seventeen," Thomas said faintly. "That's..."

"A lifetime's work. My father started when I was five years old." She pulled out another manuscript. "But you have an advantage I didn't. You can read the text yourself. Find your own anchor points. I had to memorize where every anchor word sat on every page. You just need to know which words to look for." She met his eyes, something vulnerable flickering there. "We need each other, you see? You can read what I can't. I can remember what you'll forget. Together, we're stronger than either of us alone."

"Again," Margarethe said, her merchant mask back but something warmer underneath. "Find me another location."

Thomas worked through it. This one was harder—a dense commentary on Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy*, the Latin theological and philosophical. But he understood the principle now. He scanned for repeated phrases, found one that seemed right: *"fortune's wheel."*

"Is this an anchor?" he asked, pointing.

Margarethe leaned over, studied the position without reading the words. "Yes. That's one my father taught me. Align the key there."

Thomas positioned the key carefully, counted the dots that came into focus.

"Two red, three yellow. Position two-three."

"Cologne. Private collection, merchant district. Second house, third door." Margarethe took the key back, fingers brushing his. "You're learning fast. Too fast, maybe."

"Is that a problem?"

"It is if you start thinking you're invincible." She closed the manuscripts with finality. "The cipher is powerful, Thomas. It can tell you where to hide, who to trust, how to survive. But it can't tell you everything. It can't warn you about betrayal from someone who seems trustworthy. It can't predict when someone you consider a friend will sell you to the Order for fifty gold marks and a promise of safety."

"Anyone would? For the right price or the right threat?" She stood, walked to the warehouse door, checked the street through a crack in the weathered boards. Morning light slanted in, harsh and revealing. "Which is why we're going to the market. You need to see how the network really works. Not on vellum and in theory, but in flesh and blood and lies."

"You're taking me to meet more people? After you just said anyone might betray us?"

"No. I'm taking you to watch people. There's a difference." She turned, expression serious, almost grave. "The cipher is information. But information without context is just noise. You need to learn to read people the way you read manuscripts. Their tells, their patterns, their weaknesses."

"I'm not good at that," Thomas admitted.

"I know. You're transparent as glass." She pulled a cloak from a hook on the wall, tossed it to him. It smelled like rosemary and old smoke—like her. "Which is why you're going to keep your mouth shut and watch. Understood?"

Thomas caught the cloak, pulled it around his shoulders. "Understood."

"Good." Margarethe pulled her own cloak on, checked that her daggers were concealed beneath the fabric. "And Thomas?"

"Aye?"

"If something happens—if we're separated, if the Order finds us—you run. You don't try to be brave. You don't try to save me. You just run and don't look back."

He wanted to argue. Wanted to say he wouldn't abandon her the way everyone in his life had abandoned him.

But the look in her eyes stopped him.

"I'll run," he lied.

She studied him for a long moment, and he saw the exact instant she knew he was lying. But instead of calling him on it, she just sighed. "You're a terrible liar. But I appreciate the effort."

Then she opened the door, and they stepped into the plague-haunted streets of Mainz.

## Scene 2: The Market - Gathering Intelligence

The market square was a ghost of what it must have been.

Half the stalls were empty, their owners dead or fled. The merchants who remained sold food at criminal prices—turnips for a silver penny, half a loaf of bread for three. Clothing stripped from plague houses, blood stains scrubbed out but death still clinging to the fabric. Amulets and charms promising protection, carved with saints' names or stranger symbols.

The smell hit Thomas like a wall.

Rotting vegetables mixed with unwashed bodies. The sharp tang of vinegar—people dousing themselves with it, hoping it would ward off the plague. Smoke from braziers burning herbs someone swore would purify the air. And underneath it all, seeping up from the gutters and the cellars and the mass graves beyond the city walls, the sweet-sick stench of death.

The noise was worse. Haggling voices gone shrill with desperation. A woman weeping over a cartload of her dead husband's tools. Somewhere, a child coughing—that wet, rattling sound that meant plague or consumption. Everyone who heard it took a step back.

Thomas pulled his hood lower, trying not to breathe too deeply.

Margarethe moved through the crowd with practiced ease, transformed again—not the hardened survivor from the warehouse, not the patient teacher. Now she was a cloth merchant, shrewd and focused, examining goods with a critical eye. She'd wrapped a scarf around her lower face—plague caution, but also disguise. The scar on her jaw was hidden.

Thomas followed three steps behind, as she'd instructed.

"Third stall on the left," she murmured without turning her head. "Bookseller. Gray beard, green tunic. Watch him. Don't stare, but watch."

Thomas looked.

The bookseller was old—seventy at least, maybe older. Gray-white beard, face weathered like old leather. He sat behind a small stall displaying religious texts and prayer books. Nothing remarkable.

But then Thomas noticed the pattern.

People didn't browse casually. They came with purpose. A man in merchant's clothes would approach, lean close, whisper something. The bookseller would nod, pull a specific book from his stock, open it to a specific page. The merchant would look, nod back, and leave. No money changed hands. No book was purchased.

A woman in widow's black. Same pattern. Whisper, specific book, specific page, nod, leave.

A younger man, nervously glancing around. Whisper, book, page, and this time the man's face went pale before he hurried away.

"Information trading," Margarethe said quietly. "Oskar doesn't sell books. He sells what's in them."

Thomas watched as another woman approached—middle-aged, well-dressed, probably a merchant's wife. She whispered something urgent. The bookseller—Oskar—pulled a psalter, showed her a page. The woman's face went white. She nodded once and walked away fast enough to be running without actually running.

"What did he tell her?"

"Probably that her husband's road is no longer safe. Or that the plague has reached the next town. Or that the Order is asking after their name." Margarethe angled them closer, stopping at a neighboring stall that sold fabric dyes. "The network lives on news. Oskar is a knot in their web. He knows who's traveling, who's died, where the Order is searching, which roads are safe, which cities are sealed. People pay him in gossip and coin. He pays them back in survival."

She picked up a small vial of indigo dye, examining it while watching Oskar from the corner of her eye. Thomas mimicked her, pretending to study the pigments.

"Why don't we just ask him what we need to know?"

"Because asking costs more than observing. Information freely given is worthless—he'd wonder why we didn't already know. And because—" She went rigid. "Don't turn around. Gray Robe. Two stalls behind us, near the vegetable seller. He's watching the bookseller."

Thomas's heart hammered. He forced himself to stay still, to keep examining a jar of dye. The pungent smell of woad and madder root filled his nose.

"Is he looking at us?"

"No. He's watching Oskar. The Order knows he's a network contact. They're watching who speaks to him, trying to name the rest of the web." Margarethe's voice was tight, controlled. "We need to approach, but carefully."

She purchased the vial of dye, paid in small copper coins that she counted out slowly, making conversation about the weather and the plague like any normal customer. Then she moved away from the stall. "Follow. Not directly. Circle around. Act like we're shopping."

Thomas obeyed. They moved through the market like water finding a path, never stopping in one place too long, never drawing attention. Margarethe paused at a vegetable stall, haggled over withered turnips. Moved to a rack of used clothing, examined a cloak with holes. All the while, she was angling them toward Oskar's stall from a different direction, keeping crowds between them and the watching Gray Robe.

Finally, she stopped at a stall selling candles. From here, they had a clear line of sight to Oskar, but the Gray Robe was blocked by women haggling over fish.

"Now," Margarethe said. "Quick and quiet."

They crossed to Oskar's stall. Up close, the bookseller was even older than Thomas had thought. Ink-stained hands, fingers bent with age or arthritis. But his eyes were sharp behind wrinkled lids. Intelligent. Watchful.

He looked at Margarethe and went completely still.

"Heinrich's daughter," he said softly, voice rough from disuse. "I thought you'd fled the city. People said you left after... after your father."

"I tried." Margarethe leaned over a displayed gospel, feigning interest, keeping her voice low. "Got pulled back. Unfinished business."

"The dangerous kind, I imagine." But his eyes were kind. Sad. "Heinrich was a good man. I'm sorry for what they did to him."

"So am I." Margarethe turned a page, casual. "I need information, Oskar."

"Information always costs." He glanced toward where the Gray Robe was still watching, though his view was obscured. "Though for Heinrich's memory, perhaps we can negotiate a discount."

"The Order is hunting someone. Wilhelm of Eltville's grandson." She turned another page. "What's the bounty?"

Oskar's expression didn't change, but his voice dropped even lower. "Fifty gold marks. Alive, preferably. Dead if necessary. They want what he's carrying more than they want him breathing."

Thomas felt cold despite his cloak. Fifty gold marks was more money than most people in Mainz saw in a lifetime.

"Who's hunting?"

"Everyone." Oskar glanced again toward the Gray Robe. "Fifty marks is a year's income for a skilled craftsman. Two years for a laborer. The Order has made it clear—turn in the boy, no questions asked. Half the city is looking. The other half is staying quiet and hoping he gets away."

"And which half are you?"

"The half that remembers Wilhelm was a good man who deserved better." Oskar pulled another book from his stock—a prayer book, small and worn. He showed Margarethe something inside. Thomas couldn't see what. "There's talk of a cathedral search. Tonight. The Order believes Wilhelm hid something there before he died. Documents, maybe. Or another key."

"Did he?" Margarethe's voice was carefully neutral.

"If he did, I don't know where. He kept his secrets close." Oskar looked at Thomas for the first time. Really looked, sharp eyes taking in every detail. "You have his eyes. The pattern-seeing gift. I heard it ran in the family, but seeing it..." He shook his head. "Try not to die, boy. The world needs what you're carrying."

Thomas swallowed. "I'm trying."

"Try harder." Oskar handed the prayer book to Margarethe. "For your troubles. No charge. Consider it a gift in Heinrich's memory."

Margarethe took it, slipped it into her cloak without examining it. "Thank you, old friend."

"Don't thank me yet. The Order is tightening the circle. If you're still in Mainz tomorrow, you'll be in chains. Or dead." He looked past them, expression shifting. "And you're being watched. The Gray Robe is coming this way. Move. Now."

"Run or hide?"

"Neither. Disappear." Oskar raised his voice suddenly, addressing the crowd. "These merchants were just leaving! No purchase today, alas, but perhaps next market when their prices are more reasonable!"

Margarethe nodded, loud enough for others to hear. "Your prices are too high, old man. I'll find better elsewhere."

"My prices are fair!" Oskar shot back, equally loud. "It's your purse that's too light!"

They moved away, blending into the crowd. Thomas's pulse thundered in his ears. He wanted desperately to look back, but Margarethe's earlier instruction held firm: *Don't draw attention.*

They wove through the market. Past the plague cart loading new bodies—Thomas tried not to look, but caught a glimpse of a small hand, a child's hand, dangling over the edge. Past a preacher standing on an overturned crate, shouting about God's judgment. Margarethe's hand found Thomas's elbow, guided him left into a narrow space between stalls, then right into an alley that stank of piss and rotting garbage.

Finally, they stopped in a recessed doorway—some abandoned shop, its door boarded over. Margarethe checked the street behind them, hand on her dagger.

"Did we lose him?"

"Perhaps. Or perhaps he knows where we're going and doesn't need to follow." She pulled out the prayer book Oskar had given her and opened it carefully.

Inside the front cover, written in tiny, cramped script barely legible, was a message:

*Cathedral. Tonight. Midnight. Library accessible through chapter house. Code: Psalm 117, verse 1. Brother Marcus is sympathetic. Will let you in. But leave before Lauds or Anselm will know. One hour. No more. —O*

Margarethe read it twice, lips moving silently as she memorized each word. Then she tore out the page, balled it up, and put it in her mouth. Chewed.

Thomas stared. "You're eating—"

She swallowed. "Information that can't be stolen is information that survives." She closed the book, dropped it in the gutter where it landed in something foul. "We have our window. Midnight. That gives us eight hours to prepare."

"Prepare how?"

Margarethe's smile was sharp as broken glass. "I'm going to teach you not to be completely useless. Come on."

They disappeared into the warren of Mainz's back streets, leaving the market and its hunters behind.

## Scene 3: Margarethe's Training Montage

Back in the warehouse, Margarethe transformed from merchant to teacher.

Not the patient teacher from this morning. This was a harder kind of teaching. The kind that left bruises.

"First lesson," she said, bolting the door behind them. "Silence."

Thomas blinked. "What?"

"You move like a drunken cow. Every step announces you're coming." She pointed to the far end of the warehouse, where stacks of fabric bales created a makeshift maze. "In the cathedral tonight, you need to be a ghost. Walk from here to there without making a sound."

Thomas looked at the distance. Perhaps thirty feet. "That's all?"

"Try it."

He walked. Tried to step carefully, to be quiet.

His sandals scuffed against the stone floor—a harsh scraping sound that echoed. A crate creaked as he brushed past it. Somewhere in the darkness, a rat squeaked and fled.

Margarethe shook her head. "Again. Slower this time. Think about each step."

Thomas tried. Concentrated on placing each foot carefully.

His robe caught on a protruding nail, ripped with a loud *tear* that made him flinch.

"I'm not built for this," Thomas said, frustration rising.

"No one is built for this. It's a skill, not a talent." Margarethe walked the same path—silent, fluid, barely disturbing the air. "Watch my feet. I step heel-to-toe when I need to be quiet, toe-to-heel when I need speed. I test my weight before committing fully to each step."

She demonstrated again, slower so he could see every movement. The placement of her feet. The way she shifted her weight. The deliberate care.

"Your turn."

He tried. Failed. The floor creaked under his weight.

Tried again. His robe swished too loudly.

An hour passed. Thomas's frustration mounted with each failure.

"Stop," Margarethe said.

Thomas stopped.

"You're in your head. You're thinking too much, trying to control every tiny movement." She walked over, adjusted his posture with firm hands on his shoulders. "Feel the floor. Don't think about stepping—feel where your weight wants to go. Your body knows how to move. Trust it."

Thomas closed his eyes. Tried to feel instead of think.

He took a step. Quieter. Not silent, but better.

"Good," Margarethe said. "Again."

By the end of the second hour, Thomas could cross the warehouse with only occasional small sounds. Not perfect. Not even close to Margarethe's ghost-like silence. But no longer announcing himself like a town crier.

Margarethe nodded approval. "Better. You learn fast when you stop fighting yourself. Next lesson."

She pulled out a simple door lock—old iron, common to half the buildings in Mainz. Set it on the table with two thin pieces of metal. Lockpicks. "The cathedral library will be locked. I can pick it, but if something happens to me, you need to be able to get in."

She demonstrated, sliding the thin metal picks into the mechanism with practiced ease. "Feel for the pins. Gentle pressure. Don't force it. Let the lock tell you what it wants."

She popped it open, made it look effortless.

"Your turn."

Thomas took the picks. They were awkward in his hands—too thin, too delicate.

He slid them into the lock. Felt... nothing. Just metal scraping metal.

"I don't feel anything."

"You're not listening." Margarethe guided his hands, adjusting the angle. "The lock is a pattern. Pins arranged in a sequence. Each one has a position it wants to reach. Your job is to find that position. It's not about force—it's about understanding."

Pattern.

The word shifted something in Thomas's mind. Not force—pattern-sense. Like the cipher. Like the dots in the manuscripts. A puzzle waiting to be solved.

He tried again. This time, focusing not on opening the lock but on *listening* to it. Feeling for the pattern.

He felt... something. A resistance in one pin. A tiny amount of give. He adjusted the tension on his pick, felt the resistance shift slightly.

Click.

One pin set.

"There," Margarethe said, surprised. "You felt it."

Thomas's concentration narrowed to the lock and nothing else. The second pin was harder to find. He probed gently, searching for the pattern.

Click.

Third pin. Fourth pin.

The lock opened.

Thomas stared at it, stunned.

"How did you—" Margarethe took the lock back, examining it. "That took me three weeks to learn. You just did it in ten minutes."

"It's patterns," Thomas said, still breathless. "Like the cipher. Each pin is a position in a sequence. Once I stopped thinking of it as forcing something and started thinking of it as solving a puzzle..."

Margarethe sat back, studying him with new eyes. "Wilhelm really did see something in you. Third lesson: lying."

"What?"

"You need to be able to lie convincingly. If we're caught, if we're questioned, you need to sell a story." She crossed her arms, face settling into stern skepticism. "Convince me you're a pilgrim on your way to Cologne. Go."

Thomas tried. Stammered through a story about visiting a shrine to Saint Ursula, praying for his sick mother.

Margarethe's expression was flat. "I'd arrest you immediately. Again."

He tried again. Somehow worse. He could feel his face flushing, his tells broadcasting every lie.

Fifteen minutes later, Margarethe held up a hand. "Stop. Just stop. You're hopeless at this."

"I know."

"No, I mean truly, catastrophically, dangerously hopeless. Your face gives away everything you're thinking." She pinched the bridge of her nose. "Fine. New plan: you don't talk. If we're questioned, you're a mute. Took a vow of silence. Religious devotion. I'll do all the talking."

"I can try to get better—"

"No. Some people are born liars—they slip into falsehoods like water. You're born honest. It's not a flaw in normal life. But in this life, in this moment, it's a liability." She sighed. "Just don't talk unless absolutely necessary. Understood?"

"Understood."

"Last lesson." She pulled out two practice daggers—blunted blades, rounded edges, safe for training but still heavy enough to hurt. "Defense. I'm not teaching you to fight. I'm teaching you not to die immediately when someone tries to kill you."

She handed him one blade. "Hold it like this. Blade down, close to your body. It's not a sword—you don't swing it around. You thrust, you slash close, you create distance and run."

She demonstrated. Fast, economical movements that wasted no energy. Efficient violence.

"Now defend yourself."

She came at him—slow, telegraphed, giving him all the time in the world to react.

Thomas stumbled backward, nearly dropped the blade, tripped over his own feet, and fell hard on his ass. The impact knocked the wind out of him.

Margarethe stood over him, one eyebrow raised.

Then she laughed.

It was the first real laugh Thomas had heard from her—not bitter, not sharp with dark humor, just genuine. It transformed her entire face, softened every hard line. For a moment, she wasn't a survivor or a killer or a keeper of dangerous secrets. She was just a woman who found something funny.

"I'm sorry," she said, still laughing, extending a hand. "I know I shouldn't laugh. But you looked exactly like a startled chicken."

Thomas took her hand, let her pull him to his feet. He was grinning despite his embarrassment. "I warned you I was useless."

"Useless is a strong word. Entertainingly incompetent, perhaps." She was still smiling. "Again. And this time, try to stay on your feet for at least three seconds."

They trained for another hour. Thomas didn't improve much—he simply wasn't built for physical combat. But Margarethe taught him a few basics: how to create distance, how to protect his vitals, how to run effectively without tripping over his own robe.

"You're not going to win a fight," she said finally, wiping sweat from her forehead. "But you might survive one long enough for me to kill whoever's attacking you."

"Reassuring."

"It's realistic." She sheathed the practice blades. "You're a scholar, Thomas. Not a warrior. Use your strengths—patterns, locks, thinking three steps ahead. Leave the violence to me."

"What if something happens to you?"

"Then you run." Her expression softened—just a flicker before the mask returned. A pause, then: "You asked about my scar."

Not an answer. A deflection. But the closest thing to trust she'd offered.

Thomas looked at the thin line along her jaw.

"I got it trusting the wrong person," Margarethe said quietly. Her hand drifted unconsciously to her daggers. "A merchant who said he was part of the network. Said he could get me into a library the Order had seized. Turned out he was an informant. Led me straight into a trap."

"How did you escape?"

"Oskar got me out. Traded information for my release—valuable locations, names of safe houses." Her voice was tight with old grief. "Three people died because of what he gave up. The Order hunted them down one by one over six months. Good people. Part of the network. All dead because of my mistake. Because I trusted when I should have been suspicious."

Thomas's chest tightened. "I'm sorry."

"Don't be. It taught me a lesson: trust is expensive, and betrayal is cheap." She met his eyes directly. "I'm telling you this because tonight, in that cathedral, we're going to be trusting a lot. Trusting Oskar's information. Trusting Brother Marcus to let us in. That's a dangerous amount of trust."

"Are you saying we shouldn't go?"

"No. I'm saying be ready for it to go wrong. Be ready to run." She pulled her cloak on, checked that her real daggers—sharp blades, killing blades—were secure. "And Thomas?"

"Aye?"

"If something goes wrong tonight—if I tell you to run—you actually do it this time. You're carrying your grandfather's legacy. That matters more than my life or yours."

"You matter to me," Thomas said before he could stop himself.

Margarethe froze. For a moment, something flickered across her face—surprise, maybe, or fear. Something vulnerable that she quickly hid.

"Don't," she said quietly. "Don't make me matter. Everyone who matters to me dies."

"Perhaps the pattern is wrong," Thomas said. "Perhaps you've just been unlucky."

"There's no such thing as luck. Just preparation and consequences." But there was something in her eyes now—not warmth, exactly. But perhaps the possibility of it. A crack in the wall. "Come on. It's almost nightfall. We need to eat something, rest if we can. Midnight comes faster than you think."

She walked past him toward the corner where they'd stored the food.

Thomas stood there a moment longer, feeling the weight of the coming night settle on his shoulders.

Tonight, they would break into the cathedral.

Tonight, they would face the Order on their own ground.

And tonight, he would discover if he was truly Wilhelm's grandson—or just another weak boy pretending to be brave.

But for now, in this moment, with Margarethe's rare laughter still echoing in the warehouse and the iron key heavy in his pocket, Thomas allowed himself to hope.

Perhaps they could do this.

Perhaps they could survive.

Perhaps he wasn't useless after all.

## Scene 4: The Cathedral Infiltration

Midnight.

They crouched in the shadows across from Cathedral St. Martin, watching. Waiting.

The great church loomed against the night sky like a mountain of stone and faith, its spire a dark needle pointing toward uncaring stars. Plague bells rang somewhere in the city—another cart of bodies making its slow journey to the mass graves. The distant rumble of wooden wheels on cobblestones, the murmured prayers of the cart drivers, all of it muffled by November cold. Thomas pulled his cloak tighter, breath coming out in visible clouds.

"Remember the plan," Margarethe whispered. "Chapter house entrance on the south side. Psalm 117, verse 1—*Praise the Lord, all nations*. Brother Marcus should let us in. If he doesn't, if something's wrong, we run. No hesitation."

"And if he does?"

"Then we have one hour. Perhaps less. The Gray Robes change guard at Lauds—that's our deadline." She checked the street—empty, silent except for distant coughing from a plague house three doors down. "Stay close. Stay quiet. And for the love of God, don't trip over your own feet."

"I've been practicing."

"I know. You're almost competent now." But she was smiling in the darkness.

They crossed the square quickly, keeping to the shadows cast by the moon. The cathedral's side entrance was a small wooden door, unremarkable and easy to miss unless you knew it was there.

Margarethe knocked. Three soft raps.

Silence.

Thomas's heart hammered hard enough that he was sure it could be heard. This was it. If Oskar had been wrong, if Brother Marcus wasn't sympathetic, if the Order was waiting—

The door opened a crack.

A young monk peered out, his face pale and nervous in the candlelight he carried. "Yes?"

Margarethe leaned close, pitched her voice just loud enough to be heard. "*Praise the Lord, all nations*," she quoted softly.

The monk's eyes widened. Recognition and fear in equal measure. He glanced behind him into the darkness of the chapter house, then opened the door wider. "Quickly. Before someone sees."

They slipped inside.

The chapter house was dark except for the single candle the monk carried, its flame barely pushing back the shadows. The smell hit Thomas immediately—frankincense and myrrh, sweet and heavy, mixing with the musty scent of old stone and the clean bite of beeswax. Wood polish. Parchment dust. So different from the plague-stink of the streets that it almost made him dizzy.

"Brother Marcus?" Margarethe asked.

"Aye." The monk bolted the door behind them—three heavy bolts sliding home with solid clicks. He was young, Thomas realized. Perhaps twenty-five, with the kind of face that looked like it wanted to be kind but had learned not to be. "Oskar sent word you'd come. I've been watching for you since Compline."

"The library?"

Marcus gestured down a dark corridor with his candle. "Through the nave, past the high altar, behind the choir. The door is locked, but..." He looked at Margarethe's face, seemed to understand. "You'll manage, I imagine."

"Why are you helping us?" Thomas couldn't stop himself from asking.

Marcus's smile was sad, tinged with old grief. "Because Wilhelm of Eltville was kind to me when I was a novice and everyone else treated me like I was invisible. Because knowledge should serve God and humanity, not power and control. Because the Order has forgotten what mercy means." He handed Margarethe the candle, and his hand shook slightly. "One hour. No more. After that, I can't help you. I won't even be able to pretend I didn't know you were here."

"Thank you," Margarethe said quietly.

"Don't thank me until you're safely out." He opened the interior door. "Go. And may God protect you, because I can't."

They went.

The nave of the cathedral was vast and dark, their footsteps echoing despite Thomas's best efforts at silence. Each step seemed to announce itself to the vaulted ceiling, to the painted saints staring down from the walls. Moonlight filtered through stained glass windows, painting the floor in patches of deep red and blue—colors that looked like blood and bruises in the darkness.

They reached the high altar. Behind it, a smaller door—the entrance to the choir and, beyond that, the library.

Locked.

Margarethe pulled out her picks, but Thomas stopped her with a touch on her arm. "Let me try."

She hesitated for just a moment, then nodded. "Fast as you can. We're exposed here."

Thomas knelt before the lock. Inserted the picks with trembling hands. Felt for the pattern the way Margarethe had taught him.

This lock was more complex—six pins instead of four, the tolerances tighter, the springs stiffer. But the principle was the same. Pattern recognition. Understanding the sequence. Listening to what the lock wanted to tell him.

First pin.

Second pin.

Third—

The third pin stuck, resisting. Thomas adjusted the tension on his pick, probed more gently.

*Come on. Show me where you want to be. Tell me the pattern.*

Click.

Fourth pin.

Fifth pin.

Sixth pin.

The lock opened.

"Forty seconds," Margarethe breathed. Her eyes widened. "You did that in forty seconds."

Thomas stood, hands shaking from adrenaline and intense concentration. "The pattern was clear once I stopped trying to force it."

"You're going to be insufferable about this, aren't you?" But she was smiling.

They slipped into the library.

And Thomas forgot how to breathe.

The room was enormous—two full stories of manuscripts and knowledge, treasures beyond counting. Chained volumes lined the walls and filled standing desks. Latin, Greek, even some Arabic texts that must have cost a fortune. Theological works, natural philosophy, astronomy, medicine. The collected wisdom of centuries.

Or hoarded and controlled, depending on your perspective. Depending on whether you were Wilhelm, who believed knowledge should be free, or the Order, who believed it should be guarded.

"There," Margarethe pointed to a desk near the back. "The Boethius commentaries. That's where Hamo was working before they killed him."

Thomas crossed to the desk on legs that felt suddenly unsteady and found the commentary—the same thick volume he'd seen that terrible morning. Someone had cleaned it carefully, but he could still see faint brown stains on the vellum.

Hamo's blood.

He pushed down the grief that rose like bile in his throat. *Later. Grieve later. Right now, finish what he started.*

He pulled out the iron key, studied the manuscript margins. Colored dots, carefully placed with precision that spoke of years of practice. Red and blue and yellow, encoding information that had cost so many lives.

"Which page?" Margarethe asked. She'd moved to watch the door, hand resting on her dagger.

"I don't... wait." Thomas flipped through carefully, treating the blood-stained vellum with reverent hands. The dots appeared on multiple pages, but one section had a much denser concentration. The commentary on *Consolation of Philosophy*, Book V. The section discussing fate and free will and the nature of divine knowledge.

How grimly appropriate.

He found what looked like a textual anchor—the phrase *"divine foreknowledge"* underlined in faded ink. Aligned the key with careful precision.

Through the aperture, dots appeared in sharp focus. Thomas counted automatically.

"Five red, two blue, three yellow," he murmured. "Position five-two-three. That's..." He closed his eyes, searching his memory. "I don't know that code."

"Let me see." Margarethe leaned over his shoulder, close enough that he could smell rosemary and steel. She looked at the pattern and her face went pale. "That's not a location code."

"Then what is it?"

"A warning." She pointed to the specific arrangement with a trembling finger. "See how the blue dots bracket the red ones in pairs? That's a danger pattern—it means the information itself is dangerous. And the count—five-two-three—that's a date marker."

"Date of what?"

"I don't know. The cipher usually marks places, not events." She stopped, staring at the page more closely. "Thomas. Look at the text itself. Not the dots. The actual words Hamo was copying."

Thomas read the Latin. Standard theological discussion of divine providence and whether God knew the future or whether free will existed.

Except.

In the margin, in tiny script barely visible even in the candlelight, was a notation in different ink. Fresher ink. Hamo's handwriting.

*They know what's coming. They always have. The network didn't predict the plague—they prepared for it. How far back does this pattern go?*

Thomas's hands went cold.

"He figured it out," Margarethe whispered. "Hamo realized the cipher wasn't just recording plague survival patterns. It was—"

"Expecting it." Thomas flipped back through the manuscript with growing urgency. More marginal notes in Hamo's increasingly frantic hand:

*Dots placed fifty years before plague arrival in some regions. Impossible unless they knew it was coming. Unless they planned it.*

*Check Constantinople records. Plague of Justinian, 541 CE. Do the patterns match?*

*If true, then Wilhelm was right. The network is old. Older than the Church. Older than—*

The last entry stopped mid-sentence, ink trailing off. That must have been when the Gray Robes came for him. When Brother Anselm's blade ended his work forever.

"We need to copy this," Thomas said urgently. "All of it. If Hamo died to understand this—"

"No time." Margarethe had moved to the door, listening intently. "We've been here twenty minutes already. We need to find what we came for and leave before—"

Footsteps in the corridor outside.

Multiple sets. Heavy boots, not the soft sandals of monks.

Margarethe's hand went to her dagger in one smooth motion. "Gray Robes."

"Brother Marcus—"

"Either he betrayed us or they caught him. Doesn't matter which." Margarethe looked around the library with quick, desperate eyes. One entrance. No windows large enough for escape. Stone walls too thick to break through. "We're trapped."

The footsteps were getting closer. Calm voices, methodical:

"—check the library first—"

"—Brother Marcus confessed they entered through the chapter house—"

"—Brother Anselm wants them alive if possible, but dead is acceptable—"

"The key," Margarethe hissed. "Use it. Find the next location—now. If we're going to die here, at least understand what we're dying for."

Thomas turned back to the manuscript, hands shaking so badly he could barely hold the key steady. Forced himself to concentrate. *Breathe. Focus. See the pattern.*

Another page. Even denser concentration of dots, arranged in patterns he'd never seen before. He aligned the key, barely breathing.

Seven red, seven blue, seven yellow. Position seven-seven-seven.

"What does that mean?"

Margarethe's eyes widened as she looked at the pattern. "Seven. That's the sacred number. Seven days of creation, seven cardinal virtues, seven—"

"Seven keys," Thomas breathed, understanding hitting him like cold water. "This isn't encoding a location. It's encoding a revelation."

"What?"

Thomas stared at the pattern, forcing his grandfather's gift to work. Pattern recognition. See the whole, not just the parts.

He looked at the entire page, not just the dots in isolation. The text, the illuminations around the margins, the structure of how everything fit together.

And he saw it.

The dots weren't just markers for locations or warnings. They formed lines. Connections. A map.

But not a map of places.

A map of people. Of bloodlines. Of families tracked across generations.

"They weren't just recording who survived the plague," Thomas said, voice shaking. "They were tracking bloodlines. Specific families. Specific traits passed down through generations." He pointed to the genealogical pattern in the dots. "This manuscript is from 1200—nearly 150 years old. The plague came in 1347. They were tracking these families for over a century before the plague arrived. Before it was supposed to arrive."

The footsteps were in the library now. Searching systematically. Getting closer.

"—check between the manuscript stacks—"

"—they have to be here somewhere—"

Margarethe grabbed Thomas's arm hard enough to hurt. "We need to go. Right now."

"There's no other way out—"

"Then we make one." She looked up at the library's second floor—a gallery with more manuscripts. And high on the wall, narrow windows. Too small for a grown man to fit through.

But perhaps not too small for a thin scribe.

"Up," Margarethe commanded. "Move!"

They ran for the spiral stairs to the gallery. The Gray Robes saw them—shouts echoed through the library:

"There! The upper level!"

"Cut them off at the stairs!"

Thomas and Margarethe reached the gallery, iron-shod boots pounding up the stairs behind them. The high windows were twenty feet above the ground outside.

Margarethe pulled out a dagger, used the pommel to smash the glass. The sound was deafeningly loud—glass shattering, shards falling. An alarm for anyone within a hundred yards.

Shouts below. Multiple voices. Footsteps thundering on the stairs.

"Go!" Margarethe boosted Thomas toward the broken window. Sharp glass edges tore at his clothes, cut his arms, but he was thin enough to fit. Barely. He squeezed through, emerged onto a narrow stone ledge running along the cathedral's exterior.

Two stories up. The cobblestone square far below, hard and unforgiving.

Margarethe followed him through the window with practiced grace. A Gray Robe behind her tried to squeeze through, got stuck halfway, cursed.

"Can you climb?" Margarethe asked, already scanning the wall.

Thomas looked up at the rough-cut stone, the carved decorations, the gargoyles leering from the eaves. "I have no choice, do I?"

"Not really."

Behind them, the Gray Robe was pulling himself back inside, shouting orders: "Get archers! Now!"

Thomas started climbing.

He jammed his fingers into carved grooves meant for gargoyle drainage. Stone bit into his palms. His arms screamed protest, muscles burning from shoulder to wrist. Sharp edges opened his fingertips, blood making every grip slick and uncertain.

One foot slipped.

For a terrible moment he hung by his fingers alone, two stories of empty air below him.

"Don't look down," Margarethe called. She climbed beside him with easy grace. "Focus on the next handhold. One at a time."

Thomas didn't feel fine. He felt like he was about to die.

But he climbed.

His world narrowed to stone and breath and burning muscles.

Reach.

Grip.

Pull.

Find the next hold.

Don't think about falling.

He reached the roof edge and pulled himself over with the last of his strength, lay gasping on the cold tiles. His arms felt like they were on fire.

Margarethe landed beside him, barely winded. "We need to keep moving. They'll have archers—"

An arrow struck the tiles three inches from Thomas's head, the impact sending chips of ceramic flying.

"—if they think to post them," Margarethe finished. "Move!"

They ran.

The cathedral roof was a landscape of slopes and spires, gargoyles and gutters, treacherous tiles that cracked and slid under their feet. Wind bit through Thomas's thin robes—November cold at rooftop height, sharp enough to steal breath. Below, more shouts. The Gray Robes were gathering, spreading around the building like wolves circling prey.

"There!" Margarethe pointed to an adjacent building—a wealthy merchant's house, its roof close enough that the gap between them was perhaps six feet.

She didn't hesitate. Ran and jumped the gap in one smooth motion, landing in a crouch on the other roof.

Thomas skidded to the edge. Six feet of empty air yawned between the buildings. The cobblestones below swam in his vision—three stories down, hard enough to shatter bone. His legs locked.

"Thomas!" Margarethe called. "Jump! Now!"

More arrows whistled past. Behind him, a Gray Robe was pulling himself onto the roof, sword in hand.

Thomas backed up three steps. Ran. Leaped into the void.

For a moment, he was flying. Suspended over empty space. The ground was so far below. Time stretched and slowed and he was certain he wouldn't make it—

Then Margarethe's hand caught his wrist, yanking him forward with strength he didn't know she had. He crashed onto the merchant's roof tiles hard enough to knock the wind out of him.

"I hate you," Thomas gasped.

"You love me," she shot back, grinning wildly despite everything. "Now move before they start shooting at us again!"

They ran across the merchant's roof, down to a lower level, through someone's rooftop garden with its winter-dead plants, finally dropping into a narrow alley. Margarethe landed in a perfect crouch. Thomas landed in an ungraceful heap that would definitely leave bruises.

"I really need to teach you how to fall properly," Margarethe said, pulling him to his feet.

They ran through the night-maze of Mainz's back streets, the alarm bells ringing behind them, the Order rousing to hunt them down with renewed fury.

But they were alive.

And they had the knowledge Hamo had died to protect.

The cipher wasn't just a map of hidden texts and secret knowledge.

It was a map of human bloodlines, tracked for generations before the plague. Before it should have been possible to know it was coming.

The network had known. Had prepared. Had guided specific families to specific places.

The question was why.

And Thomas suspected—no, he *knew*—that the answer was going to be worse than he could possibly imagine.

## Scene 5: The Confrontation

They didn't make it back to the warehouse.

The streets were too watched now, too dangerous. The Order had turned out in full strength—Thomas could hear whistles in the distance, calling search parties together. They'd killed Brother Anselm's men yesterday. Now they'd broken into the cathedral itself. The Order wouldn't rest until they were captured or dead.

Margarethe led them instead to a different safe house—a tanner's shop in the tanners' quarter, abandoned since the plague had killed its owner two months ago.

The smell hit Thomas like a fist. Half-worked hides rotting in their lime baths. Tanning lye so caustic it burned the back of his throat. Underneath it all, the sweet corruption of flesh left too long in summer heat and never properly cleared away. His eyes watered. His stomach turned.

But it was shelter.

It was hidden.

They'd been there perhaps an hour, catching their breath, tending to Thomas's scraped hands and Margarethe's cut arm from the broken window glass, when the door opened.

No warning.

No sound of picking the lock.

Just the door swinging open silently, hinges moving without even a whisper of protest.

Brother Anselm walked in alone.

He moved like water flowing downhill—calm, inevitable, impossible to stop. The lamplight they'd lit caught his gray robes, made the silver key pendant at his throat gleam. His footsteps were soundless on the wooden floor. His face was gentle, sad, almost grandfatherly. Kind eyes in a weathered face. He looked like someone who would listen to your confession and offer comfort.

Thomas had never been more terrified in his life.

Margarethe's dagger was out instantly. "Don't move."

"I'm not here to fight, Heinrich's daughter." Anselm raised his hands slowly, showing they were empty. His voice was soft, reasonable—the voice of a patient teacher. "I'm here to talk. To offer you both a choice before this ends badly for everyone."

"The Order doesn't talk," Margarethe said. Her hands trembled, knuckles white around her dagger hilts. "It tortures and kills. Like it did to my father."

"Sometimes. When necessary. When there's no other option." Anselm's voice carried genuine regret. "Your father, for instance. I took no pleasure in his death, Margarethe. None. But he wouldn't listen. Wouldn't understand what was truly at stake."

Margarethe's hand shook with fury. "You *murdered* him."

"I gave him a chance to join us. To help preserve what matters most—human knowledge, human survival. He chose ideology over pragmatism." Anselm looked at Thomas, eyes genuinely sad. "Wilhelm made the same choice. Brilliant men, both of them. Gifted with intelligence and vision. Wasted on impossible ideals."

"Don't speak about my grandfather."

"Why not? I knew him well. We studied together in Paris, decades ago. Before he decided that knowledge should belong to everyone instead of being protected by those wise enough to guard it." Anselm moved further into the room with slow, non-threatening steps. "You found the Boethius manuscript. You saw what your friend Hamo discovered before we had to stop him."

"The bloodlines," Thomas said. "You're tracking families across generations."

"We're preserving families." Anselm's voice carried absolute conviction. "Do you know what happened before Rome fell? Before the Bronze Age collapsed into darkness? Before every great realm in history died and left nothing but ruins?"

Neither of them answered.

"Knowledge was shared freely," Anselm continued. "Libraries open to anyone. Texts copied and distributed without control. Every fool with a question could access power they didn't understand—medicine that could kill, geometry that could destroy, philosophy that could tear apart the bonds that held cities and kingdoms together. And every time—every time—they destroyed themselves with the tools they didn't respect."

"So you hoard knowledge to prevent catastrophe," Margarethe said bitterly.

"We preserve knowledge to ensure survival." Anselm looked at her directly, real pain in his eyes. "Your father's work, Wilhelm's cipher network—you think it was about spreading knowledge, freeing learning, serving some noble ideal of universal enlightenment. It wasn't. It was about selection. About ensuring the right knowledge reached the right people at the right time."

"Who decides what's right?" Thomas demanded. "Who gave you the authority to choose?"

"Those who understand the patterns. Those who can see the long arc of history instead of just the immediate moment." Anselm gestured to the iron key visible in Thomas's pocket. "That key is one of seven. Each one unlocks a piece of the Genesis Protocol—the complete accumulated knowledge of how kingdoms fall and how they might be saved. The network compiled it after Rome fell, to ensure the next age would be better. More stable. Less prone to collapse."

"By controlling who survives?"

"By ensuring the people who can survive—who carry the right traits, the right knowledge, the right bloodlines—are positioned correctly when catastrophe comes." Anselm pulled out a manuscript from his robes—Thomas recognized it as another copy of the Boethius commentary. "The plague currently killing Europe isn't random chance or God's punishment. It follows specific patterns. Trade routes. Population centers. Specific family lineages. The cipher tracks who has natural resistance in their blood, who doesn't. We guide the resistant ones to safe locations. We... redirect the others to places where the plague will find them."

Margarethe made a strangled sound. "You're helping the plague kill people. You're not just allowing it—you're *directing* it."

"We're helping the right people survive while the wrong ones die." Anselm's voice was patient, like explaining simple mathematics to a slow child. "Without our intervention, the plague kills indiscriminately. With our guidance, it becomes selection. Painful, yes. Tragic, certainly. But necessary for what comes after."

"Madness," Thomas whispered.

"Survival." Anselm looked at him with something almost like pity. "Thomas, you have your grandfather's gift. Pattern sense. Uncanny memory. The ability to see connections others miss. Do you think that's coincidence? That you just happened to be born with those exact traits?"

Thomas's world tilted sideways.

"No," Anselm continued softly. "You're carrying the very traits the network has been breeding for forty generations. Your family line was selected, guided, preserved specifically to make someone like you. Your grandfather's marriage was arranged. Your grandmother's bloodline was chosen. Even your parents' union, reluctant as it was—all of it guided by the network to create the perfect combination of traits."

"No," Thomas said. The word came out hollow, unconvincing even to his own ears.

"Aye. Wilhelm understood this. He realized what he was—their handiwork, built through generations of careful breeding. And it horrified him. So he tried to rebel, to spread knowledge freely, to unmake the thing that had made him. And it killed him." Anselm took a step closer, voice gentle. "You can make a different choice, Thomas. Join us. Use your gift to help select the next generation. Help humanity survive what's coming."

"What's coming?" Margarethe asked, voice deadly quiet.

"Worse than plague. Worse than Rome's fall. Worse than anything that's come before." Anselm's face was grave. "The cycles are accelerating. Bronze Age collapse, 1200 BCE. Rome, 400 CE. Now, 1347. Again and again, everything burns. The Genesis Protocol was meant to ensure that when it does, the right people are positioned to raise the world again from ash. But only if we complete it. Only if we preserve the bloodlines and the knowledge through this collapse."

"And if we refuse?" Thomas asked, though he already knew the answer.

"Then you die tonight. Margarethe dies tonight. And the knowledge you're carrying dies with you." Anselm's voice hardened slightly, iron underneath the gentle surface. "I'm offering you a choice your grandfather didn't get, Thomas. Join us willingly, or die fighting us uselessly. But either way, the Protocol continues. It always continues."

Thomas looked at Margarethe. She was rigid with fury, her dagger still pointed at Anselm's heart, hand perfectly steady despite everything. But her eyes met Thomas's, and in them he saw a question:

*What do we do?*

Thomas thought about Hamo, dying in the scriptorium with a blade in his chest. About his grandfather Wilhelm, murdered for believing knowledge should be free. About all the people the Order had killed in the name of preservation and survival.

And he thought about the bloodlines in the manuscript. The families tracked for generations like breeding stock. The certainty that his entire existence had been shaped, bred, guided. That he wasn't a person so much as something made.

"No," he said quietly. The word felt like stepping off a cliff.

Anselm's expression didn't change. "You're certain? You understand what you're refusing?"

"Knowledge should be free," Thomas said, voice growing stronger. "Even dangerous knowledge. Especially dangerous knowledge. Because the only thing more dangerous than knowledge freely shared is knowledge hoarded by those who think they're wise enough to control it."

He pulled out the iron key, held it up. "My grandfather believed that. So do I."

"Then you're a fool, like he was." Anselm's hand moved—faster than seemed possible for a man his age—and suddenly there was a dagger in it. Long, thin, deadly. "And fools don't survive long in this world."

He lunged at Thomas.

Margarethe intercepted, her blade meeting his with a sound like breaking bells. Steel on steel, sharp and harsh in the small space.

They fought.

Anselm moved with brutal efficiency, every movement precise and economical. No wasted motion. No flourishes. Just practical, practiced violence from someone who'd killed more times than Thomas could imagine.

Margarethe fought with desperate skill, her father's blades extensions of her arms. But Thomas could see immediately that she was outmatched.

Anselm was better. Older, more experienced, utterly calm even in combat. He drove Margarethe back step by step, corraling her toward the corner, cutting off her angles of escape.

"You fight like your father," Anselm said, almost conversationally, not even breathing hard. "Brave. Skilled. But ultimately predictable."

Margarethe's response was a vicious slash that Anselm barely dodged, the blade close enough to cut a thin line across his cheek.

Thomas scrambled back, useless, weak, unable to help. Everything Margarethe had taught him—the silence, the locks, even the pathetic combat training—none of it mattered now. He couldn't fight someone like Anselm.

But patterns. There had to be a pattern. Some way to help.

He looked around frantically. The room. Anselm's positioning. The way he was deliberately driving Margarethe toward the corner, cutting off her escape routes, controlling the space.

Which meant his back was to Thomas. Exposed.

On the table—a leather-working tool the previous owner had left behind. Heavy. Sharp. Meant for cutting thick hides.

Thomas grabbed it.

Margarethe saw him out of the corner of her eye, understood instantly what he was planning. She feinted left—Anselm moved to block—then dove right, throwing herself clear of the line between Thomas and Anselm.

Thomas threw the tool with every ounce of his weak, useless strength.

It flew poorly—tumbling end over end, no accuracy, no power.

But it struck Anselm in the shoulder. Not lethal. Not even seriously damaging. But surprising.

The Brother spun, his dagger coming up to face this new threat—

Margarethe's blade found his exposed side, sliding between the ribs with the ease of someone who knew exactly where to strike.

Anselm made a small, surprised sound. Looked down at the blood spreading across his gray robes, dark and wet in the lamplight.

"Fools sometimes survive," Margarethe said coldly, breathing hard. "Because they're unpredictable. Because they work together instead of alone. Because they care about something more than just survival."

Anselm collapsed slowly, controlled even in falling. His breathing was shallow, wet. He looked at Thomas, and his expression was... sad. Not angry. Not hateful. Just profoundly, deeply sad.

"You don't understand what you've done," he whispered. Blood flecked his lips. "The Protocol... someone has to complete it... or everything burns... again... always again..."

"Then we'll rebuild without controlling who gets to survive," Thomas said. He knelt beside the dying man, and found himself crying for reasons he didn't fully understand. "We'll find another way."

Anselm smiled—blood on his teeth, the light fading from his eyes. "Wilhelm... would be proud... and wrong... you'll see... when the burning comes... you'll understand..."

He died.

Thomas knelt there beside the body, shaking. He'd killed someone. Not with his own hand, but his action had led directly to this death. Two nights ago, he'd been a scribe, copying texts and praying with the brothers. Now he was a killer. Now there was blood on his hands, even if the blade had been Margarethe's.

"We need to go," Margarethe said. Her voice was steady, controlled, but Thomas saw her hands shaking as she cleaned her blade. "The Order will find his body. They'll come for us with everything they have. We just killed their lead inquisitor."

"Where do we go?"

"Out of Mainz. Tonight. Before they seal the city gates." She looked at Anselm's body, and for a moment something flickered in her eyes—grief, maybe, or regret, or just exhaustion. "He was wrong. But he believed he was right. Believed it absolutely. That's almost worse than being evil. He thought he was saving the world."

Thomas nodded. Couldn't speak. His throat was too tight.

They gathered what little they had—the iron key, the manuscripts Margarethe had hidden in her cloak, a small purse of coins. Slipped out into the plague-haunted streets for what Thomas knew would be the last time.

Behind them, Mainz—the city of his grandfather's birth, the place where Brother Hamo had died, where Thomas had discovered both the cipher and Margarethe—slept on, unaware.

Ahead lay the river docks and whatever Margarethe had arranged there.

Thomas clutched the iron key in his pocket and tried not to think about Anselm's dying words:

*Someone has to complete the Protocol, or everything burns.*

What if the Order was right?

What if knowledge, freely shared, really did lead to catastrophe—to collapse—to the burning of everything they'd built?

What if he and Margarethe were wrong to fight them?

What if his grandfather had been wrong?

The questions followed him into the darkness toward the river.

But one thing was absolutely certain:

He was committed now. For better or worse, for right or wrong, there was no going back.

The path forward was set.

And soon Mainz would wake, and the hunt would begin.

# CHAPTER 3 - THE ROAD TO STRASBOURG (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: Leaving Mainz - The Escape

### Rhine River Docks, Dawn

The alarm bells started ringing while they were still half a mile from the river.

Clanging.

Urgent.

Relentless.

Thomas heard them echo across Mainz—cathedral bells, church bells, even the plague bells repurposed for a different kind of death. The Order had found Anselm's body. And now every soul in the city would be looking for them.

"Faster," Margarethe hissed, pulling him down a narrow alley reeking of tanning chemicals and waste. The stench was a wall—acrid and rotten, with a sickly sweetness beneath it. Thomas swallowed bile and forced himself forward.

They'd been moving through the pre-dawn darkness for over an hour, avoiding the main streets, keeping to the shadows between buildings. Margarethe knew every back way, every hidden path through the tanners' quarter. But the bells changed everything.

Now doors would be opening. Shutters would be thrown wide. Citizens stumbling into streets, asking questions, looking for the source of alarm.

And the Gray Robes would be hunting.

"Will he be there?" Thomas asked, breathing hard, lungs burning. "Your smuggler?"

"He'd better be. I paid him enough." Margarethe checked the street behind them, hand on the knife at her belt. Clear for now. "And if he's not, we steal a boat and hope we can outrun arrows better than guards."

The Rhine came into view between buildings—a dark ribbon reflecting the first gray light of dawn.

Water sounds reached them: the slap of small waves against pilings, the creak of wood straining against rope, the distant call of a boatman somewhere upriver. The smell changed—fish and wet wood replacing the tannery stench, morning mist carrying the scent of reeds and river mud.

The docks were mostly empty at this hour. A few fishermen were preparing their nets with the steady efficiency of decades. A merchant boat was being loaded with barrels, probably wine. And there, at the farthest dock, almost hidden in morning mist, a small river craft with a single figure waiting beside it.

Margarethe grabbed Thomas's arm, grip iron-strong. "That's him. Otto the Weasel. Don't let the name fool you—he's the best smuggler on the Rhine."

They crossed the open ground to the dock, exposed and vulnerable. Thomas kept expecting shouts, arrows, the sound of boots on cobblestones. His shoulders hunched involuntarily, anticipating the bite of steel. But they reached the boat without incident.

Otto the Weasel was aptly named—thin as a starving dog, nervous energy radiating from him, with quick eyes that never stopped moving. He looked at Margarethe and his expression shifted from relief to horror.

"You're late. And the whole city is—" He stopped, listening to the alarm bells echoing across the water. "Mother of God. What did you do?"

"Nothing that concerns you," Margarethe said, already climbing into the boat with practiced ease. "You're being paid to ask no questions and get us downriver. So get us downriver."

"I'm being paid to smuggle goods, not fugitives who've—" Otto's eyes went wide as he looked past them. "Robes. Gray Robes on the docks. They've seen us!"

Thomas spun. Three men in gray robes were running toward them, perhaps a hundred yards away and closing fast. Two had bows already strung, arrows nocked. Morning light caught on the metal arrowheads.

"Cast off!" Margarethe shouted. "Now!"

Otto fumbled with the mooring rope, hands shaking so badly he could barely grip the hemp. "This is madness, this is—"

"Now!"

The rope came free. Otto shoved the boat away from the dock with a long pole, muscles straining. They drifted into the current, agonizingly slow.

Too slow.

"Down!" Margarethe yanked Thomas to the deck.

The first arrow whistled overhead—a sound like wind through reeds but sharper, angrier. It struck the mast with a solid *thunk* that vibrated through the entire boat, wood shuddering under the impact.

The second arrow—Thomas didn't see it coming. Just heard Margarethe's sharp intake of breath, saw her stumble.

"Margarethe!"

She was still standing, but an arrow protruded from her left shoulder, just below the collarbone. Her face had gone pale as parchment. Blood was already spreading through the fabric of her cloak, a dark stain growing with terrifying speed.

"I'm fine," she said through gritted teeth. "Stay down."

A third arrow hit the side of the boat, splintering the wooden hull. A fourth splashed into the water beside them, close enough that spray hit Thomas's face.

But they were in the current now, picking up speed. Otto worked the sail with frantic efficiency, catching the morning wind that came down the valley. The distance between them and the docks grew—fifty yards, seventy-five, a hundred.

The Gray Robes had stopped shooting. Too far now. But Thomas could see more figures gathering on the docks. Someone was shouting orders. They'd pursue, of course. On horseback along the river road, or in faster boats.

"Let me see," Thomas said, reaching for Margarethe.

"I said I'm fine." She swayed, catching herself against the mast. Her breathing had gone shallow.

Thomas ignored her protest and moved closer to examine the wound. The arrow had gone deep into the muscle, maybe touching bone. Blood soaked her cloak, dripped onto the deck. Too much blood.

"You're not fine. You're bleeding."

"It's just a—" She stopped, took a breath that caught in her throat. "Just a flesh wound."

"Deep in the muscle. Maybe touching bone." Thomas had seen enough injuries in the monastery infirmary to recognize a serious wound. "It needs to come out. Now."

Otto called from the tiller, voice high with panic: "There's a medical kit under the forward bench. Basic supplies. But if you're thinking of pulling that arrow, do it quick. We can't have her screaming when we pass the watchtowers downstream."

Margarethe's jaw tightened. "I don't scream."

"Everyone screams," Otto muttered.

Thomas found the kit—leather satchel containing bandages, a bottle that smelled like strong wine, needle and thread, a knife. He brought it back to where Margarethe sat, or rather half-collapsed.

"This is going to hurt."

"Everything hurts. Just do it." But he could see the fear in her eyes now, the way her good hand gripped the mast so hard her knuckles had gone white.

Thomas had never done this before. Had watched the infirmarian treat injuries, had helped hold patients still, had fetched supplies. But never actually extracted an arrow from flesh.

He examined the shaft. Smooth wood, well-made. At least it wasn't barbed—those were nightmares to remove. This should come clean if he pulled straight.

Should.

"On three," he said. "One—"

He pulled.

Margarethe's breath hissed between her teeth, a sound like steam escaping. Her body went rigid, every muscle locked tight. But she didn't scream. Didn't even cry out. Just went taut as a drawn bowstring, her good hand clenching the mast hard enough that her knuckles cracked.

The arrow came free. Blood followed, dark and thick.

Thomas grabbed a cloth from the kit, pressed it hard against the wound. "Hold this. Press as hard as you can."

Margarethe obeyed, her face gray now, eyes slightly unfocused.

Thomas opened the bottle—definitely wine, strong and cheap. "This is going to hurt worse than the arrow."

"Just—" Margarethe managed through clenched teeth. "Just do it."

He pulled the cloth away and poured wine directly into the wound.

This time she did cry out—a sharp, bitten-off sound that ended almost before it began. Her whole body went taut, back arching.

But she didn't pass out. Just breathed in ragged gasps while Thomas worked, packing the wound with clean cloth, wrapping bandages tight around her shoulder and under her arm. His hands shook but he forced them steady.

"Done," he said finally. "It's... it's not good, but it's the best I can do."

Margarethe nodded, not speaking. Her eyes were closed, breathing shallow. Sweat beaded her forehead despite the cool morning air.

"You should lie down. Rest."

"Can't. Need to watch for—" She swayed.

Thomas caught her before she fell, eased her down onto a pile of canvas sailcloth that smelled of mildew and fish. "I'll watch. You rest."

"Thomas—"

"I'll watch," he repeated firmly. "You saved my life yesterday. Today I save yours. That's how this works."

Margarethe looked at him for a long moment. Then, finally, she nodded and closed her eyes.

Within minutes, her breathing had evened out into sleep. But Thomas could see the flush starting on her cheeks, the sweat beading despite the chill.

Fever. The wound was already infected.

Otto called softly from the stern: "She's tough, that one. Tougher than most men I've smuggled."

"How long to Strasbourg?"

"Three days if the wind holds and the weather cooperates. Perhaps four if we hit rain." Otto spat over the side. "She got family there? Someone who can care for her properly?"

"A contact. An alchemist."

"An alchemist?" Otto's eyebrows rose. "Well, they know about poisons and cures, I suppose. Let's hope he's the healing kind and not the other."

Thomas looked down at Margarethe. Her face was peaceful in sleep, but he could see the pain in the lines around her eyes, the tension in her jaw even unconscious. Three days. Perhaps four.

She had to survive that long.

Behind them, Mainz faded into the morning mist—cathedral spires disappearing, bells still ringing their alarm. Ahead, the Rhine stretched toward Strasbourg and whatever came next.

Thomas pulled out the iron key from his pocket, turned it over in his hands. The eye symbol stared back at him, unblinking. They'd killed for this. Margarethe was wounded, maybe dying, because of this.

But sitting there on a smuggler's boat with a wounded woman who trusted him to keep watch, Thomas made a silent promise to himself.

Whatever this key opened, whatever secrets the cipher held, he would see it through. Not for glory or power or even curiosity.

For his grandfather Wilhelm, who'd built this network to preserve knowledge.

For Brother Hamo, who'd died pressing this key into Thomas's palm.

For Margarethe, who'd given everything to protect knowledge she couldn't even read.

He would see it through to the end.

Whatever that end might be.

## Scene 2: The River Journey - Fever and Discovery

### Day Two on the Rhine

Margarethe's fever started that night.

Thomas tried to keep her comfortable—rigged a crude shelter from canvas to protect her from the wind, got her to drink water when she was lucid enough to swallow. But as the sun set on their first day on the river, painting the water blood-red and gold, her skin grew hot to the touch and her breathing turned ragged.

By the second morning, she was delirious.

"Father," she mumbled, eyes open but unseeing, pupils dilated and unfocused. "I remembered. All 217. I didn't forget a single one."

Thomas dampened a cloth with river water—cold, slightly muddy, probably not clean enough but better than nothing. He pressed it to her forehead, felt the fever burning through her skin. "Shhh. Rest. Save your strength."

"Can't rest. Have to remember. If I forget, they're lost." Her good hand clutched at Thomas's sleeve. "Promise me. Promise if I forget..."

"You won't forget," he said softly. "You've never forgotten. You won't start now."

"Everyone forgets eventually."

Otto glanced over from the tiller, weathered face concerned. "How is she?"

"Worse." The bandage on her shoulder was stained dark, and when he'd changed it that morning, the wound had looked angry and red, the flesh around it hot and swollen. Infection setting deep into the muscle.

"There's willow bark in the kit. Might help with the fever."

Thomas had already tried that. It hadn't helped.

He stayed by Margarethe's side as the boat drifted downstream, watching the riverbanks slide past. Small villages clustering around church spires. Terraced vineyards climbing the hillsides, grapes ripening in the autumn sun. The occasional watchtower where Otto would tense until they'd passed and no alarm was raised.

Life continuing on, oblivious.

To keep himself from panic, to keep his mind from spiraling into useless fear, Thomas turned to the manuscripts they'd taken from the cathedral library. Spread them carefully on the deck, weighted down with stones from the ballast. Used the iron key to decode what he could.

And that's when he saw it.

The pattern that changed everything.

He'd been studying a merchant's ledger from 1290—nearly sixty years old, the vellum yellowed and brittle. The marginal cipher dots were faded but still visible. He aligned the key with the textual anchor point, counted the dots that appeared through the aperture.

Position marker: 4-2-7. That corresponded to a location—somewhere in Bavaria, if he was reading the system correctly.

But beside the position marker was something else. A different notation. Not colored dots but tiny scratched symbols that looked like Roman numerals, barely visible against the vellum.

DCCXL.

Thomas's education kicked in automatically. D = 500. CC = 200. XL = 40.

740.

A date? In what system? It couldn't be years since Christ's birth—this manuscript was from 1290, so 740 would be 550 years earlier.

His heart started beating faster. He pulled out another manuscript—a theological commentary from 1215. Found a similar notation in the cipher marks.

DCCC.

800.

He checked manuscript after manuscript. Every cipher entry had two components: the position marker (location) and a number scratched beside it (date?).

But the dates didn't make sense. The manuscript from 1290 referenced 740. The one from 1215 referenced 800. The dates were before the manuscripts were created, sometimes by centuries.

Unless...

Unless the cipher wasn't recording when the knowledge was hidden. It was recording when the knowledge was created. Or when the events the knowledge described had occurred.

Which meant the network wasn't tracking current information. It was tracking ancient information—knowledge that predated the manuscripts by centuries, maybe longer.

He found the oldest manuscript they had—a fragment of text from what looked like the 9th century, barely legible, the vellum crumbling at the edges. The cipher marks were primitive compared to the later ones, cruder in execution but following the same basic structure.

And the date notation: CCCC.

400.

Four hundred CE. When Rome was falling. When the Western Empire was collapsing into the chaos that would become the Dark Ages.

"My God," Thomas whispered.

"What?" Otto called from the stern. "Find something?"

Thomas looked up. "The cipher. It's not medieval. It's... it's ancient. The network has been operating since at least the fall of Rome. Perhaps longer."

Otto shrugged, unimpressed. "Means nothing to me. I just smuggle things and avoid customs men."

But it meant everything to Thomas.

Wilhelm hadn't been part of a recent conspiracy. He'd been part of something that stretched back nearly a thousand years. An organization that had survived the fall of Rome, the chaos of the migration period, centuries of war and plague and upheaval.

And they'd been preserving knowledge the entire time. Hiding it. Protecting it. Encoding information about events from Rome's fall, from before Rome's fall, from the ancient world that had crumbled into darkness.

From what?

From the Order, obviously. But why did the Order want to destroy knowledge from the ancient world?

What could possibly be so dangerous about information that old?

"Thomas."

He looked down. Margarethe's eyes were open, lucid for the first time in hours.

"How long?" she asked, voice rough as sandpaper.

"Since yesterday morning. The fever started last night." He touched her forehead—still burning hot. "How do you feel?"

"Like I'm dying." But she tried to smile. It came out more like a grimace. "Which means I'll probably live. The dying ones don't feel anything."

Thomas wasn't sure that was true, but he didn't argue. "Can you drink?"

She nodded. He helped her sit up enough to take water from the skin. She drank greedily, then fell back, exhausted by the effort.

"Did I talk?" she asked after a moment. "In the fever?"

Thomas hesitated. He could lie. Should lie, probably. But he was terrible at lying and Margarethe would see through it anyway.

"Some."

"Did I say anything..." She looked away. "Anything you shouldn't know?"

He considered his answer carefully. "You talked about your father. About remembering 217 locations. About being the library that can't burn."

Margarethe closed her eyes. "So you know."

"Know what?"

"That I'm a fraud. That I can't actually read." The words came out bitter, self-loathing. "That everything I know is just... memory. Tricks."

Thomas was quiet for a moment. Then: "Margarethe, do you know how many locations I can remember without the key?"

"What?"

"Perhaps ten. Fifteen if I really concentrate and write them down immediately. And I can read, I can use the key, I have my grandfather's so-called 'gift.'" He leaned closer so she could see his face. "You've memorized 217 locations perfectly. Kept them in your head for years, navigated them without a single note. That's not fraud. That's brilliance."

"It's a party trick—"

"A trained animal doesn't understand what it's remembering. You do." Thomas gripped her good hand. "You navigate the network. You know which locations connect to which. You understand the structure, the relationships, the meaning behind the patterns. Your father didn't just teach you to memorize. He taught you to be a living index. To BE the network in human form. Do you understand how extraordinary that is?"

Margarethe's eyes were wet. She blinked, turned her face away. "You're just saying that because I'm dying."

"You're not dying. And I'm saying it because it's true." He squeezed her hand. "I've spent my life around scholars. Men who can read ten languages, who memorize entire books, who debate philosophy and theology for hours. And not one of them could do what you do. Not one."

She was quiet for a long time. Then, so softly he almost didn't hear: "Thank you."

They sat in silence as the boat drifted on. The sun climbed higher, warm despite the autumn chill. On the banks, farmers worked their fields, children played near the water. Life continuing on.

"Thomas," Margarethe said finally. "If I don't make it—"

"You'll make it."

"If I don't," she continued firmly, "the locations are in my head. All 217. I should tell you. In case the knowledge is lost."

"You'll tell me in Strasbourg. When you're recovered. When we have time to write them all down properly." Thomas smiled. "I'm not letting you off that easily. You're going to have to actually work to teach me. Proper lessons. No fever-induced shortcuts."

Despite everything, Margarethe laughed—a weak, breathy sound, but genuine. "You're more stubborn than you look."

"I learned from the best."

Her hand was still in his. Thomas realized he'd been holding it this entire time, and neither of them had pulled away.

She fell asleep again after that, more peacefully this time. The fever still burned, but something in her face had relaxed, the lines of pain easing slightly.

Thomas returned to the manuscripts, to the ancient dates hidden in cipher marks, to the revelation that the conspiracy he'd stumbled into was far older and stranger than he'd imagined.

But he didn't let go of her hand.

## Scene 3: Margarethe's Confession

### Day Three, Dawn

Margarethe woke to the smell of fish and river water and her own stale sweat.

She was alive. Surprising.

The last coherent memory she had was Thomas pulling an arrow out of her shoulder, pain so bright and consuming it had whited out her vision. After that, fragments. Fever dreams. Her father's voice reciting locations like a prayer. The terror of forgetting, of failing.

Now the fever had broken. She could tell by the way her thoughts moved clearly again, sharp-edged instead of blurred.

She opened her eyes.

Thomas was asleep beside her, slumped against the mast with a manuscript open in his lap. He looked exhausted—dark circles under his eyes like bruises, hair matted with dried sweat and river spray, robes stained with her blood. His hand was close to hers, like he'd been holding it before sleep took him.

He'd stayed with her. The entire time.

Margarethe tried to sit up. Her shoulder screamed in protest—the wound was still there, still angry, but no longer infected. Someone had changed the bandages, cleaned the wound with something herbal and medicinal. Thomas, obviously. Weak, useless Thomas who'd never treated an injury more serious than a paper cut.

She managed to lever herself up to sitting. The world swayed, then steadied.

They were still on the river. Otto the Weasel was at the tiller, watching her with cautious interest.

"You're awake," he said quietly. "Good. The boy's been half mad with worry. Hasn't slept more than an hour at a time since we left Mainz."

Margarethe looked at Thomas again. His hand was so close to hers. Had he been holding it while she fevered?

"How long?"

"Three days. We'll be in Strasbourg by evening if the wind holds." Otto spat over the side. "You should wake him. He needs to eat something that isn't stale bread and anxiety."

But Margarethe didn't wake Thomas yet. Just sat there, watching him sleep, trying to understand the strange feeling in her chest.

People didn't stay. That was the fundamental lesson life had taught her. Her mother had died before Margarethe could even form memories of her face. Her father had been tortured to death. Every network contact who'd tried to help had eventually been killed or disappeared.

People left. That was what they did.

But Thomas had stayed.

Weak, naive, completely unprepared Thomas who couldn't lie to save his life and stumbled over his own feet.

He'd stayed.

He'd pulled an arrow from her shoulder. Had nursed her through three days of fever. Had changed her bandages and kept her hydrated and probably talked to her unconscious body about manuscripts and patterns.

He'd stayed when anyone sensible would have taken the manuscripts and the key and continued on alone.

Finally, she reached out and touched his shoulder. "Thomas."

He jerked awake, disoriented. Saw her sitting up and his face transformed—relief so profound it was almost painful to witness. "Margarethe! You're—the fever, is it—"

"Broken." She managed a smile. "You did well. For someone who's never played nursemaid before."

"I was terrified the entire time." He rubbed his eyes. "I kept thinking I was doing everything wrong. That you'd die because I didn't know what I was doing."

"But I didn't die."

"No." He smiled, exhausted but genuine. "You didn't."

"Thank you." She tested her shoulder—stiff, painful, but functional. "Thanks to you."

"You saved my life first. On the river path from Mainz, remember? I was just repaying the debt."

"Is that what we are now? Keeping accounts?"

Thomas looked at her directly, scholar's eyes serious. "No. I don't think it is."

The moment stretched between them, something unspoken and complicated. Margarethe looked away first, uncomfortable with whatever was building.

"We should talk," she said. "About what I said. In the fever."

"You don't have to—"

"I do." She took a breath. "You know I can't read. You know about the 217 locations, about being a living library. But there's more."

Thomas settled himself more comfortably, giving her his full attention. "I'm listening."

So Margarethe told him.

About her mother, who'd been part of the network before Margarethe was born. A brilliant woman who could read Greek and Arabic as easily as Latin, who'd helped translate ancient texts that the Order wanted destroyed. Who'd been targeted while pregnant, forced into hiding.

About the pregnancy that had been complicated from the start. About her mother bleeding out while midwives tried desperately to save her. Dying hours after Margarethe was born.

About her father, Heinrich, raising a daughter alone in a world that didn't value daughters the way it valued sons. Teaching her everything he knew.

"I was perhaps five when he realized I couldn't learn to read the way other children did," Margarethe said, voice distant with memory. "The letters would move around on the page. Change places. I could never hold them still long enough to make sense of them."

"Some people are born that way," Thomas said gently. "It's not a deficiency. Your mind just processes information differently."

"That's what my father said. But he also knew it meant I couldn't be a scholar the way my mother had been. Couldn't translate texts or copy manuscripts or decode cipher marks by reading them." Margarethe touched the scar on her jaw unconsciously. "So he found another way. If I couldn't read, I could remember. And I was good at remembering."

She described the training. Hour after hour of her father teaching her to visualize, to create memory palaces in her mind, to link information in chains and patterns that wouldn't fade.

"He'd show me a page from a manuscript. Not the words—I couldn't read those anyway. But the dots. The geometric patterns of colored dots in the margins. And I'd remember. Perfectly. Every position, every color, every detail."

"That's extraordinary."

"That's survival." Margarethe's voice was flat. "The network needed people who could carry information that couldn't be written down safely. People who could walk into a city, recite locations to contacts, and walk out without ever touching a piece of paper. I became that person."

"Your father must have been proud."

"He was terrified." Margarethe looked out at the river. "Because if the Order ever discovered what I could do, they wouldn't just kill me. They'd torture me for every location in my head. All 217 of them. And I'd break eventually. Everyone breaks."

"You didn't break when they killed your father."

"I wasn't captured. If I had been..." She shook her head. "I know myself, Thomas. I'd like to think I'm strong enough to die before talking. But I don't know if that's true."

They sat in silence for a while. Otto called something about a watchtower ahead.

"Why tell me all this?" Thomas asked finally.

Margarethe considered the question. "Because you trusted me with your grandfather's key. Because you stayed with me through the fever when you could have taken the manuscripts and continued on alone. Because..." She stopped, looking for the right words. "Because I need you to understand what I am. Not some warrior woman from a romance tale. Just a woman who can't read, who's been running her entire life, who's terrified every single day that she'll forget one of the locations and betray everything her father died for."

"You won't forget."

"How can you know that?"

"Because forgetting isn't something you do. Remembering is who you are." He met her eyes. "And for what it's worth, I don't see you as a warrior from a tale or a scholar. I see you as Margarethe—brilliant and terrifying, and the person who matters. Deeply."

Margarethe felt her throat tighten. "You barely know me."

"I know enough."

She wanted to argue. Wanted to push him away the way she pushed everyone away.

But she was too tired. Too worn down by fever and pain and three days of him caring for her without asking for anything in return.

"Thank you," she said finally. "For staying. For..." She gestured vaguely at the bandages, the water skin. "All of it."

"Thank you for not dying. It would have been very inconvenient."

Despite everything, Margarethe laughed. It hurt her shoulder but she didn't care.

Otto called from the stern: "Strasbourg ahead! We'll dock within the hour!"

Margarethe tried to stand. Her legs trembled but held.

Thomas was beside her instantly, offering support. She took it, leaning on him more than she'd ever leaned on anyone.

"We'll need to find Johannes the Alchemist," she said, forcing her mind back to the task. "He's our contact here. My father's friend. If anyone can help us understand the cipher better..."

"Then we find him." Thomas adjusted his grip, taking more of her weight. "Together."

"Together," Margarethe agreed.

And as the spires of Strasbourg appeared on the horizon—cathedral towers reaching toward heaven, city walls promising safety or at least temporary refuge—she allowed herself to believe, just for a moment, that perhaps she didn't have to carry everything alone anymore.

Perhaps, impossibly, she'd found someone who would stay.

## Scene 4: Strasbourg - The Alchemist

### Evening, Strasbourg

Johannes the Alchemist was not what Thomas expected.

The address Margarethe had given him led to a narrow building in the university quarter, wedged between a bookbinder's shop and a tavern that smelled of spilled beer and roasted meat. Three stories of timber and plaster, windows darkened with soot, and a small sign by the door showing a serpent eating its own tail—the ouroboros, ancient symbol of alchemy and eternal cycles.

"This is it," Margarethe said, leaning heavily on Thomas's arm. Pale from the walk from the docks, but upright and conscious.

Thomas knocked.

No answer.

He knocked again, louder.

From inside, a crash. Cursing in what sounded like Latin mixed with German and possibly Arabic. Then footsteps, rapid and uneven.

The door flew open.

The man standing there was young—perhaps twenty-five at most—with wild dark hair tied back in a hasty queue, several days' worth of beard, and bright, slightly manic eyes. He wore robes that might once have been scholarly but were now stained with substances Thomas couldn't identify—ash, blood, something green and viscous. His hands were ink-stained and burned in places.

"Yes? What? I'm in the middle of a very delicate—" He stopped, staring at them. Focused on Margarethe. "Wait. Margarethe?"

"Hello, Johannes."

"Margarethe! By all the saints!" He grabbed her good arm, pulled her inside with surprising strength. "You look half dead. What happened? Who shot you? Never mind, tell me inside. You!" He pointed at Thomas. "You're the boy? Wilhelm's grandson?"

"I'm Thomas, yes, but I'm not—"

"Excellent! Come in, come in!" Johannes pulled them both through the doorway and slammed it shut behind them, throwing multiple bolts with practiced efficiency.

The interior of the building was controlled chaos.

The ground floor was part library, part laboratory, part workshop, part disaster area. Bookshelves lined every wall, crammed with volumes in multiple languages—Latin, Greek, Arabic, Hebrew, some in scripts Thomas didn't recognize. Tables were covered with alchemical apparatus: glass vessels connected by copper tubing, crucibles suspended over small flames, distillation equipment bubbling with colored liquids. Strange substances in jars labeled in code. The partial skeleton of something arranged on a side table. Charts on the walls showing astrological symbols, anatomical drawings, geometric patterns.

The air smelled like sulfur and roses and something that probably shouldn't be inhaled in quantity.

"Excuse the mess," Johannes said, clearing a space on a bench by shoving aside what looked like someone's skull. "I've been working on a translation of Galen's medical texts and somewhere in there I started an experiment with mercury amalgamation and—well, you don't care about that. Sit. Both of you."

Margarethe sat gratefully. Thomas remained standing, too fascinated by the surroundings to sit.

Johannes was examining Margarethe's shoulder with gentle efficiency. "Arrow wound. Three, four days old? Infected, obviously. You've been treating it with what, wine and hope?"

"Wine and clean bandages," Thomas said.

"Better than nothing. Barely." Johannes moved to a shelf, began pulling down jars with practiced speed. "I have yarrow, calendula, honey from bees that feed on specific wildflowers—and if you're very lucky, I still have some of the Arab physician's salve that Margarethe's father gave me five years ago."

He moved with frantic energy, mixing substances in a mortar. Thomas watched, amazed.

"You're not what I expected," Thomas admitted.

"What did you expect? Old man with a long beard muttering about turning lead to gold?" Johannes laughed while grinding herbs. "I'm thirty-eight. I studied at Paris until my experiments set fire to my dormitory—three times, actually, but they only expelled me after the third. Now I work here, translating ancient texts, occasionally blowing things up by accident, trying to understand why the human body fails the way it does."

"He's being modest," Margarethe said through gritted teeth as Johannes cleaned her wound. "He's one of the most brilliant natural philosophers in Europe. And completely insane."

"The two often go together." Johannes applied a thick paste to the wound. The smell was sharp, medicinal, oddly pleasant. "This will hurt. But tomorrow it will hurt less. And in three days, you'll be functional again. Probably."

Margarethe hissed in pain but didn't pull away.

Thomas watched Johannes work. Fast, efficient, clearly knowing exactly what he was doing. Within minutes, the wound was cleaned, treated, and wrapped in fresh bandages.

"You're part of the network," Thomas said. Not a question.

"Was. Am. Will be." Johannes washed his hands in a basin. "My father was Wilhelm's friend. They studied together in Paris. When Wilhelm joined the network and started expanding it, my father was one of his first recruits. He taught me everything before he died. Where the texts are hidden. How the cipher works. What we're really protecting."

"Which is?"

Johannes looked at him with those bright, intense eyes. "Everything. The accumulated learning of mankind going back two thousand years. Medical texts that could save thousands of lives. Mathematical treatises that could transform trade and building. Philosophical works that could change how we understand the world."

He gestured at his shelves. "The Church says this knowledge is dangerous. That it leads to pride, to questioning God, to catastrophe. The Order says it must be controlled or destroyed. They're both right, in a way. Knowledge IS dangerous. But the alternative is worse."

"The alternative is ignorance," Margarethe said quietly.

"Exactly!" Johannes tied off the bandage with a final flourish. "There. Keep it clean, don't use that arm for a week, and you'll probably survive to annoy me again. Now—" He turned to Thomas. "You have the Iron Key, yes? Wilhelm's key?"

Thomas pulled it from his pocket. The eye symbol seemed to catch the lamplight.

Johannes took it with reverent hands. "My God. I've only seen this in drawings. Your grandfather guarded it with his life."

"He died for it."

"I know. I'm sorry." Johannes set the key on his workbench. "Do you understand what you're carrying?"

"A tool to decode the cipher. Margarethe showed me how."

"It's more than that." Johannes pulled out a manuscript from one of his shelves, opened it to a page covered in marginal cipher dots. "The cipher has layers. Surface layer—locations and basic information. Anyone with patience can decode that. Second layer—dates, connections, patterns. Requires skill. Third layer—the deep structure. The real knowledge. That requires a key."

He demonstrated, showing Thomas how the Iron Key revealed patterns invisible without it. Connections between locations. Hidden notations.

"Your grandfather helped build this web. The keys aren't just tools—they're proof you're trusted with the network's deepest secrets."

"How many keys are there?"

"Seven." Johannes's face was serious now. "One for each of the founding families. Wilhelm's family held the Iron Key—the Eye that Sees. It reveals locations and patterns."

"What about the others?"

"Scattered across Europe. Hidden. Protected. Some have been lost over the centuries." Johannes hesitated. "The Silver Key. I found it. Five years of hunting—it's here. In Strasbourg. Held by Bishop Heinrich Vogel."

"A bishop?" Thomas frowned. "Is he part of the network?"

"No. That's the problem." Johannes started pacing. "The Silver Key—the Hand that Guides—was held by the Strasbourg family line for generations. But the line died out thirty years ago. The key passed to the Church as part of an estate. The bishop has no idea what it is. To him, it's just an ornate religious artifact."

"Can we buy it from him?"

Johannes laughed, harsh and humorless. "With what money? And even if we had gold, he wouldn't sell. That treasury is his pride."

"So we steal it," Margarethe said flatly.

"So we steal it," Johannes agreed. "Which is why I've been planning this for two years."

He went to a desk, pulled out drawings. Detailed sketches of a cathedral—St. Thomas's. The drawings showed floor plans, guard rotations, treasury location, entry points.

"The bishop hosts a feast tomorrow night," Johannes explained, spreading the drawings. "Important clergy, wealthy merchants, nobility. It's an annual event. The cathedral will be full of people, guards will be distracted, and the bishop will be too busy entertaining to pay attention to his treasury."

"You want us to steal from a cathedral full of guards during a feast?" Thomas said slowly. "Madness."

"It is!" Johannes grinned. "But it's also our only chance. After tomorrow, the bishop leaves for Rome. Won't be back for months. The treasury will be sealed, triple-guarded. Tomorrow night is the window."

Margarethe studied the drawings. "What's the plan?"

"You and Thomas go in as guests. Wealthy merchants from Mainz, fleeing the plague, seeking the bishop's blessing. I've already forged invitations. During the feast, you excuse yourselves, slip away, break into the treasury, take the Silver Key, return before anyone notices."

"That simple?"

"Of course not. There will be complications. But—" Johannes looked at both of them seriously. "This is our chance to reunite two of the seven keys. To access deeper layers of the cipher. To understand what Wilhelm and Heinrich and all the others died to protect."

Thomas looked at Margarethe. She was pale, injured, had nearly died three days ago.

"I can do it," she said, reading his expression. "One night. I can manage one night."

"You can barely walk."

"So you'll have to be charming enough for both of us." She almost smiled. "Think you can manage that?"

Thomas thought about everything he'd become since that key had been pressed into his palm.

"I suppose I'll have to learn."

Johannes clapped his hands together. "Excellent! Then we begin preparation immediately. You'll need proper clothes—merchants, not monks. I'll teach you the right way to speak to bishops and nobility. And Thomas, you'll need to learn how to pick a better lock than a cathedral library door. The treasury uses a Bavarian mechanism. Very sophisticated. Very difficult to pick if you don't know the trick."

"Wait," Thomas said. "Tomorrow night? We just arrived. Margarethe is wounded—"

"Which is why we start now." Johannes was already pulling clothes from a chest—fine wool, expensive dyes. "No rest for the wicked, as they say. And we, my friends, are about to become very wicked indeed."

Despite everything—the pain, the exhaustion, the sheer insanity of what they were planning—Thomas found himself smiling.

For the first time since fleeing Mainz, he felt something other than fear.

Something loosened in his chest—the first breath after drowning.

And perhaps, just perhaps, the chance to actually succeed.

## Scene 5: The Second Key

### Johannes's Workshop, Late Night

Hours later, Thomas's head was spinning with information.

Johannes had spent the evening teaching them everything—how to walk like merchants (confident, appraising everything in terms of value), how to speak to nobility (deferential but not servile, aware of your own worth), which wines were fashionable this year, how to navigate a formal feast without revealing you'd spent your life eating monastery food.

And most importantly, how the Bavarian lock mechanism on the treasury door worked.

Now, past midnight, Margarethe had finally collapsed into exhausted sleep on Johannes's guest bed. Johannes and Thomas sat in the workshop, the Silver Key drawings spread between them, a single lamp burning low.

"She's stronger than she looks," Johannes said quietly.

"I know."

"My father told me about Heinrich. About what the Order did to him. About Margarethe memorizing everything because she couldn't read." Johannes poured wine into two cups. "When my father died—natural causes, thankfully—I thought about giving up the network. Going into hiding. Just being a normal alchemist blowing things up for paying customers."

"Why didn't you?"

"Because someone has to remember. Someone has to keep trying." Johannes took a drink. "Your grandfather understood that. He knew the risks. Took them anyway because the alternative—letting the knowledge die—was worse than death."

Thomas turned the Iron Key over in his hands. "Johannes, what IS the Genesis Protocol? Everyone keeps mentioning it, but no one will explain."

Johannes was quiet for a long moment. "I don't know. Not fully. The knowledge was fragmented deliberately—split across the seven keys. But from the fragments I've translated, it seems to be... instructions."

"Instructions for what?"

"For surviving what comes next." Johannes pulled out an old manuscript, pointed to marginal notes in faded ink. "The network's founders—at least those who shaped its seven-key form—believed civilization moves in cycles. Rise, flourish, peak, collapse. Rise again. They thought they saw a pattern—major collapses coming in waves, separated by centuries. Bronze Age collapse around 1200 BCE. Rome's fall around 400 CE. If the pattern holds..."

Thomas felt cold. "We're due for another collapse."

"Within the next century or two, yes. Perhaps sooner if the plague spreads." Johannes tapped the manuscript. "The founders experienced Rome's fall firsthand. They watched knowledge burn, libraries destroyed, civilization fragmenting into chaos. Centuries of accumulated wisdom lost in decades."

"So they created a system to preserve it."

"Not just preserve. To transmit. To ensure that when the next collapse comes, the knowledge survives. Not just in books—those burn too easily. But in people. In networks. In systems that can adapt and survive."

Thomas thought about the dates he'd found in the cipher. The references going back to 400 CE, to Rome's fall, possibly earlier. "The network—at least in its present form—has been operating for nine hundred years."

"Or trying to prevent the collapse. The texts aren't clear on that point." Johannes looked troubled. "Which brings me to my question: Why split it? Why not just write down the complete Protocol in one place?"

"Because power corrupts," Thomas said slowly. "Knowledge concentrated in one place, in one person's hands, becomes dangerous. It becomes control instead of preservation."

"Exactly. Split across seven keys, held by seven different families, no single person can abuse it. You need collaboration. Agreement. Trust."

"What happens if someone gets all seven keys?"

"Then they can read the complete Protocol. Understand exactly what the network's founders intended. See the full pattern, the complete knowledge." Johannes's expression was troubled. "But here's the question that keeps me awake: What if the Protocol's original purpose isn't what the Order thinks it is? What if the network itself has been corrupted over centuries?"

Thomas frowned. "What do you mean?"

"The Order claims the network is trying to control humanity, to play God. The network claims it's just preserving information. But what if both are true? What if the Protocol started as preservation and became something else over nine centuries?"

He stood, began pacing. "Consider it. Nine hundred years. Dozens of generations. Knowledge passed from parent to child. How much could change in that time? How much could be misunderstood, misinterpreted, corrupted?"

Thomas thought about Anselm's dying words. *The Protocol... someone has to complete it... or everything burns...*

"Anselm believed the network was essential. That without it, civilization would collapse."

"Or perhaps he believed a corrupted version of the truth." Johannes stopped pacing, looked directly at Thomas. "What if the complete Protocol says something different than what either the Order or the network currently believes?"

"Then we need all seven keys," Thomas said. "We need to know the truth."

"Agreed. Which is why tomorrow night is so important." Johannes rolled up his drawings. "Get some sleep. Tomorrow you need to be charming, sophisticated, and most importantly, able to pick a Bavarian lock in under two minutes."

"Two minutes?"

"That's how long it takes guards to do their rotation past the treasury corridor. Any longer and we're caught." Johannes grinned. "No pressure."

Thomas went to his own sleeping space—a pile of blankets in what Johannes called the "storage room" but was actually more like a library that had achieved critical mass and exploded. He lay down, exhausted but unable to sleep.

Tomorrow night they would steal from a bishop. Would break into a cathedral treasury during a feast. Would attempt to reunite two of the seven keys for the first time in generations.

And every step took them deeper into a conspiracy that stretched back a thousand years.

Thomas closed his eyes and tried not to think about all the ways this could go wrong.

Instead, he thought about the pattern. The beautiful, terrible pattern hidden in centuries of cipher marks.

Knowledge and catastrophe. Preservation and control. Truth and corruption all woven together so tightly that perhaps they couldn't be separated anymore.

Perhaps that was the real secret of the Genesis Protocol.

Not how to save civilization.

But how to understand that civilization might not be saveable. That the cycles would continue regardless. That the only choice was whether knowledge survived the burning or was lost forever.

Thomas opened his eyes in the darkness, staring at Johannes's ceiling covered in astronomical charts and alchemical symbols.

Wilhelm had understood this. Had spent his life building a network to preserve knowledge through the coming darkness. Had died to protect a key that might unlock the truth.

The least Thomas could do was see it through.

For Wilhelm. For Hamo. For Margarethe.

For the possibility that perhaps, just perhaps, the next collapse could be different if enough knowledge survived.

He closed his eyes again and this time, sleep came.

Tomorrow, they would steal a key.

Tomorrow, the real work would begin.

# CHAPTER 4 - THE BISHOP'S FEAST (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: Preparation - Becoming Merchants

### Johannes's Workshop, Morning

Thomas had never worn clothes this fine.

The merchant's outfit Johannes had procured fit surprisingly well—deep blue wool doublet with silver buttons, linen shirt that felt clean and soft instead of scratchy monastery wool, hose that didn't itch, leather boots that didn't pinch. He looked at himself in Johannes's cracked mirror and barely recognized the person staring back.

Not a monk hunched over manuscripts. Not a fugitive. A merchant—wealthy, confident.

"I look ridiculous."

"You look prosperous. There's a difference." Johannes stepped back, examined his work critically. "The key is in the posture. Shoulders back—aye, like that. Chin up slightly. Not arrogant, just confident. Like money flows through your hands the way prayer flows through a priest's."

Thomas tried. Pulled his shoulders back. Lifted his chin.

Failed. Slouched back into his natural posture of someone who'd spent years hunched over manuscripts.

"This is hopeless. I look like a monk playing dress-up."

"It's not hopeless. It's just unfamiliar." Margarethe's voice came from the stairs, amused. "You'll adapt. You always do."

Thomas turned.

His breath caught.

Margarethe descended the stairs transformed. Deep green dress with gold embroidery, the fabric expensive enough that it caught the light with a subtle sheen. Her hair was actually styled instead of roughly tied back—braided and coiled and pinned, with a delicate veil. Her wounded shoulder was carefully concealed under the dress's construction, no sign of the bandages beneath.

She moved differently too. Not the fighter's prowl Thomas knew. Something more measured, graceful. The walk of a woman who'd been raised with wealth and refinement.

Beautiful. Dangerous and beautiful and completely unlike the Margarethe who'd killed men without hesitation.

"Close your mouth," she said with the ghost of a smile. "Merchants don't gape at their own wives."

"Wives?"

"You didn't think we'd attend separately?" Johannes was grinning. "A wealthy merchant and his wife, fleeing the plague in Mainz, seeking the bishop's blessing for safe passage to their holdings in Italy. Perfect cover. Sympathetic. Believable."

Thomas forced himself to think practically. "What if someone questions our story?"

"Then you improvise using details you actually know." Johannes handed him a leather purse heavy with coins. Real coins—Thomas could feel the weight, hear the clink of silver and gold. "You grew up near Mainz, you understand the wool trade, you're educated enough to discuss theology if the bishop tests you. Use truth whenever possible. It's easier to remember and harder to catch in lies."

He demonstrated how to hold the purse. "Don't flash this around, but let it show occasionally. Wealthy people are casual about their wealth. Poor people pretending to be wealthy are too careful. They count coins. They worry. You? You expect to have money. Always have. Always will."

Margarethe took the purse from Thomas, secured it to her belt. "I'll handle the money. Wives often do. And I can read people better than Thomas can."

"I can read people," Thomas protested.

"You can read manuscripts. You're terrible with people. You think everyone is honest because you're honest." She touched his arm, softening the criticism. "It's not a weakness. It's just not useful tonight when we need to lie convincingly."

Johannes cleared the table with a sweep, sending half-finished experiments to the floor. He spread out his cathedral drawings one final time. "Review the plan. One more time. Everything must be perfect."

Thomas studied the layout, committing it to memory. St. Thomas Cathedral dominated central Strasbourg—massive Gothic structure with twin towers, side chapels, and the bishop's private residence attached to the north transept like a fortified wing. The treasury was in the residence basement.

"The feast begins at sunset," Johannes said, tracing the route. "You'll arrive fashionably late—wealthy people always do. Enter through the main doors, present your invitations. He'll show you to the great hall."

"How many guests?" Margarethe asked.

"Forty, perhaps fifty. Clergy from across the region, wealthy merchants, minor nobility. Enough that two strangers won't stand out, but not so many that you'll get lost."

Johannes tapped the treasury location. "You'll need to slip away during the third course. That's when the entertainment begins—musicians, perhaps acrobats. Maximum distraction. That's your window."

Margarethe leaned closer, studying the route. "The treasury is here, basement level. How do we reach it?"

"Servants' corridors." Johannes traced a path. "Exit the great hall through the side door—excuse yourselves to visit the privy. Turn left instead of right. Down this corridor, through the kitchen annex—absolute chaos during the feast, servants running everywhere. Down the stairs to the basement level. The treasury is the third door on the left."

"Guards?"

"Two. Stationed outside the treasury door during the feast. But they rotate every hour. Three minutes during rotation when the hallway is empty. You'll need to be fast."

Thomas looked at the lock drawing—detailed sketch of what Johannes called a Bavarian design. Complex, expensive, supposedly unpickable. Johannes had spent all of yesterday teaching Thomas how it worked.

"Two minutes to pick the lock," Thomas said, reviewing the timing. "Two minutes inside to locate the Silver Key. One minute to lock it again and disappear before the new guards arrive."

"Five minutes total," Johannes confirmed. "Can you do it?"

Thomas's hands were already sweating. "I don't know."

"Yes, you do. You picked my practice lock in ninety seconds this morning. Seventy-five seconds on your best attempt yesterday afternoon." Johannes gripped Thomas's shoulder with surprising strength. "You see patterns. Locks are just metal patterns that need to be understood. Trust your training."

Margarethe caught Thomas's eye, held his gaze. "I'll watch the corridor. Keep you aware of timing. You focus only on the lock."

"What about you?" Thomas asked Johannes.

"I'll be at the feast. Separate from you—we can't appear to know each other." Johannes gestured to a pile of books. "I'll be playing the eccentric scholar invited to discuss Greek medical texts. It gives me an excuse to wander, to create diversions if needed, and to signal if anything goes wrong."

"What's the signal?"

"If I spill wine on myself—loudly, dramatically—abort immediately. Something's wrong. Get back to the feast through whatever route is safest. Don't worry about the lock or the key. Just survive." Johannes rolled up the drawings. "But it won't come to that. This plan is solid. I've been refining it for two years."

Thomas wasn't so sure. Plans sounded solid until they collided with reality.

But he didn't say that. Just nodded.

"One more thing," Johannes said, voice dropping, becoming serious. "If this goes wrong, if you're caught, you don't know me. You're just thieves who heard about the treasury. You don't mention the cipher, the network, the keys, Wilhelm, Margarethe's father—nothing. Nothing that puts the rest of us at risk."

"And if they torture us?" Margarethe asked bluntly.

"Then you resist as long as you can. Give us time to disappear, to hide evidence. But everyone breaks eventually given enough time and pain. So we make sure it doesn't come to that by not getting caught." Johannes looked at both of them, his young face suddenly old. "Are you ready?"

Thomas looked at Margarethe. She looked at him.

Neither were ready. But they were out of time. Out of options. Out of everywhere to run.

"Aye," Thomas lied.

"Good," Johannes said. "Then let's go steal from a bishop."

## Scene 2: The Feast - Playing the Part

### St. Thomas Cathedral, Evening

The great hall was overwhelming.

Light first—hundreds of candles reflecting off gold leaf painted on the ribbed vaulting overhead, turning the vast space into something between cathedral and treasure vault. Three stories high. The air itself seemed to glow.

Then smell: roasted meat, wine, beeswax from the candles, expensive perfumes from wealthy women, incense from nearby chapels mixing with it all into something almost intoxicating.

Tapestries covered the walls—hunting scenes, biblical tableaux, heraldic devices rendered in threads that must have cost more than a peasant family earned in a lifetime.

The tables were covered in white linen so fine and clean it looked like snow. Set with silver plates, crystal goblets, knives with handles of carved ivory.

Sound: laughter, conversation in multiple languages, the clink of goblets, musicians tuning instruments in the corner.

And the food.

Roasted swan served in its own feathers, gilded beak gleaming. Venison in wine sauce. Pike in aspic shaped to look like the fish was swimming. Peacock re-dressed in its own plumage. Fruits Thomas had never seen—oranges from Spain, figs from the Mediterranean. Breads sculpted into castles and crowns. Wine that tasted like liquid gold.

All this while plague ravaged the countryside outside. While bodies burned in mass graves. While the poor starved.

"Stop staring," Margarethe murmured beside him, hand light on his arm in a gesture that looked affectionate but felt like a warning. "Merchants are used to wealth."

Thomas forced himself to relax. Took a measured drink of wine—not gulping despite the fact that it was the best he'd ever tasted. Tried to appear bored by the opulence.

They'd been seated near the middle of the hall—not close enough to the bishop's high table to draw scrutiny, but not so far away as to seem unimportant. Perfect positioning. Johannes had clearly bribed someone.

Speaking of Johannes—Thomas spotted him three tables away, deep in animated conversation with an elderly priest. Wine glass in hand, expensive but not ostentatious clothes, the picture of a man comfortable in his own intelligence.

The bishop himself sat at the high table under a carved canopy, resplendent in purple robes. Heinrich Vogel was in his fifties, with a florid face that suggested he enjoyed feasts regularly. Soft hands, expensive rings, the satisfied look of a man who'd never gone hungry.

Somewhere beneath this building, beneath all this wealth and luxury, the Silver Key waited.

"Third course soon," Margarethe said quietly. "The entertainment will begin. That's when we move."

"I know the plan."

"I'm reminding you because you're nervous. When you're nervous, you forget things. You overthink." She took a delicate bite of venison. Perfect manners. "Just follow the plan. Trust your training. We'll be fine."

She was right. He was nervous. His hands were shaking slightly under the table. He hid them in his lap.

A merchant across from them—portly man named Dietrich who owned vineyards—was telling a story about escaping Mainz just before the plague took hold. "The plague bells were ringing day and night. Bodies in the streets, piled like cordwood. The Order's Gray Robes going house to house, burning buildings. No trials. No mercy. Just fire and death."

"Terrible times," Thomas agreed.

"And you escaped when?" Dietrich asked, suddenly focused on Thomas.

Thomas's mind went blank. When had they supposedly escaped? What date had Johannes told them to use?

"Three weeks ago," Margarethe said smoothly. "Just before the river crossings closed. My husband had already heard rumors from his trading contacts. We saw what was coming. Packed what we could carry. Lost half our goods in the rush—looters, fires, the Order confiscating entire warehouses. My husband's warehouse in the Mühlenstadt quarter—burned by the Order as a plague precaution. Everything gone. Forty years of inventory. Gone in an afternoon."

Perfect fabrication. Every detail crisp, specific. Her face showed exactly the right blend of bitterness and fear—practiced grief that looked utterly genuine.

Dietrich nodded sympathetically. "I heard the Mühlenstadt quarter was hit particularly hard. You were fortunate to escape with your lives."

"Fortune favors those who don't hesitate." Margarethe took a sip of wine. "We saw the signs, we acted immediately. Those who waited..." She shrugged eloquently.

The conversation moved on to other topics—trade routes disrupted by plague, whether it was safe to travel to Venice.

Thomas breathed again.

"That was close," he murmured when Dietrich's attention shifted.

"You froze. Don't freeze again when it matters." But Margarethe's hand found his under the table, squeezed once. Reassurance, not just criticism.

The third course arrived with ceremonial flourish—servants carrying the swan on a massive silver platter, the bird gilded and re-feathered. The crowd applauded. The bishop stood to say a blessing that was more about his own piety than gratitude to God.

And with the swan, the entertainment.

Musicians entered the hall—lute, viol, tambourine. A juggler in motley began tossing knives and torches. Two acrobats began performing impossible feats of balance and strength, drawing gasps and applause.

Everyone's attention shifted. Even the guards were watching.

"Now," Margarethe breathed.

They stood. Casual, unhurried. Thomas caught Johannes's eye across the hall. The alchemist gave the smallest nod.

*Go. May God protect you.*

They walked toward the side door. Slow, relaxed. No one paid attention. Everyone was watching acrobats flip through the air.

Through the door. Into the corridor beyond, sudden quiet after the noise.

Left instead of right.

And the real work began.

## Scene 3: The Heist - The Bavarian Lock

### Cathedral Basement, Treasury Corridor

The kitchen annex was controlled chaos.

Cooks shouting orders in German and French and kitchen Latin. Servants rushing between kitchens and great hall with platters and wine. Steam and heat rolling from ovens. The smell of roasting meat and bread and spices, overlaid with sweat and smoke.

No one gave Thomas and Margarethe a second glance. Servants assumed they were lost guests. Kitchen staff was too busy to care.

Down the stairs. Stone steps worn smooth from centuries of use. Temperature dropping with each step. Sound of the feast fading, replaced by silence and dripping moisture.

The basement corridor was quiet. Empty. Torches providing flickering yellow light.

And at the end, two guards stationed outside a heavy oak door reinforced with iron bands.

Thomas and Margarethe pressed themselves into a side alcove. Watched. Waited.

"Guard rotation?" Thomas whispered.

Margarethe was already counting. "Four minutes. Perhaps five."

They waited. Thomas's heart hammered. His palms were sweating. His mouth was dry.

Three minutes.

Four minutes.

At five minutes, footsteps on the stairs. The new guards arriving.

Brief conversation. "Anything?"

"Nothing. Dead quiet down here."

"The bishop's feast always is. Everyone's too busy stuffing their faces to think about stealing."

Laughter. Good-natured complaining about being stuck on guard duty while others enjoyed the feast.

Then the original guards headed up the stairs. The new guards settled into position, adjusting their sword belts.

"Damn," Thomas breathed. "We missed the window."

"Wait," Margarethe said.

One of the new guards pulled out a wineskin—leather, probably containing something stronger than the watered wine served at the feast. Shared it with his companion. Both laughed.

Then both guards walked down the corridor. Away from the treasury. Toward what looked like a servants' privy at the far end.

"They're abandoning their post?"

"Drinking on duty. Can't be seen right in front of the treasury door. They'll relieve themselves, pass the wine, complain. Be back in two, three minutes." Margarethe pushed him forward urgently. "Go. Now. This is the only chance we'll get."

Thomas ran. Light-footed, quiet. Stay on the balls of your feet. Don't let your heels strike. Roll your weight.

Reached the treasury door.

Heavy oak, iron reinforcements, and in the center: the lock.

Bavarian mechanism. Beautiful and deadly and supposedly unpickable.

Thomas pulled his picks from his sleeve—thin metal tools Johannes had forged specifically for this. Knelt before the lock. Inserted the first pick.

The Bavarian lock used multiple rotating cylinders, each with different pin configurations that had to be set in exact sequence. One mistake and everything reset.

Thomas closed his eyes. Shut out the corridor, the guards, the fear. Focused entirely on the lock.

This was just like the manuscripts. Patterns. Sequences. Each click was a letter. Each cylinder was a line. The entire lock was a page he had to decode.

First cylinder. He felt the pins—delicate pressure, listening with his fingers. Applied tension with his other pick. Rotated.

Click.

Second cylinder. More complex. Five pins instead of three. Different sizes. He worked carefully.

Click.

Behind him, Margarethe's urgent whisper: "One minute."

Third cylinder. The pins were uneven sizes, one tiny pin hidden between two larger ones that could trap the pick. Thomas's hands were sweating. He wiped them quickly, started again.

One pin. Two pins. Three pins—there was the small one—

"Thirty seconds."

Fourth pin. Fifth pin. Come on, align, please—

Click.

The lock opened.

Thomas pulled the heavy door. It swung inward on recently oiled hinges, silent and smooth.

"I'm in."

"Twenty seconds. Hurry."

Thomas slipped inside, leaving the door ajar.

The treasury was smaller than he expected—perhaps ten feet square. Shelves lined the walls, covered with church valuables. Gold chalices encrusted with gems. Jeweled crosses. Illuminated manuscripts. Silver reliquaries holding fragments of saints' bones.

And there, on the center shelf at eye level: a key.

Not like the Iron Key. This one was silver, gleaming. Ornately carved with a level of detail that must have taken months. The symbol on the head was different—a hand reaching upward, fingers spread wide, palm inscribed with tiny markings.

The Hand that Guides.

Thomas grabbed it. Felt the weight—heavier than the Iron Key, denser metal. The craftsmanship was extraordinary.

Outside, Margarethe's voice sharp with controlled panic: "Guards coming back. Thirty seconds. Get out now."

Thomas ran to the door. Pulled it closed. His picks were still in the lock mechanism inside—no time to retrieve them.

The lock. He had to lock it again or they'd know immediately.

But Bavarian locks were designed to prevent exactly that. Couldn't be re-locked from the outside without the original key or specialized tools Johannes hadn't been able to make.

"Thomas!" Real fear now.

"I can't lock it from out here!"

"Leave it. Move now!"

Thomas dove into the alcove beside Margarethe just as voices echoed from the far end. The guards returning, laughing, the wineskin sloshing.

They settled back into position outside the treasury, adjusting their sword belts.

Without checking the door.

Thank God. They hadn't noticed.

But eventually someone would. The next rotation in an hour, or when the feast ended, or when the bishop showed a visitor his treasures. The unlocked door would be discovered. The missing key would be noticed.

They had hours at most.

Margarethe pulled Thomas deeper into the shadows, back toward the stairs. They climbed slowly, every step careful.

Up the stairs. Through the kitchen annex—still chaotic.

Into the corridor leading back toward the great hall—

A hand grabbed Thomas's shoulder from behind.

He spun, heart stopping, reaching for a knife he didn't have.

Johannes. The alchemist's face was pale, urgent, fear in his eyes for the first time.

"We have a problem," Johannes whispered, voice tight with controlled panic. "Don't go back to the feast. There are Gray Robes in the hall. Three of them. Asking questions about strangers from Mainz. Checking invitations against names."

Thomas felt cold wash through him. "How did they find us?"

"I don't know. Perhaps someone at my workshop. Perhaps the Order has infiltrated the bishop's household. Doesn't matter. We need to leave. Now. Before they start questioning guests one by one."

"The Silver Key—" Thomas started.

"Do you have it?"

Thomas nodded.

"Then we go. Forget the feast. Forget the cover story. Follow me. Servants' exit."

They followed Johannes through a maze of corridors, servants' passages winding through the cathedral like intestines. Behind them, shouts echoed. Running feet.

Someone had discovered the treasury.

"Run," Johannes said, abandoning stealth. And they ran.

## Scene 4: The Escape - Johannes's Sacrifice

### Strasbourg Streets, Night

They burst from the cathedral through a servants' entrance into a narrow alley reeking of kitchen refuse and waste. Rain was falling—cold autumn rain that soaked through Thomas's fine merchant clothes in seconds.

"This way!" Johannes led them through the maze of streets, his knowledge of Strasbourg proving invaluable. Behind them, cathedral bells started ringing. Alarm bells. The entire cathedral guard would be rousing.

"Where are we going?" Thomas gasped, lungs burning.

"Safe house. Tanner's quarter. Not far if we can avoid the patrols."

They ran through rain-slicked streets, water streaming from roofs. Around corners sharp enough that Thomas nearly fell. Through passages so narrow his shoulders brushed both sides.

Margarethe kept pace despite her wounded shoulder, face set in grim determination.

Behind them, torches bobbed. Shouts echoed. The pursuit was organized, coordinated.

"They're too close," Margarethe said, glancing back. "We need to split up. Draw them in different directions."

"No," Thomas and Johannes said simultaneously.

"Aye." Margarethe grabbed Thomas's arm, thrust him toward Johannes. "You have both keys. You're what matters. I'll draw them off, lose them in the tanners' quarter. I know this city better than they do."

"Margarethe—"

"Don't argue. We don't have time." She was already moving, shouting in German to draw attention. "Hey! Over here! Follow me if you want the thieves!"

The Gray Robes snapped toward her voice—three sets of eyes, three blades already half-drawn. They moved like pack hunters.

"No!" Thomas tried to go back.

Johannes grabbed him, pulled him in the opposite direction. "She gave us a chance. Don't waste it by getting captured trying to save her. She knows what she's doing. Trust her."

They ran. Thomas looked back once, saw Margarethe disappearing into shadows, the Gray Robes pursuing.

Then Johannes pulled him around a corner and she was gone.

They ran for ten more minutes. Through the tanners' quarter—the smell overwhelming even in the rain. Across a small plaza, empty except for a drunk sleeping in a doorway. Down an alley barely wide enough for one person.

Finally, Johannes stopped at a nondescript door. Knocked a specific pattern—three fast, pause, two slow, pause, one fast.

The door opened immediately. They tumbled inside, soaked and exhausted.

A woman stood in the doorway—middle-aged, stern-looking, practical clothing. She locked the door behind them, threw multiple bolts. "Trouble?"

"Cathedral guards. Gray Robes. They'll search the quarter methodically but they won't find us here. This location isn't in any network records." Johannes was breathing hard, water streaming from his hair. "Are you hurt?" he asked Thomas.

"No. But Margarethe—"

"Margarethe knows how to disappear. She's better at this than either of us. She'll lose them and make her way here when it's safe." Johannes sounded confident, but Thomas heard the worry underneath.

They waited in the safe house—a modest building that felt more like a working-class home than a hiding place. The stern woman—she never gave her name—brought them dry clothes, towels, bread and cheese.

Thomas paced, unable to sit. Changed mechanically. Imagining Margarethe cornered, captured, interrogated. Tortured. Killed.

Johannes sat still, conserving energy, thinking through next steps.

An hour passed. Two hours.

No sign of Margarethe.

Thomas pulled out the Silver Key, turned it over. The Hand that Guides. Beautifully crafted. Heavy. Valuable.

Was this worth Margarethe's life?

At three hours, there was a knock. The same pattern Johannes had used—three fast, pause, two slow, pause, one fast.

The stern woman opened cautiously.

Margarethe stumbled in, soaked, bleeding from a cut on her forehead that had already started to clot, but alive. Gloriously alive.

"Lost them," she gasped, leaning against the doorframe. "Doubled back four times through the tanneries. They gave up after the second hour."

Thomas caught her before she fell. "You're hurt."

"I'm alive. That's what counts. Head wounds bleed more than they hurt." She looked past him to Johannes. "Did we get it? Tell me we got the damned key."

Thomas held up the Silver Key.

Margarethe smiled despite the blood and exhaustion. "Then it was worth it."

But before anyone could celebrate, there was another knock. Different pattern. Urgent and random, just desperate pounding.

The stern woman opened the door a crack. A young man stood outside, rain-soaked, terrified.

"Message for Johannes the Alchemist. Urgent. The Gray Robes raided your workshop an hour ago. They're looking for you specifically. They know you were at the feast."

Johannes's face went pale. "My work. Everything—"

"Burned," the young man said, apologetic. "I'm sorry. I tried to warn you sooner but they had the street blocked. They torched everything. Your books, your equipment, your notes. All of it. Gone."

For a moment, Johannes looked lost. Devastated. His life's work destroyed.

Then he straightened, visibly gathering himself. "Doesn't matter. The knowledge is here." He tapped his head. "They can burn buildings and books but they can't burn what I've already learned. The network taught us that generations ago—knowledge in the mind survives when everything else burns."

"There's more," the young man said, reluctant. "They have the guest lists from the feast. The bishop's steward is going through them methodically, looking for discrepancies. The couple from Mainz who disappeared during the third course. They're asking questions. Checking names against city records."

Thomas's stomach dropped. "They'll figure out our names were false."

"Not immediately. I forged those invitations perfectly. But eventually, aye. Within a day, perhaps two at most." Johannes looked at the Silver Key Thomas still held. "We need to leave Strasbourg. Tonight. But first, we use what we risked everything to obtain."

He spread a manuscript on the table—one from his workshop, saved because he'd brought it to the safe house days ago. Marginal cipher dots covered the pages.

"The Iron Key alone showed us locations," Johannes said, alchemist's mind already working despite the disaster. "Single points of information scattered across Europe. Let's see what two keys together reveal that one alone couldn't."

Thomas placed the Iron Key on the manuscript first. The eye symbol aligned with the text. Cipher dots appeared through the aperture, exactly as they had countless times before.

Then Johannes placed the Silver Key beside it, positioning the hand symbol to overlap slightly with the eye.

And the pattern changed.

The dots didn't just show locations anymore. They showed connections—lines between locations, visible only when both keys were present. A web spread across the page.

"My God," Johannes breathed. "It's not just marking where knowledge is hidden. It's showing how it's connected. How information moves through the network. How knowledge passes from hand to hand, generation to generation."

Thomas saw it with the clarity that came from years of reading manuscripts. Each location was a knot in a vast web. Each connection a path for knowledge.

"And look here." Johannes pointed to a cluster of connections. "These aren't just knowledge repositories. These are families. Bloodlines. See—this mark indicates a family line. This one indicates a strategic marriage. This one indicates offspring who carry specific traits."

Margarethe leaned closer despite her exhaustion. "They're tracking genealogies? Like breeding horses?"

"Not just tracking. Guiding. Orchestrating." Johannes traced the connections with growing horror. "Look—this family in Cologne produces children with pattern recognition. That family in Vienna has children who remember entire pages. The network arranges a marriage between the bloodlines. Three generations later, their descendants have both gifts combined."

Thomas felt cold understanding spreading through him. "They're not just preserving knowledge in books. They're breeding people to carry it. Creating living libraries who can hold information that can't be safely written down."

"For forty generations," Johannes confirmed, pointing to date markers that showed tracking back centuries. "Creating living vessels for knowledge. People who can remember perfectly. People who can see patterns. People who can survive with the information humanity needs to rebuild after the next collapse."

The room was silent except for rain drumming on the roof. The magnitude slowly settling over them.

"That's what Wilhelm was part of," Thomas said quietly, voice hollow. "Not just hiding manuscripts. Creating people who could BE manuscripts. Who could carry the knowledge in their blood and pass it to their children."

"And the Order knows," Margarethe said, voice hard. "That's why they hunt us so viciously. We're not just carrying forbidden knowledge in bags. We are forbidden knowledge. Our existence is the crime. Our breeding is the heresy."

Before anyone could respond, there was pounding on the door. Not a polite knock. A demand.

"Open! In the name of the bishop and the Holy Church! Open or we break this door down!"

The stern woman looked at Johannes with terror. "The Gray Robes found us. How?"

"Doesn't matter how. Back exit. Now." Johannes grabbed the manuscripts, thrust them at Thomas with desperate urgency. "Take these. Both keys. Run. Don't stop until you're miles outside the city walls."

"What about you?" Thomas asked, though he already knew.

"I'll delay them. Buy you time." Johannes was already moving to the front of the house, positioning himself between the door and Thomas. "Go. Now."

Margarethe pulled Thomas toward the back of the house, grip iron-strong. "He's right. We have to go. The keys matter more than any of us."

"I'm not leaving him to die!"

"You don't have a choice! The knowledge dies if we're all captured!"

The front door shattered inward, wood splintering. Gray Robes poured in—six of them, armed, disciplined. Thomas caught a final glimpse of Johannes facing them, unarmed but unbowed.

Then Margarethe dragged Thomas out the back exit into the rain-soaked night, and they were running again, and Johannes was behind them—captured or dead or worse—and all Thomas could think was: *This is my fault. All of this. My fault.*

They ran until dawn, until their lungs burned and their legs gave out and they collapsed in an abandoned woodcutter's cottage five miles outside Strasbourg's walls.

Behind them, the city bells continued their iron clamor—relentless, accusing.

They never stopped.

## Scene 5: The Revelation - Bloodlines and Patterns

### Abandoned Cottage Outside Strasbourg, Morning

They found refuge in a woodcutter's abandoned cottage five miles from the city, deep enough in the forest that smoke from their small fire wouldn't be visible. Collapsed on the dirt floor that smelled of old leaves and mouse droppings. Too exhausted to speak. Too shocked to process what they'd discovered.

Thomas stared at the ceiling beams, watching spiders build webs with patient, methodical precision. Intricate geometric patterns. Everything was patterns. The cipher. The bloodlines. The web of connections spreading across Europe.

And he was one of them. A man shaped by patterns laid generations before he was born.

"Johannes is captured," he said finally. The words came out flat, emptied of everything but fact.

"Or dead," Margarethe said with brutal honesty. She was checking her shoulder wound—reopened during the night's running, bleeding fresh. "The Order doesn't take prisoners unless they want information. If they think he doesn't know anything useful, they'll just kill him. If they think he does..." She didn't finish.

"He knows everything. The network. The keys. Our names."

"He'll resist as long as he can. Give us time."

"And then he'll break. Everyone breaks." Thomas was using Margarethe's own words, thrown back like weapons. "We have days at most before they know everything."

"So we waste those days on guilt?" Margarethe's voice cut sharp. "That what you want? Sit here feeling sorry until the Gray Robes find us?"

She didn't wait for an answer. "Or we move. Learn what we came for. Make his pain count for something."

She was right. Thomas knew she was right.

Margarethe finished bandaging her shoulder with strips torn from her ruined merchant's dress. They looked like what they were: fugitives barely surviving.

Thomas pulled out the two keys. Iron and Silver. The Eye that Sees and the Hand that Guides.

And the manuscript Johannes had given him—the one showing the bloodline connections revealed only when both keys were used together.

"He said forty generations," Thomas said, unrolling the manuscript. "The network has been shepherding specific families for forty generations. That's almost a thousand years of controlled breeding."

"Since Rome fell," Margarethe confirmed, moving to sit beside him despite her pain. "Since the network was supposedly founded to preserve knowledge through the dark ages. They haven't just been preserving information in books. They've been creating people who could survive the next collapse with the right traits already bred into their bloodlines."

Thomas studied the manuscript more carefully in the morning light. The bloodline patterns were extraordinarily complex—too complex to be accident, too steady to be chance. Families in Cologne wed to families in Paris. Their children wed again—into Rome, into Vienna. Each match chosen like a stitch in a tapestry.

The same word kept appearing: *cut*. Like the network was pruning a garden.

"My grandfather," Thomas said slowly, voice catching. "Wilhelm. His family line must be in here somewhere."

He searched, following connections and notations, using the two keys together to reveal the complete pattern. Found it after several minutes.

Wilhelm von Eltville. Father: Friedrich von Eltville. Grandfather: Conrad von Eltville. Back and back through generations, each name carefully noted.

All the way back to someone named Adalbert von Eltville in the 4th century. Forty-one generations. The bloodline had been tracked for over a thousand years.

And beside each name, cold notations that made Thomas's blood run cold:

*Wilhelm von Eltville (1287-1347): Mind for space: strong. Eye for patterns: rare. Memory: keen. Wit: sharp. Marked for cipher-work.*

*Friedrich von Eltville (1261-1312): Eye for patterns: good. Mind for space: fair. Memory: steady. Gifts pass true to children.*

Back and back, generation after generation, each one evaluated like livestock at market.

"They bred him," Thomas whispered. "Bred his entire line across forty generations to produce someone who could read the cipher naturally. Who could see patterns that others couldn't. Who had the exact combination of traits needed to be a living key to the network's knowledge."

"And you," Margarethe said quietly beside him. "You have his gift. Which means—"

"Which means I'm not an accident. I'm their handiwork." The realization hit Thomas like a physical blow. "Everything about me—my ability with patterns, my memory, the way I can see connections—it's not natural talent. It's not God-given. It's wrought. Planned. Bred into me by people who died centuries before I was born."

Panic pulled him down into darkness.

Margarethe gripped his shoulders hard enough to hurt, forced him to look at her. "Listen to me, Thomas. I can't read. Never could, never will. The letters move around on the page. My father turned that limitation into an ability. Made me a living library because the bloodlines needed one and I was the daughter he had. I spent my entire life wondering if I was a person or a tool. A human being with free will or an instrument made to serve someone else's purpose."

"And what did you decide?" Thomas asked, desperate for an answer.

"That I'm both. And that's fine." Her eyes were fierce. "You can be their handiwork and still make your own choices. The two aren't mutually exclusive. What you do with your gifts matters more than how you got them. Wilhelm was bred to read ciphers, but he chose to use that ability to preserve knowledge instead of hoarding it. That choice was his. The network gave him tools but he decided how to use them."

Thomas wanted to believe that. Needed to believe it.

"Show me your family," he said, voice rough. "In the bloodlines. Let me see what they did to you."

Margarethe hesitated, face showing reluctance and fear. Then she took the manuscript. Used the two keys together. Found her line after several minutes.

Margarethe of Mainz. Father: Heinrich the Scholar. Mother: Anna the Translator. Grandmother: Elisabeth the Herbalist. Back and back through her maternal line, each generation carefully tracked.

And beside her mother's name, a notation that made Margarethe go absolutely still:

*Anna the Translator (1319-1343): Tongues: Latin, Greek, Arabic, Hebrew, French. Eye for patterns: strong. Memory: keen. Wit: sharp. Fit for breeding.*

*Wed Heinrich the Scholar 1342 (match approved—bloodlines answer well).*

*With child 1343 (approved).*

*Died in childbirth 1343. Cause: made. Killed for refusing the next command to bear again. Example set to keep the line obedient.*

Margarethe went very still.

"They killed her," she said finally, voice flat and emotionless and terrible. "My mother. She refused some network order about having more children—probably wanted to stop after one difficult birth—and they killed her. Made it look like natural complications. Left me motherless and my father devastated as punishment for her disobedience."

Thomas saw more in the detailed notations. Anna had been brilliant—spoke five languages fluently, could translate ancient texts. But when the network had ordered her to have more children to further spread those traits, she'd refused.

The network had allowed the initial pregnancy with Heinrich to proceed—the bloodline match was acceptable. But they'd arranged for "complications" during childbirth that killed Anna while leaving Margarethe alive. Punishment for disobedience. Warning to others.

"I'm sorry," Thomas said.

"Don't be. I'm angry. And anger is useful." Margarethe's hands were shaking as she searched for her father's entry. "Anger can be directed. Controlled. Used as a weapon."

She found Heinrich's death notation. Read it with eyes gone cold and hard:

*Heinrich the Scholar (1315-1347): Eye for patterns: strong. Tongues: many. Gift for teaching: rare. Memory: keen.*

*Became a risk after Anna's killing. Resisted commands. Refused a new match. Began to pry into Anna's death.*

*Killed by Order hands 1347. Put to the question; spoke little. Bloodline continues through daughter (Margarethe—living library).*

"They used the Order to kill him," Margarethe said, voice steady but eyes burning with rage. "Let the Gray Robes torture him to death because he'd figured out they killed my mother. Made it look like the enemy killed him when it was really them cleaning up their own mistake. Using their supposed opponents as tools for murder."

She was shaking. With rage or grief or both, Thomas couldn't tell.

Finally, Margarethe rolled up the manuscript with careful, controlled movements. "We need to find the other keys. All seven. Understand the complete Genesis Protocol."

"Why?" Thomas asked. "What will that accomplish?"

"Because if we don't, my parents died for nothing. Johannes was captured for nothing. Your grandfather's murder was meaningless. All the people killed by this network across forty generations—all for nothing." She met Thomas's eyes with fierce determination. "But if we complete it. If we understand what the network is actually trying to do, what the Protocol truly says when all seven keys are brought together... then perhaps we can stop them. Or expose them. Or at least make all those deaths mean something."

Thomas thought about it. About the choice in front of them, stark and unforgiving.

They could run. Disappear. Live quiet lives, have children who would carry what had been bred into them.

Or they could continue. Seek the remaining five keys scattered across plague-ravaged Europe. Unlock the full truth. Risk everything to understand everything.

"Johannes said the third key is a person," Thomas said slowly, making his decision even as he spoke. "A living descendant who carries cipher knowledge in their blood memory. Not a physical object like the Iron and Silver Keys, but a human being whose blood carries the knowledge. He mentioned Prague as the location."

"That's weeks of travel. Through plague country. With the Order hunting us specifically. With Johannes likely breaking under torture and revealing where we're headed." Margarethe didn't sound opposed. Just realistic.

"I know."

"We could die. Probably will die before we reach the seventh key."

"I know that too."

Margarethe looked at him—really looked at him. "You've changed, Thomas. Three weeks ago you were a monk who'd never left the monastery. Never killed anyone. Never stolen anything. Now you're talking about traveling across Europe through plague and war, chasing keys to unlock a thousand-year-old conspiracy. Like it's not insane."

"We killed a man," Thomas said quietly, remembering Anselm's surprised face as he fell, Margarethe's blade finding its mark. "Stole from a bishop's treasury. Ran from the Order. Discovered I'm bred to serve a purpose I never chose. I'm already damned by every measure that matters. Might as well finish what we started and at least make the damnation meaningful."

Despite everything, Margarethe laughed. Dark, bitter, but genuine. "Very well. We go to Prague. Find the human key, whatever that means. Unlock the next layer of this nightmare. See how much worse it can get before it kills us."

"Together?"

"Together." Margarethe held out her hand.

Thomas took it. Shook once. A pact sealed between two people bred for purposes they'd never consented to, choosing their own path.

Thomas felt a hard steadiness take root. Not peace—that was too much to ask. But purpose. Direction. A path forward that was his choice.

They had two of seven keys. A manuscript showing bloodline patterns going back forty generations. The knowledge that the network had been playing God with humanity for a thousand years.

And now they knew the truth about their own families. The calculated murders. The breeding. The casual cruelty disguised as preservation.

Outside the abandoned cottage, dawn light was strengthening. A new day. A new chapter in a quest that would probably kill them but felt necessary anyway.

Thomas packed the keys and manuscript carefully, wrapping them in oiled cloth. Margarethe checked her weapons—knife still sharp, small crossbow still functional.

They should leave. Head east immediately. Every hour they delayed was an hour the net could tighten.

But first, they needed rest. Real rest. Their bodies were reaching their limits—Margarethe's arrow wound still healing, Thomas's hands scraped raw, both of them running on fear and determination alone.

"We rest here today," Margarethe said. "Move tonight under cover of darkness. Travel is slower but safer."

Thomas nodded, grateful. He spread blankets on the cleanest section of floor.

They took turns sleeping, one always on watch. The autumn sun climbed higher. Birds sang in the forest beyond, oblivious to human concerns.

Thomas woke to footsteps approaching—boots on frost-brittle grass, deliberate and unhurried. Not trying to hide.

He grabbed the knife Margarethe had insisted he keep within reach, heart hammering.

Margarethe was already awake, positioned by the window with her crossbow aimed at the door.

The footsteps stopped outside. A pause. Then a voice, young and uncertain:

"I have a message. For Thomas of Mainz. From... from someone who says you'll understand the name Wilhelm."

Thomas and Margarethe exchanged glances. A trap? Or genuine?

"Leave it by the door," Margarethe called out, voice hard. "Then step back twenty paces. We'll retrieve it when you're clear."

"I can't. It's not written down. Too dangerous." The voice was definitely young—perhaps sixteen, seventeen at most. Male. Scared but determined. "The message is in my head. I was told to speak it directly to Thomas, no one else."

Margarethe's jaw tightened. Messages carried in memory—classic network tradecraft. Safer. But also potentially a way to lure them.

Thomas made the decision quickly. "I'm opening the door. If you're armed, if there are others, we'll kill you before you can call for help. Understand?"

"I understand. I'm alone. Unarmed. Just a runner for the network. Please—I need to deliver this message and get back before I'm missed."

Thomas cracked the door open, knife ready.

A boy stood there—sixteen at most, dressed in apprentice's clothes, with nervous energy. His hands were visible, empty, trembling slightly.

"Quickly," Thomas said. "Your message."

The boy took a breath, reciting with careful precision: "Johannes the Alchemist lives. Captured during the raid on the safe house two nights past. Currently held in the cathedral's prison—beneath the bishop's residence, same complex where you stole the Silver Key. The Order is interrogating him for information about the network, about you, about the theft. He's being tortured but hasn't broken yet—my contact says he's been asking for specific herbs the guards think are for pain relief but which might be something else entirely. This is day two of captivity. The Order typically executes prisoners after five days of questioning if they haven't extracted useful information. You have three days, perhaps four at most, before they either kill him or transfer him to a more secure location."

The boy paused, then added in his own voice: "My master—the one who sent me—says it's suicide to try a rescue. That you should run east while you still can. That Johannes would want you to complete the quest rather than die trying to save him. But the master also said you'd want to know Johannes is alive. That you'd want the choice."

Thomas felt ice and fire warring in his chest. Johannes was alive. Being tortured. Three days, perhaps four.

"Who's your master?" Margarethe asked, crossbow still steady.

"I can't say. Network security—you understand. But someone who knew Wilhelm. Who knew Heinrich. Who remembers when the network was about preserving knowledge instead of..." He trailed off.

"Instead of breeding people like livestock and killing those who resist," Margarethe finished coldly.

The boy nodded, miserable. "I should go. I've delivered the message. What you do with it is your choice." He turned to leave, then paused. "For what it's worth—I hope you run. I hope you survive. Someone needs to understand what the Protocol really says. What it was before they corrupted it."

He disappeared into the autumn forest.

Margarethe lowered the crossbow slowly, expression unreadable. "It could be a trap."

"I know."

"The Order could have forced someone to send that message. Could be using Johannes as bait to lure us back where they have every advantage."

"I know that too."

"We should run. East toward Prague like we planned. Every hour we waste is an hour they have to find us." Her hand drifted to her weapon, then fell away—uncertainty she never showed.

Thomas looked at the manuscript spread on the floor—bloodline patterns tracked across forty generations. His family. Margarethe's family. Johannes's family. All of them shaped and guided and bred like animals.

And Johannes, who'd helped them understand that horror, who'd taught them what the keys meant, who'd given them everything they needed to steal the Silver Key—he was being tortured right now. Suffering because he'd helped them.

Thomas took one last look at Strasbourg in the distance, smoke rising from the cathedral quarter.

Not east.

Not yet.

First, they had a rescue to attempt.

Two people bred for someone else's purpose, choosing their own path.

The pattern broken. The design rejected.

Even if it killed them.

# CHAPTER 5 - THE RESCUE (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: The Moral Choice - To Save or Abandon

### Forest Outside Strasbourg, Two Days After the Heist

The abandoned shepherd's hut stank.

Rot and mouse droppings, yes, but also the particular decay that came from human structures surrendering to nature—moldering thatch, wood gone soft with fungus, the musty sweetness of decomposing straw. Thomas sat on a collapsed beam that had once been part of the roof, studying the manuscript by pale morning light that filtered through gaps in the walls where the wattle and daub had crumbled away entirely. Outside, autumn wind rattled dead leaves. A crow called, harsh and mocking.

The bloodline patterns were clearer now. Not just abstract connections between places and ideas, but families tracked across generations like breeding stock. Traits identified, evaluated, concentrated through strategic marriages that must have seemed like coincidence or good fortune to the people involved. His own line—forty-one generations documented with cold precision—bred deliberately to produce someone with Wilhelm's gifts.

Someone like Thomas.

A man made on purpose.

"We should keep moving."

Margarethe stood in the doorway, sharpening her knife on a whetstone scavenged from the hut's debris. The rhythmic scraping—steel on stone, steel on stone—was oddly soothing in its steady simplicity. "East toward Prague, like Johannes said before they took him. Every hour we stay near Strasbourg is an hour they can find us."

"I know." Thomas didn't look up from the manuscript.

The scraping stopped. "You've been staring at that same page for twenty minutes. That's not reading. That's avoiding making a decision you've already made."

Thomas looked up, met her eyes. "Johannes."

The name hung in the cold morning air between them like smoke from a funeral pyre.

Margarethe's hand froze mid-stroke on the whetstone, the blade at the exact angle of sharpening. "Johannes is captured. Probably dead by now—the Order doesn't waste resources feeding prisoners who can't give them useful information."

"We don't know that. Two days isn't long enough to—" Thomas stopped, forced himself to be honest instead of hopeful. "If he's alive, they're torturing him. For information about us. About the network. Every hour he doesn't break is an hour of agony we're causing by not being far enough away to make his information useless."

"Which is why we need to leave. Put distance between us and whatever he tells them under torture." Margarethe set down the knife and whetstone with deliberate care. "Running is the smart play. The practical play. The play that keeps the keys safe and the quest alive."

"Or we rescue him," Thomas said quietly.

The words hung there. Absurd. Impossible. Suicidal.

Margarethe stared at him like he'd suggested they fly to the moon by flapping their arms very hard. "That's suicide. The bishop's prison is in the cathedral complex—the same cathedral we just stole from two nights ago. They'll have the highest watch. Guards everywhere, patrols doubled, every door eyed, every stranger weighed."

"Probably," Thomas agreed. His hands shook despite his steady voice.

"Then why are you suggesting it?" But there was something in Margarethe's voice that wasn't quite dismissal. More like she was testing him, seeing if he'd thought this through or was just reacting emotionally to guilt.

Thomas thought about Brother Hamo, dying in the monastery scriptorium to give him the Iron Key, blood pooling on the stone floor where Thomas had spent peaceful afternoons copying manuscripts. About Wilhelm, tortured to death by the Order for crimes he'd committed trying to preserve knowledge instead of hoard it. About Margarethe's parents—her mother murdered by the network during childbirth disguised as complications, her father tortured by the Gray Robes in a purge the network ordered to smother questions.

"Because I'm tired of leaving people behind to die." He met her eyes. "Hamo died for me. Wilhelm died for this. Your parents died for refusing to be tools. And now Johannes—who helped us despite the risk, who taught us what the keys meant, who gave us everything we needed to steal the Silver Key—is being tortured because we involved him in our theft."

"He chose to help us." Margarethe's voice had softened slightly.

"And we chose to accept that help knowing it might get him killed. Knowing the Order was hunting us. Knowing they'd trace connections back to anyone who aided us." Thomas stood, the manuscript slipping from his lap, forgotten. "I know it's suicide. I know the smart thing—the pragmatic thing—is to run. But I can't keep running while people who helped me suffer and die. I can't keep prioritizing the quest over the people who make the quest possible."

Margarethe was quiet for a long moment, studying him with those sharp eyes that missed nothing—not the tremor in his hands, not the set of his jaw, not the way he was trying very hard not to show how terrified he was of what he was proposing.

Then she surprised him: "You've changed."

"You said that before."

"I mean it more now." She picked up her knife again, testing the edge against her thumb with casual familiarity. "The Thomas I met two weeks ago would have chosen the pragmatic option without hesitation. Would have prioritized the quest over the person, the objective over the individual. Now you're talking about throwing away everything—both keys, all our knowledge, your own life—for a rescue that probably won't work."

"I know."

"Good. Because I agree with you."

Thomas blinked. "What?"

"Johannes saved my life when I was sixteen. After my father died—after the Gray Robes tortured him to death while the network looked the other way—I had nothing. No family, no home, no purpose except carrying two hundred seventeen cipher locations in a brain that can't even read the written word." Margarethe began checking her weapons with methodical efficiency. "Johannes took me in. Taught me to navigate the network alone. Gave me contacts and safe houses and the training to survive. Helped me understand that being unable to read didn't make me useless—it made me valuable in different ways. Made me understand that my mother's death and my father's murder weren't my fault, weren't because I failed somehow, but because the network had become something monstrous that would kill its own to maintain control."

She strapped on her knife, her small crossbow that folded for concealment, a length of rope coiled precisely. "I've been trying to convince myself to leave him behind because it's logical. Because I've spent my entire life learning to shut down emotions that could get me killed, learning to make hard choices, learning that sentiment is a luxury people like us can't afford. But logic isn't everything. Sometimes you have to do the stupid, dangerous thing because it's right. Because leaving people to die alone after they helped you makes you exactly like the network—viewing humans as tools to be used and discarded when no longer useful."

Relief washed through Thomas like cold water, sharp and clean. "So we try?"

"We try. And we probably die trying. But we try anyway because the alternative is becoming what we're fighting against." Margarethe's expression was grim but determined. "We'll need a plan, though. The cathedral will be heavily guarded after the theft. This needs to be clean. Precise. In and out before they even realize we were there."

"I have an idea," Thomas said slowly, the plan already forming with the clarity of his mind for patterns—a gift bred into him. "But you're not going to like it."

"Most good plans are ones nobody likes," Margarethe said. "Tell me anyway."

## Scene 2: The Infiltration - Returning to the Cathedral

### Strasbourg Cathedral, Evening of the Third Day

Thomas walked through the cathedral's main entrance wearing monk's robes he'd stolen from a traveling friar's cart that morning while the friar was inside a tavern getting drunk on cheap wine and cheaper company.

His heart hammered.

So hard he was sure everyone could hear it—the guards flanking the massive wooden doors carved with scenes of judgment and damnation, the priests moving through the nave with practiced purpose, the handful of wealthy petitioners waiting to see the bishop about this favor or that dispensation. Every person who looked at him was a potential threat. Every glance could be recognition. Every moment could be exposure.

Incense hung thick in the air, cloying and sweet. Somewhere in the vaulted heights above, a choir practiced evening psalms, their voices echoing off stone like the sound of angels trapped in a cave.

But he kept his head down in the proper monastic posture of humility, moved with the shuffling gait of someone old and tired from travel, and headed toward the side chapel dedicated to Saint Catherine.

People didn't look closely at monks. Especially not in plague times when holy men were everywhere like crows at a battlefield—praying for the sick, administering last rites to the dying, begging for alms to support their monasteries. Just another brown robe in a sea of brown robes, invisible through sheer ubiquity.

Thomas found the side chapel he'd scouted that morning while pretending to pray—small, dark, tucked into the north transept where almost nobody went. Knelt as if in prayer before the altar with its slightly dusty statue of Saint Catherine holding her wheel of martyrdom. Waited.

Margarethe should be in position now. The plan was insane—they'd both agreed on that while hastily preparing over the past few hours—but it was all they had.

Thomas pulled out a small vial from his sleeve, the glass warm from his body heat. Oil of vitriol—Johannes had taught him to make it during their brief time together, demonstrating the chemical's properties with the enthusiasm of someone who genuinely loved the natural philosophy others called alchemy. Dangerous stuff. Burned through almost anything organic given enough time and concentration.

He poured three careful drops on the lock mechanism of the side door leading to the cathedral's interior passages. The acid hissed quietly, eating through the bronze with patient chemical hunger. Forty-five seconds until the lock would be destroyed—long enough that he wouldn't be waiting there obviously doing nothing, short enough that the metal wouldn't drip or smoke or otherwise draw attention.

Behind him, footsteps on stone. Slow, measured, purposeful.

Thomas froze mid-prayer, head bowed, hands clasped, every muscle tense.

"Brother," a voice said. Male, older, with the particular authority of someone used to being obeyed in religious spaces. "The chapel is closing for evening prayers. You'll need to—"

An explosion. Distant but loud enough to shake the cathedral stones, to rattle the leaded glass windows, to send dust drifting down from the vaulted ceiling high overhead. From the direction of the bishop's residence on the other side of the complex.

The priest—Thomas hadn't dared look up to confirm but the voice suggested someone senior, perhaps a canon or archdeacon—ran toward the sound immediately, robes flapping, calling out questions to other clergy who were also running to see what had happened.

Margarethe's diversion. She'd promised to make it spectacular, and apparently she'd delivered.

Thomas stood, tested the door lock with gentle pressure. The mechanism crumbled, the acid having done its work perfectly. The door swung open on hinges that had been oiled recently—the cathedral staff maintained their spaces well, which was fortunate since squeaking hinges would have announced his presence to everyone in the transept.

He slipped into the interior passages, pulling the broken lock free and taking it with him. No point leaving obvious evidence of sabotage where anyone could find it in the next few minutes.

The cathedral was chaos in the specific way any great house became when something unexpected happened. Guards running toward the explosion, their boots loud on stone floors. Priests shouting orders to each other, trying to organize a response. Somewhere, bells were ringing—not the normal call to prayer but the sharp, irregular clanging of alarm bells that meant danger or fire or both.

Thomas moved against the flow of traffic, heading down instead of up. Toward the dungeons beneath the bishop's residence where prisoners were kept when the bishop wanted them close for questioning.

He passed three guards running upstairs toward the chaos. None spared him a glance—just another monk seeking shelter from whatever catastrophe was happening above.

Down stone stairs worn smooth by centuries of feet. The temperature dropped with each step, the air growing colder and damper, acquiring that particular underground quality of spaces that never saw sunlight. The sounds of chaos above faded, replaced by the drip of moisture on stone and the scurrying of rats in the walls and his own breathing that seemed too loud in the confined space.

The dungeon corridor stretched ahead, lit by torches in wall brackets that created more shadows than illumination. And at the far end, two guards stood outside the heavy wooden door that led to the cells where Johannes was being held.

Young men, both of them. Nervous energy visible even from this distance. Clearly wanting to go see what the explosion was about, to be part of the action instead of stuck down here guarding one barely-conscious alchemist who'd been tortured for two days and wasn't going anywhere.

Thomas took a breath, steadied himself, and walked toward them with the confidence of someone who absolutely belonged here.

"Brothers," he said in Latin, keeping his voice gentle and old-sounding. "I've been sent to pray with the prisoner. The bishop wants his soul saved before the execution at dawn."

The guards exchanged glances. The younger one—perhaps nineteen, with the sparse beard of someone who desperately wanted to look older—spoke first: "No one's allowed down here. Bishop's orders. The prisoner is being questioned and—"

"The bishop himself sent me," Thomas interrupted, pulling out the forged document he and Margarethe had hastily created—crude work, but with a wax seal that looked official enough in torchlight. "To offer last rites before the execution. The prisoner is connected to the theft from the treasury. The bishop wants him shriven before death so his soul can confess properly without the burden of mortal sin preventing honest testimony."

It was theological nonsense—confession didn't work that way—but it sounded official and religious and these were guards, not priests. They wouldn't know the theological details.

The older guard squinted at the document, trying to read wax seal impressions in dim torchlight. "We weren't told about any execution."

"That's because it's secret. Quiet. The prisoner is connected to important people the bishop doesn't want to embarrass publicly. Better to deal with him quietly and avoid scandal." Thomas let impatience enter his voice—the irritation of a monk who'd walked all the way down here only to be delayed by guards questioning clear authority. "Look, you can send someone to verify with the bishop's steward if you want. I'll wait. But when the steward tells you I'm here on the bishop's direct orders and you've wasted his time making him confirm it, don't be surprised if you're both assigned to midnight guard duty in the plague quarter for the next month."

The threat was specific enough to sound real. Guard duty in the plague quarter was everyone's nightmare—constant exposure to disease, constant risk of infection, constant presence of death.

The guards looked at each other. Youth and inexperience warring with the desire to avoid making a mistake that would result in punishment. The explosion above was still audible—distant shouts, running feet, the continued chaos of Margarethe's diversion keeping everyone's attention focused elsewhere.

"Five minutes," the older guard finally said, unlocking the heavy door with a key from his belt. "That's all. And we'll be right outside listening, so don't try anything suspicious."

"I'm here to pray, not to fight," Thomas said mildly, which was technically true even if the prayer would be very brief and focused on _please God let this work_.

He entered the cell corridor, and the door closed behind him with the particular sound heavy wood makes when designed to contain people who don't want to be contained.

Three cells, all occupied. Thomas could see figures in the first two—prisoners from other crimes, other problems the bishop wanted to deal with quietly. He found Johannes in the farthest cell.

The alchemist was barely recognizable. His face had been beaten into pulp—purple-black bruising, one eye swollen shut, the other a slit leaking red from burst vessels. Chains suspended him by the wrists, forcing him half-upright in a position that screamed shoulder dislocation. His scholar's robes hung in tatters, stiff with dried blood and worse.

"Johannes," Thomas whispered urgently.

The alchemist's good eye opened—or tried to, the swelling made it difficult. Focused. Recognition dawned, followed immediately by horror.

"Thomas?" His voice was rough, damaged from screaming. "You idiot. You absolute idiot. You shouldn't be—"

"Shut up. I'm getting you out." Thomas pulled out his lock picks, started working on the cell door. His hands shook—from fear or adrenaline or the horror of seeing what they'd done to Johannes in just two days—but his fingers remembered the training. The lock was simpler than the Bavarian mechanism from the treasury. Basic church-quality security, meant to keep peasants and drunks contained, not sophisticated thieves with specialized tools.

"You'll be captured," Johannes said, his voice gaining strength despite the damage. "Both keys will be lost. Everything we've worked for will—"

"I said shut up." The first pin aligned. Click. "Margarethe's creating a diversion. Ten minutes. Maybe less. Save your breath for walking."

"Margarethe's here too?" Johannes sounded caught between gratitude and horror. "You're both—"

"Insane. Yes. You mentioned." The second pin aligned. Third pin. Fourth. The lock clicked open. "Did you think we'd leave you?"

A beat of silence. Johannes didn't answer the question. Didn't need to—they both knew the smart move was abandonment.

"I thought you'd do the smart thing. The logical thing." But Johannes was trying to smile despite the split lips and broken teeth and blood crusting his mouth.

"We tried. Turns out we're both terrible at logic when it involves leaving people to die." Thomas entered the cell, started working on the chains. These locks were even simpler—basic restraint mechanisms designed for security rather than sophistication. "How badly are you hurt?"

"Ribs broken. Internal bleeding, I think—I keep coughing blood and it tastes like copper and rot. Left leg twisted when they dropped me—might be fractured, might just be badly sprained." Johannes listed his injuries with the cold precision of someone trained in natural philosophy. "They tortured me for information. Asking about the network, about you, about where you'd go. What you knew. Who else was involved."

"What did you tell them?" Thomas got the first chain open, supporting Johannes's weight as the alchemist's arm dropped, unable to hold itself up.

"Nothing useful. Lies. Misdirection. Sent them chasing shadows in the wrong directions." Johannes coughed, and blood flecked his lips—bright red, which was bad. Internal bleeding. "But everyone breaks eventually, Thomas. You know that. Another day, perhaps two, and I would have told them everything. Would have given them your names, your descriptions, where you planned to go, what you were looking for."

"But you didn't. That's what matters." Thomas got the second chain open. Johannes collapsed against him, unable to stand on his own, all his weight suddenly transferred to Thomas's shoulders. "Can you walk?"

"I can barely stand. Walking might be optimistic." Johannes tried to put weight on his injured leg, gasped in pain, nearly fell. "You need to leave me. Take the keys. Run while you still can. I'm just dead weight that will get you captured."

"Stop talking and lean on me." Thomas wrapped Johannes's arm around his shoulders, took most of the alchemist's weight. They stumbled toward the cell door together, moving with painful slowness.

Outside the cells, shouts. The guards had figured out something was wrong. Perhaps the explosion hadn't been as long a diversion as they'd hoped. Perhaps someone had sent word about a suspicious monk. Perhaps the guards' instincts had kicked in and they'd decided to check on the prisoner.

Thomas and Johannes stumbled into the corridor. The two guards were drawing swords, their young faces shifting from confusion to alarm to determination.

"Sorry about this," Thomas said.

Then Margarethe dropped from the ventilation shaft above—a narrow access tunnel for air circulation that she'd somehow squeezed through, her body contorting in ways Thomas hadn't thought possible—and landed between Thomas and the guards like a cat falling four stories and landing on its feet.

She was a blur of motion. Knife flashing in torchlight. One guard down, clutching a bleeding arm—cut precisely to disable without killing, exactly where the major tendons would sever and make sword-holding impossible. The other guard backing away, his sword raised but his eyes showing fear.

"Go!" Margarethe shouted. "Get him out! I'll handle this!"

Thomas half-carried Johannes toward the stairs, the alchemist's breathing labored and wet-sounding. Behind them, sounds of fighting. Margarethe was good—better than good, trained by her father and survival and necessity into something dangerous and precise—but she was fighting trained soldiers in an enclosed space where her mobility advantage was limited.

They made it to the stairs. Started climbing, every step an agony for Johannes who couldn't put weight on his injured leg.

More guards above, coming down to investigate the sounds of fighting. Three of them, armored, with swords drawn and that trained readiness that meant cathedral guards, not city watch. These were men who'd fought in wars, who knew what they were doing, who wouldn't hesitate.

Thomas looked around desperately, searching for options in a stone stairwell that offered none. Nowhere to hide. Nowhere to run. Trapped between guards above and below, with a wounded alchemist who could barely stand and no weapons except his lock picks that were absolutely useless in a fight.

Then a door opened to the side—Thomas hadn't even noticed it in the stone wall, so well did it blend with the masonry. A priest stood there, old, wrinkled, with kind eyes that nevertheless showed sharp intelligence.

"This way. Quickly."

Thomas didn't question. Didn't hesitate. Just pulled Johannes through the door while the old priest held it open.

The priest led them through a hidden passage, moving with surprising speed for his age, his footsteps sure on stone floors he'd obviously walked many times before. "I'm Father Marcus," he said quietly as they moved. "I knew Wilhelm—your grandfather. Good man. Brilliant mind. We studied together in Paris, decades ago. This network he was part of… it's not what it used to be. Not what it was meant to be when it was founded."

They emerged in a storage room filled with cleaning supplies and church vestments and the accumulated debris of centuries of cathedral life. Father Marcus handed Thomas a key, his gnarled fingers pressing it into Thomas's palm with gentle insistence. Then he pulled a scroll from within his robes—old vellum, carefully preserved. "Wilhelm's notes. He left them with me for safekeeping years ago, said if anything happened to him, I should give them to his successor. I think that's you."

Thomas took the scroll, felt the weight of his grandfather's legacy in his hands.

"Side exit. Through the garden on the north side," Father Marcus continued urgently. "Guards are all dealing with the fire and the fight. You have minutes—perhaps less—but it's your only chance."

"Thank you." Thomas tucked the scroll inside his robe.

"Don't thank me. Just survive. Understand what the Protocol really means, not what they've corrupted it into. And stop them if you can. Stop what the network has become." Father Marcus disappeared back into the passage, the door closing behind him so smoothly Thomas could barely see where it had been in the wall.

Thomas and Johannes stumbled through the storage room, out into a garden that smelled of late autumn and decay and the particular sweetness of roses going to winter sleep. The cold night air hit them after the stuffy interior—shocking and sharp, frost crackling underfoot, the smell of woodsmoke from distant hearths mixing with frozen earth.

"Where's Margarethe?" Johannes whispered.

As if summoned by her name, she appeared from another direction, breathing hard, blood on her knife and a cut on her cheek that hadn't been there before. "Guards down. Not dead but they won't follow for a while. We need to—"

An arrow struck the ground between them, its iron point burying three inches into the frozen earth.

Gray Robes. Four of them, emerging from the shadows of the garden like manifestations of death itself. Bows drawn. More arrows nocked and aimed.

"Run," Margarethe said. Her face had gone blank—the warrior's mask.

They ran.

## Scene 3: The Escape - Johannes's Final Words

### Safe House, Strasbourg Outskirts, Deep Night

They made it to the safe house—a different one from before, further from the city, a half-collapsed barn that smelled of old hay and animal waste and abandonment—just as Johannes collapsed completely.

Thomas caught him, tried to ease him down gently onto the straw-covered floor, but the alchemist's weight was dead weight and they both went down in a tangle of limbs and blood and broken bodies.

"He's dying," Margarethe said bluntly, kneeling beside them to check Johannes's injuries with hands that knew such wounds from bitter experience. "Blood inside. Look at his belly—swollen, tight. Probably broken ribs driven inward during the torture. And his breathing is too shallow, too fast. A lung pierced, perhaps, or just too much damage to his chest."

"Can we—" Thomas started.

"No. Not without a physician, and we can't risk bringing one here. Even if we could, he's too far gone." Margarethe shook her head. "He'll be dead by morning. Perhaps sooner."

Johannes coughed, and blood bubbled from his lips—frothy, bright red, the color of arterial bleeding. "Not… dead yet. Just… resting. Taking a brief pause to appreciate… the absurdity of being rescued only to die anyway."

"Save your strength," Thomas said. His throat tightened, eyes burning.

Another person dying for knowledge he carried. Another debt he'd never repay.

"For what? More bleeding? More dying?" Johannes grabbed Thomas's hand with surprising strength considering how broken he was. "No. Stop pretending and listen. I need to tell you about the third key before I run out of time and breath."

Margarethe leaned closer.

"The third key is a person," Johannes interrupted, speaking with the urgency of someone who could feel time running out. "Not a metal object like the Iron and Silver Keys you carry. Not a manuscript or hidden location. A human being. A living descendant who carries cipher knowledge in their blood memory. Written into their very blood, passed down through forty generations like hair color or eye color or any other trait parents give children."

He coughed again, more blood painting his chin red. Thomas tried to wipe it away but Johannes pushed his hand back. "Let me talk. Need to… need to get this out while I still can."

"Where is this person?" Thomas asked gently.

"Was here. In Strasbourg. Woman named Adela—brilliant scholar, could read Latin and Greek and Hebrew, memorized entire manuscripts in languages she'd only studied casually. The network bred her line specifically to carry the third fragment of the Protocol. Forty generations of careful breeding to create someone who could reach back into blood memory and hold knowledge too dangerous to trust to parchment." Johannes's breathing was getting more labored, each word coming with visible effort. "But the plague took her. Three weeks ago. I tried to save her with every remedy I knew—Greek medicine, Arabic treatments, even desperate compounds I mixed specifically for the pestilence—but nothing worked. She died screaming, covered in buboes, drowning in her own infected fluids."

"Then the third key is lost," Margarethe said. Her shoulders sagged.

"No. She had a daughter. Girl perhaps fourteen years old, named Maria. The key passed to her through bloodline inheritance—that's how blood memory works. The mother's knowledge, the grandmother's knowledge, all the way back forty generations, all of it carried in the child's blood, waiting to be woken." Johannes's good eye fixed on Thomas with desperate intensity. "You need to find her. Bring her with you. She's the third key. Without her, you can't access the next layer of the Protocol. Without her, you're operating blind with only fragments of the complete picture."

"Where?" Thomas asked, already knowing they'd go, already knowing there was no choice but to continue forward no matter how dangerous it became.

"St. Catherine's Convent. Outside the city on the eastern road. The nuns took her in when Adela died—the girl had nowhere else to go, no other family, just a dead mother and a legacy in her blood she doesn't understand." Johannes coughed violently, his whole body shaking with the force of it. When he pulled his hand away from his mouth, it was completely covered in blood—bright arterial red mixed with darker venous blood and what looked like tissue fragments. "You need to find her before the Order does. Before they realize Adela's daughter inherited the key. Before they hide her away for breeding—or kill her—to keep us from accessing the third fragment."

"We'll find her," Thomas promised, gripping Johannes's bloody hand. "We'll protect her. We'll use her knowledge to unlock the Protocol and understand what the network is really doing."

"Good. Good." Johannes's breathing was becoming shallower, more irregular, the pauses between breaths growing longer. "The Protocol… it's not what they think. The network… they corrupted it. Changed it. Turned preservation into something else. Something darker."

"What was the original purpose?" Margarethe asked, leaning close to hear Johannes's weakening voice.

"To prevent collapse. Not make it. Not control who survives. But they… they twisted it over centuries. Turned saving humanity into playing God. Turned knowledge preservation into bloodline manipulation." Johannes's eye was losing focus, his gaze going distant. "The seven keys. They don't just unlock information. They unlock locations. Seven places across Europe where fragments of the original Protocol are hidden. Each fragment shows part of the truth. Together… together they show what it was meant to be before corruption."

"Where are these locations?" Thomas asked urgently, sensing they were running out of time.

"Strasbourg has one fragment—I found it years ago, memorized it before the Order destroyed the original manuscript. Prague has one—that's where Maria can help you access it using her blood memory. Constantinople. Cairo. Somewhere in England, I never learned exactly where. Two others…" Johannes coughed again, violently, his whole body convulsing. "Two others I never discovered. Lost to time or fire or the Order's purges."

His breathing was slowing. The pauses between breaths growing long enough that Thomas thought each one might be the last.

"The complete Protocol," Johannes whispered, his voice barely audible, "shows the truth about kingdoms falling. About the pattern. About what happens when cities and kingdoms grow too tangled and fragile. The network was created to guide recovery. To preserve knowledge through the dark ages that follow collapse. But guiding recovery became making collapse. Preserving knowledge became controlling who gets to survive to use that knowledge."

"We'll find all seven fragments," Thomas said, though he had no idea how they'd manage it. "We'll understand the complete truth."

"You have to. Because…" Johannes's eye closed. His breathing became irregular, shallow gasps separated by long pauses. "Because they're planning something. The modern network. Centuries from now. They're building toward something terrible using the corrupted Protocol as justification. I've seen fragments of their long-term planning. Breeding programs that extend for generations. Plans to shape whole populations. Schemes to steer collapse. They're not trying to prevent catastrophe anymore. They're trying to cause it in a way they can control."

His hand went limp in Thomas's grip.

For a moment, Thomas hoped he was just unconscious, just too weak to maintain consciousness but still alive, still breathing, still savable.

But when Margarethe checked for a pulse in Johannes's neck, her expression told Thomas everything he needed to know.

"He's gone," she said quietly.

Thomas sat back, numb. Another death. Another person who'd helped them, dead because of it. Hamo. The guards they'd injured escaping. Now Johannes. How many more before this was finished? How many more lives would be spent purchasing knowledge about a conspiracy most people didn't even know existed?

"We should bury him."

They buried Johannes in the forest behind the barn, digging in cold earth that fought them with every shovelful. No marker. No ceremony. Just two people committing a good man to the ground and hoping God would judge him kindly despite the fact that he'd helped thieves and defied bishops and died outside the Church's grace.

When it was done, Thomas stood over the unmarked grave in silence, trying to find words that would make this mean something, make Johannes's death count for something more than just another body the network had created through its manipulations.

"We need to find Maria," Margarethe said finally, pulling him back to practical concerns. "The third key. If she's at St. Catherine's Convent, we need to get her before the Order figures out she exists."

"How do we approach this?" Thomas asked, grateful for planning that didn't require him to name his grief. "She's a girl who just lost her mother. We can't just walk in and tell her she's a key bred for a thousand-year conspiracy. She'll think we're insane."

"We tell her the truth. Show her the cipher. Let her see what she carries." Margarethe started walking back toward the barn, moving with purpose now that there was a clear next objective. "And we give her a choice. Come with us and unlock her heritage, or stay hidden and hope the Order never finds her. It's more choice than most people in the network ever got. More than your grandfather got. More than my parents got. More than Johannes got."

Thomas looked back at the unmarked grave one last time. "Will this ever be worth it? All these deaths?"

"I don't know," Margarethe said honestly. "But we're already too far in to stop. Too many people have died for us to give up now. So we keep going. Find Maria. Unlock the third fragment. Continue toward Prague and whatever answers wait there. Make all these deaths mean something even if we die ourselves in the process."

She was right. They were committed now. No going back. No undoing what had been done.

Only forward, into more danger, toward answers that might be worse than ignorance.

Thomas followed Margarethe back to the barn, and together they began planning their approach to St. Catherine's Convent and the girl who carried the third key in her very blood.

## Scene 4: St. Catherine's Convent - Finding Maria

### St. Catherine's Convent, Next Morning

The convent sat on a hill overlooking Strasbourg like a watchful stone sentinel, far enough from the city to avoid the worst of the plague's ravages but close enough to take in refugees and orphans and women fleeing domestic situations that had become dangerous in desperate times.

Thomas and Margarethe approached at midmorning, when the nuns would be finished with prime prayers but not yet deep into the day's work. They'd cleaned themselves as best they could in a stream, changed into the least bloodstained of their remaining clothes, tried to look like travelers seeking legitimate shelter rather than fugitives seeking access to a human key bred for someone else's purpose.

The abbess who met them at the gate was a severe woman in her fifties, with the kind of face that suggested she'd seen every form of human suffering and deception and wasn't impressed by new variations. "We have little to spare for wayfarers," she said without preamble, her eyes cataloging Thomas's monk's robes—stolen and ill-fitting—and Margarethe's practical traveling clothes that suggested a woman who'd learned to move through the world without male protection. "The plague has brought many to our door. We do what we can but our resources are limited. If you're seeking food or shelter—"

"We're seeking someone specific," Margarethe interrupted gently. "A girl named Maria. Her mother was Adela."

The abbess's expression shifted—not quite suspicion but definitely wariness. "What business do you have with Maria?"

Thomas pulled out the pendant Johannes had saved from his workshop before it was raided and burned—small silver piece with cipher marks worked into the decorative elements so subtly they looked like ornamentation to anyone who didn't know what to look for. "Her mother was… connected to our family. Distant connections through scholarly networks. We promised to look after her daughter if anything happened to Adela. To make sure Maria was cared for and protected."

It was half truth and half calculated deception. The best kind of lie, built on enough reality to be convincing.

The abbess took the pendant, examined it with sharp eyes that missed nothing. Turned it over in her weathered hands, studying the cipher marks without appearing to recognize them as anything more than pretty geometric patterns. "This was Adela's. I saw her wear it many times during her visits here over the years. She came to pray when the plague fears first started spreading, seeking divine protection for her daughter."

She looked up at them, her gaze sharp and assessing. "You're not pilgrims, are you? You're not travelers seeking shelter. You're from the network."

Thomas and Margarethe tensed simultaneously, ready to run if this was betrayal, ready to fight if necessary, though fighting nuns seemed like a fast track to damnation even in their current situation.

But the abbess continued: "Adela told me some of it before she died. Not everything—she was careful even fevered and knowing she had days left at most—but enough. About the cipher. About what Maria carries. About the knowledge encoded in bloodlines and passed through generations like original sin. She made me promise to protect the girl from the Order—from the Gray Robes who hunt your people, who burn your knowledge, who view the network as heresy incarnate."

The abbess handed the pendant back to Thomas. "But she also said someone from the network might come eventually. Someone who needed what Maria has. Someone who would try to use the girl's gift for their purposes. So before I take you to her, tell me honestly: Are you here to use her? Or to help her?"

Thomas thought about Johannes dying in a barn, about Wilhelm tortured to death for his knowledge, about Margarethe's parents murdered by the same network that had created them through generations of breeding. About how easy it would be to view Maria as a tool—a living key to unlock information they needed—rather than a person with her own fears and desires and the right to choose her own path.

"Both," he said honestly, because lying to an abbess seemed worse than admitting uncomfortable truths. "We need her help to access knowledge we can't reach without her. But we also want to protect her from people who would use her without her consent, who would breed her for the next generation, who would see her as breeding stock rather than a human being. We're offering her a choice—come with us and learn what she carries, or stay here hidden and hope the Order never realizes she exists."

The abbess studied him for a long moment, searching his face for deception or malice or the particular manipulation that characterized people who'd learned to use pretty words to disguise ugly intentions.

Finally, she nodded. "That's more honest than most people in your position would be. And more choice than Adela ever had—she was born into the network's breeding program, married to a man the network chose because their bloodlines suited its purposes, became pregnant because the network decided her bloodline needed continuation. The only choice she ever truly made was refusing to have more children after Maria. That defiance cost her dearly even before the plague took her."

She gestured for them to follow. "Come. I'll take you to her. But if you hurt that girl, if you use her purely as a tool, if you betray the trust she's about to give you, I will personally ensure the Order finds you. Do we understand each other?"

"Aye, Mother Superior," Thomas said, using the respectful form of address automatically.

They followed the abbess through the convent—quiet stone corridors that smelled of beeswax candles and old incense and the particular cleanliness of spaces maintained by people who viewed tidiness as godliness. Past the chapel where nuns were singing Terce, their voices rising in plainchant harmonies that were genuinely beautiful. Past the kitchen where lay sisters were preparing the midday meal, working with the efficient precision of people who'd done the same tasks for years.

To a small room in the living quarters where a girl sat by the window, reading by the gray autumn light that filtered through oiled parchment windowpanes.

"Maria," the abbess said gently. "These people knew your mother. They need to speak with you about… family matters."

The girl looked up from her book—Latin text, Thomas noticed, something theological or philosophical. She was perhaps fourteen, with her mother's dark hair worn in two long braids, sharp intelligent features that suggested Adela's scholarly gifts had passed to the daughter, and eyes that were red-rimmed from recent crying but currently clear and focused.

"About what?" she asked, her voice wary.

"About what you are," Thomas said carefully, stepping forward slowly so as not to startle her. "About what your mother was. About the knowledge you carry without fully knowing it."

Maria set down the book—carefully, marking her place with a strip of cloth, the gesture of someone who respected written words. "I don't understand."

"Your mother was part of something," Margarethe said, her voice softer than Thomas had ever heard it. "A network that preserves knowledge across generations. Not just in books and manuscripts—those burn too easily, get lost too frequently. In people. In bloodlines. In blood memory that passes from parent to child like eye color or height or any other inherited trait."

"That's not possible," Maria said, but her voice lacked conviction. Like someone denying what they already suspected was true.

Thomas pulled out the manuscripts, the Iron and Silver Keys. Spread them on the small table beside Maria's chair. "Your mother could access memories from her ancestors. Knowledge they knew, encoded in her very being. You have the same ability—stronger, if anything, because you're younger and the memories are clearer when accessed earlier."

He demonstrated, placing both keys on the manuscript in the specific alignment that revealed the cipher. "This is knowledge hidden in plain sight. Encoded so that only people with the right keys—and the right blood memory to interpret it—can access the full information."

Maria leaned forward despite herself, curiosity overcoming wariness. She was a scholar's daughter, raised on books and learning, unable to resist the lure of hidden knowledge being revealed.

"Touch the page," Thomas said gently. "While the keys are active. Just your fingertips against the parchment where the cipher marks appear."

Maria hesitated, her hand hovering over the manuscript, intelligent enough to recognize this was a threshold moment—that touching would change something fundamental about how she understood herself and her place in the world.

Then she reached out. Her fingers brushed the manuscript where the cipher marks glowed with the light of both keys working together.

And gasped.

Her eyes went wide, unfocused, seeing something beyond the room. Her pupils dilated. Her breathing changed, becoming rapid and shallow like someone experiencing intense emotion or shock or both simultaneously.

"What's happening to her?" the abbess demanded, moving to intervene.

"Blood memory—activating," Margarethe said, gently restraining the abbess with one hand on her arm. "The cipher is waking inherited memory carried in her bloodline. Memories from her mother, grandmother, great-grandmother, all the way back. Just watch. Don't interrupt. The activation needs to complete or it could damage her mind."

Maria's hand moved across the page, tracing patterns no one else could see. She began speaking, but not in German. In Latin first—formal classical Latin, not the Church Latin spoken in services but the pure Roman dialect that scholars studied from ancient texts. Then Greek—Koine Greek, the language of the New Testament and Byzantine scholars. Then Arabic—flowing script-sounds that Thomas couldn't follow but recognized from hearing traveling merchants.

"Location marks," Margarethe whispered, recognizing the pattern. "She's reciting the third fragment of the Genesis Protocol. The part her bloodline was bred to carry. Knowledge her ancestors memorized and written into their very blood so it could survive even when the original texts were destroyed."

Maria spoke for what felt like hours but was probably only ten minutes, her voice steady and clear despite the fact that her conscious mind was clearly elsewhere, accessing memories that weren't hers but somehow were, inherited knowledge coded into the very structure of her being.

She drew symbols in the air with her free hand, traced patterns on the table, spoke words in languages that sounded ancient and powerful—perhaps pre-Roman, perhaps something older, languages that existed before Latin spread across Europe like cultural wildfire.

Then she collapsed.

Margarethe caught her before she could hit the floor, eased her gently into the chair. "It's all right. You're all right. The first activation is always overwhelming—like trying to drink from a river using your hands instead of a cup. Too much information flowing too fast. But you did beautifully. Just breathe. Come back slowly."

Maria's eyes focused again—not immediately, but gradually, like someone surfacing from deep water into light. Normal brown eyes instead of the distant unfocused stare of blood memory access. But changed somehow, like she was seeing the world through different perspectives, understanding it through the accumulated wisdom of forty generations.

"I remember," she whispered, her voice hoarse. "Not my memories. Theirs. My mother. My grandmother. Back and back and back. Forty generations of women carrying this knowledge. Memorizing it. Encoding it in their blood and bones so it could survive when everything else burned."

She looked at her hands like they belonged to someone else, like they were vessels containing more than just flesh and blood and bone. "They made us into living books. Didn't they? The network. They bred us specifically to carry information that couldn't be safely written down. Made blood memory into a library that can't burn."

"Aye," Thomas said, because lying seemed pointless when she could literally access her ancestors' memories and learn the truth herself.

"And my mother died protecting this?" Maria's voice was steady but her eyes were filling with tears. "This curse? This burden of carrying knowledge I never asked for?"

"Your mother died of plague," the abbess said firmly, moving to put her hands on Maria's shoulders in a gesture of comfort and grounding. "Not because of the network. The plague takes everyone—rich and poor, chosen bloodlines and random peasants. It doesn't care about bloodline schemes or secret plots."

"But the plague came because the network let it come," Maria said with the eerie certainty of someone accessing ancestral memories. "I can see it now in the fragments. The pattern. They've done this before—made it happen, or at least stood aside while it spread—because it served their purposes. Thinning the weak. Testing the bloodlines. Leaving fewer mouths when stores ran low. This plague isn't an accident to them. It's part of their design."

She stood abruptly, suddenly decisive despite having just experienced something that should have left her shattered. "You're going to Prague. To find the fourth fragment. To continue unlocking the Protocol piece by piece across Europe. And you need me to access memories you can't reach, to interpret knowledge written in blood rather than ink."

"We're giving you a choice," Thomas said, echoing what he'd told the abbess. "You can stay here. Safe. Hidden. The Order doesn't know you're the third key yet—doesn't know your mother's death passed the blood memory to you. You could live a quiet life, take vows and become a nun, or eventually leave the convent and marry someone of your choosing and have children who might or might not inherit your gift."

"A normal life?" Maria laughed, and the sound was bitter and sharp and far too old for a fourteen-year-old's throat. "I just felt forty generations of memories flow through me like a river. I know things no one my age should know. I dream in languages I've never consciously learned. I can see patterns in history and blood and the ways of men that took scholars lifetimes to understand."

She looked at her hands as though they belonged to someone else. "Normal died the moment I touched that manuscript and woke something that's been sleeping in me since birth."

She looked at the abbess with eyes that seemed to contain more than one person's perspective. "Mother Superior, I need to go with them."

"Maria—" the abbess started.

"I need to understand what I am. What my mother was. What forty generations of my family died to protect or were murdered for refusing to cooperate with." Maria's voice was steady, resolved in the way that suggested she'd already made the decision and was just informing everyone else. "And I can help stop whatever the network is planning. Use this curse for something good instead of just carrying it forward to burden the next generation."

She picked up a small bag—already packed, Thomas noticed, like she'd been waiting for this moment or something like it. "Where's Prague?"

"East," Thomas said, making his own decision in that moment to accept her help despite knowing they were leading a fourteen-year-old girl into danger. "Weeks of travel through plague country. Past territories controlled by nobles who might be network members or Order supporters or just bandits exploiting the chaos. The Order is hunting us specifically—they captured and tortured our friend for information about where we're going and what we know. This will be dangerous. Probably fatal. We might all die before reaching the seventh fragment."

"Then we should start walking," Maria said with the practical determination of someone who'd just accessed forty generations of human experience and decided that fear was less important than purpose. "I can tell you what I saw in the memories. About the fourth fragment's location. About what the Protocol actually says when you combine all the pieces instead of working from corrupted fragments."

She turned to the abbess, her young face showing both gratitude and sorrow. "Thank you for taking me in when mother died. For protecting me when I had nowhere else to go. For keeping me safe these weeks while the world fell apart outside these walls."

"Child—" the abbess's voice was thick with emotion she was clearly trying to control.

Maria hugged her quickly, fiercely, the embrace of someone who knew they might never see this person again. "I'll remember you. You'll be part of my memories now, written into my blood, passed to my children if I live long enough to have any. That's how our gift works—we remember everything important, preserve it in our very being, carry it forward through generations."

She pulled back, adjusted the bag across her shoulder with the practical efficiency of someone much older than fourteen. "I'm ready."

They left the convent as the sun climbed toward noon, three people carrying two metal keys and one human key whose blood carried knowledge shaped across forty generations of careful breeding.

Behind them, the abbess watched from the gate until they disappeared into the forest that lined the eastern road.

Then she went inside and began burning records—any mention of Maria's name, her mother's name, the family connection to the network. Erasing what she could of the girl's past to protect her future, even though that future was heading directly into danger the abbess could neither prevent nor protect against.

The third key had been found.

The quest was continuing.

And somewhere in Strasbourg and beyond, the network and the Order both realized that the bloodlines were gathering, the keys were uniting, and the Genesis Protocol might actually be read in its entirety for the first time in centuries.

The race was accelerating.

## Scene 5: The Road to Prague - What Maria Knows

### Forest Road East of Strasbourg, Late Afternoon

They walked in silence for the first hour, giving Maria time to take in what had happened, giving themselves time to adjust to the reality of traveling with a fourteen-year-old girl who carried forty generations of memory in her head and seemed simultaneously ancient and achingly young.

The forest was quiet except for their footsteps and the occasional bird call and the rustle of wind through bare branches. Autumn was deepening toward winter, the cold becoming sharper, the days growing shorter, the signs of coming hardship visible in everything from the angle of sunlight to the behavior of animals gathering food for lean months ahead.

"They bred my mother specifically to create me," Maria said suddenly, breaking the silence. "I can see it in the memories. Not her choice—their choice. The network's. My father was chosen from the lines they approved. Go-betweens arranged the match and called it chance, romance, fate. They said my parents' blood would suit. I wasn't conceived through love or desire or even accident. I was made."

Thomas and Margarethe exchanged glances but said nothing, letting her talk until the words ran thin.

"My mother tried to resist after I was born," Maria continued, her voice steady but her eyes distant, accessing memories that weren't quite hers. "The network wanted more children—wanted to spread her bloodline traits wider, create more carriers of the third key in case something happened to me. But she refused. Said one child was enough. Said she wanted to focus on raising me properly rather than bearing more children for the network's purposes. They… didn't take that refusal well."

"We know," Margarethe said quietly. "We found the records in the bloodline manuscripts. The notation about your mother's 'complications' during your birth. How it was listed as deliberate. An example to enforce compliance."

Maria looked at her sharply. "You've seen the bloodline tracking? The breeding records?"

"We've seen enough," Thomas said. "Enough to understand that the network has been playing God with bloodlines for longer than anyone should. Enough to know that you, me, Margarethe—we're all their handiwork. Products of generations of manipulation. Things made for purposes we never chose."

"But you can be a tool and a person," Maria said, and her voice held the accumulated wisdom of women who'd struggled with this same question for forty generations. "My grandmother said that. And her grandmother before her. They knew they were bred for purpose, knew they carried knowledge that made them valuable to the network, but they chose what to do with that knowledge. Some cooperated. Some resisted. Some tried to ignore it and live normal lives until the network forced the issue. The breeding makes us valuable. Our choices make us human."

Margarethe smiled grimly. "You sound like someone much older."

"I am someone much older. I'm fourteen years old and also every woman in my bloodline simultaneously. Their memories are my memories now. Their knowledge is my knowledge. Their struggles are mine to inherit, along with the blood they gave me." Maria adjusted her bag, kept walking with determination. "The fourth fragment is in Prague. Hidden in a location my family has protected for nineteen generations since it was first encoded into our bloodline. The scholar's library in the Jewish quarter—a place that's burned and been rebuilt three times, but the knowledge survived because we carried it in our minds rather than trusting only paper and ink."

"What does the fragment say?" Thomas asked, curious despite knowing that fragmentary knowledge was dangerous, that understanding pieces without the whole often led to worse misunderstanding than ignorance.

"It's part of a warning," Maria said, her voice taking on a distant quality that suggested she was accessing deep memory rather than speaking from conscious knowledge. "The original Genesis Protocol wasn't written to bring ruin. It was meant to fend it off—to teach the signs of a coming fall. How kingdoms grow rich and tangled until they cannot bend. How pride and plenty make them brittle. How one hard blow can topple what seemed unshakable."

She walked in silence for a moment, her young face showing concentration as she pulled more information from blood memory. "The Bronze Age collapse. The fall of Rome. The current plague that's killing a third of Europe. Same pattern. Same cycle. Same ending—when people chase ease and power and forget to keep stores and spare ways."

"So the Protocol was meant to break the cycle?" Margarethe asked.

"It was meant to shepherd the cycle. Accept that falls come—at least until humanity learns to build differently—but make sure knowledge survives the dark ages that follow. Preserve the hard-won wisdom of the last world so the next can rebuild faster, better, with fewer mistakes repeated." Maria's voice was certain, speaking knowledge her ancestors had memorized and encoded. "But somewhere over the centuries, shepherding the cycle became hastening the fall. Preserving knowledge became controlling who gets to use that knowledge. The network corrupted the original purpose. Turned a defensive safeguard into an offensive weapon."

Thomas thought about the bloodline manuscripts, the breeding programs, the casual way the network discussed arranging marriages and shaping whole families. "They're not trying to prevent catastrophe anymore. They're trying to control it. Make sure the 'right people' survive."

"Exactly. And if the pattern continues into future centuries, they'll take it even further. Loose plagues or poisons, break trade and coin, starve cities—anything to thin the world to a number they can rule, while keeping protected bloodlines safe to rebuild on their terms."

Maria stopped walking, turned to face Thomas and Margarethe directly. "That's what we're trying to stop. Not just some cloistered plot. A thousand-year plan that reaches beyond our lifetimes. A corruption so deep that the people carrying it out honestly believe they're saving humanity when they're actually committing the greatest atrocity in history."

The forest seemed very quiet around them. Very cold. Very empty of hope.

"Can we stop it?" Thomas asked, almost afraid to hear the answer.

"I don't know," Maria said honestly. "The complete Protocol might contain the answer—might show how the original founders intended to prevent collapse without making it, how to preserve knowledge without breeding people like livestock, how to break the cycle instead of perpetuating it. But we need all seven fragments. All seven keys. The complete picture instead of these pieces we're working from."

She started walking again, her young shoulders squared with determination that came from forty generations of women who'd carried this burden before her. "So we go to Prague. Find the fourth fragment. Continue gathering keys and knowledge across Europe. And we hope that somewhere in the complete Protocol is the information we need to stop what the network has become."

"Even if it kills us," Margarethe added.

"Even if it kills us," Maria agreed. "Because the alternative is letting them win. Letting the corruption continue. Letting a thousand years of knowledge preservation become centuries of bloodline manipulation and made calamity. My mother died of plague. My grandmother died in childbirth after refusing another pregnancy. My great-grandmother was murdered for trying to leave the network. I won't let their suffering be for nothing."

They walked on through the autumn forest, three people carrying impossible knowledge toward an uncertain destination, knowing they were probably going to die but choosing to continue anyway because the alternative was surrender.

Thomas felt the weight of both keys in his pack—Iron and Silver, the Eye that Sees and the Hand that Guides. And walking beside him, the third key—not metal but human, a fourteen-year-old girl who contained forty generations of memory and seemed simultaneously too young for this burden and older than all of them combined.

Somewhere ahead, Prague waited. And the fourth fragment. And more danger than they could probably survive.

But they walked anyway.

Because sometimes the only choice was between surrendering to the inevitable and fighting against it even when fighting seemed hopeless.

And they'd all already made that choice.

Together.

# CHAPTER 6 - THE QUEST BEGINS (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: The Decision - From Running to Seeking

### Forest Road East of Strasbourg, Three Days After Leaving the Convent

Thomas's feet hurt in ways he hadn't known feet could hurt.

Not the sharp pain of blisters—though he had those too. Burning reminders of how monastery life hadn't prepared him for walking fifteen miles a day through November cold.

This was deeper. Bone-deep exhaustion that radiated up through his calves and thighs and settled into his lower back like stones.

Three days of continuous walking. Roads that alternated between frozen mud and sharp stones. A pack that seemed to get heavier with each mile despite containing only manuscripts and keys and the minimum supplies needed for survival.

The physical pain was bearable. Almost welcome, actually—something concrete and immediate to focus on instead of the abstract terror of being hunted by the Order or the nameless dread of pursuing a quest that would probably kill them all.

"Tell me about the seven keys again," Thomas said, partly to distract himself from his aching feet, partly because repetition helped him set patterns in order.

They were walking through forest slowly surrendering to rolling farmland—the landscape changing as they moved east, fewer dense beech and oak woods, more cleared fields. Fields that should have been brown with autumn plowing but instead showed the irregular patterns of abandonment. Crops left half-harvested, their brown stalks rotting where they stood. Stone walls crumbling from lack of maintenance. The visible scars of plague reducing populations faster than the land could adjust.

Maria answered without hesitation, her young voice carrying knowledge that predated her birth by centuries: "Seven keys unlock seven locations where fragments of the Genesis Protocol are hidden. The complete Protocol was too dangerous to keep in one place—too vulnerable to destruction by fire or conquest or the Order's systematic purges. So the founders fragmented it deliberately. Divided the knowledge across Europe in places they deemed secure enough to survive dark ages and catastrophe."

She walked with confidence that seemed impossible for a fourteen-year-old girl who'd left the only home she'd known just days ago. But Thomas was learning that Maria's youth was deceptive—her blood carried forty generations of memory and experience, making her simultaneously the youngest and oldest person in their small company.

"The Iron Key you carry"—Maria touched the pack where Thomas kept Wilhelm's inheritance carefully wrapped in oiled cloth—"unlocks the first fragment. The Eye that Sees. It reveals locations, shows where knowledge is hidden across Europe in patterns only those who understand the cipher can read. The Silver Key you stole from the bishop's treasury—the Hand that Guides—unlocks the second fragment. It shows connections between locations, reveals how information flows through the network."

The cold November wind picked up, carrying the smell of distant wood smoke and the particular scent of approaching winter—frost on dying vegetation, earth settling toward dormancy, the sharp clean smell of air too cold for decay. Thomas pulled his stolen monk's robe tighter, grateful for the thick wool even if it itched constantly.

"And you're the third key," Margarethe added from where she walked slightly ahead, her eyes constantly scanning the horizon for threats.

"Exactly." Maria's expression grew distant, accessing blood memory in the way that still made Thomas deeply uncomfortable. Her eyes unfocused, her gait becoming slightly stiff, like someone sleepwalking through landscapes only she could see. "I carry interpretation. Context. The human understanding needed to translate cipher marks and location marks into actual comprehension of what the Protocol says. Metal keys show you where and how. I show you why and what it means."

"Four more keys to find," Margarethe said. "Any idea where they are? Or are we just wandering Europe hoping to stumble across ancient conspiracies that might or might not want to kill us?"

"Two of them, aye—I have definite knowledge from blood memory passed down through my mother's line." Maria's eyes focused again, returning from whatever ancestral landscape she'd been accessing. "The fourth key is in Prague. That's our immediate destination. It's called the Voice that Speaks, and it's hidden in the Jewish quarter's scholar's library. My family—my mother's family, stretching back nineteen generations—has protected knowledge of its location. Memorized the specific building, the exact shelf, the countersigns needed to identify ourselves to the guardians who protect it."

"And after Prague?" Thomas asked.

"Constantinople. The fifth key—the Foot that Walks—is hidden somewhere in the ruins of the Great Library. The library that supposedly burned during the Fourth Crusade but actually survived partially, with network members preserving its most valuable holdings in hidden chambers beneath the destroyed sections." Maria frowned, accessing information that came with gaps and uncertainties. "My memories of Constantinople are less clear. The knowledge of that key's location was held by different bloodlines, different families. I have general information but not specific details like I have for Prague."

"So we might get to Constantinople and have to search blindly," Margarethe said.

"Perhaps. Or we might find network contacts there who can guide us. Or we might access the fifth key and it will reveal the sixth and seventh." Maria shrugged with the fatalism of someone carrying burdens too heavy for her age. "Blood memory isn't perfect. It's like a tale passed from mouth to mouth through forty generations—some details stay sharp, others blur."

Thomas thought about that as they walked. Forty generations of women carrying this knowledge in their blood, passing it mother to daughter through inheritance rather than written records. The network's solution to the problem of knowledge being too easily destroyed—make humans themselves into libraries that couldn't burn, archives that couldn't be torn down, repositories that survived dark ages through simple continuation of bloodlines.

Brilliant. And absolutely monstrous.

"And the other two keys?" he asked, though he already suspected the answer.

"Lost. Or at least, lost to my bloodline's knowledge." Maria's voice carried genuine regret, showing the fourteen-year-old beneath the ancient wisdom. "The sixth and seventh keys—the Heart that Feels and the Mind that Knows—were hidden so well that even the network's internal records became fragmented over centuries of wars and plagues and the natural entropy of secret organizations trying to maintain perfect security. Johannes knew more about them. He'd spent twenty years researching the sixth key specifically, had narrowed down its location to somewhere in England. But…"

Her voice faltered, cracking slightly with genuine emotion rather than blood memory. "But he died before he could tell you everything. Died in that barn outside Strasbourg with information still locked in his tortured body."

They walked in silence. Each carrying grief differently.

Thomas remembered Johannes's death—the brilliant, eccentric alchemist reduced to barely breathing wreckage. Coughing blood and broken pieces of information. Dying despite their rescue.

"He told us about you," Thomas said quietly to Maria. "Used his last conscious moments to ensure we knew about the third key, knew where to find you." He paused. "He could have saved his strength. Tried to survive. Focused on his own life. But he chose to do his duty instead."

A beat. "That matters, Maria. His death wasn't meaningless."

"Doesn't make it hurt less," Maria said, her voice very young and very tired. "They bred my mother like livestock. Killed her when she refused to produce more children. And I'm supposed to be grateful they gave me this gift?"

Her hands clenched. "Some gift."

Margarethe stopped walking abruptly, her hand moving to her knife with reflexive speed. "Decision point. We can keep heading east toward Prague—three weeks of travel through increasingly dangerous territory, with winter coming and the Order hunting us. Or we can find somewhere to hide for the winter, wait for spring when travel is easier and pursuit might have cooled. What's our choice?"

Thomas looked at both his companions. Margarethe, who'd lost both parents to the network's cruelty but kept fighting because the alternative was surrender. Maria, fourteen years old and orphaned and carrying blood memory of forty generations of oppression but choosing to help them rather than running. And himself—a monk who'd been bred across forty-one generations to read patterns, who'd killed and stolen and fled, who'd discovered his entire existence was someone else's design.

Three people shaped by purposes they'd never chosen.

Making choices that were entirely their own.

"We keep going," he said, making the decision as he spoke. "Prague for the fourth key. Constantinople for the fifth. And we search for the sixth and seventh while we're traveling. We don't stop. Don't pause. Don't give the Order time to catch us or the network time to recapture us."

"That could take years," Margarethe pointed out with brutal practicality. "Years of traveling through plague country where a third of Europe is dying or dead. Through territories controlled by the Order or the network or just warlords and bandits. Through winter cold that kills as surely as any Gray Robe's sword."

"We could," Thomas agreed. He stopped walking, turned to face both of them directly. The late afternoon sun painted everything in shades of gold and shadow, making the abandoned farmland look almost beautiful despite the decay. "Or we could run. Find somewhere remote—a mountain village or isolated monastery or forest deep enough that no one would find us. Hide with the two keys we have and the knowledge Maria carries. Live quiet lives, survive to old age, die peacefully in our beds having accomplished nothing except personal survival."

He met Margarethe's eyes first, then Maria's. "But I'm done running. Done letting other people die for this while I just try to stay alive. Brother Hamo died to give me the Iron Key—bled out in the monastery scriptorium to protect me and buy me time to escape. Wilhelm was tortured to death by the Order. Your parents, Margarethe—your mother murdered by the network, your father tortured to death by the Order. Johannes captured and killed because he helped us. All of them died for fragments of this Protocol. For pieces of knowledge they believed was worth dying to preserve and transmit."

His voice grew stronger, more certain. "I won't let their deaths be meaningless by refusing to complete what they started. Won't let Wilhelm's torture and Hamo's sacrifice and Johannes's last words amount to nothing because I was too frightened or too tired or too pragmatic to continue the quest. They died believing this mattered. I owe them the effort to find out if they were right."

Margarethe studied him with those sharp eyes that missed absolutely nothing—not the tremor in his hands, not the fear he was trying very hard to hide behind determination. But she also saw, he thought, that he meant it. That he'd made a choice and intended to follow through regardless of cost.

"You've really changed," she said finally. "The monk I met three weeks ago—that frightened scholar who'd never left the monastery, who thought copying manuscripts was the most dangerous thing he'd ever do—would have chosen survival without hesitation. Now you're talking about throwing yourself at a quest that will probably kill you. When did you become someone who makes decisions like that?"

"When we killed Brother Anselm," Thomas said quietly, remembering the weight of the leather-working tool in his hand, Margarethe's blade finding its mark. "That was the moment I stopped being someone who could just walk away. Murder changes you. Makes you responsible for consequences in ways you can never step back from. If I'm going to carry that sin, at least let it be for something more than just personal survival."

Maria spoke up, her young voice carrying accumulated weight: "My mother tried to run. After I was born, after she understood what the network had done—breeding her specifically to produce blood memory carriers, arranging her marriage to shape inheritance—she tried to create distance. Tried to have just one child and live a normal life. She wanted to focus on raising me properly rather than producing more children to serve purposes she'd never consented to."

Her voice tightened with emotion both personally felt and inherited through blood memory. "The network killed her for it. During childbirth. Made it look like natural complications. But my blood memory includes medical knowledge from healers in my bloodline, and I can see now what I couldn't understand as a one-year-old: The complications weren't natural. The midwives did something. Subtle. Undetectable. They murdered her and made it look like tragedy."

She took a shaky breath. "And my father—when he started investigating, started asking questions—the Order found him. Tortured him. And the network let it happen. Provided information that helped the Order find him. Arranged for his torture to look like standard heretic prosecution while actually being a purge."

Maria squared her young shoulders, forcing strength into her posture. "Running didn't save them. Trying to comply while secretly resisting didn't save them. The only choice that might matter is fighting back openly. Gathering all seven keys. Understanding the complete Protocol. Exposing what the network has become so publicly that they can't easily kill or silence everyone who knows. It might not work—probably won't work. But it's the only rebellion available to people like us who were bred to be tools but insist on being human instead."

Margarethe was quiet for a long moment, her hand resting on her knife hilt more from habit than perceived threat. The wind picked up again, carrying early evening cold. Finally she spoke:

"All right then. We're doing this. Seeking instead of just surviving. Hunting all seven keys instead of running with the two we have and the one we've become." She started walking again, her pace determined and steady. "Prague is three weeks away if we avoid main roads and move carefully. Perhaps four weeks with winter conditions and the need to stay ahead of pursuit. Longer if we encounter serious problems—which we will, because problems are inevitable when you're fugitives carrying knowledge that multiple powerful organizations want to destroy or control or reclaim."

"What kind of problems?" Maria asked with practical curiosity.

"Gray Robes hunting us—they're probably three or four days behind, moving faster than we can because they travel openly on main roads and have horses. Network hunters trying to drag back what they bred—that's you and Thomas; you're both too valuable for them to simply write off as losses. Plague-ravaged villages where survivors might see strangers as plague-bearers to be driven away or killed on suspicion. Bandits and deserters exploiting the chaos. Nobles whose territories we'll cross who might be network members or Order supporters or just paranoid enough to arrest anyone who can't prove they belong. Winter cold that makes sleeping rough dangerous, that makes rivers harder to cross, that reduces available food and increases the chances of sickness."

Margarethe listed threats with casual competence. "But we knew this would be hard when we started. At least now we're making active choices rather than just reacting to what's being done to us. That's worth something, even if it gets us killed."

Thomas felt the knot in his chest tighten into something harder. Not peace—that was too much to ask. But resolve. Direction. The difference between being a leaf blown by autumn wind and an arrow loosed toward a mark, even if the mark was far and the odds were bad.

They were no longer fugitives just trying to survive. They were seekers now.

It felt good. Terrifying, but good.

"Tell me about Prague," Thomas said to Maria as they continued walking. "What do you know from your family's memories?"

Maria's eyes went distant again, accessing inherited memory. When she spoke, her voice carried the strange dual quality of being both entirely hers and somehow channeling voices from women long dead:

"The Jewish quarter—the Jewish Town by the river—has been home to scholars and mystics and merchants for generations beyond counting. My blood memory extends back twenty-five generations of knowledge about Prague, but the community itself is older. The network maintained contacts there long before my bloodline's memory begins, back to the network's earliest days when it was still primarily about knowledge preservation rather than breeding people."

She walked automatically, her body moving while her mind accessed inherited information. "The Jewish quarter has survived fires and floods and persecutions—waves of violence against Jewish communities that have happened regularly for centuries. Each time the quarter burned or was destroyed, it was rebuilt. Each time documents were destroyed, they were recreated from memory. The scholar's library where the fourth key is hidden has been destroyed and rebuilt three times that my blood memory can verify. But it always comes back. The network ensured it—provided funds for rebuilding, sent scholars to help recreate lost texts."

"Why put a key there?" Thomas asked. "Why hide Christian network knowledge in a Jewish library?"

"Because the network has always worked across religious and cultural boundaries," Maria explained, her voice carrying patient wisdom from teachers who'd answered this same question for generations. "Christian monasteries. Islamic libraries in Baghdad and Córdoba. Jewish scholarship in Prague and Cairo. Greek philosophy preserved in Constantinople. The network didn't care about theology or cultural ownership—just about preserving knowledge wherever it could be safely hidden, maintained by communities that valued scholarship regardless of whose religion or empire claimed authority."

She paused, reaching for deeper memory. "The golem manuscript is the fourth key. Genuine kabbalistic teaching about creating life from clay using sacred names and mystical processes. But it's also a container. The cipher marks are hidden within the Hebrew text, invisible unless you know to look for them and have the metal keys to reveal them. The manuscript teaches mysticism on the surface, but underneath it contains the fourth fragment of the Genesis Protocol—information that has nothing to do with creating life and everything to do with understanding how kingdoms fall."

Thomas thought about the elegant simplicity of it. Hide knowledge of how kingdoms fall inside mystical teachings about divine creation. Put Christian network secrets inside Jewish texts that Christians wouldn't normally read carefully. Create layers of protection through cultural boundaries, language barriers, religious separation.

"What does the fourth fragment say?" he asked.

"Temporal patterns," Maria said, her voice distant. "The fourth key is called the Voice that Speaks because it reveals when things happen. Not just what collapses or how knowledge is preserved, but the timing. The centuries-long cycle of kingdoms failing. The certainty of it—how kingdoms grow mighty, then brittle, and fall in patterns you can learn to recognize. The warning signs that collapse is approaching: the poor crushed, the powerful grasping, roads and bridges failing, cities grown too tangled to endure a hard blow."

She focused again, meeting Thomas's eyes with disturbing intensity. "And it shows the corruption point. The exact moment—or as exact as can be determined from fragmentary records—when the network stopped trying to prevent collapse and started trying to make it. Eleventh century. A network leader named Benedict argued that since collapse seemed inevitable based on the pattern, the network should control when it happened rather than letting it occur randomly. Should choose the timing to benefit chosen bloodlines who'd be positioned to survive and rebuild."

"So the corruption is documented in the Protocol itself?" Margarethe asked.

"Aye. That's what makes it potentially powerful. If we can access all seven fragments, we'll see not just what the Protocol originally said but where and how and when it was corrupted. A complete record of the network's betrayal of its own founding principles. That's a blade, if we can figure out how to turn it against them."

They walked in silence for a while. The sun was getting lower, shadows lengthening across the abandoned farmland. They'd need to find shelter soon.

Margarethe pointed ahead to smoke rising from a village perhaps two miles distant. "We need supplies before we can continue to Prague. Food, warmer clothes if we can afford them, information about road conditions and Order activity in the region. Should we risk entering a village or bypass it and try to survive on foraging?"

Thomas thought about their remaining resources—limited money, even more limited food, clothes adequate for autumn but inadequate for deep winter. "We risk it. We can't walk to Prague and beyond eating only what we can forage. Winter is coming."

"Then we need a cover story that explains why three people are traveling together during plague times," Margarethe said. "A monk traveling with a woman and a young girl will raise questions."

Maria spoke up immediately: "Pilgrims. We're heading to Prague to seek healing at the shrine of Saint Vitus. I survived plague but was left weakened, and my mother"—she gestured to Margarethe—"is seeking miraculous healing since medicine failed. Brother Thomas is our religious guide, hired to ensure our spiritual safety during the dangerous journey."

"Good," Margarethe said with approval. "That's plausible. Desperate plague survivors make pilgrimages all the time. I'm a widow—recently bereaved, which is sadly common enough to be completely believable. You're my only surviving child. Thomas is earning modest payment for spiritual guidance. Simple story. Fits expectations. Easy to maintain."

Thomas nodded agreement. Simple lies worked better than complex ones.

They approached the village as afternoon transitioned toward evening, long shadows painting the landscape in dramatic contrasts.

Thomas felt the weight of their choice settling more firmly onto his shoulders.

They were committed now. No more running blindly. No more just reacting.

They were actively seeking all seven keys. Deliberately pursuing a quest that would probably kill them.

But at least they'd chosen it. At least they were dying—if they died—for something they'd decided mattered rather than something that had been decided for them by people dead for centuries.

It wasn't much agency. But it was more than most people bred as tools ever got.

And Thomas intended to make it count.

## Scene 2: The Village - Intelligence and Danger

### Village of Sankt Peter, Eastern Road

The village looked like every other village in plague-ravaged Europe—smaller than it should be, quieter than it should be, haunted by the absence of people who should have been there but weren't.

Sankt Peter had probably been home to a hundred people before the plague. The kind of agricultural settlement that had existed for centuries, enduring through wars and famines through sheer peasant stubbornness.

Now perhaps twenty people remained.

Thomas could see the evidence everywhere as they approached. Houses with doors hanging open to the elements, their interiors looted or simply abandoned when entire families died. Fields left half-harvested, grain rotting in brown stalks. A mass grave at the eastern edge of the village, fresh-turned earth showing where bodies had been buried recently—probably without proper rites.

The smell hit Thomas from fifty yards out—wood smoke and human waste and the sweet-rot odor of decay that seemed to permeate everything during plague times.

The living moved through Sankt Peter like ghosts themselves. Hollow-eyed survivors going through the motions of daily life with the numb look of people who'd watched too much death come too fast. A woman hanging laundry with tears streaming silently down her face. A man mending a fence with such fierce focus it felt like a charm—if he just kept working, kept the old routines, maybe the horror would stop pressing so close.

Children were notably absent. Either dead from plague, or sent away to relatives, or kept hidden indoors.

A priest met them at the village entrance—young man, perhaps mid-twenties but looking forty from exhaustion and grief. His robes were stained and rumpled, his face unshaven, his eyes gone distant from too many last rites in too short a time.

"Pilgrims?" he asked in German accented with the local Alemannic dialect.

"Aye, Father," Thomas confirmed, falling easily into the religious role. "Heading to Prague. To the shrine of Saint Vitus. Seeking healing for the girl." He gestured to Maria, who looked genuinely exhausted and pale enough to seem convincingly ill. "She survived the plague but was left weakened. We're hoping the saint's intercession might restore her strength where medicine has failed."

The priest's eyes softened with sympathy that looked both genuine and bone-deep weary. "Many are making such journeys. Faith is all we have when medicine fails and death surrounds us like wolves circling a campfire." He made the sign of the cross with automatic precision. "I'm Father Andreas. Come—I can offer you shelter in the church tonight and a meal if our stores permit. Though I confess I'm no longer certain what God provides versus what we simply manage to scrape together through desperate effort while God watches silently."

The bitter theology in that last sentence spoke to deep crisis of faith. Thomas recognized the tone—he'd felt it himself.

They followed Father Andreas into the church—small stone building that probably predated the village itself. Plain wooden benches instead of ornate pews. Simple altar with a painted wooden crucifix showing more wear than artistry. The smell of incense couldn't quite cover the underlying odor of death.

"How bad was it here?" Margarethe asked gently.

Father Andreas's face crumpled slightly before he controlled himself with visible effort. "Bad. The plague came five weeks ago. Started with the blacksmith's family—they'd hosted travelers from Mainz who seemed healthy but apparently carried pestilence invisibly. Within three days, the blacksmith's wife showed symptoms. Within a week, a quarter of the village was sick. Within three weeks, two-thirds were dead or dying or fled in panic."

He slumped against the altar, suddenly looking unable to support his own weight. "We burned the bodies when we had wood and strength for cremation. Buried them in mass graves when we didn't. I performed last rites until my voice gave out from constant prayers. Held hands as people died screaming from the pain of buboes bursting. Blessed mass graves because we couldn't dig individual plots fast enough."

His voice dropped to barely a whisper. "And I still don't understand why God allowed this. Why He punished the faithful and the wicked equally. Why children who'd never sinned died in agony while some notorious sinners survived apparently untouched. It feels random. Arbitrary. Like God threw dice with human lives and didn't care about the results."

Thomas had no answer to offer. Theology provided various explanations, but none of them felt adequate when confronted with the reality of mass death.

"We carry what faith we can," he said finally, which was both inadequate and honest.

Father Andreas nodded wearily. "Have there been strangers through here recently?" Margarethe asked, shifting to practical concerns. "We heard rumors that Gray Robes were active in this region."

Father Andreas's expression darkened with anger that cut through his exhaustion. "Aye. Three days ago. Four Gray Robes on horseback, moving with purpose. They were asking about travelers. Specifically asking about a monk traveling with a woman and possibly others. Said they were hunting heretics who'd stolen from a bishop."

Thomas felt cold wash through him despite the church's relative warmth. The Order was hunting them. Had their descriptions. Was questioning every village along the eastern roads. And they were only three days behind.

"Did you tell them anything?" he asked, trying to keep his voice casually curious.

Father Andreas met Thomas's eyes with surprising directness, intelligence showing through his grief-exhaustion. "I told them we'd seen many travelers but no one matching that specific description. I explained that the plague has driven hundreds of pilgrims through this region, that distinguishing one monk and woman from another would be impossible."

He stood straighter, anger giving him temporary strength. "I don't trust the Order anymore. They've become cruel during these plague times. They see heresy everywhere, use the chaos as excuse to persecute anyone who doesn't conform. Last month they burned an entire family in the next village for what they called 'heresy.' The family's only crime was speaking German instead of Latin during prayers because they couldn't afford Latin schooling. Five people burned alive for linguistic poverty disguised as religious error."

Father Andreas's voice filled with rare certainty. "The Order doesn't represent God's mercy. They represent fear and control and the particular cruelty that comes from men who are convinced they're doing righteousness while committing atrocity. So even if I had seen a monk traveling with a woman, I wouldn't have told them. Whoever they're hunting probably deserves protection more than persecution."

Thomas's shoulders dropped, the tension draining from muscles he hadn't realized were locked tight. "Thank you, Father Andreas. That… that means more than you know."

"Don't thank me yet. The Order is systematic and patient. They'll question every village along every eastern road. Eventually they'll find someone who saw you clearly. Three days behind becomes two days becomes one day becomes…" He didn't need to finish the sentence.

Margarethe asked the crucial question: "How far to Prague from here? And what route would you recommend for travelers who need to avoid… official attention?"

Father Andreas pulled out a rough map sketched on parchment. "Three weeks on foot if you take the main roads and don't encounter serious problems. Four to five weeks if you avoid main roads and move carefully through forests and back paths."

He traced routes with his finger. "The main eastern road goes through Regensburg—large city, well-defended, plenty of resources. But also well-watched. If the Order is looking for you specifically, they'll have men there checking pilgrims and refugees."

"Alternative routes?" Margarethe asked.

"North route goes through smaller villages but crosses territories controlled by nobility who actively support the Order. Dangerous if you're trying to avoid official notice."

Father Andreas traced a different path. "South route is longer—adds perhaps a week to your journey—but passes through lands where local lords are too busy dealing with plague and peasant revolts to care much about religious fugitives. Forests along the Danube, then north through Bohemia toward Prague. Harder travel, more time, less comfortable conditions. But also less likely to encounter organized pursuit."

"That's the route we'll take," Margarethe decided immediately. "Thank you, Father. This information is invaluable."

"Then stay tonight. Rest. Eat what little we can provide. Leave before dawn while it's still dark, before anyone in the village can get a clear enough look at you to provide detailed descriptions if the Order returns." Father Andreas pressed a small bundle into Margarethe's hands—bread, cheese, dried meat wrapped in cloth. "Not much, but enough to get you to the next village. And I'll pray for your safety, though I'm no longer certain my prayers reach anyone who cares to listen."

That night they slept in the church, taking turns on watch even though Father Andreas had offered that they were safe. Thomas couldn't fully relax despite exhaustion, kept expecting Gray Robes to burst through the doors. But the night passed quietly except for the ambient sounds of a plague-ravaged village—occasional coughs from the sick, crying from those who'd lost family.

In the morning, before dawn, Father Andreas provided them with the promised supplies and one more piece of advice:

"One more thing," he said as they prepared to leave, his voice dropping to a whisper despite no one else being present. "There are rumors. Probably nothing, conspiracy thinking born from desperation to explain the unexplainable. But people are saying the plague isn't entirely natural. That it was sent deliberately or at least allowed to spread by those who benefit when kingdoms fall. That there are organizations—secret societies older than crowns—who set such calamities loose for their own purposes."

He met Thomas's eyes with uncomfortable penetration. "I dismissed it as the kind of fantasy people create when reality is too horrible to accept without explanation. But you—you're running from something. The Order hunts you specifically. You carry yourselves like people who know dangerous secrets. And that girl looks at the world like someone much older than fourteen, like she sees patterns others miss."

Father Andreas's voice grew even quieter. "Is there truth to the rumors? Is the plague more than just God's judgment or natural disaster?"

Thomas thought about lying, about protecting Father Andreas from knowledge that might get him killed. But this priest had helped them, had lied to the Order to protect them, deserved some measure of truth.

"I don't know if this specific plague was deliberately set loose," he said carefully. "But I know there are organizations that understand patterns of how civilizations collapse. That have knowledge of how to guide—or steer—such falls to serve their purposes. Whether they caused this plague or simply allowed it to happen or are just taking advantage of it… I'm still learning. Still gathering pieces of a puzzle that's been assembled over centuries."

Father Andreas nodded slowly, resignation rather than surprise on his exhausted face. "Then may God guide your quest. And may whatever you learn help prevent future catastrophes rather than enabling them. The world has enough suffering without human hands deliberately adding more."

They left Sankt Peter as dawn broke, following the southern route Father Andreas had recommended. Through forests slowly shedding their last autumn leaves. Along the Danube's northern banks where the river ran cold and gray under November skies.

And behind them, three days back but closing the distance with systematic persistence, Gray Robes followed their trail.

The quest had truly begun. The choice had been made. The path forward was set.

Now they just had to survive long enough to complete it.

## Scene 3: Travel and Training - Maria's Education

### Danube Forest Route, One Week Later

The rhythm of travel settled into Thomas's bones over the following week—wake before dawn when frost still painted the ground silver, walk until midday when his legs began to scream, rest during the warmest part of afternoon, walk again until dusk, make camp in hidden locations away from roads.

Thomas used the walking hours to teach Maria about the cipher system, showing her how the Iron and Silver Keys worked together to reveal patterns invisible to normal reading. They practiced on manuscripts they'd taken from Johannes's workshop before it burned.

"The cipher marks look like decorative flourishes," Thomas explained one afternoon, holding a manuscript open while they walked slowly behind Margarethe. "Tiny dots and geometric patterns that scribes added to margins. Most people think they're just embellishment. But when you apply the keys at specific positions and angles…"

He demonstrated, placing the Iron Key's eye symbol against the text with precise alignment. Dots appeared through the aperture, seemingly random until he added the Silver Key, positioning its hand symbol to overlap slightly with the eye.

The dots connected into lines. The lines formed patterns. The patterns revealed hidden locations and connections.

"It's beautiful," Maria breathed, her fourteen-year-old wonder showing through. "Like a secret language written in plain sight. Everyone can see the manuscript, but only those with the keys can read the true message."

"Exactly. The network created this system during Rome's collapse, when barbarian invasions were burning libraries and Christian zealots were destroying 'pagan' knowledge. They needed a way to preserve information that couldn't be easily destroyed. So they hid it inside texts that seemed harmless—agricultural treatises, medical texts, religious manuscripts. Things that would be preserved rather than burned."

Over the following days, they practiced constantly. Maria learning to see where cipher marks were hidden, to sense patterns before Thomas could point them out, to intuitively understand connections that took him minutes of careful analysis.

Gradually, Thomas realized Maria was better at this than he was—better even than Wilhelm had been.

"You're a natural," he said one evening, watching her decode a particularly complex passage in under thirty seconds that had taken him nearly ten minutes.

"I was bred to be good at this," Maria corrected, her voice carrying philosophical weight. "Forty generations of my ancestors were bred specifically to carry and interpret this knowledge. My talent isn't chance—it's bred."

"Is there a difference?" Margarethe asked from where she sat maintaining her weapons. "You're good at cipher reading whether that skill came from random inheritance or deliberate breeding. The source of ability matters less than what you choose to do with it."

"Does it?" Maria's eyes were troubled, too old for her face. "If every choice I make is something I was bred to make—am I really making free choices? Or am I just a tool following its design?"

Thomas thought about that question deeply, because it was his question too. He'd been bred for patterns, for seeing connections others missed. Was his decision to pursue all seven keys really his choice? Or was he simply doing what forty-one generations of breeding had set him up to do?

"I think," he said slowly, "that you can be both. Bred for purpose and still making free choices. The network gave you tools—unusual gifts, blood memory, patterns most people don't see. But how you use those tools, what purposes you apply them to, whether you serve the network's goals or actively oppose them—that's your choice. That's what separates humans from mere tools."

"Your mother chose resistance," Margarethe added quietly. "The network wanted her to bear more carriers after you were born. She refused. Said no to powerful people who don't accept refusal. It cost her life, but it was her choice, not theirs."

Maria absorbed that in silence.

That night, they camped in a forest hollow sheltered from wind. Maria sat by the fire and deliberately accessed deeper blood memory than she'd previously attempted—reaching back through twenty, thirty, forty generations.

Thomas watched with fascination tinged with fear as her eyes went distant, unfocused, seeing things that existed only in inherited memory. She began speaking in languages he didn't recognize—not Latin or Greek or Arabic, but something older. Pre-Roman perhaps.

"What's she accessing?" Margarethe whispered, hand on knife hilt.

"The network's earliest days," Thomas said. "When the Protocol was first created. The founding purpose before centuries of corruption."

Maria spoke for nearly an hour, her young voice carrying tones and cadences from other people, other times. Thomas couldn't understand the words, but he could hear the emotional weight—urgency, hope, determination, fear all woven together.

Finally her eyes focused again, returning to the present. She looked exhausted, shaken, transformed.

"I saw them," she whispered hoarsely. "The founders. Roman scholars and Greek philosophers and Christian monks and Jewish teachers all working together as their civilization collapsed around them. They weren't trying to make calamity or decide who survived. They were trying to prevent future calamities by understanding the pattern of why civilizations fail."

"What pattern?" Thomas asked urgently.

"Civilizations grow," Maria said, her voice carrying the weight of forty generations. "They become powerful and tangled—and that very tangling makes them brittle, like a great house built too high on narrow beams. And every few centuries, the hard times come: failed harvests, sickness, war, trade and coin breaking. Sometimes more than one at once. And the proud structure shatters. Dark ages follow. Knowledge is lost. The survivors rebuild slowly, and eventually the cycle repeats."

She met Thomas's eyes with disturbing intensity. "The Protocol was meant to show humanity how to recognize when they're nearing that brittle peak. How to build strength instead of chasing ease. How to preserve essential knowledge so rebuilding happens faster with fewer mistakes repeated. It was defensive. Protective. Meant to help all humanity survive and learn from collapse, not to make ruin to benefit chosen bloodlines."

"But the network corrupted it," Margarethe said.

"Over centuries," Maria confirmed. "The founders died. Their disciples maintained the original purpose, but their children inherited the system without complete context. Gaps in knowledge got filled with assumptions. 'Recognize the pattern to prevent it' became 'recognize the pattern to control it.' Small corruptions each generation, gradually accumulating into something monstrous."

Thomas felt the weight of that understanding. They weren't just fighting a conspiracy—they were fighting centuries of slow corruption, of good intentions twisted imperceptibly.

"The complete Protocol will show that," he said. "All seven fragments together will demonstrate both original purpose and the corruption. That's our blade—showing the network they've betrayed everything they were founded to protect."

"If we survive to gather all seven fragments," Maria said. "That's still a very large 'if.'"

"Then we better keep working through the 'ifs,'" Margarethe said practically. "Two days to Prague. Then we access the fourth key and continue this impossible quest."

They slept in shifts that night, maintained watch, and continued east the next morning.

The quest had become clearer. The stakes had become higher.

They weren't just running anymore. They were actively seeking to understand and expose corruption that had accumulated across centuries.

It probably wouldn't work. They'd probably die before completing the quest.

But at least they'd be dying for something that mattered.

## Scene 4: The Encounter - Network Agent

### Bohemian Forest, Three Days From Prague

Marcus the Physician found them on a gray afternoon when frost painted the bare trees silver and the cold bit through even Margarethe's practical traveling clothes.

Thomas was gathering firewood, his hands numb despite gloves, when he heard footsteps behind him—too quiet to be Margarethe's purposeful stride, too careful to be Maria's youthful carelessness.

He spun, hand reaching for the knife he'd started carrying, his heart hammering with immediate terror.

A man stood ten feet away. Middle-aged, scholar's robes of good quality but not ostentatious wealth, intelligent eyes studying Thomas with analytical intensity. Hands raised, showing he carried no visible weapon.

"Thomas of Eltville," the man said calmly. "Wilhelm's grandson. Carrier of the Iron Key. I've been looking for you."

Thomas kept his knife raised despite trembling hands. "Who are you and how did you find us?"

"Someone who knew your grandfather. Worked with him. Shared his belief that the network has strayed catastrophically from its original purpose." The man took one slow step forward, movements deliberately non-threatening. "My name is Marcus the Physician. I serve the network—or served it, before I understood fully what it had become."

"Stay back," Thomas warned, hearing Margarethe approaching silently through the forest behind him.

"I'm not here to capture you or return you to network control," Marcus said patiently. "I'm here to help. To give you information that might save your life. And to ask for your help in return."

Margarethe appeared with crossbow raised and aimed at Marcus's chest. "Speak quickly and convincingly."

Marcus smiled slightly. "I believe you. You're Heinrich's daughter. The living library trained by your father before the Order tortured him to death in what everyone thought was standard heretic prosecution but was actually a network-sanctioned purge."

Margarethe's expression didn't change, but Thomas saw her finger tighten slightly on the crossbow trigger. "You know things you shouldn't."

"I know many things the network wishes I didn't. Including how to find you—reports from sympathetic men in villages you've passed through, watchers who track bloodlines, and the pattern that says you're heading to Prague for the fourth key."

"So the network knows where we're going," Thomas said, feeling dread settle into his stomach.

"The network suspects. But they haven't yet set the full hunt on you—you're still something they want to reclaim quietly. That will change when you reach Prague. When you attempt to access the fourth key. Then they'll intervene directly."

Marcus lowered his hands slowly. "Which is why I'm here. To warn you. And to give you information Wilhelm died before he could pass to you."

He pulled out a scroll sealed with wax bearing cipher marks worked into decorative patterns. "This is everything Wilhelm discovered about the sixth key—the Heart that Feels. Its location, access methods, countersigns. He spent twenty years researching it, died before he could retrieve it himself. I memorized his findings before his journals were destroyed."

Thomas felt suspicious hope warring with trained paranoia. "Why help us?"

"Hope," Marcus said simply. "Hope that someone will finally understand the complete Protocol. Will see both what it originally said and what the network corrupted it into. Will expose that corruption publicly enough that network members who still believe in the original purpose will rebel."

He held out the scroll. "I'm old, Thomas. Tired. I've spent my life serving something I thought was noble, only to slowly realize it had become evil without me noticing exactly when the transformation happened. I don't have courage to rebel openly—they'd kill me and my family. But I can help you. Give you tools to complete what I'm too frightened to attempt myself."

Thomas took the scroll cautiously, examined the seal, opened it carefully. Read cipher marks and location details written in handwriting he recognized from Wilhelm's manuscripts.

"This looks genuine."

"It is. Wilhelm trusted me with his notes. I'm trying to honor that trust by helping his grandson complete what he started." Marcus stood, prepared to leave. "But I need to warn you—the network is watching Prague. They'll be watching for anyone attempting to access the scholar's library. Be careful. Be clever. Get the key and leave before they can move to stop you."

"Why are you helping if it puts you at risk?" Margarethe asked, crossbow still aimed but finger relaxing slightly.

"Because doing nothing puts me more at risk," Marcus said quietly. "Risk to my conscience. Risk of dying knowing I served evil when I could have chosen differently. This is my small refusal. Perhaps it matters. Perhaps it doesn't. But at least I tried."

He turned to leave, then paused. "One more thing. The seventh key—the Mind that Knows—is in Constantinople with the fifth key. Both hidden in the Great Library's surviving sections. If you can reach Constantinople, you can potentially get two keys in one journey."

Then Marcus walked into the forest and disappeared between frost-silvered trees.

Thomas stood holding the scroll, uncertain whether to trust it.

"Trap?" Margarethe asked.

"Perhaps. Or genuine help from someone who still remembers what the network was supposed to be." Thomas secured the scroll carefully in his pack. "Either way, we were already going to Prague. This just gives us information about what comes after. And the warning about network eyes is probably accurate regardless of Marcus's true intentions."

Maria emerged from camp, having heard most of the conversation. "He was telling the truth. I could sense it—blood memory teaches you how to read people, how to hear the lie in a voice. He genuinely believes he's helping, genuinely regrets what the network has become."

"Then we stay careful," Thomas decided. "Three days to Prague. We access the fourth key, learn what it reveals, and decide next steps. Constantinople for two more keys. England for the sixth. We keep moving forward because stopping means capture or death."

They broke camp and continued east with even greater caution.

The quest was accelerating. The network was stirring. Time was running out.

## Scene 5: Prague - The Jewish Quarter

### Prague, The Jewish Quarter, Three Days Later

Prague was magnificent and terrifying in equal measure—sprawling across both banks of the Vltava River under gray November sky, church spires and castle towers rising like stone prayers.

The Jewish quarter occupied a walled area near the river, physically and culturally separated from the Christian majority. Guards at the gates examined their forged pilgrim papers with suspicious thoroughness that plague times had intensified.

After tense questioning and small bribes, they entered the Jewish quarter just as the afternoon sun angled toward evening.

The quarter felt like a different world—buildings pressed close together, streets narrow and winding, languages mixing in ways Thomas had never heard. The residents watched them with wariness.

Maria guided them through the maze of streets with blood memory's confidence, leading them to a modest building marked with a nearly invisible symbol worked into the stonework—a hand holding seven keys.

She knocked in a specific pattern. Three quick, two slow, three quick.

The door opened, revealing an old man with a long white beard and penetrating dark eyes that seemed to see through their pilgrim disguise immediately.

"The three from the west," he said in German. "Monk, warrior woman, and blood memory carrier. We've been expecting you. I'm Rabbi Ezra. Come inside quickly. The network is watching."

He led them down hidden stairs to a library that took Thomas's breath away—thousands of manuscripts carefully preserved, knowledge from across the known world.

"The golem manuscript," Rabbi Ezra said, pulling out an ancient text with reverence. "Kabbalistic teachings on creating life from clay. But also a container for the fourth fragment of the Genesis Protocol. Your keys will reveal what's hidden within the mystical text."

Thomas and Maria worked together, placing both metal keys on the manuscript in precise alignment. Maria touched the page, activating blood memory.

She gasped.

"I can see it," she whispered. "The fourth fragment. Temporal patterns—not just that civilizations collapse in recurring waves, but why. The certainty of it. How kingdoms grow mighty, then brittle. How chasing ease breeds weakness. How ties between cities can spread ruin faster—and how those same ties can spread recovery, if knowledge is preserved correctly."

She traced invisible patterns. "And I see the corruption point. Eleventh century. A network leader named Benedict arguing that since collapse was inevitable, they should control when it happened rather than letting it occur randomly. The moment preservation became control. The betrayal documented in the Protocol itself."

They spent an hour copying information, Maria committing crucial details to blood memory for preservation even if physical notes were lost.

Finally, Rabbi Ezra provided them with letters of introduction to network sympathizers along routes to Constantinople.

"Thank you," Thomas said. "This is dangerous for you."

"My family has protected this library for nineteen generations," Rabbi Ezra said. "Waiting for someone who would use the knowledge correctly rather than turn it into a weapon for control. You're trying to understand, not control. That makes you worth helping, even at risk."

They left the Jewish quarter as dusk fell, carrying the fourth fragment's knowledge.

Three keys down. Four to go.

Constantinople next, for the fifth and possibly seventh keys.

Then England for the sixth.

Then synthesis—if they survived.

The quest continued. The network roused itself. The Order pursued.

And three fugitives carrying pieces of a thousand-year puzzle kept moving forward because stopping meant failure and failure meant the network's corruption would continue unchallenged.

Thomas felt the weight of both metal keys in his pack, felt Maria's living memory walking beside him, felt the scroll from Marcus containing Wilhelm's notes.

They probably wouldn't survive to Constantinople. Probably wouldn't gather all seven keys.

But they would try.

And trying—choosing active quest over passive surrender—was the most human choice people bred for a purpose could make.

# CHAPTER 7 - THE THIRD KEY REVEALED (Medieval Timeline - 1347)

## Scene 1: The Journey Begins - Toward Constantinople

### Four Weeks East of Prague, Late December 1347

The cold had teeth.

Not the clean cold of autumn frost or even the sharp bite of first winter. This was December cold in the Carpathian foothills, the kind that found every gap in clothing and settled into bones like an unwelcome guest. Thomas's fingers had gone numb inside his wool-wrapped gloves two hours ago. His feet existed somewhere at the ends of his legs, theoretical rather than felt. Each breath came out in white clouds, each inhalation sharp enough to ache.

Margarethe walked ahead with steady endurance, her breath steaming in the pre-dawn darkness. She rolled her left shoulder occasionally—the old wound from Strasbourg still stiff in December cold, though she never complained. Maria followed between them, fourteen years old and carrying forty generations of blood memory like a pack she couldn't set down.

Three weeks since leaving Prague with the fourth key's knowledge. Three weeks of avoiding main roads where the Order might be watching. Three weeks of sleeping rough in forests and abandoned barns and occasionally—when they had coin and the village seemed safe—paying for a corner of someone's floor near their fire.

Winter was making everything harder.

"How much farther?" Maria asked, her young voice tight with cold and exhaustion.

"Constantinople is still eight weeks away if we maintain current pace," Margarethe said without turning. "Twelve weeks if we're realistic about winter conditions. Four months if we're honest about how plague has disrupted everything."

Thomas thought about four more months of this cold, this fear, this constant movement with the Order somewhere behind them like wolves following wounded prey. But he also thought about what they'd learned in Prague. About the fourth fragment showing temporal patterns of collapse. About the network's corruption from defensive purpose to offensive plotting.

"Tell me again what Maria saw in Prague," he said, partly to distract from the cold, partly because repetition helped his mind settle things into place. "What exactly did the fragment reveal?"

Margarethe slowed her pace slightly, letting Thomas come alongside. "Temporal patterns. The centuries-long rhythm of kingdoms rising and falling. Bronze Age collapse around 1200 BCE. Roman collapse around 400 CE. And the current collapse showing the same warning signs."

"But also the corruption point," Maria added, her breath coming in visible puffs. "Eleventh century. A network leader named Benedict arguing that since collapse seemed inevitable from the pattern, the network should control when it happened rather than letting it occur randomly."

Thomas felt anger kindle in his chest despite the cold. "So they went from trying to prevent collapse to steering it. From helping humanity survive to selecting who deserves survival."

"Over generations," Maria said. "Not a sudden shift from good to evil, but slow corruption across centuries. Each generation making small changes that seemed reasonable at the time but accumulated into something monstrous."

The path they followed was barely a track—goat trail probably, winding through hills covered in bare trees and early snow. Somewhere to their left, Thomas could hear water running, a stream not yet frozen. To the right, forest pressed close, dark pines and skeletal deciduous trees creating shadows.

"Why Constantinople?" Thomas asked. "Why hide two keys there instead of spreading them further apart?"

"Because the Great Library survived," Maria said, accessing blood memory with the distant-eyed expression Thomas had learned to recognize. "Everyone thinks it burned during the Fourth Crusade in 1204. The Latin crusaders did destroy the visible sections. But network members had been preparing for that possibility for decades. They created hidden chambers beneath the destroyed sections. Preserved the most valuable materials underground where fire and conquest couldn't reach."

"And the fifth and seventh keys are both there?" Margarethe asked.

"The fifth key—the Foot that Walks, showing movement patterns and how populations migrate during collapse—is definitely there. My blood memory is clear about that location." Maria frowned, sorting through inherited memory that came with gaps and uncertainties. "The seventh key—the Mind that Knows, which enables complete synthesis of all fragments—is probably there. At least, several bloodlines believed it was hidden in Constantinople. But the seventh key's location was more carefully obscured than the others. Multiple conflicting memories. Deliberate misdirection to protect the most important piece."

Thomas thought about gathering six of seven keys, about having most of the Genesis Protocol but not quite complete understanding. Would six fragments be enough to expose the network?

The sky was lightening now, darkness giving way to that particular gray that came before winter sunrise. Thomas could see the landscape more clearly—rolling hills covered in snow and ice, scattered villages sending thin smoke columns into cold air.

"We need to discuss what happens when we have all seven keys," Margarethe said practically. "Assuming we survive to Constantinople and then to England for the sixth key. What do we DO with complete understanding of the Genesis Protocol?"

"Expose it," Thomas said immediately. "Show the network's corruption. Prove they betrayed their founding purpose."

"To whom?" Margarethe challenged. "The Church? The Church is bought—we know Order men serve the network while pretending to hunt heretics. Secular authorities? Kings and nobles who benefit from the network's arrangements? The common people who can't read and wouldn't understand a bloodline plot this twisted?"

Thomas felt frustration war with determination. She was right—they were gathering evidence of a conspiracy so old and so careful that simply shouting it wouldn't stop it.

"Then what?" he asked. "We gather all seven keys, understand the complete Protocol, and do nothing because exposure is too difficult?"

"I'm not saying do nothing," Margarethe said. "I'm saying we need a plan beyond 'gather knowledge and hope truth naturally defeats evil.' The network has been operating for nine hundred years. They've survived times when realms broke apart, dozens of attempts to expose them."

Maria spoke up quietly: "My blood memory includes failed exposures. People who discovered pieces of the network's work and tried to reveal them publicly. Sometimes they were killed. Sometimes they were discredited. Sometimes their revelations were turned—the network admitted to a sliver of truth while hiding deeper corruption."

"So we need something more than just knowing the truth," Thomas said. "We need a way to turn the knowledge against them."

"Or a way to make suppression impossible," Margarethe suggested. "Distribute the knowledge so widely, through so many different channels, that killing us or discrediting us doesn't matter because the information continues spreading independently."

Thomas thought about manuscripts. About the network's own cipher system designed to preserve knowledge through catastrophe. About how information could survive if properly encoded and distributed.

"We use their own methods against them," he said, the idea forming as he spoke. "The network created the cipher system to preserve knowledge across generations and disasters. We use the same system to preserve evidence of their corruption. Hide it in manuscripts, in locations across Europe, encoded so that even if the network destroys most copies, enough will survive for future generations to discover."

"Long-term thinking," Margarethe said. "Planting seeds for people centuries from now. What about immediate action?"

"Both," Thomas decided. "We gather all seven keys. We understand the complete Genesis Protocol. We document the network's corruption as thoroughly as possible. Then we do two things simultaneously: Expose it to current authorities—Church, nobility, anyone who might act—and preserve the evidence for future discovery using the network's own preservation methods."

They walked in silence for a while, each feeling the weight of what they were proposing. Fighting a nine-hundred-year conspiracy with scattered evidence and desperate hope.

The sun broke over the eastern horizon, pale winter light painting the snow-covered landscape in shades of white and gray and gold. Beautiful, if you could ignore the cold trying to kill you and the knowledge that somewhere behind them, the Order was still hunting.

"Constantinople is defended by both Byzantine authorities and network members," Maria said, accessing more inherited information. "Getting into the city will be relatively easy. But accessing the hidden library chambers beneath the burned sections will be much harder. The network guards those locations carefully."

"Do you have names?" Thomas asked. "Names of network members in Constantinople who might help?"

"I have countersigns and locations where contacts should be. Whether those people are still alive after plague and unrest…" Maria shrugged. "Unknown. The blood memory is decades old—passed from my grandmother to my mother to me. Constantinople might have changed significantly."

"Then we adapt," Margarethe said. "We've adapted this far. Survived Order pursuit, stolen from bishops, accessed restricted libraries. We can handle Byzantine complications."

Something in Thomas steadied—not quite confidence, but purpose. Direction. The sense that they weren't just running blindly but moving toward something meaningful.

They were three people against a nine-hundred-year conspiracy. Three fugitives carrying stolen keys and forbidden knowledge. Three tools bred by others choosing to rebel anyway.

The odds were terrible. The likelihood of success was minimal.

So they walked on.

Because stopping meant being taken.

## Scene 2: Maria's Revelation - The Deeper Memory

### Four Weeks East of Prague, Deep Winter

The sickness took Maria on a gray afternoon when they were three days from a village that Marcus's scroll indicated should have network contacts willing to help.

She'd been coughing for two days—they all had, the harsh dry cough that came from breathing cold air and sleeping in insufficient shelter. But Maria's cough developed a wet quality that made Thomas's medical instincts scream warnings. Then she started running a fever despite the cold.

By evening, she was barely walking. By the time they made camp in a half-collapsed barn, she was shaking with fever-chills and coughing up blood.

"Plague?" Margarethe asked quietly while Maria slept fitfully.

"Perhaps," Thomas said, pressing a hand to Maria's burning forehead. "Or pneumonia from exposure and exhaustion. Or some other illness that's killing her just as effectively."

He'd been trained in monastery medicine—the basic herbalism and prayer-based healing that medieval monks practiced. Enough to know that fever this high in winter conditions usually meant death. Enough to know that coughing blood was a terrible sign.

"What can we do?" Margarethe asked, and Thomas heard the grief already in her voice.

"Keep her warm," Thomas said helplessly. "Give her water when she's conscious enough to drink. Pray, if you believe it helps. And prepare for the likelihood that she dies in the next day or two."

But Maria didn't die that night. Or the next day. She burned with fever, coughed blood, sometimes became delirious and spoke in languages Thomas didn't recognize. But she kept breathing through sheer stubborn will.

On the third night, the fever broke.

Thomas woke to find Maria sitting up, pale and weak but conscious and clear-eyed.

"I saw something," she said hoarsely, her voice barely above a whisper. "During the fever. Accessed blood memory deeper than I've ever gone before. Saw things from before the network was founded. Saw the original conspiracy's actual beginning."

"Save your strength," Thomas said, offering water that she drank gratefully.

"No," Maria insisted with surprising force. "I need to tell you now while the memory is fresh. Before my conscious mind starts editing what I saw."

Margarethe had woken at the commotion, moved closer. "What did you see?"

Maria took another sip of water, gathered herself. "The network wasn't founded during Rome's collapse around 400 CE the way everyone believes. That's the story they tell themselves, the comfortable founding myth. But my blood memory goes deeper—back to the actual beginning. And it's older. Much older."

"How much older?" Thomas asked, feeling cold that had nothing to do with December.

"Bronze Age collapse," Maria said. "Around 1200 BCE. A great breaking. Kingdoms across the Mediterranean fell almost together—Mycenaean Greece, the Hittite empire, Egypt weakened, cities burned. Not one war or one famine, but a chain: one power toppling another until the whole world of bronze cracked."

She coughed, winced at the pain, continued anyway. "The network was founded by survivors of that collapse. Scribes and priests and scholars who watched their world burn and decided the pattern needed to be understood and prevented from repeating. They created the first versions of what would become the Genesis Protocol—a reckoning of why kingdoms grow brittle when they grow great, how ruin leaps from city to city, what signs come before catastrophe."

Thomas felt his understanding shift like ground becoming unstable. "So the network is over two thousand years old? Not nine hundred years like we thought?"

"Older," Maria said. "And their original purpose was genuinely defensive—figure out why collapse happens so future generations can prevent it or at least survive with their eyes open. They tracked where knowledge was kept. They watched courts and trade, harvests and wars. Everything they could observe and write down to understand the pattern."

"When did it corrupt?" Margarethe asked.

"Gradually, across centuries," Maria said. "The Roman collapse was a test. The network had existed for nearly eight hundred years by then. They'd successfully preserved knowledge through multiple smaller catastrophes. They thought they understood the pattern well enough to prevent Roman collapse. But they failed."

"Rome fell anyway," Thomas said.

"Rome fell anyway," Maria confirmed. "And that failure broke something in them. Before, they believed the pattern could be turned aside with wise hands. After Rome, they began to believe the fall was as sure as numbers. That's when Benedict's arguments gained traction—six centuries later, but rooted in the despair Rome planted."

She took more water, continued with difficulty. "Benedict argued: If a fall will come—if two thousand years prove it—then we should steer it, not wait for it. Put chosen families in the right places at the right times. Use disaster like a lever to shape the world. And enough network members, scarred by Rome, agreed."

"How do you know this?" Thomas asked. "If this happened in the eleventh century, how does blood memory from your bloodline extend that far back?"

"Because my ancestors weren't just carriers," Maria said. "They were chroniclers. Each generation deliberately memorized the network's history and wrote it into blood for preservation. My family—going back forty generations—were the network's living archive. The library that cannot burn."

Her expression grew troubled. "But I also saw something else during the fever. Saw what happens when all seven keys are used together. When someone with full blood memory accesses the complete Genesis Protocol using all seven fragments simultaneously."

"What happens?" Margarethe asked quietly.

"The joining," Maria said. "Complete understanding not just of what the network has done but why the pattern exists. Why civilizations collapse again and again. And…" She hesitated. "And whether collapse actually is inevitable or whether the network's interventions have been causing the collapses they claim to be preventing."

Thomas felt ice settle into his stomach. "They're causing the collapses?"

"I don't know," Maria admitted. "The vision was fragmented. But I saw suggestions—implications in the deepest blood memory—that network meddling creates instability. That their attempts to shape bloodlines and 'prepare' for collapse actually make kingdoms more brittle. That the pattern they've been tracking for two thousand years is a pattern they themselves may have carved through their interference."

"So they've been making calamities for two millennia?" Margarethe asked.

"Perhaps. Or the collapses are genuinely inevitable and the network is just deluded about their ability to control events." Maria looked at them with eyes too ancient for her fourteen years. "That's why we need all seven keys. Why we need the complete Genesis Protocol. To understand whether we're fighting an evil conspiracy or a deluded one. Whether they're deliberately causing harm or accidentally causing harm while genuinely believing they're helping."

"Does the distinction matter?" Thomas asked.

"It might," Maria said. "If they're evil—consciously making calamities for their benefit—then exposure and rebellion might stop them. But if they've deceived themselves—truly believing they're helping while actually causing harm—then exposure might not work. They'll twist it. Tell themselves the proof is false. A man can swallow any truth when he's made his lies into holy duty."

They sat in silence in the cold barn.

"Constantinople," Thomas said finally. "We still need the fifth and seventh keys. Whether the network is evil or deluded, we need complete understanding to have any chance of stopping them. Maria, when you're strong enough to travel, we continue the quest."

"I'm strong enough now," Maria lied, though Thomas could see she was barely able to sit upright.

"You're strong enough in three days," Margarethe said firmly. "We rest here. Build up your strength. Three days won't make difference between escape and capture, but it might make difference between Maria surviving the journey or dying from pushing too hard too soon."

Maria looked like she wanted to argue, but another coughing fit stopped her. When it passed, she nodded reluctantly.

They spent three days in the half-collapsed barn. Thomas foraged for food and firewood. Margarethe maintained watch and repaired equipment. Maria slept and slowly recovered.

On the morning of the fourth day, they continued east.

Maria walked on her own power now, though clearly still weak. The fever had passed but left her thinner and more fragile-looking.

But she'd also accessed memories deeper than anyone had reached before. Had seen the network's true age and founding purpose. Had glimpsed what might happen when all seven keys came together.

The quest had become more urgent. More complex. More uncertain.

## Scene 3: Constantinople - Arrival and Contact

### Constantinople, February 1348

Constantinople.

After months in smaller cities and rural landscapes, the scale of it hit Thomas like a physical force.

The city stretched across hills overlooking the Bosphorus, still enormous despite centuries of upheaval and recent plague devastation. Massive walls that had protected the city for a thousand years. Church domes and palace towers rising against cold February sky. Markets that still functioned despite population reduction, still traded goods from across the known world.

The smell was different here—salt air from the straits cutting through the usual medieval stink. Spices from markets: cinnamon, clove, pepper brought overland from places Thomas had only read about in manuscripts. Fish rotting on docks. Tar from ship repairs. And underneath it all, the now-familiar sweetness of mass graves poorly covered.

And everywhere, visible evidence of the plague's passage.

Entire neighborhoods showed signs of abandonment. Buildings stood empty with doors hanging open. Mass graves marked with crosses occupied what had been public squares. The population that remained moved through streets with wariness.

"How do we find Helena?" Margarethe asked as they entered through the main gates, showing forged travel documents.

"Marcus said she runs a boarding house near the Augustaion," Thomas recalled. "The great square with old Roman statues. We find that landmark first."

Finding the Augustaion proved straightforward—the massive column and plaza were visible from multiple approach angles. Finding Helena's boarding house required another hour of wandering, asking merchants in Thomas's limited Greek until someone pointed them to a three-story building with a sign showing a ship and anchor.

The woman who answered their knock looked to be in her fifties, with silver-streaked dark hair and sharp eyes. She spoke Greek first, switched to Latin when Thomas responded, then finally settled on German.

"Pilgrims?" she asked skeptically.

"Seekers," Thomas said, using the countersign Marcus had provided. "Seven keys open seven doors but only truth opens minds."

Helena's expression shifted—not to welcome exactly, but to understanding mixed with resignation.

"Come inside quickly." Her voice dropped to barely above a whisper. "If you're using that phrase, you're either genuine network business or an elaborate Order trick. Either way, discussing it openly on the street is stupidity."

She led them into the boarding house—clean if sparse, showing signs of recent prosperity before plague reduced the traveler population. Up stairs to a private room on the third floor.

"Who sent you?" Helena asked once the door was secured.

"Marcus the Physician," Thomas said. "From the western side. He gave your name and the countersign."

"Marcus is still alive? I heard he'd died during plague." Helena's expression showed genuine pleasure. "That old fox. What does he want?"

"He wants us to access the hidden library chambers beneath the burned sections of the Great Library," Margarethe said bluntly. "We're seeking the fifth and seventh keys of the Genesis Protocol."

Helena's face went very still. "You're seeking the complete Protocol? Do you understand what you're asking? The network guards those fragments more carefully than gold. Trying for them without proper leave means death if you're caught. And I could be killed just for helping you attempt it."

"We understand," Maria said quietly. "But we have the first four keys already. We've seen what they reveal—the temporal patterns, the network's corruption from defensive purpose to offensive plotting. We need the complete Protocol to expose the full scope."

"You're network fugitives," Helena said, understanding dawning. "Not sanctioned seekers. You're rebels gathering evidence to challenge the conspiracy itself."

"Aye," Thomas confirmed. "And Marcus believed you might help because you knew the network's history before you joined. Because you believed the preserved founding purpose about helping all humanity rather than selecting survivors."

Helena was silent for a long moment. Finally she spoke:

"I joined the network forty years ago because my father was a scholar who believed in their stated mission—preserve knowledge through catastrophe, help civilizations recognize collapse patterns early enough to prevent them. He died still believing that was the network's actual purpose. I didn't learn about Benedict's corruption until I'd already served for twenty years. By then I had family, position, resources all tied to network membership. Leaving would have meant abandoning everything."

She met Thomas's eyes. "So I stayed. Told myself the corruption was exaggerated or that I could work for reform from inside. Typical rationalizations people use to justify serving powers they know are wrong. But I never stopped feeling guilty about my father dying while believing noble lies."

"Will you help us?" Margarethe asked directly.

"If I do, and you're caught, the network will know I assisted. Will trace the access back to me. Will kill me and possibly my family." Helena's voice was steady despite discussing her own potential death. "But if I don't, and you somehow succeed without my help, I'll die knowing I had a chance to honor my father's actual values and chose cowardice instead."

She stood, moved to a locked chest, pulled out a key. "I'll help. But you need to understand the reality. The underground chambers are guarded. The network doesn't trust a countersign alone—they verify bloodlines, demand seals and letters. You'll be walking into defended ground with stolen keys and no official leave."

"We've managed similar situations before," Margarethe said.

"In backwater cities with thin guards," Helena countered. "This is Constantinople—the network's eastern seat. You three—a German monk, a warrior woman, and a sick-looking girl—will look suspicious the moment you approach the underground entrance."

"Then we need disguises," Thomas said. "Sealed papers if possible."

Helena thought for a moment. "I can provide Byzantine network papers that might pass at first glance. Claim you're western scholars granted temporary leave. But the fifth and seventh keys are in the most restricted sections. Even with papers, you'll face more gates and guards."

"How long do we have once we access the chambers before the guards realize something is wrong?" Margarethe asked.

"Minutes," Helena said. "Perhaps fifteen or twenty if you're lucky. Less than five if they're on high alert. You'll need to find the keys, take whatever knowledge they hold, and get out before the response overwhelms you."

Maria spoke up: "I can access blood memory faster now. Since the fever, since reaching deeper than I ever have before, the process is quicker. If we can get me to the keys, I can read them in under ten minutes even for complex fragments."

"Then your plan is: Use papers to access the underground chambers, move quickly to the most restricted sections, locate the fifth and seventh keys, Maria reads them as fast as possible, you get out before the guards kill you." Helena's tone made it clear she thought this plan was barely better than suicide. "And if it works, you'll have six of seven keys and need to somehow survive fleeing Constantinople while the network rouses to hunt you."

"We've fled hostile cities before," Thomas said.

"Not like this," Helena warned. "Constantinople isn't Strasbourg or Prague. If they decide to bar the gates and comb the city street by street, they will find you. Your only chance is getting out immediately after accessing the chambers—within hours."

"Can you arrange ship passage?" Margarethe asked practically.

Helena considered. "Perhaps. I have contacts among merchants who owe me favors. But moving you out in haste will cost hard coin and spend favors I've saved for decades."

"We can pay," Thomas said, though their funds were nearly exhausted.

"It's not about money," Helena said. "It's about risk. Every person I contact creates more connections that network investigators can trace back to me if you're caught. I'm betting my life on you three fugitives somehow accomplishing what centuries of scattered opposition has failed to achieve."

"Others have bet on us before," Maria said quietly. "Marcus the Physician. Johannes the Alchemist. Brother Hamo. Wilhelm. Rabbi Ezra in Prague. All of them risked or sacrificed everything to help us. Not because our success is likely, but because it's possible. Because giving up means the network's corruption continues unchallenged for another thousand years."

Helena looked at the fourteen-year-old girl with ancient eyes.

"My father would have helped you," she said finally. "Would have risked everything to support people fighting for the network's original purpose. I won't dishonor his memory by being less brave than he would have been."

She pulled out more papers—seals, maps, names. "I'll arrange ship passage leaving two days from now. Merchant vessel heading to Italy, willing to take passengers who pay well and don't attract official attention. You have two days to access the underground chambers, read the keys, and get to the docks."

"Two days," Margarethe said, already planning.

"Or enough time to get discovered during reconnaissance and killed before you ever reach the keys," Helena countered. "But aye, two days is what you have. Use it wisely."

They spent the rest of the day reviewing maps and watch arrangements. Helena had detailed knowledge of the underground chambers' layout. She showed them where the fifth and seventh keys were likely hidden, what guard rotations looked like, what countersigns might help.

By evening, they had something resembling a plan.

Tomorrow they'd scout the underground entrance, verify Helena's information was current.

The day after, they'd either successfully access the fifth and seventh keys and make it to the waiting ship.

Or they'd die trying.

Constantinople had seemed like a distant goal when they started this quest months ago in Germany. Now they were here. Two days from potentially gathering six of seven keys.

Two days from either the greatest success of their quest or catastrophic failure that would end everything.

Thomas lay in the boarding house room that night, listening to Margarethe and Maria sleeping, thinking about all the people who'd helped them reach this point. Thinking about whether their sacrifices would be honored by success or wasted by failure.

Two more days.

Then Constantinople's underground chambers and the network's most carefully guarded secrets.

Then westward toward England and the sixth key.

Then—if they survived—understanding complete enough to challenge a conspiracy that had existed for two thousand years.

The quest was approaching its critical phase.

Success or death waited in Constantinople's hidden libraries.

And Thomas wasn't sure which outcome was more likely.

# CHAPTER 8 - THE UNDERGROUND LIBRARY (Medieval Timeline - 1348)

## Scene 1: The Infiltration - Entering the Hidden Chambers

### Constantinople, February 1348, Pre-Dawn

The underground entrance was exactly where Helena said it would be—a maintenance door in the rubble of the Great Library's burned sections, marked with a stone carving weathered to look like natural erosion unless you knew to recognize the cipher symbol for "knowledge preserved."

Thomas stood in pre-dawn darkness with Margarethe and Maria, all three carrying Byzantine papers Helena had forged. Their packs held the Iron and Silver Keys wrapped in oiled cloth. Around them, Constantinople slept in that particular quiet that came just before the city woke—the smell of baking bread already drifting from monastery kitchens, roosters beginning their calls, the distant creak of fishing boats leaving harbor.

"Remember," Thomas whispered. "We're western scholars with temporary leave. Helena's papers should get us past the first gate. But once we're in the restricted sections, guards will become suspicious quickly. Maria needs to read both keys in under ten minutes, then we leave."

"And if guards challenge us before we reach the keys?" Margarethe asked, her hand resting on the concealed knife she'd sharpened to razor edge.

"Talk first. Fight only if necessary." Thomas pulled out the countersign Helena had provided. "Our goal is knowledge, not combat."

Maria looked pale in the dim light, still weak from her illness weeks earlier. "I can read fast. Since the fever, the process is quicker. Get me to the keys and I'll extract everything in under ten minutes."

Thomas knocked on the weathered door in the specific pattern—three quick, two slow, one final.

Silence.

Then bolts sliding, locks disengaging. The door opened slowly, revealing a guard in Byzantine network livery—disciplined, alert, hand resting casually near his weapon.

"Purpose?" the guard asked in Greek.

"Study," Thomas responded, holding up their forged papers. "Western scholars, granted leave by the network. We seek knowledge preserved in the archives."

The guard examined their papers with careful thoroughness, comparing seal impressions to references, checking names against descriptions.

Thomas's heart hammered. Helena's forgeries were excellent—authentic network seals, forms copied precisely. But excellent wasn't perfect. Any small discrepancy could end this before they'd even entered.

"Western scholars," the guard said slowly. "We've had few visitors from Latin territories recently. Plague has thinned the roads—even for the network."

"Which is why our work is urgent," Margarethe said, her Greek carrying a German accent but remaining comprehensible. "What was preserved from earlier falls matters more now, as this one spreads."

The guard studied them for another long moment, clearly suspicious but finding no obvious fault.

"Proceed," he said at last, stepping aside. "But hear me—some shelves are closed without special leave. Do not set foot beyond doors marked with red cipher signs. Those places are warded, and the watch is quick to spill blood."

"Understood," Thomas said, moving past before he could reconsider.

They descended stairs cut from bedrock centuries ago, walls showing tool marks from ancient construction. Down into chambers that smelled of stone and preserved parchment and the particular cold that came from being deep underground.

The archives were larger than Thomas had imagined. Rooms extending in multiple directions, shelves holding thousands of manuscripts, copying stations where scribes worked to preserve deteriorating texts. Storage areas held clever contrivances—dry air, sealed cabinets, stone that stayed cold—that kept parchment from mold and rot for ages.

"These devices…" Maria whispered. "No one outside these walls would believe them."

"The network has been preserving knowledge for two thousand years," Thomas said quietly. "This is what defense looked like—before corruption transformed preservation into control."

They moved deeper, following Helena's map toward the restricted sections. Passed other scholars working quietly at manuscript tables. Passed guards stationed at regular intervals, watching everyone with hard-eyed vigilance.

The red cipher symbols appeared on doors deeper in—warning markers indicating restricted access.

"This is it," Margarethe said, consulting Helena's notes. "Fifth key in the chamber marked 'foot that walks.' Seventh key in 'mind that knows.' Both behind these doors."

"We need to slip past the guards," Thomas said, studying the doors and watchmen stationed nearby. "Distraction or countersigns?"

"We don't have time for slow," Maria said, accessing blood memory with that distant-eyed expression. "The network will already have noticed our papers being used. Within an hour, maybe less, they'll know we're fugitives with forged documents."

Margarethe made the decision with blunt practicality. "Distraction. I'll make noise in the eastern archives—knock over shelves, set off warning bells. When guards rush to stop that, you two access the restricted chambers. Regroup at the meeting-place Helena marked."

"That means splitting up," Thomas said. "If you're caught—"

"Then I'm caught and you continue without me." Margarethe was already moving. "The quest matters more than individual survival. Get the keys. Understand the Protocol. That's what matters."

She disappeared toward the eastern archives before Thomas could argue.

He grabbed Maria's hand, pulled her toward the restricted chamber marked with the "foot that walks" symbol. Used the Iron and Silver Keys to manipulate the door's lock mechanism—clever design that responded to specific key shapes and alignments.

The lock clicked open.

Behind them, the sound of crashing—Margarethe's distraction beginning. Shouts. Running footsteps. Warning bells ringing with harsh regularity.

Thomas and Maria slipped into the restricted chamber, found themselves in a smaller room lined with manuscript cases containing materials clearly older and more carefully preserved than anything in the general archives.

"The fifth key," Maria breathed, moving immediately toward a case marked with cipher symbols. "The Foot that Walks."

She placed her hands on the manuscript, activated blood memory with visible effort.

Her eyes went distant, unfocused, accessing inherited information from women who'd memorized this fragment across forty generations.

Thomas watched the door, listening for approaching guards.

Maria spoke in that strange dual-voice that came when blood memory dominated—partly her own consciousness, partly channeling ancestors:

"The fifth fragment shows movement. Migration patterns during Bronze Age collapse, Roman collapse, current medieval collapse. How people flee failing civilizations. Where they go. The network tracked these patterns deliberately—positioned themselves along migration routes to intercept refugees carrying skills and information worth preserving."

Her voice grew troubled. "But also how they guided the migrations. Didn't just observe—they influenced. Created lures for skilled people to flee toward network-held lands. Steered the movement of populations to serve network purposes rather than letting disaster patterns determine who survived where."

"They were steering the refugees?" Thomas asked, horrified.

"Not causing the disasters that made refugees," Maria clarified. "But absolutely shaping where refugees went, who survived the migrations, what knowledge was preserved versus lost. The defensive purpose was: Help people survive and preserve their knowledge. The corrupted use became: Decide which people survive where to serve the network's breeding designs."

Heavy footsteps approached outside. Multiple guards, moving with disciplined coordination.

"Can you access all of it?" Thomas asked urgently.

"Enough to document the corruption. The parts that prove intentional guidance rather than passive preservation—I have those now."

"Good. We need the seventh key."

They moved to the second restricted chamber, used the keys to bypass another sophisticated lock, found themselves in an even more protected room containing a single manuscript case.

The seventh key. The final joining.

Maria approached it like approaching something sacred or cursed—both reverence and fear visible in her expression.

"This is it," she whispered. "The Mind that Knows. The fragment that shows how all seven pieces connect, what they mean together."

She placed her hands on the manuscript, closed her eyes, activated the deepest blood memory Thomas had seen her access.

And gasped like she'd been struck.

"What?" Thomas demanded. "What does it show?"

"The truth," Maria said, her voice shaking. "The Genesis Protocol wasn't created to prevent collapses. It was created to understand them. To document why civilizations fail in cyclical patterns, to preserve that knowledge so future generations could learn from the pattern."

"But the network thought they could prevent collapses by understanding them."

"They tried," Maria confirmed, tears streaming down her face. "Bronze Age survivors who created the original Protocol—they thought if they just understood why collapse happened, they could help prevent future collapses by teaching civilizations to avoid the same mistakes. That was the genuine defensive purpose: Learn the pattern, teach people to recognize warning signs, help them turn aside before a hard break came."

"What went wrong?"

"They failed," Maria said bitterly. "Rome collapsed anyway despite the network's best efforts to steady it. All their study, all their knowledge preservation, all their attempts to see the fall coming and turn it aside—worthless. Rome fell the way the Bronze Age had fallen, following the same lines despite eight hundred years of network hands trying to break the cycle."

She accessed deeper memory, her expression showing horror. "That failure transformed them. Before Rome's collapse, they believed knowledge could save civilization. After Rome's collapse, they believed collapse was inevitable—that the pattern was too powerful to break through understanding alone. That's when Benedict's argument gained traction: If collapse is truly inevitable, we should control when and how it happens rather than letting it occur randomly."

"So they went from trying to prevent collapse to steering it."

"Gradually, across centuries. But aye—that's the core corruption. The seventh fragment shows it clearly: Original purpose was learning the pattern to break it. Current purpose is accepting the pattern as inevitable and learning to exploit it. The network's meddling doesn't create the pressure that breaks kingdoms, but it does shape the timing and outcome to benefit chosen bloodlines."

Maria pulled her hands away from the manuscript, opened her eyes with visible effort. "I have it now—six of the seven fragments. Everything except the sixth key. The Genesis Protocol's shape is clear, and the documentation of corruption is undeniable."

The restricted chamber door burst open. Six Byzantine guards with drawn weapons, disciplined and efficient, surrounding them before they could reach the secondary exit.

"Fugitives with forged papers," the lead guard said grimly. "In restricted rooms without leave. You're under arrest for conspiracy against the preservation order."

Thomas reached for his knife—six trained guards against one exhausted monk and a fourteen-year-old girl. Hopeless.

Then Margarethe materialized behind the guards, crossbow leveled at the lead guard's spine. Her left shoulder screamed protest—the old wound making the weapon shake—but her aim held steady enough to kill at this range.

"Lower your weapons or your commander dies," she said in Greek. "I've killed men for less. I'll do it again without hesitation."

The guards froze, assessing whether she was bluffing.

She wasn't. Thomas could see it in her eyes—the cold calculation of someone who'd survived through willingness to do violence others hesitated over.

"Maria has the knowledge," Margarethe continued. "Six of the seven fragments accessed through blood memory. Killing us doesn't erase what she's learned. Let us leave, and maybe your network survives the exposure. Force violence, and we'll make sure what we have reaches everyone who can use it against you."

"The network survived two thousand years," the lead guard said. "It will survive three fugitives revealing fragments."

"Will it survive complete documentation of how you corrupted your founding purpose?" Margarethe challenged. "Will it survive current members learning that Benedict's transformation occurred because the network failed to prevent Rome's collapse and decided controlling catastrophe was easier than preventing it?"

The guard hesitated, clearly weighing thoughts he'd never let himself think.

"We're leaving," Margarethe said firmly. "Through the service passages. You can report our escape. Can hunt us across Europe. But right now, you're letting us walk out because killing us accomplishes nothing except your commander bleeding out on these archive floors."

She gestured with the crossbow toward the secondary exit. "Move. Both of you. Now."

Thomas grabbed Maria's hand, pulled her toward the service passages Helena had described. Margarethe backed away from the guards, keeping her crossbow aimed.

They reached the tunnel entrance—narrow passage cutting through bedrock, barely wide enough for single file. Plunged into darkness that smelled of moisture and ancient stone.

Behind them, shouts as the guards recovered and organized pursuit.

They ran through darkness, hands trailing on wet stone walls, splashing through water that had leaked into passages from cisterns above.

Then light ahead—natural light from surface, filtering down through cistern grates.

They emerged in one of Constantinople's massive cisterns, vaulted chambers supported by hundreds of columns, water reflecting light in shimmering patterns.

"This way," Maria said, accessing blood memory that held Constantinople's hidden ways from ancestors who'd lived here generations ago. "Eastern cistern connects to passages leading to the Augustaion."

They waded through cistern water, following Maria's inherited knowledge.

Behind them, pursuit continued—guards entering the cisterns with torches.

But Constantinople's underground was vast. Tunnels and cisterns and ancient passages extending for miles. And the network guards didn't have blood memory showing them every route.

They climbed out through a surface grate in an alley near the Augustaion, emerged into late morning light after hours in underground darkness.

"Docks," Margarethe said, already moving. "Helena's ship leaves at noon. We have maybe thirty minutes."

They ran through Byzantine streets, three obvious foreigners moving with suspicious urgency.

The docks appeared ahead—forests of masts and sails, merchant vessels from across the Mediterranean.

Helena was waiting at the designated pier, standing next to a merchant galley flying Italian colors.

"You got them?" she asked urgently.

"Both keys accessed," Thomas confirmed. "Maria has six of the seven fragments—including the final joining and the documentation of corruption. Only the sixth key remains."

"Then get aboard before the Byzantine guards realize you're here," Helena said, gesturing toward the ship. "Captain has been paid to take you to Italy with no questions. You'll be in Venetian territory within a week."

"What about you?" Thomas asked. "The network will know you helped us."

"I'll disappear into Constantinople's crowds. I've prepared for this for years. False names, hidden coin, roads out." Helena's expression was determined. "Don't worry about me—worry about using what you've learned to actually expose the corruption."

Thomas grabbed her in a brief embrace. "Thank you. You risked everything."

"My father would have done the same. Now go."

They boarded the merchant galley, the captain immediately ordering ropes cast off. The ship began moving away from the dock.

On the pier, Helena stood watching them leave, already starting to blend back into the crowd.

In the distance, Byzantine guards appeared, searching the docks. But the merchant galley was already moving into the Bosphorus current, heading toward open water.

Constantinople receded behind them—massive walls and church domes and the Great Library's ruins containing knowledge preserved across two millennia.

Thomas stood at the ship's stern, watching the city disappear. They had six of seven keys accessed through Maria's blood memory. Understood most of the Genesis Protocol, including the final joining that revealed how preservation had curdled into control.

But they still needed the sixth key in England. Still needed to figure out how to expose it in ways that would actually challenge the network's power.

Maria joined him at the stern, exhausted but determined.

"I saw something else in the seventh fragment," she said quietly. "About the current collapse—this plague we're living through. The network didn't make the Black Death come, but they knew it was coming years in advance. Had predictions based on the collapse pattern. And they positioned themselves to benefit from it rather than prevent it."

"How long in advance?" Thomas asked.

"Thirty years," Maria said. "The network's leadership saw the signs in the 1310s—failed harvests, strange weather, kingdoms splintering. They knew major collapse was coming within a generation. And they feared plague in particular. So they prepared: positioning chosen bloodlines, securing knowledge, arranging for their people to survive what they knew would kill a third of Europe."

Thomas's hands gripped the ship's rail. "They let it happen. Knew it was coming and did nothing to prevent or minimize it."

"Worse," Maria said. "They helped it spread faster than it would have on its own. Guided trade so plague reached the greatest cities. Whispered against closing gates or keeping the sick apart. Not because they loosed the plague itself, but because they recognized it as the collapse they'd been waiting for and wanted it to do its worst."

"That's mass slaughter by choice."

"That's what the network has become," Maria confirmed. "They went from trying to prevent collapse, to accepting collapse as inevitable, to actively facilitating collapse whenever it appeared naturally because they'd positioned themselves to benefit from catastrophe they claimed was beyond their control."

The ship caught wind, sails filling, speed increasing as they moved from Bosphorus into the Sea of Marmara.

Margarethe joined them at the stern. For a moment, the three of them stood in silence, watching Constantinople disappear over the horizon.

Then Margarethe's hand—calloused from weapons training, scarred from old fights—brushed against Thomas's on the rail. Not quite touching, but close enough that he could feel the warmth of her skin.

He glanced at her. Found her looking not at him but at the receding city, her profile sharp against the gray sky. She'd risked everything in those underground chambers. Had faced down six guards alone with nothing but a crossbow and absolute conviction. Had gotten them out when capture seemed certain.

"Thank you," he said quietly. "For coming back for us. You could have escaped alone."

"We're in this together," Margarethe said, still not looking at him. "All three of us. To the end, whatever that looks like."

Her hand moved slightly closer to his on the rail. Thomas let his fingers shift to meet hers—just a brief contact, barely more than an accidental brush. But enough that his heart hammered in a way that had nothing to do with fear or exhaustion.

Then the moment passed. Margarethe pulled her hand back, turned to scan the horizon for pursuit with her usual vigilance.

But Thomas had seen something shift in her expression. Something that suggested she'd noticed the same thing he had—that somewhere between Strasbourg and Constantinople, between Johannes's death and Maria's fever, between a dozen near-death escapes and this desperate quest, they'd stopped being just allies bound by necessity.

They were becoming something more.

Something terrifying. Something worth protecting even more than the keys.

He just didn't know what to do about it yet.

## Scene 2: Network Encounter - The Sympathizer

### Milan, Late March 1348

The safehouse contact was supposed to meet them at the church of Sant'Ambrogio at vespers. Helena's documents listed him as "Giacomo the Scribe"—network member, manuscript preservation specialist, noted as "morally conflicted."

Thomas watched from the shadows as evening gathered around the ancient basilica. The church was nearly empty—plague had reduced attendance at all but essential services. The few worshippers present prayed with desperate intensity.

Margarethe stood guard at the opposite entrance. Maria waited with Thomas, accessing blood memory for any information about this contact.

A man appeared at the south entrance—middle-aged, scholar's robes, carrying the described leather satchel. He glanced around with practiced wariness.

Thomas approached using the countersign Helena had provided: "Knowledge preserved serves all humanity."

The man's response should have been: "When preservation becomes control, humanity is betrayed."

Instead, he said: "I know who you are. And I know the network is positioning to intercept you before you reach England."

Thomas's hand moved toward his knife. "You're not Giacomo."

"I am Giacomo," the man said quickly, holding up empty hands. "But I'm not here to trade countersigns. I'm here to warn you that the network knows you're using Helena's contact network. They've identified several of her sympathizers. Some have been arrested. Others are being watched, waiting to see if you'll contact them so they can capture you."

"Why warn us?" Margarethe asked, materializing from the shadows. "If you're laying a trap, this is elaborate."

"Because I've spent thirty years preserving manuscripts for the network while believing we were serving humanity," Giacomo said with quiet intensity. "Believing the justifications about collapse being inevitable, about bloodline breeding helping humanity survive. Then Helena's letter reached me two weeks ago."

He pulled a letter from his satchel—Helena's handwriting, the summary showing the network's transformation from defensive to offensive purpose.

"I read this," Giacomo continued, his voice shaking, "and realized I've been complicit in mass slaughter disguised as preservation. The breeding programs I helped arrange treated humans as livestock. The catastrophes I thought inevitable were being facilitated by the same organization claiming to protect humanity."

"So you're turning," Thomas said, studying Giacomo for signs of deception.

"I already turned. Burned my ledgers. Warned families I was supposed to watch. Sent letters to other network members I thought might be sympathetic." Giacomo met Thomas's eyes with desperate sincerity. "But turning doesn't undo thirty years of complicity. Doesn't save the families I helped manipulate."

"Turning disrupts the network's work," Maria said quietly. "Every person who chooses conscience over continued service makes their arrangements harder. That matters even if it doesn't undo past harm."

"Perhaps," Giacomo said. "But I didn't just come to confess. I came to warn you: The network knows you're heading to England for the sixth key. They know it's in York. They've set watchers at the monastery where the key is hidden. Walking into that situation without preparation means capture or death."

"Do you have alternative suggestions?" Thomas asked.

"Don't go to York directly. The network expects you to move through standard routes—ports, main roads, obvious monastery connections. Instead, approach from the north through Scotland. Use smaller ports, rural routes, contacts outside the normal network web. It will add weeks to your journey but increases your chances of reaching the key before they realize you've bypassed their positions."

Giacomo pulled out maps and detailed route information.

Thomas's suspicion warred with hope. This could be genuine help from a conscience-stricken network member. Or elaborate trap—send them on remote northern routes where they'd be isolated and vulnerable.

"Why should we trust this?" Margarethe asked bluntly.

"You shouldn't." Giacomo met her eyes without flinching. "I'm network-trained. I know exactly how to build trust before betraying it. This could all be theater."

He didn't offer reassurances. Didn't protest innocence. Just laid out the reality and waited.

He placed the maps on the stone floor, stepped back. "But I'm giving you the information anyway. What you do with it is your choice. If you trust me, the northern route might save your lives. If you don't, ignore this and proceed with whatever plan you had. Either way, I've done what my conscience demanded."

Thomas studied Giacomo's face. Saw only exhaustion and guilt and desperate hope.

"Maria," he said quietly. "Blood memory. Does your inheritance include anything that might help identify whether Giacomo is genuine?"

Maria accessed memory with that distant-eyed expression.

"The network trains members to lie convincingly, to build trust before betraying it," she said slowly. "But those who are genuinely torn show patterns too—they offer details you can test instead of demanding blind trust, they risk themselves by what they give away, they admit uncertainty instead of wrapping lies in certainty."

She looked at Giacomo directly. "He's showing those patterns. Gave us maps we can verify before committing. Admitted he could be lying. Didn't ask us to trust him unconditionally. That's consistent with genuine turning rather than trap."

"Could be sophisticated deception," Margarethe cautioned.

"Everything could be trap," Thomas said with sudden decision. "But we've made it this far by making calculated judgments about who to trust based on incomplete information."

He picked up Giacomo's maps. "Thank you. We'll use this information. If it's genuine, you may have saved our lives. If it's trap, at least we walked into it with eyes open."

"One more thing," Giacomo said. "Helena is alive. The network arrested her in Constantinople but she escaped during transport to Avignon. She's in hiding somewhere in the Balkans, continuing to organize those who've turned and distribute the Protocol. I thought you should know."

Relief flooded through Thomas. Helena had risked everything to help them. Knowing she'd survived gave their escape meaning beyond just their own lives.

"Tell her we're grateful," he said. "If you have contact."

"I'll try," Giacomo said. "But communication is fragmented now. Many of us are operating in isolation, doing what we can locally without shared plan. We're more scattered groups than organized rebellion."

"That's how resistance survives," Margarethe said with hard wisdom. "Scattered. Separate. No single head for the network to cut off. Messier than organized rebellion but harder to destroy completely."

They left the church separately, each taking different routes.

That night, studying Giacomo's maps in a cold room they'd rented with the last of their gold, Thomas made the decision.

"We trust it. The northern route through Scotland. It's longer and harder but if the network really is positioned in York, walking directly into that trap accomplishes nothing."

"And if the northern route IS the trap?" Margarethe asked.

"Then we fight or die in Scottish wilderness instead of York monastery. I'm not sure that's worse." Thomas traced the route Giacomo had suggested. "But my instinct says Giacomo was genuine. The guilt in his voice wasn't performance."

Maria nodded agreement. "My blood memory says the same. Giacomo was the conflicted type—serving for decades while questioning, finally choosing to act on the questions. That's real, not performance."

"Then Scotland it is," Margarethe said, already marking the route and calculating travel times. "Six weeks if weather cooperates. Eight weeks more likely given spring storms and rough terrain. But alive at the end instead of captured in York."

They rested briefly in Milan, then continued northwest toward the French coast, following Giacomo's route suggestions.

Behind them, if Giacomo was truthful, network hunters waited in York for fugitives who would never arrive.

Ahead, Scottish highlands and rough northern approach that might save their lives or might be elaborate trap.

But they kept moving forward anyway.

Because calculated risks were all they had.

## Scene 3: The Scottish Highlands - Northern Approach

### Scottish Coast, Late April 1348

Scotland looked like the edge of the world—rugged coastline where waves crashed against black rocks, green hills rising into mist, scattered settlements clinging to harsh landscape with stubborn determination.

They'd reached the Highlands after six weeks of brutal travel. Six weeks of small ships tossed by spring storms, rough coastal paths that tested their endurance, villages so remote that strangers were treated with suspicion bordering on hostility.

The cold here was different from continental winter—wetter, penetrating, the kind that got into bones and stayed there. Thomas's monastery robes were inadequate. Maria looked half-frozen despite the extra cloak they'd purchased in a coastal village. Even Margarethe, who rarely complained about physical discomfort, moved with the stiffness of someone fighting constant cold.

"How much farther to York?" Maria asked, her teeth chattering.

"Three weeks overland through the Highlands, then south through Scottish borderlands into northern England," Margarethe said, consulting Giacomo's maps. "Rough terrain. Few settlements. We'll be exposed, vulnerable, moving through territories where clan warfare makes the plague look organized."

"But not where the network expects us," Thomas said. "That's worth the hardship."

They'd hired a local guide in the last village—a grizzled Highlander named Callum who spoke heavily accented English and seemed to view their quest as madness but was willing to take their coin anyway. He led them now through paths that were barely visible, across moorland that stretched endlessly in shades of brown and green.

"English monastery you seek," Callum said in his thick accent. "York. Dangerous place now. Plague killed many. Raiders taking advantage. English authorities weak in the north—too busy fighting French to care about border security."

"We have to reach it anyway," Thomas said.

Callum shrugged with the fatalism of someone who'd seen countless travelers pursue doomed quests. "Your coin. Your death. I guide you to the border, then you're on your own. I don't cross into English lands—too many old feuds, too many people who'd kill a Highland guide on sight."

They traveled through landscape that seemed designed to break human will. Endless hills, sudden storms that turned paths to mud, rivers swollen with spring melt that had to be forded carefully or risk being swept away. Food was scarce—cold provisions, occasional game when Margarethe's hunting skills paid off, nothing hot because fires attracted attention they couldn't afford.

On the fifth night into the Highlands, huddled in a stone shieling that offered minimal shelter, Thomas watched Maria shiver despite being wrapped in both available cloaks.

"She's not going to survive this," Margarethe said quietly, checking Maria's temperature with a hand to her forehead. "The fever from before weakened her. This cold and exposure is breaking her down again."

"We can't stop," Maria said through chattering teeth. "If we stop, we fail. If we fail, everything—Johannes, Wilhelm, your parents, Helena, Giacomo, everyone who helped us—it's all for nothing."

"You'll be dead for nothing if we push too hard," Margarethe said bluntly. "Tomorrow we rest. Find a village, pay for indoor shelter, let you recover strength before continuing."

"The network—"

"Won't find us in the middle of the Scottish Highlands," Thomas interrupted gently. "Margarethe's right. We rest tomorrow. Build your strength back. Then we continue to York when you can actually survive the journey."

Maria looked like she wanted to argue but another violent shiver stopped her.

That night, Thomas took first watch while Margarethe and Maria slept. He sat at the shieling entrance, wrapped in his inadequate robes, watching mist roll across the moorland and thinking about how close they were to completing the quest.

Six of seven keys. The Genesis Protocol almost whole. Just the sixth fragment waiting in York—the Heart that Feels, showing the emotional cost of the network's breeding programs. The human suffering beneath the bloodless language of improvement and population shaping.

Then they'd have everything. Complete documentation of the network's corruption. Evidence spanning two thousand years from noble founding to monstrous present.

The question that kept him awake wasn't whether they'd reach York and access the sixth key. It was what they'd do with complete understanding once they had it.

How did three fugitives challenge a conspiracy that had survived for two millennia? How did they expose corruption to people who benefited from that corruption? How did they create change when the network controlled so much influence through churches and courts and merchant networks?

Margarethe emerged from the shieling, wrapped in a blanket, and sat beside him without speaking. For a long moment they just sat together in companionable silence, watching mist move across the landscape.

"You're thinking too much," she said finally. "I can hear the wheels turning from here."

"We're almost there," Thomas said. "One key left. Then we have to figure out how to actually use what we've learned. That's the part I can't see clearly. The pattern of how this ends."

"Maybe there is no pattern," Margarethe said. "Maybe we just do what seems right in each moment and hope it adds up to something meaningful. Expose the corruption wherever we can. Give the information to people who might use it well. Plant seeds for future generations who'll find the evidence we preserve in cipher manuscripts."

"That's not very satisfying."

"Most worthwhile things aren't." Margarethe pulled the blanket tighter. "We've already accomplished more than anyone expected. Three fugitives with stolen keys, surviving months of pursuit, accessing six of seven fragments despite the network's best efforts to stop us. That matters even if we don't live to see the network fall."

Thomas thought about that. About how quests in songs always ended with clear victories or noble deaths. About how reality was messier—small successes mixed with failures, unclear outcomes, consequences that wouldn't be known for generations.

"I'm glad you're here," he said quietly. "I couldn't have done this alone. Wouldn't have survived past Strasbourg without you."

"You would have found a way. You're more capable than you think." Margarethe glanced at him, her expression unreadable in the darkness. "But I'm glad I'm here too. Glad I didn't just run after my father died. Glad I chose to help you instead of disappearing into some remote village to live out my days carrying useless knowledge in my head."

"The knowledge isn't useless. The cipher locations you memorized have helped us multiple times."

"You know what I mean. It's the using of it that matters. The choosing to act instead of just surviving." Margarethe's voice grew quieter. "My father taught me to fight. Taught me to survive alone. But he also taught me that survival for its own sake is just slow death. That you need purpose to actually be alive instead of just breathing."

Her hand found his under the blanket—intentional this time, not accidental brush. Her fingers were cold but her grip was steady.

"This is purpose," she said. "This quest. This fight against corruption. These people we've met who chose conscience over cowardice. This matters in ways simple survival never could."

Thomas squeezed her hand, felt his heart hammering again in that particular way that had nothing to do with fear.

They sat like that for a while, hands clasped under the blanket, watching mist roll across the Highlands and not talking about what this meant or where it was going or what they'd do about the growing connection between them.

Some things didn't need words yet.

Some things just needed to exist quietly until the time came to name them.

Eventually Margarethe's watch shift arrived. She squeezed his hand once more before letting go, disappearing into the darkness to patrol the perimeter with her usual vigilance.

Thomas returned to the shieling, lay down near Maria's sleeping form, and tried to rest despite his mind spinning with thoughts about patterns and purposes and a hand holding his in the Scottish darkness.

They reached the border three weeks later, crossing into northern England on a gray morning that smelled of rain and approaching summer. Callum the guide left them there with gruff instructions about roads and settlements and warnings about English raiders who'd rob travelers without hesitation.

Then they were alone again. Three fugitives approaching York and the final key that would complete their understanding of a conspiracy two thousand years in the making.

The network was positioning ahead of them—Giacomo's warning had been clear. They'd bypassed the main trap but there would still be guards, still be watchers, still be dangerous moments when discovery seemed certain.

But they'd made it this far through worse.

One key left.

Then synthesis.

Then whatever came after—exposure, rebellion, perhaps death, perhaps something more.

They walked south toward York through English countryside slowly recovering from plague, through villages rebuilding after devastation, through landscape marked by mass graves and abandoned farms and the visible scars of catastrophe the network had known was coming and done nothing to prevent.

The quest was almost finished.

The danger had never been higher.

But surrender still wasn't an option they'd consider.

# CHAPTER 9 - THE SIXTH KEY (Medieval Timeline - 1348)

## Scene 1: England - Arrival in York

### York, May 1348

They reached York on a gray morning two days after leaving the abandoned barn.

Gray sky.

Gray rain.

Gray stone walls rising from gray mud.

England looked greener than the continent despite May's cold rain—less obvious plague devastation than France or Italy. The air smelled different here: wet wool, peat smoke, salt from distant coast mixing with the usual medieval stench. The rain washed everything cleaner, or at least quieter.

But Thomas knew the appearance deceived. The Black Death had reached England, was spreading through ports and cities, would kill a third of the population just as it had across Europe.

The network had known it was coming. Had positioned themselves to benefit.

That knowledge burned as they approached York's walls.

York was ancient—Roman foundations visible in walls and streets, Viking history layered over Roman, Norman structures rising above both. A city that had survived multiple collapses across a thousand years. And somewhere within those walls, hidden in a monastery's restricted library, the sixth key waited.

The Heart that Feels—the record of the human cost of the network's breeding programs.

"The key is in the Minster's library," Thomas said. "St. Peter's Cathedral. Restricted section accessible only to senior clergy. We'll go in as scholars seeking religious texts."

"Or just steal it," Margarethe suggested. "Break in at night, find the key, Maria reads it, we slip out."

"That worked in Constantinople because Helena helped from inside. We don't have network contacts here. We don't know the library's plan or guards. Going in blind risks capture more than a careful approach."

Maria had been quiet since entering England, accessing blood memory more frequently, her young face showing strain from carrying forty generations of inherited memory. Now she spoke:

"The sixth key is different from the others. The first five fragments and the seventh contain practical knowledge—locations, connections, routes, timing. But the sixth fragment is personal. It records individual stories. Families broken by breeding programs. Children raised as bloodline experiments. Communities fragmented to serve the network. The human cost written in tears rather than ledgers."

"Why set that down at all?" Margarethe asked.

"Because the founders wanted future generations to understand what bloodline breeding actually costs. Wanted them to see that 'shaping the people' becomes real lives broken, loves stolen, children taken for purposes they never chose."

Thomas thought about that as they entered York through the main gates—the network's founders had documented their own moral doubts, had preserved evidence of the human cost, had tried to warn future generations.

And those future generations had proceeded anyway.

"St. Peter's Minster is in the city center," Thomas said, navigating by visible landmarks. "We approach as pilgrims, request library access for prayer and study. If that fails, we consider more direct methods."

The cathedral dominated York's skyline—massive Gothic structure still under construction in some sections but already magnificent. They approached as afternoon transitioned toward evening, three travel-worn pilgrims seeking shelter.

A monk met them at the entrance—elderly man with kind eyes and patient manner.

"Welcome, travelers," he said in English. "I'm Brother Edmund. You seek shelter or prayer or both?"

"Both, Brother," Thomas responded in the same language, grateful for months of travel that had given him rudimentary English. "We're scholars seeking library access. We study cipher marks hidden in manuscript ornament."

Brother Edmund's expression shifted—not suspicion exactly, but heightened interest. "Cipher systems? That's specialized knowledge. Most pilgrims seek spiritual guidance, not scholarly access to restricted materials."

"We're not most pilgrims," Margarethe said. "We've traveled from the continent specifically to study materials preserved in English monasteries that might not have survived plague and war elsewhere."

"From the continent," Brother Edmund said slowly. "The plague is worse there, we hear."

"A third dead or dying in many regions," Thomas confirmed. "Which makes preserving accumulated knowledge critical. We're documenting cipher systems before the scholars who understand them die and the knowledge is lost."

Cover story and truth together—they were absolutely trying to preserve knowledge, just not the knowledge Brother Edmund imagined.

"I'll need to consult with the library's keeper," Brother Edmund said. "We don't grant access to restricted materials lightly, especially to foreign visitors during plague times. Wait here."

He disappeared into the cathedral's interior.

"He suspects something," Margarethe said quietly.

"Suspects we're more than simple pilgrims," Thomas agreed. "But suspicion isn't certainty. If we're careful, we might gain legitimate access."

Brother Edmund returned twenty minutes later with an older monk—the library keeper, his expression showing the particular wariness of someone protecting valuable materials from potential damage.

"I'm Father Aelred," the older monk said, studying them with sharp intelligence. "Brother Edmund says you seek access to cipher manuscripts. Explain your purpose and learning."

Thomas had prepared for this. "I studied manuscript preservation at a German monastery before plague forced me to flee. Margarethe has an eye for cipher patterns in decorative elements. Maria has family knowledge of scribal traditions passed through generations."

All technically true even if massively incomplete.

Father Aelred considered them for a long moment. "The library contains materials dating to Roman and Anglo-Saxon periods. Some manuscripts show cipher marks in margins we've never interpreted. If you genuinely understand such systems, you might help us catalog materials we've preserved but never fully comprehended."

"We'd be honored to assist," Thomas said.

"Tomorrow, then," Father Aelred decided. "You'll have supervised access to general collection materials. If you demonstrate legitimate scholarship rather than treasure hunting or heresy, we might grant access to more restricted holdings. For tonight, you'll stay in the pilgrim quarters. Meals are provided. Prayers are expected."

They were led to simple rooms—clean if sparse, better than many places they'd slept during months of travel. Thomas lay on his narrow bed that night thinking about how close they were. One more fragment. One more piece of the complete Genesis Protocol.

Then they'd have everything except the way to actually use it to challenge the network.

## Scene 2: The Library - Gaining Access

### St. Peter's Minster Library, Next Morning

The library was beautiful in a way that made Thomas's scholar's heart ache.

Rows of carefully preserved manuscripts. Copying desks where monks recreated deteriorating texts. Centuries of accumulated knowledge about maintaining materials. The smell of parchment and oak gall ink, beeswax and old leather bindings. Quiet broken only by the scratch of quills and the soft rustle of turning pages.

Thomas had to force himself not to weep. This was what Grandfather Wilhelm had fought for. What Brother Hamo had died protecting.

Father Aelred supervised their initial access personally, watching as Thomas examined manuscripts showing cipher marks in margins.

"These symbols aren't random," Thomas said, pointing to decorative elements most people would dismiss as embellishment. "They hide additional meaning. See how certain flourishes appear at specific intervals? That's deliberate positioning marking key passages."

He demonstrated using techniques Wilhelm had taught him, showing how apparent decoration actually served as guide for hidden knowledge.

Father Aelred's expression shifted from skepticism to genuine interest. "We've preserved these manuscripts for generations without understanding that dimension. The decorations are functional rather than purely aesthetic?"

"Both," Thomas said. "Beautiful and functional. The cipher system hides information in plain sight—make it look like normal decoration so that destroying encrypted knowledge requires destroying all decorated manuscripts, which even barbarians hesitate to do."

He spent the morning demonstrating cipher identification, building credibility while Margarethe and Maria examined different manuscripts looking for signs of which restricted materials might contain the sixth key.

By afternoon, Father Aelred was convinced.

"The restricted collection is below," he said, leading them down stone stairs into underground storage where oldest materials were kept for preservation. "We maintain materials here that date before the Norman conquest. Some texts are unique—only surviving copies of works that exist nowhere else. You'll treat them with appropriate reverence."

"Of course, Father," Thomas said.

The underground chamber was colder than the main library, kept cool by stone and depth. Manuscripts lined shelves in careful organization.

Maria moved through the collection with blood memory guiding her—forty generations of women who'd preserved knowledge of where the sixth key was hidden, what manuscript contained it, what countersigns would identify it among thousands of preserved texts.

She stopped at a particular shelf, pulled out a manuscript marked as medical text from the twelfth century.

"This one," she said quietly.

Thomas examined it—appeared to be herbal medicine compendium, discussing treatments for various ailments. The kind of practical knowledge monasteries preserved routinely.

But the cipher marks in the margins told different story.

"Father Aelred," Thomas said carefully, "this manuscript shows cipher encoding more sophisticated than the examples upstairs. Would you permit us to study it more carefully? We might decode additional medical knowledge the original scribes preserved."

"Proceed," Father Aelred said. "But I'll remain present. That manuscript is valuable—one of our oldest medical texts."

Thomas and Maria worked together, using the Iron and Silver Keys to decode the cipher marks, revealing hidden content beneath the surface medical text.

Maria's hands trembled as she accessed blood memory, pulling up inherited knowledge about what this manuscript really contained.

Her eyes went distant, unfocused, seeing through forty generations.

Then tears started streaming down her face—quiet, controlled crying she tried to hide but couldn't quite manage.

"What's wrong?" Father Aelred asked with genuine concern.

"The text," Maria said, her voice breaking. "It's recording suffering. Real people's suffering. Families separated. Children taken from parents for breeding. Women forced into marriages they didn't want to shape bloodlines. Men whose entire lives were arranged by others to serve purposes they never understood."

She accessed deeper memory, her young voice carrying accumulated grief:

"Anna of Kent, married at fourteen to a man three times her age because the network's reckonings said their blood would produce children with sharper eye for patterns. She died in childbirth at sixteen, having never wanted the marriage or pregnancy, having no power to refuse."

"Robert of York, separated from his siblings at age seven and sent to a monastery not for God, but because the network wanted him raised where learning would be pressed into him. He never saw his family again."

"Margaret the Weaver, whose community was deliberately scattered—families relocated to different regions, marriages annulled and rearranged, children redistributed—all to serve network breeding schemes that treated humans as livestock rather than people with relationships worth preserving."

Maria's voice grew stronger through the tears: "The sixth fragment records hundreds of individual stories like these. Real people. Real suffering. Real wounds inflicted by treating bloodline breeding as more important than human choice or family bonds. The founders included this as warning—if you do this, here's the cost in human lives and broken families, harm that compounds across generations."

Father Aelred looked disturbed. "This manuscript documents some kind of methodical cruelty? Hidden beneath medical text?"

"Yes," Thomas said quietly. "A conspiracy that's survived for more than two millennia. For centuries treating humans as tools for breeding rather than people with inherent worth beyond reproductive value. The sixth fragment is evidence of their crimes written in the emotional language of those who suffered."

He met Father Aelred's eyes directly. "We're not treasure hunters or heretics, Father. We're exposing a conspiracy older than the church itself—corruption that taught it to play God with human bloodlines and call it preservation."

Father Aelred was quiet for a long moment, wrestling with whether to believe them or call for guards.

Finally he spoke: "I've preserved manuscripts for forty years without understanding many of them fully. Trusted that knowledge worth hiding was knowledge worth preserving, even when I didn't grasp its complete significance. If what you say is true—if this conspiracy has operated across centuries—then exposing it matters more than guarding one monastery's rules of access."

He stepped back from the manuscript. "Take what time you need to study this fully. I'll ensure you're not disturbed. But understand—if this knowledge is as dangerous as you suggest, people will try to stop you from using it. Are you prepared for that?"

"We've been running from people trying to stop us for months," Margarethe said. "We're prepared."

Father Aelred nodded slowly, then left them alone in the underground chamber—tremendous act of trust from a man who'd spent his life protecting these materials.

Maria returned her attention to the sixth fragment, accessing the complete emotional documentation with deep blood memory.

Thomas watched her process centuries of suffering—every family broken by network interference, every child raised as bloodline experiment, every person whose life had been controlled and weighed and reduced to breeding value rather than human worth.

The cost of the network's corruption written in tears and broken lives.

When Maria finally finished accessing the fragment, she looked exhausted and grief-stricken and determined.

"I have it all now," she said. "All seven keys complete in blood memory. The sixth fragment shows the human cost in ways that make the network's evil undeniable. Anyone who reads these stories—anyone who sees what bloodline breeding actually means—will understand we're fighting not just a shadow plot but cruelty refined over centuries."

"All seven," Thomas said quietly. "We're done gathering."

"So we figure out how to use what we've learned to actually challenge them," Margarethe said.

Thomas thought about that—seven keys worth of evidence showing the network's corruption from noble defensive purpose to catastrophe-making. Proof they'd known the Black Death was coming and positioned to benefit. Evidence of centuries of treating humans as breeding stock.

And one monk, one warrior woman, and one fourteen-year-old girl carrying all that knowledge while being hunted across Europe.

"We need to get this information to people who can use it," he said. "Not just preserve it for future generations, but distribute it now in ways that create immediate rebellion within the network. Force current members to confront what they've become."

"How?" Maria asked. "We can't shout it from rooftops. Most people can't read. The church and lords are riddled with network men who'll smother anything that threatens them."

"Then we use the network's own preservation methods against them," Thomas said. "Encode the complete Protocol into manuscripts using their cipher. Distribute copies to monasteries and libraries across Europe—so many, in so many places, that destroying all of them becomes impossible. Hide the evidence in plain sight using the same methods they used to preserve knowledge across collapses."

"That's long-term preservation," Margarethe objected. "What about immediate action?"

"We also send plain letters to network members directly," Thomas said. "Use countersigns and names Helena and Marcus provided. Show them what the complete Protocol actually says, forcing them to choose between continued service to corruption or rebellion to restore the original purpose."

Maria nodded slowly. "Some will suppress the information. Some will kill us for distributing it. But some—maybe enough—will read it and realize they've been serving evil disguised as preservation. Internal rebellion combined with preserved evidence. Both strategies together."

"We'll need time," Thomas said. "Months to encode the complete Protocol, distribute copies, send letters, create conditions for both immediate and long-term challenge."

"Time we might not have," Margarethe warned. "The Order is still hunting us. The network knows what we've been gathering. They'll set men to intercept us before we can distribute the evidence."

"Then we move carefully," Thomas said. "Stay in England where we have some distance from continental pursuit. Use monastery resources to create manuscript copies. Send them through church roads and trusted hands while avoiding men the network has bought."

Father Aelred returned as evening approached. "Have you learned what you needed?"

"Yes," Thomas said. "And more than we wanted. The sixth fragment documents suffering we wish didn't exist but must be preserved as evidence of crimes that need to be exposed."

"Then preserve it properly," Father Aelred said. "The library's resources are available for your copying work. I'll provide materials and space. What you do with that preservation is your choice—I'm too old to fight conspiracies that have existed longer than kingdoms. But I can help ensure the knowledge survives long enough for someone to use it correctly."

Relief washed through Thomas. "Thank you, Father. Your help matters more than you know."

### That Evening

They spent the week in York creating manuscript copies, encoding the complete Genesis Protocol using cipher methods that would survive future catastrophes. Working in St. Peter's Minster's scriptorium alongside monks who thought they were just helping scholars preserve medical and philosophical texts.

The sixth key's emotional record was hardest to encode—the human stories, the individual suffering, the accumulated hurt of centuries.

But it was also the most important. Because bloodless language about "population shaping" could be rationalized. But individual stories of children separated from parents, women forced into unwanted marriages, families destroyed for breeding purposes—those stories created emotional impact that cold reasoning couldn't achieve.

Anyone reading the sixth fragment would understand the network's evil not just intellectually but viscerally.

On the fifth evening, Maria fell asleep at her copying desk just after compline, exhausted from channeling blood memory for hours. Father Aelred had the monks carry her to the pilgrim quarters, told Thomas and Margarethe to rest as well.

Thomas walked Margarethe back through the darkening cloister, neither speaking until they reached the corridor where their cells waited.

She stopped at her door, turned to face him. Lamplight caught the sharp angles of her face, the warrior's readiness that never quite left her posture even in sanctuary.

"We did it," she said quietly. "Seven keys. Complete Protocol. Everything we set out to gather."

"We did," Thomas agreed. His heart was hammering, and not from exertion or fear—that particular rhythm he'd learned to recognize over months of traveling beside her, fighting beside her, choosing to continue forward together when running would have been safer.

Margarethe's hand found his—deliberate this time, not accidental like on the ship from Constantinople or tentative like in the Highland night. Her fingers laced through his with the same precision she brought to everything, and he felt the calluses from crossbow work, the scars from old fights, the strength that had kept all of them alive.

"Whatever comes next," she said, meeting his eyes directly, "we face it together. Not just the quest. Not just survival or gathering keys or distributing protocols. But this. Everything."

Thomas turned her hand in his, felt his pulse jumping beneath her fingertips. "Together," he said. "To whatever end."

She stepped closer, and he could see the lamplight reflecting in her eyes—fierce and certain and for once unguarded in ways she never allowed during daylight hours when anyone might be watching.

"I didn't choose this quest because of conscience," Margarethe said, her voice barely above a whisper. "I chose it because of you. Because the monk who stood up to the Order in that Strasbourg alley looked like someone worth following. Worth protecting. Worth..." She paused, searching for words that clearly didn't come easily. "Worth everything."

She finally met his eyes. "I loved my father. But he died years before they killed him. You're the first person since then who made me want to live for something besides revenge."

Thomas felt something in his chest expand and tighten simultaneously—the scholar who'd spent his life with manuscripts instead of people, suddenly confronted with this warrior woman choosing him not because he was useful but because she wanted to.

"I'm not brave," he said honestly. "I shake before every fight. Second-guess every decision. Spend half my time terrified we're going to fail—"

"And you keep going anyway," Margarethe interrupted. "That's what bravery is. Not the absence of fear but the refusal to let it stop you." Her other hand came up to touch his face, and her touch was surprisingly gentle despite the calluses and scars. "You're the bravest person I know. And if we're going to keep risking our lives distributing this Protocol, fighting this network, I need you to know that I'm not just in this for the quest. I'm in it for you."

He kissed her then—or she kissed him; afterward Thomas couldn't say who moved first, only that suddenly they were pressed together in the shadowed corridor, her hands in his hair and his arms around her waist, and everything that had been building between them for months finally given permission to exist openly.

It wasn't long—the monastery corridor was no place for extended intimacy, and both of them were too aware of propriety and danger to forget themselves completely. But it was enough. Enough to acknowledge what they'd been carefully not naming. Enough to make the choice explicit.

When they pulled apart, Margarethe's expression showed the same fierce certainty it always did before a fight.

"Whatever comes," she said again. "Together."

"Together," Thomas agreed, and meant it with everything he had.

She slipped into her cell then, leaving him standing in the corridor with his heart still racing and the absolute certainty that he'd just committed to something far more binding than any monastic vow.

The quest had started as duty—exposure of corruption, preservation of truth, honoring Wilhelm's sacrifice.

Somewhere along the way it had become about her. About them. About choosing to face whatever came next not because it was righteous but because facing it alone was unthinkable.

Thomas returned to his own cell and lay awake for a long time, thinking about the future they were choosing together—dangerous and uncertain and probably shorter than either of them hoped.

But theirs. Chosen consciously. Together.

Some things didn't need more words than that.

## Scene 3: The Order Arrives - Final Confrontation

### York, Two Days Later

The Gray Robes came on the seventh day.

Thomas was in the scriptorium working on the final pages of the seventeenth manuscript copy—carefully encoding the sixth fragment's emotional documentation into what appeared to be a treatise on herbal remedies—when Brother Edmund rushed in with fear bright in his elderly eyes.

"Four men," he gasped. "Gray cloaks. Armed. Asking for you by description—a German monk, a woman warrior, a young girl. They're searching the Minster methodically. Father Aelred is trying to delay them but they're not listening to his authority."

Thomas's hands stilled on the manuscript. After months of flight, the Order had finally caught up.

"How long?" Margarethe asked, already moving to gather essential materials—the Iron and Silver Keys, the one completed manuscript they couldn't afford to lose.

"Minutes," Brother Edmund said. "Maybe less. They're on the main floor now—"

The door burst open.

Four Gray Robes, exactly as described. Disciplined. Armed. Moving with practiced precision of men who'd hunted fugitives across Europe and finally cornered their quarry.

The lead Gray Robe spoke in Latin: "Thomas of Eltville. Margarethe of Strasbourg. Maria of Mainz. You are charged with heresy, conspiracy against the church, theft of sacred materials, and murder of church officials during your flight. Surrender peacefully and you'll be granted trial. Resist and we'll take you by force."

"Murder?" Thomas said. "We've killed no one except in self-defense when attacked."

"Brother Wilhelm of Prague died during your theft of network materials. Several guards in Constantinople were injured during your break-in. These crimes will be answered for." The Gray Robe's hand moved to his sword hilt. "Surrender. Now."

Maria accessed blood memory with that distant expression. "They're not here to arrest us for trial," she said quietly. "They're here to execute us and destroy the manuscripts. The church trial is formality they'll arrange after we're dead."

"You can't know that," the Gray Robe said.

"I can," Maria countered, ancient certainty in her young voice. "Forty generations of women hunted by the Order. I know exactly how this ends if we surrender. You'll kill Thomas and Margarethe immediately. You'll take me to network custody for breeding purposes since I'm young enough to be useful. And every manuscript we've created will be burned before anyone outside this room can read them."

The Gray Robe's expression shifted fractionally—not quite confirmation, but not denial either.

Father Aelred appeared in the doorway behind them, his elderly face showing distress and determination. "I don't know what authority you claim, but this is sanctuary. These scholars are under my protection. You will not arrest them without proper church procedure."

"Stand aside, old man," the Gray Robe commanded. "Network business supersedes local church authority."

"Network business," Father Aelred repeated slowly, understanding dawning. "You're part of the conspiracy they've been exposing. Part of the corruption that's existed for centuries inside church ranks." He stepped forward despite obvious danger. "I won't let you murder scholars under protection of my library."

"Father, no—" Thomas started.

The Gray Robe moved with trained speed—struck Father Aelred across the face with mailed fist, sent the elderly monk crashing into the stone wall.

Father Aelred crumpled. Didn't move. Blood pooled beneath his head where it had struck stone, spreading dark across the scriptorium floor.

Margarethe had her crossbow up before Thomas could breathe, aimed at the lead Gray Robe with lethal efficiency.

"You just murdered a priest. In a cathedral. In front of witnesses." Margarethe's voice stayed flat, factual. "Whatever authority you claim—you just destroyed it."

She didn't flinch from Reynard's gaze. "Or does the network operate above God's law now?"

"He's not dead," the Gray Robe said, though Thomas couldn't tell if that was true given the spreading blood. "And you're not scholars—you're fugitives who've stolen network secrets and are attempting to distribute them in violation of agreements that have maintained order for two thousand years."

"Agreements made without consent of the people being bred like livestock," Maria said. "Agreements that treated human lives as resources to shape. The network betrayed its founding purpose. We're exposing that betrayal. That's not heresy—that's truth."

"Truth that will destroy carefully maintained orders," the Gray Robe said. "Truth that will create chaos when humanity needs order to survive coming catastrophes. You think exposing the network serves good? You're naive children playing with forces you don't understand."

The other three Gray Robes were spreading out, trying to surround them. Thomas's hands were shaking. His breath came short and fast. Sweat ran down his spine despite the scriptorium's cold.

They could surrender—be executed, have their work destroyed, accomplish nothing. Or fight—almost certainly die given four trained warriors against three exhausted fugitives—but maybe create enough chaos that the manuscripts survived.

"Margarethe," he said quietly. "The window behind you. Can you break through?"

"Ten-foot drop to courtyard. We'd be exposed during escape but alive long enough to run." Her crossbow never wavered. "Your call."

Thomas grabbed the completed manuscript—the one containing all seven fragments plus synthesis notes. Wrapped it in oiled cloth, sealed it carefully.

"Brother Edmund," he said to the terrified monk pressed against the wall. "This manuscript contains everything. The complete Genesis Protocol including documentation of network corruption. If we die, you distribute it. Give copies to monasteries across England. Send messengers to the continent. Make sure it survives even if we don't."

Brother Edmund took the wrapped manuscript with trembling hands. "I don't understand—"

"You don't need to. You just need to preserve knowledge and distribute it. That's what you've spent your life doing. This is just one more manuscript that deserves preservation." Thomas met his eyes with desperate intensity. "Promise me. Whatever happens in the next few minutes, this survives."

"I promise," Brother Edmund whispered.

The Gray Robes were advancing.

"Now," Thomas commanded.

Margarethe shot—crossbow bolt taking the lead Gray Robe in the shoulder, not fatal but incapacitating enough to drop him. She spun, smashed the scriptorium window with the crossbow stock, knocked out remaining glass with quick efficient strikes.

"Go!" she shouted.

Maria went first—young and agile, dropping through the window with blood memory guiding her landing. Thomas followed, hit the courtyard stones hard enough to jar his teeth, rolled to absorb impact the way Margarethe had taught him.

Margarethe came last, crossbow abandoned, knives in both hands, dropping and rolling and coming up already running.

Behind them, shouts as the remaining Gray Robes organized pursuit. Boots on stone. Professional hunters who wouldn't be slowed long by one injured companion.

They ran through York's streets—three fugitives who'd been cornered but refused to surrender, carrying nothing but the clothes on their backs and the knowledge encoded in Maria's blood memory.

The manuscripts were behind them. The complete Protocol written in cipher-encoded copies that Brother Edmund had promised to distribute.

Whether that promise would be honored, whether Father Aelred survived, whether their months of work accomplished anything—all unknown.

But they were alive. Still fleeing. Still carrying the knowledge the network wanted destroyed.

They reached York's walls as dusk gathered, found a drainage culvert that led beneath the fortifications, crawled through filth and darkness to emerge in fields beyond the city.

Behind them, York's spires rose against darkening sky. Somewhere in that city, the Order searched for them. Somewhere in that city, manuscripts containing the complete Protocol waited for distribution or destruction depending on whether Brother Edmund chose courage or safety.

"We can't go back," Margarethe said. "The Order controls the Minster now. Probably searching every manuscript, every scholar, looking for evidence of what we created."

"So we trust it," Thomas said, though trust seemed inadequate foundation for months of work. "Trust that Brother Edmund keeps his promise. Trust that Father Aelred survives to ensure distribution. Trust that some good monks choose preservation over obedience to authority the network has bought."

"And if they don't?" Maria asked quietly.

"Then the Protocol died in York," Thomas admitted. "And we failed everyone who died helping us gather it. Wilhelm and Hamo and Johannes and Helena and all the others who risked everything thinking we'd accomplish something meaningful."

They walked away from York through spring fields turning to summer growth, three fugitives carrying only blood memory and desperate hope.

The quest that had begun in Mainz nine months ago had reached its completion point—seven keys accessed, Protocol understood, evidence encoded in manuscripts left behind for others to distribute.

Whether that evidence would survive and create the rebellion they'd fought for, or be destroyed and suppressed like so many previous attempts—that was beyond their control now.

They'd done what they could with the time and knowledge they had.

Now they could only hope it was enough.

## Scene 4: Aftermath - Choosing the Path

### Rural England, Three Days from York

They'd been traveling north for three days, avoiding roads and settlements, living on foraged food and water from streams, when the messenger found them.

A young monk, barely twenty, riding a horse pushed hard. He'd clearly been searching methodically along northern routes from York, following likely escape paths for fugitives fleeing the city.

"Thomas of Eltville?" he called, spotting them in a forest clearing. "Margarethe? Maria?"

Margarethe had her knife ready before Thomas could respond, hard-won wariness making her assume trap until proven otherwise.

But the messenger held up empty hands, showed no weapons except a scroll tube bearing Father Aelred's seal.

"I bring news from York," he said. "From Brother Edmund. He asked me to find you, to tell you what transpired after you fled."

Thomas's heart hammered. "Father Aelred—did he survive?"

"Barely. Head injury that may never fully heal. But he lives, and he's already ensured your manuscripts are being copied and distributed." The messenger pulled out the scroll. "Brother Edmund wrote this three days ago. I've been riding hard to find you before the Order realized someone had been sent."

Thomas unsealed the scroll with trembling hands, read Brother Edmund's careful script aloud so Margarethe and Maria could hear:

*Thomas, Margarethe, Maria—*

*Father Aelred lives, though he may never fully recover. But in his moments of clarity, he has been more committed than ever to ensuring your work survives.*

*The Gray Robes found most of the manuscripts. Destroyed eleven copies, claiming they were heretical materials.*

*But they didn't find them all.*

*Father Aelred had moved three copies to hidden archive locations the night before the Order arrived. He suspected pursuit was closing in. Those three copies survive.*

*Moreover, I kept the promise. The complete manuscript you gave me—I made two copies immediately after you fled, working through the night. Sent one to Canterbury with our most trusted courier. Sent another to Dublin via ship. The original remains hidden in York.*

*So the network destroyed eleven copies. But six survive. And we are creating more.*

*Father Aelred has worked with other English monasteries. Within the month, twenty copies will exist. Within a year, perhaps a hundred distributed widely enough that destroying all becomes impossible.*

*You succeeded. The Protocol will survive. The evidence will be preserved for future generations.*

*But you cannot return to York. The Order has declared you excommunicated heretics. Your names and descriptions sent to church authorities across Europe. You are hunted now, with prices on your heads and warrants for execution without trial.*

*I'm sorry your reward for this service is continued flight and danger. But know that what you accomplished matters. The network's corruption is documented. Their betrayal preserved in multiple locations. Future generations will know the truth you risked everything to expose.*

*May God protect you in whatever comes next.*

*—Brother Edmund of York*

Thomas finished reading and passed the scroll to Maria so she could see it herself.

"Six copies," he said quietly. "Not the twelve we'd hoped for. But enough."

"And twenty more within a month," Margarethe added. "A hundred within a year. The network can't suppress that. Can't destroy manuscripts spread across that many locations without revealing themselves publicly."

Maria accessed blood memory briefly. "This is the farthest any rebellion has gotten. Previous exposures were suppressed before multiple copies existed. Single manuscripts destroyed. Individual truth-tellers killed before they could distribute evidence widely. But six copies surviving, with twenty more coming—that's unprecedented. That's dangerous to the network in ways they can't easily counter."

Relief hit Thomas—sharp and disbelieving—then settled into something quieter. Not victory—they were still hunted fugitives. But accomplishment. Purpose fulfilled. They'd set out to gather the complete Genesis Protocol and distribute it in ways that would survive suppression.

Against all odds, they'd done it.

"So what now?" Margarethe asked. "We're hunted across Europe. Can't return to any major city. Can't use our real names or show our faces anywhere the Order might have informants. We've completed the quest but we're no closer to safety than when we started."

The messenger spoke up hesitantly: "If I may—Brother Edmund also asked me to relay an offer. There's a monastery in the Welsh mountains. Small, remote, very old. Founded before Norman conquest, maintained by monks who value knowledge preservation above obedience to current church authority. Brother Edmund has connections there. He can arrange for you to stay, to live under borrowed names, to have sanctuary while the network's attention focuses elsewhere."

"Teaching," Maria said suddenly. "That's what my ancestors did when they needed to hide. They taught. Passed knowledge to the next generation through patient instruction instead of banners and blood. Quiet work that lasts when loud defiance gets you killed."

Thomas looked at Margarethe, saw her watching him with that same fierce certainty she'd shown in the monastery corridor five nights ago.

"Wales," he said slowly. "Teaching. Living quietly while the manuscripts do their work."

It wasn't retreat. It was recognizing they'd done the dramatic part—gathering and distributing the evidence. Now the patient part began—preserving knowledge, teaching future generations, maintaining the quiet work that accumulated across time.

Margarethe nodded. "Like Helena. Like Giacomo. Like all the ones who read the Protocol and chose conscience. We add ourselves to that line—not through martyrdom, but through work that lasts."

She took his hand—openly this time, in front of Maria and the messenger, no longer hiding what had solidified between them during their week in York.

"Together," she said, echoing the promise from the corridor. "Wales. Teaching. Building something that lasts beyond just survival."

"Together," Thomas agreed, squeezing her hand. "Whatever that becomes."

Maria smiled—the first genuine smile Thomas had seen from her in weeks. "Teaching sounds good. Better than running. Better than hiding. Actually doing something with the knowledge we've gathered instead of just preserving it in blood memory for another forty generations."

The messenger looked relieved. "Then I'll guide you to the monastery. Three days' travel into the mountains. Remote enough that Order pursuit would be very difficult. You'll be safe there. As safe as anyone can be in these times."

They traveled west into Wales, following the young monk through increasingly wild terrain. Left behind the populated lowlands where church authority was strong. Climbed into mountains where ancient monasteries clung to hilltops like stubborn reminders of older faiths layered beneath Christian conversion.

The monastery was exactly as described—small, ancient, maintained by a handful of elderly monks who cared more about preserving knowledge than enforcing current political orthodoxy.

The abbot welcomed them without too many questions. Assigned them cells. Provided library and scriptorium access. Asked only that they contribute through teaching and manuscript preservation work.

It was sanctuary. Not freedom—they'd probably never be free of the Order's hunt. But safety enough to live instead of just survive. Space enough to teach instead of just flee.

That first evening, after Maria had gone to bed exhausted from travel, Thomas and Margarethe walked the monastery's small garden together. Spring was turning to summer, and wildflowers grew in patches between herbs the monks cultivated for medicine.

"This is it, then," Margarethe said, looking out over the Welsh mountains purple with distance. "The end of the quest. The beginning of... whatever comes next."

"Teaching," Thomas said. "Preserving knowledge. Passing it forward to students who might use it better than we could."

"Living," Margarethe added quietly. She turned to face him, took both his hands in hers. "Building a life together. Not just sharing a quest or surviving pursuit. Actually choosing each other. Choosing this."

Her meaning was clear. Wales wasn't just sanctuary from the Order. It was commitment—to each other, to the work, to whatever future they could build in this remote place.

Thomas felt his heart hammering again in that particular way he'd learned to recognize.

"I'm not easy to live with," he warned. "I spend too much time with manuscripts. Second-guess everything. Get lost in scholarly rabbit trails—"

"And I'm a fighter who doesn't know how to be soft," Margarethe interrupted. "Who'll probably keep weapons under the bed and wake at every sound expecting attack. Who's better at violence than vulnerability." She stepped closer, and her expression was surprisingly gentle despite the scars and calluses. "But we've traveled across Europe together. Fought together. Nearly died together more times than I can count. If we can survive Constantinople and the Order and distributing the Protocol, I think we can survive building a life together."

She kissed him then—not furtive like in the monastery corridor, but open and certain under the Welsh sky, with nothing left to hide or deny.

When they pulled apart, Thomas was smiling despite everything—despite being hunted, excommunicated, exiled from the only life he'd ever known.

"Together," he said again, the word becoming promise and foundation and future all at once.

"Together," Margarethe agreed. "To whatever end."

They stood in the garden as evening deepened to night, hands clasped, watching stars emerge over the Welsh mountains, and for the first time in nine months of flight and danger and desperate quest, Thomas felt something like peace.

The complete Genesis Protocol existed in six preserved copies spreading across Europe. Twenty more being created. A hundred coming within a year.

The network's corruption documented in ways they couldn't fully suppress. Evidence of their betrayal spreading beyond network control.

They'd gathered the seven keys. Understood the complete Protocol. Distributed it in ways that honored Wilhelm and Hamo and Johannes and all the others who'd died believing exposure mattered.

Behind them, the quest was complete. The rebellion was distributed.

Ahead, the slow work of preservation and teaching and hoping it was enough.

And between them—chosen consciously, built deliberately—a future they'd face together.

Whatever came next, they'd meet it side by side.

That was enough.

That was everything.

# CHAPTER 10 - SMALL REBELLIONS (Medieval Timeline - 1348-1349)

## Scene 1: Sanctuary - The Welsh Monastery

### Welsh Mountains, Summer 1348

The monastery had no name that Thomas ever learned—just "the old place" in the Welsh the villagers spoke, or "Llan Hen" when they wanted to be specific. Ancient beyond reckoning, built on foundations that predated Christian conversion, maintained by a handful of elderly monks who cared more about preserving knowledge than enforcing orthodoxy.

Thomas stood in the scriptorium on his third morning there, looking out at mountains that disappeared into perpetual mist. Margarethe had already left for the garden where she'd promised to help the monks with herb cultivation—practical work suited her better than manuscript preservation. The abbot had given them adjoining cells in the pilgrim quarters, a quiet acknowledgment of their partnership that required no explanation or justification. They were beginning to build the rhythms of shared life.

The room smelled of old parchment and ink and the particular mustiness that came from centuries of accumulated manuscripts slowly deteriorating despite careful preservation efforts.

"You're the pattern-reader," a voice said behind him.

Thomas turned to find the abbot—a man so old his face had collapsed into a map of wrinkles, but his eyes remained sharp and knowing. Brother Cadfael, they called him, though Thomas suspected that was a name assumed long ago rather than one given at birth.

"I read patterns in manuscripts," Thomas said carefully. "Cipher systems. Hidden knowledge encoded in decorative elements."

"You were bred for it," Brother Cadfael said, not a question. "Forty-one generations of careful pairing to make a man who can see marks ordinary people walk past. The network's work. And from what the messenger told me, you've turned that gift against them."

Thomas felt his hand move instinctively toward the concealed Iron and Silver Keys he still carried. "How do you know about the network? About bloodline breeding?"

"I'm ninety-three years old," Brother Cadfael said, lowering himself onto a bench with careful movements. "I've preserved manuscripts for seventy years. Read things the network thought safely hidden in obscure Welsh monasteries where no bishop or lord would ever look. Learned enough of their breeding work to know what they've become."

He gestured at the shelves surrounding them. "These manuscripts hold scraps of knowledge the network has been trying to bury for centuries. Not the complete Genesis Protocol—I never had access to all seven keys. But enough that a patient man could scent the trail. I've been waiting decades for someone who might use it honestly."

"And you think I will?" Thomas asked.

"I think you already have. The messenger said you distributed the complete Protocol. Created copies spreading across Europe. Forced network members to choose between continued service and rebellion. That's more than anyone has accomplished in centuries of resistance." Brother Cadfael's ancient face showed something like satisfaction. "Now you need to survive long enough to see whether your distribution matters."

"By hiding here," Thomas said, feeling the weight of retreat even as he recognized its necessity.

"By teaching here," Brother Cadfael corrected. "The village has forty children. None can read. Most spend their lives scratching bread from cold soil. They're exactly the kind of people the network dismisses as irrelevant—too poor, too remote, too untaught to matter in bloodline work or catastrophe-making."

The old monk stood with effort, moved to the window beside Thomas. "Teach them anyway. Give them letters and arithmetic. A man who can read a charter is harder to cheat. A child who can count is harder to rob. That's how you break a hand that grips too tightly—one finger at a time."

Thomas thought about Maria's blood memory of forty generations of women who'd done exactly that—taught and preserved and witnessed, keeping truth alive by putting it into mouths that couldn't be burned.

"How long can we stay?" he asked.

"As long as you choose," Brother Cadfael said. "The Order's reach doesn't extend effectively this far into the Welsh mountains. The network has better targets than fugitives who've already distributed the evidence they were trying to suppress. You're probably safer here than anywhere else in Europe."

"And when the situation changes?"

"Then you adapt. Move if you must. Teach somewhere else. That's how this kind of work survives—by carrying it in your hands, not in one set of walls." Brother Cadfael smiled slightly. "Your ancestors understood that. Forty-one generations of it. Now you add your own small piece."

## Scene 2: Teaching Begins - The First Students

### Three Weeks Later

The children arrived on a bright August morning, shepherded by parents who looked simultaneously hopeful and skeptical about the strange monk offering to teach their offspring without charging the coin that normally made learning impossible for poor families.

Eight children ranging from six to fourteen. Thomas recognized the type immediately—bright eyes, quick movements, the barely contained energy of young minds desperate for stimulation beyond farm labor and survival.

"This is Master Thomas," Brother Cadfael introduced him. "He'll teach your children to read Latin and do arithmetic and understand basic natural philosophy. No fees. No requirements except regular attendance."

The parents exchanged glances—the offer was too generous, which meant there must be a catch.

"I'm a fugitive," Thomas said bluntly, deciding honesty was better than elaborate cover stories. "Hunted by church authorities for exposing corruption. I can't go to cities or use my real name where the Order might find me. But I can teach. And teaching your children costs me nothing except time I have in abundance while hiding."

The honesty helped. They understood fugitives—plague and war and failed harvests had created plenty of refugees. A monk hiding from church authority while teaching children was unusual but not incomprehensible.

They left their children and returned to their farms.

Thomas looked at his eight new students—five boys, three girls, ages six to fourteen, all staring at him with curiosity and wariness.

"Who can write their own name?" he asked in simple English.

None raised their hands.

"Who can count to one hundred?"

Two hands went up—the two oldest boys, probably taught basic arithmetic for market transactions.

"Who wants to learn things nobody in your village currently knows?"

All eight hands shot up immediately.

Thomas smiled. "Then we'll get along fine. Sit down. We'll start with the alphabet."

He'd prepared materials—scraped vellum too damaged for church use but serviceable for practice, charcoal for writing, simple primers he'd created from memory. Had arranged the scriptorium's least valuable benches into student seating.

Had not anticipated how difficult teaching would actually be.

The youngest children couldn't sit still for more than a few minutes before fidgeting. The oldest boy, Owain, clearly resented being grouped with younger students. The three girls were quiet to the point of silence, apparently trained to believe learning wasn't meant for them. And none had the background knowledge Thomas had unconsciously assumed—they didn't know Latin existed as a different language, didn't understand what "reading" meant, couldn't grasp why anyone would want to learn letters when farming didn't require literacy.

By midday, Thomas was exhausted and frustrated and beginning to question whether he was suited for teaching at all.

Maria found him in the scriptorium after the children had left, sitting amid scattered practice materials.

"How did it go?" she asked, though his expression probably answered the question.

"Terribly," Thomas admitted. "They can't focus. They don't have the background knowledge I need to build on. I don't know how to explain concepts that seem obvious to me but are completely foreign to them. I thought teaching would be straightforward—you know things, you share them, they learn. But it's nothing like that."

"It's like the cipher," Maria said, accessing blood memory. "When you first started learning, you couldn't see the pattern. Wilhelm had to show you again and again, from different angles, until suddenly it clicked. Teaching is the same—you show them the shape of it from more than one side until it takes in each mind."

"Wilhelm was patient," Thomas said. "I'm apparently not."

"Wilhelm was also teaching one student bred for pattern recognition who desperately wanted to learn. You're teaching eight students with ordinary minds and no particular motivation beyond vague parental pressure." Maria sat beside him. "Lower your expectations. Success isn't 'they all learn Latin in a week.' Success is 'they're slightly more literate at the end of a month than they were at the beginning.'"

Thomas thought about Wilhelm's patience over years of instruction. About how his own learning had been gradual accumulation rather than sudden revelation.

"So it's small work," he said quietly. "A letter at a time."

"Exactly," Maria said. "You're not creating scholars in a week. You're giving children a way to ask why, to weigh words against deeds, to read a charter and know when they're being cheated. That's real even if no one sings about it."

The next morning, Thomas adjusted his approach. Shorter lessons with more breaks for physical activity. Simpler explanations using concrete examples from farm life. Margarethe helped with the practical aspects—she'd suggested grouping students by ability rather than age, and her no-nonsense approach to discipline worked better than Thomas's scholarly patience.

By the end of the second week, the youngest children could recognize and write five letters. The middle students were working on simple words. Owain, the oldest boy, was reading basic Latin sentences and asking uncomfortable questions about why church officials claimed only priests should read scripture if reading was actually this learnable.

It wasn't dramatic. But it was progress.

And progress, Thomas was learning, was enough.

## Scene 3: News from Outside - Helena's Letter

### Late September 1348

The letter arrived on a cold morning when autumn was beginning to assert itself. A traveling merchant—part of Helena's network of sympathizers—delivered it along with trade goods for the monastery, accepted payment and left without comment.

Thomas brought the sealed package to the cell he shared with Margarethe, opened it to find Helena's careful script:

*Thomas, Margarethe, Maria—*

*Four months after your distribution:*

*The network is fractured but adapting. Forty-three members have broken away openly. Perhaps twice that number have turned quietly—appearing loyal while undermining the work they're told to oversee. Marriage arrangements in twelve cities have collapsed. Several catastrophe-making plots have been prevented by members who couldn't justify continued service after reading your testimony.*

*But the network is tightening its grip. Replacing those who've turned with harder men. Building new walls inside their own house. Inventing arguments that let them read your evidence and still call it necessary.*

*Cardinal Matteo remains at Avignon. He's prevented several schemes but also continues arranging network work. Whatever internal conflict he experiences hasn't translated into meaningful rebellion.*

*The Order has fractured internally. About a third of Gray Robes now question whether they're hunting heresy or guarding corruption. The split has blunted them—instead of one clean pursuit, they're arguing with each other.*

*Your manuscripts continue spreading. The six copies that survived York have been recopied dozens of times. Monasteries across Europe now have versions of the complete Protocol, hidden in their archives. The knowledge won't be lost.*

*Most importantly: the hunt has slackened. The Order's fracturing means no unified pursuit. The network has decided chasing three fugitives who've already scattered the pages gains them little. You're still wanted, but if you maintain low profiles, you could probably live quietly for years.*

*Several others who've turned have chosen paths like yours—slipping into remote places, teaching, copying. Your choice seems to be giving them courage to do the same.*

*Stay safe. Keep teaching. The work matters even when results aren't visible.*

*—Helena*

Thomas read the letter aloud to Margarethe and Maria that evening.

Forty-three who'd turned. Schemes disrupted. The Order fractured. Pages spreading too far to gather back.

Not victory. But not defeat either. The messy reality of resistance against powers too large to destroy but still capable of bleeding.

"Forty-three who turned," Margarethe said, showing something like satisfaction. "More than we dared hope for. Not enough to dismantle the network completely, but enough to matter."

"And the Order fracturing," Maria added. "Every other attempt was smothered before the Gray Robes could turn on each other. Now they're split, arguing over what they serve. That matters."

Thomas thought about Cardinal Matteo's 'experiment' with allowing the Protocol to spread. About network members torn between the story they told themselves and the evidence now in their hands.

"We wounded them," he said slowly. "Didn't kill them. Didn't stop the breeding programs outright. But we made it harder for them to keep going, harder for people to serve without thinking, made space for resistance that didn't exist before."

"Which means we chose right," Margarethe said quietly. "Hiding. Teaching. Letting the pages do their work while we stay alive long enough to keep copying."

Thomas folded Helena's letter carefully, stored it with the other materials he was preserving.

Outside, Welsh mountains disappeared into autumn mist.

Inside, words were moving hand to hand in places the network couldn't watch all at once.

Not the ending they'd hoped for when they started in Mainz nine months ago.

But perhaps the ending that was actually possible.

And maybe—maybe—that was enough.

## Scene 4: Winter Teaching - Owain's Questions

### Welsh Mountains, December 1348

Snow covered the mountains by mid-December, transforming the landscape into white wilderness that made travel nearly impossible and isolation absolute. The village hunkered down for winter, families surviving on stored food and hoping spring would arrive before supplies ran out.

Teaching continued despite the cold. The children came three days a week now, bundled in whatever warm clothing their families could spare, bringing small contributions of food as implicit payment for lessons their parents increasingly valued.

Thomas had twelve students now—four more children whose parents had heard about the free teaching. Ages six to fifteen, all progressing at different speeds, all learning things their village had never provided access to before.

Small work. The kind that didn't change kingdoms or topple networks.

The kind that changed one mind at a time.

Owain, the oldest, had become Thomas's star pupil—reading full Latin texts now, working through basic geometry, asking questions that showed genuine philosophical curiosity.

"Master Thomas," he asked one cold morning after the other students had left. "The priest says we should obey church authority because they represent God's will. But you're teaching us things that contradict what the priest says. Which should we believe?"

Thomas felt the weight of the question. Letters were the easy part. This—the habit of asking why, of noticing when a holy voice hid an unholy hunger—was harder.

And dangerous. Teaching children to question church authority could get them—and him—killed.

"What do you think?" he asked.

"I think..." Owain hesitated, working through implications. "I think the priest believes what he's saying. He's not lying deliberately. But he doesn't know things you know—hasn't read the manuscripts you've read, doesn't understand how orders can betray their first vows. So his certainty comes from ignorance rather than wisdom."

"That's a dangerous conclusion," Thomas said carefully. "Authorities don't react well to being told their certainty comes from ignorance."

"I know," Owain said. "But it's true anyway. Just because something is dangerous to say doesn't make it false."

Thomas thought about the network and the Order and centuries of corruption held in place because most people chose safety over truth.

"You're right," he said quietly. "Truth doesn't become false just because it's dangerous. But recognizing truth requires wisdom about when and how to speak it. Some truths get people killed if shared carelessly. Other truths need to be shared despite danger because suppressing them causes more harm than speaking them."

"How do you know the difference?"

"You learn by living," Thomas said. "You watch who profits and who bleeds. You think through what happens if you speak, and what happens if you stay silent."

He pulled out one of the cipher-encoded manuscripts—his own record of their quest, the complete Genesis Protocol preserved in a form that might survive the next burning.

"This manuscript contains truths that could get everyone who reads it killed if the wrong hands find it. So I hide them inside harmless-looking texts. I keep them for people who might use them when we cannot. Not because the truths are less true today—but because shouting them in the wrong place only feeds the gallows."

Owain studied the manuscript with focused intensity. "Will you teach me the encoding methods? So I can preserve truths even when speaking them is too dangerous?"

The choice came hard and clear. Teaching Owain cipher systems meant making another person who could recognize and preserve hidden knowledge. It also meant painting a mark on his back—the network hunted people with those abilities.

But the network already knew those capabilities could be developed through teaching, not just bloodline breeding. And having more people capable of preserving knowledge through catastrophe served the founders' original purpose better than maintaining scarcity.

"Yes," Thomas decided. "I'll teach you. But understand: This knowledge makes you valuable and dangerous. People will want to use you. Other people will want to suppress you. You'll need wisdom to survive having these capabilities."

"I understand," Owain said with genuine seriousness. "My father says every skill is both tool and weapon. Depends on who's holding it and what they choose to do with it."

"Your father is wise," Thomas said. "Remember that wisdom as you learn. This cipher serves humanity best when it's used to preserve and teach, not to bind and rule."

He began teaching Owain the basics of cipher recognition. Pattern identification. Substitution systems. The decorative elements that could encode additional information within apparent ornamentation.

The same knowledge Wilhelm had taught him. The same knowledge that could serve resistance or control depending on the choices its bearer made.

Outside, winter deepened over the Welsh mountains.

Inside, a boy learned how to tuck truth under ash.

## Scene 5: Spring Renewal - One Year Complete

### Welsh Mountains, May 1349

Thomas stood in the monastery garden on a bright May morning, exactly one year after they'd arrived at the Welsh sanctuary. The garden showed early spring growth—herbs pushing up through thawed soil, vegetable beds prepared for planting, evidence of life renewing after winter dormancy.

A year of teaching. A year of hiding. A year of wondering whether their distribution of the Genesis Protocol had mattered.

Margarethe found him there, carrying a basket of herbs she'd been harvesting for the monastery's kitchen. She looked different from the warrior woman who'd fled York twelve months ago—still competent, still dangerous when necessary, but changed in ways that went deeper than just the softening around her eyes or the ease with which she moved through this place they'd made home.

A year of living together had changed them both. They shared a cell now—the monastery's tacit acknowledgment of their partnership, however irregular by church law. What had begun as adjoining rooms in summer had become shared quarters by autumn. By winter, Brother Cadfael had handfasted them in the old Welsh way—no church ceremony that would require real names or histories they couldn't safely provide, but binding nonetheless. Margarethe had worn wildflowers in her hair. Maria had witnessed. The elderly monks had blessed them with rituals that predated Rome.

A year of waking beside her. A year of building rather than running. A year of choosing partnership not because the quest demanded it but because they wanted it.

"Helena's spring letter arrived," Margarethe said, sitting beside him on the low stone wall that bordered the herb garden. She handed him the sealed package, but didn't let go of his hand when he reached for it.

Thomas felt the familiar calluses from weapons work, the newer ones from garden tools. Felt the warmth of her fingers laced through his, comfortable and certain in ways that still sometimes surprised him.

He opened the letter with one hand, read Helena's update:

*One year since your distribution:*

*Sixty-seven network members have broken away openly. More resist quietly—appearing loyal while breaking what they can. The breeding work is stumbling: too many stewards refuse to continue, too many ledgers vanish. The network endures but adapts slowly, wounded.*

*The Order has split three ways: traditionalists serving heresy prosecution, reformers seeking to purge network influence, and radicals speaking of dissolving the Order entirely. The split has ended unified pursuit.*

*Your manuscripts continue spreading. By my reckoning, two hundred copies of the complete Protocol now exist across Europe, North Africa, and the eastern Mediterranean. No purge can reach them all. Future generations will have access regardless of current authorities.*

*Most significantly: thirty-seven former network men and Gray Robes are now teaching in remote places, copying and tending small fires. Your choice to teach rather than keep striking has kindled something. People bred to serve corrupt powers are choosing to serve the founders' first intent instead.*

*I don't know if this movement will survive beyond a generation. The network is powerful. Habit and fear favor corruption. But you've created space for resistance that didn't exist before.*

*That matters. Even if we don't see full results in our lifetimes, it matters.*

*Keep teaching.*

*—Helena*

Thomas read the letter aloud to Margarethe, his voice steady as he processed the implications.

Sixty-seven who'd turned. Two hundred manuscript copies. Men who'd once hunted now hiding and teaching.

"We did something," Margarethe said quietly. "Not perfectly. Not cleanly. But enough that they can't put it back the way it was."

"And now we live," Thomas said, looking at the garden where new growth emerged from winter dormancy. "We teach. We copy. We keep it moving."

He folded the letter, set it aside, turned to face Margarethe fully. A year together had taught him to read the subtle expressions that crossed her face—the warrior's mask she wore for the world, and the softer vulnerability she showed only to him and Maria.

Right now, she looked uncertain in ways he rarely saw.

"There's something else," she said quietly. "Something I need to tell you."

Thomas felt his heart hammer. "What?"

Margarethe took his hand, placed it gently against her stomach. Still flat beneath the simple wool dress she wore for garden work. But her meaning was clear.

"I think... I'm almost certain... we're going to have a child."

Thomas stared at her, his mind racing to catch up with words he'd heard but couldn't quite process. A child. Their child. Life they'd created together in this remote place while distributing evidence of the network's corruption and teaching village children their letters.

"Are you sure?" he managed.

"Not completely," Margarethe admitted. "But sure enough. Two months without bleeding. Sickness in the mornings. The way my body feels different." She met his eyes with that fierce certainty he loved. "I'm carrying your child, Thomas. Our child. And I don't know whether to be terrified or hopeful or both."

Thomas pulled her close, felt her lean into him with the comfort of a year of shared life. His mind spun with implications—a child meant permanence, meant commitment beyond just the two of them, meant building a future in this place instead of just surviving present danger.

It meant hope. Despite the network's continued power. Despite the hunt that might still find them. Despite all the ways the world was dangerous and uncertain.

It meant they were choosing life anyway.

"Both," he said, his voice rough with emotion. "Terrified and hopeful both. But mostly..." He pulled back enough to see her face, saw the warrior woman who'd saved his life a dozen times now looking at him with vulnerable hope. "Mostly grateful. That we're alive. That we found each other. That we're building this."

Margarethe kissed him then—soft and certain, full of promise and fear and the particular courage it took to choose hope when the world kept burning.

"I told Maria this morning," she said when they pulled apart. "She started crying and hugged me so hard I thought she'd crack my ribs. Said something about forty generations of blood memory and how this feels like choosing the future instead of just preserving the past."

Thomas thought about that. Thought about forty-one generations of breeding meant to create pattern-readers who would serve the network. Thought about choosing to serve humanity instead. Thought about their child growing up in this remote Welsh monastery, learning to read and think and question, carrying forward the work they'd started.

"We'll teach them," he said quietly. "Not just letters and cipher systems. But the habit of asking why. The courage to resist when resistance matters. The wisdom to know when to shout truth and when to carry it quietly."

"And if the network finds us?" Margarethe asked. "If the Order comes?"

"Then we adapt," Thomas said, echoing Brother Cadfael's words from a year ago. "Move if we must. Teach somewhere else. Carry the work in our hands, not in one set of walls."

Maria joined them in the garden, having apparently seen them from the scriptorium window and wanting to share the news properly. Her young face—sixteen now, no longer the frightened fourteen-year-old who'd helped them gather the Protocol—showed joy and something like wonder.

"A baby," she said, sitting beside them on the wall. "A baby who'll grow up knowing the truth about the network from the beginning. Who'll learn to read and think and question. Who'll carry this forward."

"If they choose to," Thomas said. "We can teach them. But the choices they make with that teaching are theirs to determine."

"Just like Owain," Margarethe said. "Like all the students. You give them the tools. What they build with those tools is up to them."

They sat together in the spring garden as morning deepened toward afternoon. Three fugitives who'd become teachers. Who'd distributed evidence that wounded an ancient corruption. Who'd chosen building over martyrdom and were now bringing new life into a dangerous world.

Behind them, the complete Genesis Protocol existed in two hundred preserved copies spreading across Europe. Evidence of the network's betrayal spreading beyond their control.

Ahead, years of teaching and copying and hoping the work mattered.

And between them—in Margarethe's still-flat stomach—a future they'd chosen deliberately.

That afternoon, Thomas taught his largest class yet—seventeen students, ranging from five to sixteen, all learning at different speeds.

Owain was helping now—kneeling beside the youngest, guiding their hands as they scratched letters into slate while Thomas worked with the older students on words and numbers. The scritch-scratch of charcoal on slate filled the room. Someone giggled. Someone else shushed them, then giggled themselves.

Outside, spring transformed the Welsh mountains from winter dormancy into seasonal renewal. Birds sang. Sheep bleated in distant pastures. The smell of new grass came through the open window, sweet and green.

Inside, a shy girl who'd never spoken above a whisper sounded out her first word and then laughed, startled by the sound of her own voice.

The Genesis Protocol was out in the world now, hidden in other hands.

The rebellion they'd started was distributed, spreading in ways they couldn't track or control.

The network endured but stumbled, wounded in ways that might heal or might compound depending on choices dozens of people made in places Thomas would never see.

Here, in this room, all he could do was teach.

One child at a time.

One letter at a time.

One small rebellion at a time.

Building where the network sought to bind.

Preserving where they sought to bury.

Teaching where they preferred ignorance.

It wasn't the dramatic ending they'd imagined when the quest began.

But it was the ending that was actually possible.

And maybe—maybe—that was enough.

Maybe it was everything.

# EPILOGUE - THE PATTERN CONTINUES

## Medieval Timeline - Eighteen Months Later

### Small Village in the Welsh Mountains, Spring 1350

The cottage smelled of fresh bread and spring warmth after a hard winter. Their son—born the previous autumn in the monastery's infirmary with Maria and the elderly monks in attendance—slept in his cradle near the hearth. Wilhelm, they'd named him, after the mentor who'd started it all. Ten months old now, healthy despite the valley's isolation, already showing signs of his father's curious nature in the way his eyes tracked movement and his hands reached for everything within grasp.

Margarethe sat by the window, her hands resting on her swollen belly—six months pregnant with their second child, showing now despite layers of practical clothing for mountain farm life.

Thomas watched her from across the room where he was preparing tomorrow's lessons for the village children. Eighteen months in this remote valley—long enough for fear to loosen its grip, not long enough to forget why they'd come.

"Another letter from Helena," Margarethe said, holding the sealed parchment that had arrived with the monthly messenger—the thin thread that still tied their valley to the wider world. "Sixty-one network members have turned now. In eleven cities, breeding lists have broken. Cardinal Matteo stopped three calamity plots and may be trying to pull the network apart from within."

She read further, her expression shifting. "But the network is also hardening. Replacing turncoats faster than rebellion creates gaps. Whispering of heretical attacks to justify tighter watch. They're wounded, but they're not dead."

Thomas came to sit beside her, his hand joining hers on her belly, their second child moving beneath their palms while Wilhelm slept peacefully nearby. "We knew this wouldn't end in a season. The pages would stir people, but they wouldn't topple a two-thousand-year house overnight."

"Our children will grow up with this shadow in the world," Margarethe said quietly, her voice carrying both hope and concern. "They'll learn what the network does. And they'll have to choose what kind of person to be in its reach."

Maria entered—sixteen now, looking more like a young woman than the fourteen-year-old girl who'd fled Mainz eighteen months ago. She carried a basket of herbs from the mountain slopes, her face showing the particular peace that came from having found purpose after all she'd endured.

"I've been careful with the blood memory," she said, setting down her basket and joining them by the window. "Trying to listen without drowning. And I think... I think the founders would have wanted this. Bread. Letters. Neighbors who know more than fear."

She placed her hand on Margarethe's belly, feeling the child move beneath her palm. "This baby will carry your blood. Maybe your gifts. But blood isn't a chain unless you let it be."

Thomas thought about that—about Wilhelm sleeping in his cradle, about the second child who would be born in three months, about the students in the village who would grow up learning letters and how to ask why.

Who would have to decide whether to continue the fight or whether their generation could finally rest.

A knock came at the door—unusual, because the village messenger had already come and gone, because few travelers reached this isolated valley, because knock patterns mattered when you'd spent months being hunted.

Thomas opened the door cautiously, his hand near the knife he still kept despite months of peace.

Marcus the Physician stood on the threshold—travel-stained and thinner than when they'd last seen him in the frost-silver forest, but with the same careful posture and watchful eyes. His expression wasn't hostile. Was, if anything, uncertain.

"Thomas of Eltville," Marcus said in accented German. "The monk who distributed the Genesis Protocol."

"Marcus," Thomas said, surprise sharpening into caution. "I thought you'd stay in the shadows."

Marcus pulled out a letter bearing Helena's seal. "I tried. Then your copies reached me. Helena said you might be willing to teach me how to serve the Protocol as it was meant, not as it has become."

Thomas glanced at Margarethe, saw her tiny nod of acceptance despite the risk.

"Come in," he said, stepping aside. "We can always use more teachers."

Marcus entered, looking around the modest cottage with something like relief. "I've been searching for months. The network is hunting turncoats hard now, trying to choke the rebellion by making examples of those who choose conscience. Helena said you survived by choosing teaching over grand gestures. By building instead of burning. I'm hoping to learn that."

"What news?" Margarethe asked.

"I thought I understood what I was serving," Marcus said heavily. "Then I read the complete Protocol and realized the cruelty I'd been keeping alive. I can't undo forty years of complicity, but I can use what I know to help villages survive. If you'll teach me how."

Thomas studied the old physician—travel-stained robes, hollowed cheeks, hands trembling as though they still felt phantom manacles.

"We'll teach you," he decided. "Because we need every honest hand we can get."

Marcus's expression crumbled with relief and grief mixed together. "Thank you. I'll spend whatever time I have left trying to serve the Protocol's actual purpose rather than its corruption."

That night, Thomas sat at the window looking out at the dark valley, thinking about whether anything they'd done would truly stop what was coming—or only change its shape.

Margarethe joined him, her pregnant belly pressed against his side.

"The network will rebuild," she said quietly. "Find new men, new excuses. Most people will go along."

"I know," Thomas agreed. "So we'll keep teaching. As long as we can."

She was quiet, then added: "Small work."

"Small work," Thomas agreed, his hand on her belly feeling their second child move. "Teach the next. Leave them something better than fear."

Outside, spring night settled over the Welsh mountains. Somewhere in Europe, the network tightened its watch. Somewhere, men and women who'd turned hid pages in monastery cupboards. Somewhere, the Order hunted heretics and argued with itself.

And in one small cottage in one remote valley, three fugitives taught children to read.

Wilhelm stirred in his cradle but didn't wake. The baby in Margarethe's belly kicked beneath Thomas's palm.

Two children. A new generation beginning. New choices approaching.

Thomas returned to his lesson planning, dipped his charcoal, and wrote tomorrow's first letter on scrap vellum.

Small acts, repeated. Patterns accumulating across time.

Not bred for a purpose. Not guided by ancient conspiracies.

Just chosen. Day by day.

It was small.

But it was his.
