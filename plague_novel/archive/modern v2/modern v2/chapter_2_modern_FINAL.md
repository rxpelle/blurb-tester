# CHAPTER 2 - THE NETWORK (Modern Timeline - Present Day)

## Scene 1: Arrival in New Haven / Meeting Dr. Marsh

### Greyhound Station, New Haven - 6:47 AM

Sarah stepped off the bus into gray dawn light that made everything look washed out and temporary, like a photograph left too long in the sun.

New Haven was supposed to feel safe. It didn't.

The bus station smelled like diesel fuel and burnt coffee, harsh fluorescent lights buzzing overhead with that particular frequency that made her teeth ache. A handful of other passengers shuffled toward exits with the glazed expressions of people who'd been traveling all night—faces blank with exhaustion, eyes focused on nothing. Sarah moved with them, just another tired traveler in wrinkled clothes that smelled like fear-sweat and the recycled air of the Greyhound. Invisible.

Or so she hoped.

She'd spent the entire four-hour bus ride from Boston watching every car on I-91, cataloging which ones stayed in view too long, which ones changed lanes when she did. Paranoia, maybe. Or pattern recognition—her brain doing what it had been trained to do, finding correlations in noise, identifying threats the way it identified genetic sequences.

The November morning outside was knife-sharp and cold. Trees mostly bare, their branches like dark veins against the pale sky. Frost covered the parked cars in the lot, turning windshields opaque. Sarah's breath came out in visible clouds that dispersed and vanished. She pulled her jacket tighter—the same lab jacket she'd been wearing for eighteen hours straight, still smelling faintly of ethanol and the sharp chemical tang of her GenVault workstation.

First priority: blend in. Stop looking like a fugitive scientist.

She found a 24-hour diner two blocks from the station, neon sign flickering: *Rosie's Diner - Open 24/7*. Inside, vinyl booths worn smooth by decades of use, fluorescent lights that made everyone look slightly ill, the smell of bacon grease so thick you could taste it. A waitress with tired eyes and a name tag reading *KAREN* barely glanced at her.

"Coffee. Black," Sarah said, sliding into a corner booth that gave her a clear view of both the door and the street outside.

"Food?"

"Just coffee." Sarah's stomach was too tight for food.

Karen walked away without comment, her white sneakers squeaking against the linoleum floor.

Sarah pulled out the photocopied manuscripts Dr. Aris had given her in the parking lot outside GenVault—was that really only last night? It felt like weeks ago. She spread them carefully on the table, mindful not to let them touch where coffee might spill. The paper was rough photocopier stock, but the images were clear enough. Medieval manuscript pages with their coded marginal notes. The cipher dots stared back at her like eyes, encoding seven centuries of terrible secrets.

*Red dots: Safe locations, high-frequency protective genetic alleles.*

*Blue dots: Dangerous zones, decimated populations.*

*Yellow dots: Neutral ground, intermediary genetic zones.*

Except now Sarah understood it was so much more than that. The cipher wasn't just predicting plague survival patterns. It was tracking BLOODLINES. Specific families across generations, like breeding records for livestock.

Her coffee arrived in a chipped ceramic mug. Sarah wrapped both hands around it, grateful for the warmth seeping into her cold fingers. Took a sip. It tasted like burned dirt, but it was hot and caffeinated and that was enough.

Think. Plan. Prioritize.

1. Find Dr. Evelyn Marsh at the address Aris provided.
2. Get answers about the cipher's real purpose.
3. Figure out what GenVault was actually doing with seventy years of genetic data.
4. Survive long enough to expose it.

But first, she needed to look less conspicuous.

Sarah caught her reflection in the diner window. Even without the lab coat, she still screamed *scientist*. Practical slacks. Sensible button-down shirt. Shoes designed for standing at lab benches for twelve hours straight. And—God, she was stupid—her GenVault ID badge lanyard was still around her neck.

She yanked it off, shoved it deep in her jacket pocket. Her hands were shaking.

Better. But not enough.

Across the street, visible through the diner's plate glass window, was a Goodwill thrift store. Sarah left a five-dollar bill on the table—cash, like Aris had taught her in the car, *never use credit cards, they track everything*—and crossed the empty street.

The thrift store smelled like mothballs and fabric softener and the particular musty odor of other people's lives compressed into cardboard boxes. She moved quickly through the racks: found jeans that almost fit, a dark gray hoodie, cheap canvas sneakers that would be better for running than her sensible work shoes. Changed in the bathroom, stuffing her old clothes deep in the trash can under damp paper towels.

In the mirror, she barely recognized herself. The fluorescent lights made her look hollow-eyed, haunted. She pulled out the travel sewing kit she'd bought and used the small scissors to cut her hair shorter. Rough, uneven cuts that would have made her mother cry, but different enough. The hair fell into the sink like dark rain.

Not Dr. Sarah Chen, MIT PhD, GenVault geneticist.

Just Sarah. Nobody. Anonymous.

It would have to do.

### Residential New Haven - 9:15 AM

The address Aris had given her led to a quiet residential neighborhood. Tree-lined streets. Middle-class houses with careful lawns and basketball hoops over garage doors. The kind of place where neighbors definitely noticed strangers walking by.

Sarah walked past 27 Maple Street twice, observing the way Aris had taught her during that desperate car ride. *Look but don't stop. Notice patterns. Trust your instincts.*

Two-story colonial. White clapboard siding with black shutters. Well-maintained but not ostentatious—the house of someone who valued privacy over display. A car in the driveway—old Honda Civic, at least ten years, primer-spotted. All the curtains drawn despite it being mid-morning.

And across the street, parked three houses down: a dark sedan with tinted windows.

Sarah's pulse spiked, adrenaline flooding her system.

The sedan had been there both times she'd passed. Engine off, but someone was definitely inside—she could see the faint glow of a phone screen through the windshield's tinting. Just sitting there. Watching.

Watching Marsh's house.

Or waiting for someone to approach Marsh's house.

Sarah kept walking, forcing herself not to change pace, not to look directly at the sedan. Circled the block, her mind racing through options:

1. Approach directly through the front door. Get grabbed by whoever was in that sedan.
2. Leave. Abandon the only lead she had. Let Marsh die alone like everyone else who'd discovered the truth.
3. Find another way in.

Option three.

The neighborhood backed onto a small greenbelt—maybe fifty yards of scrubby woods separating this development from the next. Sarah found a gap in the chain-link fence two streets over and slipped through.

The woods were quiet except for the sound of dead leaves crunching under her new sneakers. Bare branches overhead, gray November sky visible through the skeletal canopy. She moved as carefully as she could, remembering childhood camping trips with her father. *Walk heel-to-toe like you're stalking deer. Test your weight before committing. Don't snap branches—they sound like gunshots in quiet woods.*

She counted houses through the trees. One. Two. Three. That should be Marsh's—yes, there was the white colonial, approached from the back where the sedan couldn't see.

The back door was visible. No watchers at this angle.

Sarah moved to the edge of the tree line. Waited in the cold, listening. Watching for movement, for anything that felt wrong.

Nothing. Just November wind rattling dead branches.

She crossed the backyard quickly, expecting shouts or alarms at any moment. Reached the back door—solid wood, multiple locks visible. Knocked softly.

Silence.

She knocked again, a little louder.

A voice from inside, sharp and afraid and absolutely certain: "Go away or I'll call the police."

Sarah pressed close to the door, keeping her voice low. "Dr. Marsh? My name is Sarah Chen. Dr. Otto Aris sent me. He said to tell you it's about the Aethelred manuscripts."

Long silence. Then, suspicious: "Otto's dead. Has been for years."

"No, he's alive. He's been hiding, but he's alive. He helped me escape from GenVault last night—picked me up after I crawled through the ventilation system." Sarah's voice was shaking now, exhaustion and fear catching up with her. "Please. They're hunting me. Everyone who knows about this is dead or hiding or compromised. You're the only person who can help me understand what's happening."

More silence. Sarah could feel someone on the other side of the door, listening. Weighing her words. Deciding whether to trust or to call the police or to stay locked in paranoid safety.

Finally, locks began to click. One. Two. Three. Four. The sound of a chain sliding free.

The door opened a crack.

A face peered out—woman, probably seventy, with wild gray hair that looked like it hadn't seen a brush in days. Eyes sharp and bright behind wire-rimmed glasses, the kind of intelligence that made Sarah think of birds of prey. Assessing. Calculating.

"You're Chinese," the woman said. Not a question.

"Chinese-American. My name is Sarah Chen."

"Chen." The woman's eyes narrowed behind her glasses. "Your family from Guangdong province?"

Sarah blinked, thrown. "How did you—"

"Because the cipher tracked eleven specific families from Guangdong. Marked them since the 1300s for particular genetic markers—pattern recognition, spatial memory, eidetic traits." Dr. Marsh opened the door wider, just enough. "Get inside. Quickly. Before they see you."

Sarah stepped through into a dim kitchen that smelled like old coffee and dust.

Marsh bolted the door behind her immediately. All four locks, plus the chain. Then she turned and studied Sarah with an intensity that made the GenVault security interrogation feel gentle.

Up close, Dr. Evelyn Marsh looked like she'd been living underground for forty years. Thin to the point of gaunt, her hands stained with ink and what might have been coffee or might have been something else. She wore layers—cardigan over sweater over thermal shirt, like she was always cold. Her hair was more white than gray, pulled back in a messy bun secured with what looked like a pencil.

The house smelled like old books and paranoia and something medicinal Sarah couldn't quite place.

"Dr. Aris really sent you?" Marsh asked, still studying Sarah like a specimen under a microscope.

"Last night. Maybe two in the morning. He picked me up in the GenVault parking lot after I escaped through the bathroom vent." Sarah was shaking now, the adrenaline wearing off. "He gave me photocopied manuscripts. Told me about the cipher. Said you were the only person who had the complete picture."

"The vents." Marsh laughed—sharp, bitter, humorless. "That's new. Creative. In my day we used the loading docks and bribed the security guards." She walked deeper into the house. "How is Otto? Still paranoid? Still jumping at shadows?"

"Terrified. Said they've been watching him for twenty years."

"They have been. They watch everyone who figures it out. The ones they don't kill outright, they watch. Waiting for us to slip up. Waiting for us to lead them to others." Marsh gestured down a hallway lined with bookshelves. "Come on. But don't touch anything without asking. And if I tell you to run, you run immediately. No questions. Understood?"

"Understood."

Sarah followed Marsh through the hallway. The bookshelves were crammed with an odd mixture—medieval history next to population genetics textbooks, statistical analysis monographs beside ancient language grammars. Into a study that looked like the aftermath of an explosion in a research library.

Papers covered every surface. Manuscripts—some photocopied on yellowing paper, some clearly original vellum in archival sleeves. A massive cork board dominated one wall, covered with photographs and documents connected by colored string like a detective's murder board. Maps of Europe with colored pins. Genetic charts. Population data graphs. Mathematical equations scrawled in the margins of printouts.

It was simultaneously beautiful and terrifying—the physical manifestation of obsession.

"Welcome to forty years of my life," Marsh said, gesturing at the controlled chaos. "Tea? You look like you could use some."

"I... yes?" Sarah didn't know what else to say.

Marsh disappeared into the kitchen. Sarah heard a kettle being filled, the click and hiss of a gas stove igniting.

She stood in the study, trying to process the sheer volume of information. The cork board alone was overwhelming—medieval manuscript images connected by red string to modern genetic data printouts, population migration maps, genealogical charts going back centuries, news clippings about disease outbreaks and fertility clinic scandals.

And there, in the center of the web of strings, a photograph of a medieval manuscript page. The marginal dots clearly visible. The same cipher pattern Sarah had found in GenVault's restricted database.

Marsh returned with two chipped mugs. The tea smelled like chamomile and something herbal and slightly medicinal that Sarah couldn't identify.

"Sit," Marsh commanded, gesturing to a chair buried under stacks of paper.

Sarah carefully moved the papers to the desk—they appeared to be photocopies of 14th-century merchant ledgers—and sat.

Marsh remained standing, studying Sarah over the rim of her mug. "Otto said you found the correlation. The impossible one. Medieval cipher predicting modern genetic patterns."

"Ninety-four percent accuracy," Sarah confirmed. "CCR5-Delta32 population distribution mapping perfectly onto 14th-century manuscript marginal notations. I ran it three times thinking it had to be an error in my code."

"And you realized that's impossible."

"Unless the cipher-makers had knowledge they couldn't possibly have possessed." Sarah set her untouched tea down. Her hands were still shaking. "Dr. Marsh, what is the Aethelred Cipher?"

Marsh smiled—sharp, humorless, the smile of someone who'd learned terrible truths. "You're asking the wrong question, Dr. Chen. The right question is: What were the cipher-makers really doing?"

Sarah leaned forward. "Then what were they doing?"

"Breeding humans like livestock." Marsh set her own tea down and pulled out a manuscript from a protective sleeve. Original vellum, beautifully preserved. "This is from 1338. Nine years before the Black Death reached Europe. Look at the marginal notations."

Sarah looked. Red and blue dots, arranged in geometric patterns around Latin text discussing agricultural yields and trade routes.

"Now look at THIS." Marsh pulled out a modern genetic chart—printed on regular paper, the kind that came from a color printer. Family trees with genetic markers highlighted in fluorescent yellow. "This is the Chen bloodline. Your bloodline, Sarah. Going back twelve generations to Guangdong province, China."

Sarah stared. Her family name was there. Dates of births and deaths. Locations. And highlighted: genetic markers for CCR5-Delta32, for pattern recognition cognitive traits, for spatial memory enhancement, for—

"How did you get this?" Sarah's voice was barely a whisper.

"I stole it. In 1982, when I realized what the project was really about." Marsh carefully overlaid the medieval manuscript on top of the modern chart, lining up specific points. "Watch."

The red dots in the medieval manuscript aligned perfectly—impossibly perfectly—with the genetic markers highlighted in Sarah's family tree.

"That's not possible," Sarah breathed.

"Exactly. It's completely impossible for 14th-century monks to have knowledge about 21st-century genetics. About specific gene frequencies in populations that wouldn't exist for another six hundred years." Marsh pulled out more documents, spreading them across the desk. "Unless... they were tracking the same thing across time. Bloodlines. Inheritance patterns. Phenotypic expressions."

"But they didn't have the technology. They didn't even understand genetics—"

"They didn't need modern technology. They had something better: TIME." Marsh spread more manuscripts across every available surface. "The cipher network started—as far as I can trace back—around 400 CE. Right after Rome fell. A group of scholars decided that knowledge was too precious to lose during the collapse. So they created a system to preserve it across generations."

Sarah's mind was racing, making connections. "A secret network of knowledge preservation."

"Yes. Initially. But it evolved over centuries." Marsh pointed to a complex genealogical chart. "They realized that knowledge alone wasn't enough. You needed the RIGHT PEOPLE to understand it, to apply it, to pass it on correctly. So they started tracking bloodlines. Identifying families with desirable traits—intelligence, memory, pattern recognition, teaching ability. Then they began... guiding those bloodlines."

"Guiding how?"

"Arranged marriages. Directed migration. Documented births and deaths. Protected certain families during conflicts. Eliminated competing bloodlines." Marsh's voice was flat, clinical. "Proto-eugenics, Dr. Chen. In the 14th century, they refined it even further. The Black Death gave them an unprecedented opportunity—a massive population die-off that they could actively guide."

Sarah felt sick. The tea smell was suddenly too strong, too sweet. "They let the plague kill people. Selectively."

"Worse than that. Much worse." Marsh pulled out another document—modern trade route maps overlaid with historical plague spread patterns. "I think they CAUSED it. Released it deliberately. Look at this: the first documented plague cases appeared in trading posts controlled by families that appear in the cipher network. And it spread along routes they controlled. The timing is too perfect. The geographic distribution too precise."

"That's..." Sarah couldn't finish the thought. It was too monstrous. Too calculated. Too horrific to fully comprehend.

"And it worked," Marsh continued relentlessly, her voice like a scalpel cutting away comforting illusions. "After the plague burned through Europe, the population was smaller but genetically concentrated for very specific traits. Disease resistance. Intelligence markers. Specific metabolic adaptations. The cipher network had successfully BRED a new type of human population."

Sarah stood abruptly, needing to move. Started pacing. Her untouched tea sat on the desk, going cold. "And GenVault found this? The complete cipher?"

"Not just GenVault." Marsh pulled out a printed list. Sarah recognized some of the company names: GenVault, BioMerge International, Helix Dynamics, Prometheus Bio-Solutions. "Seven major corporations, all descended from the original seven cipher families. They've been continuing the work for seventy years, using modern technology to do what the medieval network did with patience and time."

"Continuing how? What are they doing?"

"Fertility treatments that aren't really about fertility—they're about selecting specific embryos. Genetic screening programs that identify and track bloodlines. Targeted vaccine distribution that..." Marsh hesitated. "That ensures certain populations survive while others don't."

Sarah's hands were shaking so badly she had to grip the edge of the desk. "To what end? What's the goal?"

"That," Marsh said quietly, "is what I've been trying to understand for forty years. What is the ENDGAME? What are they building toward?"

Sarah looked at the cork board again, really studied it this time. Saw patterns she'd missed on first glance. Migration routes showing population movements over decades. Climate data. Disease outbreak predictions. Genetic marker concentrations increasing in specific geographic regions while decreasing in others.

"They're preparing for something," Sarah said slowly, the pieces clicking together. "Another collapse event. Another plague or war or disaster. Something that will kill most of humanity. And they're positioning the 'right' people—the people with the right genetic markers—to survive it."

Marsh nodded grimly. "That's my theory. And based on the acceleration in their activities over the past five years, I think it's coming soon."

"How soon?"

"Six months. Maybe less." Marsh pulled out recent news clippings—disease outbreaks, climate disasters, political instabilities, resource conflicts. "Look at the pattern. It's accelerating. Exactly like it did before Rome fell. Exactly like the Bronze Age collapse. The cipher network has successfully predicted every major civilizational collapse for two thousand years. And they've survived every single one by carefully MANAGING who lives through the catastrophe."

Sarah's legs felt weak. She sat back down hard. "We have to stop them."

"With what evidence?" Marsh's laugh was bitter, defeated. "Photocopied manuscripts? Conspiracy theories? Correlation studies?" She gestured at the room full of research. "Otto tried to publish his findings twenty years ago. GenVault acquired his entire lab six months later and buried everything. I tried to go public in 1982 with stolen documents. Car accident three weeks later. Three broken ribs, a punctured lung, and a very clear message delivered in my hospital room: shut up or next time you die."

"But I have data from GenVault's servers. My cousin David is hacking their backup systems right now—"

"Data they can easily claim is fabricated. Taken out of context. The work of a disturbed employee." Marsh shook her head. "No. We need something they can't dismiss. We need the SOURCE. The original cipher keys. The complete Genesis Protocol that proves not just what they DID but WHY. Their ultimate intent."

"What's the Genesis Protocol?"

"The master plan. The complete blueprint for guiding human evolution through collapse events. Managing civilizational cycles." Marsh pointed to a notation on one of the manuscripts, written in faded medieval Latin. *Seven keys unlock the Genesis truth.* "According to the fragments I've decoded, it's encoded in seven separate pieces, each originally held by one of the founding cipher families. Get all seven keys, decode the complete protocol, and you can prove everything—the full scope, the original intent, the endgame."

"Where are the keys?"

"That's the problem I've never solved." Marsh pulled out her own notes—decades of research compressed into cramped handwriting. "I've found references to three. But their locations are encoded in ways I never fully cracked."

Before Sarah could respond, there was a crash from the front of the house. Glass breaking. Wood splintering.

Both women froze.

Marsh's face went pale. "Basement. Right now."

"What—"

"GO!"

## Scene 2: The Source Documents / The Attack

Sarah ran.

Marsh grabbed a stack of manuscripts from the desk—originals, irreplaceable—and shoved them into Sarah's arms. "Basement door, behind the bookshelf. There's a tunnel. I built it in the '80s after the car accident. Leads to the neighbor's property two houses down."

Footsteps upstairs. Multiple sets. Professional. Quiet. Moving through the house with practiced efficiency.

"What about you?" Sarah whispered urgently.

"I'm seventy-three years old. I can't run. You can." Marsh pushed her toward a bookshelf that looked built-in but swung aside on hidden hinges. "Make this matter, Sarah. Make my life's work mean something. Don't let them bury the truth again."

"Dr. Marsh, I can't just—"

"GO!" Marsh's voice was sharp, commanding, final. "They want you alive. Me, they'll just kill. So go. Now. Before they realize there's two of us."

Sarah went.

Down narrow stairs into a basement that was exactly what she'd expected from a paranoid academic: climate-controlled, meticulously organized, and full of things that were definitely stolen. Manuscripts in archival sleeves. Original vellum from the 14th century. Modern corporate files that Marsh absolutely shouldn't have had access to. Genetic databases printed on stacks of paper. Government documents stamped CLASSIFIED.

Forty years of evidence. Forty years of obsession. Forty years of one woman's desperate attempt to expose the truth.

Above her, she heard voices filtering down through the floorboards:

"—check the basement—"

"—Dr. Marsh, we just want to talk, there's no need for—"

Then Marsh's voice, loud and defiant: "You're forty years too late! The information is already out! I've sent copies to everyone!"

A lie. Buying Sarah time.

Sarah found the tunnel entrance exactly where Marsh said it would be—behind a false panel in the wall, obvious once you knew to look for it but invisible otherwise. She grabbed what she could carry: the manuscripts Marsh had given her, a laptop from the desk, a camera with what looked like hundreds of photographs on the memory card.

Behind her, the voices grew louder. Angrier.

"—WHERE IS SHE—"

"—Chen was seen approaching this house—"

"—find the basement access—"

Then a sound that might have been a gunshot. Or might have been something breaking. Sarah couldn't tell and couldn't stop to find out.

She crawled into the tunnel, pulling the panel closed behind her. Complete darkness. The smell of damp earth and age. The walls were rough concrete, scraping her elbows as she crawled. The manuscripts pressed against her chest, precious and fragile.

Behind her, muffled through walls and earth, she heard shouting. Then another sound that was definitely a gunshot.

Sarah froze, tears burning her eyes. *Make this matter. Make my life's work mean something.*

She crawled faster. Hands scraping against concrete, knees bruising, the laptop banging against her hip. The tunnel sloped upward, turned, opened into another finished basement.

Someone else's house. Empty. Quiet.

Sarah climbed out, closed the tunnel entrance carefully. Found stairs. Emerged into a kitchen that smelled like bacon and coffee—normal morning smells in a normal house where normal people lived normal lives completely unaware that next door, an old woman had just died protecting terrible secrets.

She grabbed a coat from a hook by the door—windbreaker, not hers, didn't matter—and walked out the front door like she belonged there.

Outside, sirens were wailing. Police or ambulance, converging on Marsh's address. Sarah forced herself to walk, not run. Just a woman out for a morning walk. Nobody important. Nobody suspicious.

Two blocks away, she ducked into an alley between a dry cleaner and a pizza place. Let herself shake, let the tears come, let the reality of what just happened crash over her.

Marsh was dead. Or captured. Either way, gone.

Sarah looked at the manuscripts clutched in her arms. The laptop. The camera. This was all that remained of forty years of research. Of one woman's desperate, lonely crusade against a conspiracy so vast it had survived for seven centuries.

She found a quiet corner behind a dumpster that smelled like old grease and sat down on the cold pavement. Opened the laptop Marsh had grabbed. Battery at 43%. No password protection—Marsh had prepared for this scenario, had prepared for someone to take this if she died.

The desktop was organized with obsessive precision. Folders labeled: BLOODLINES. CORPORATIONS. CIPHER KEYS. GENESIS PROTOCOL. EVIDENCE. CONTACTS.

Sarah opened the Genesis Protocol folder with trembling hands.

Inside were scans of manuscript pages. Medieval Latin, dense theological text. And in the margins, the cipher dots in their coded patterns. But also extensive notes—Marsh's notes, decades of translation work and analysis.

Sarah read:

*Genesis Protocol - Master Summary (as far as I've decoded):*

*1. Civilization collapse is CYCLICAL. Approximately every 600-800 years, major civilizations undergo catastrophic failure. Bronze Age collapse (1200 BCE), Roman collapse (400 CE), Medieval collapse (1347 CE). Next predicted collapse: 2025-2050.*

*2. The cipher network was created to MANAGE these collapses, not prevent them. Goal: Ensure specific bloodlines carrying specific genetic traits survive with accumulated knowledge intact.*

*3. Method: Track families across generations. Guide reproduction through arranged marriages. Direct migration patterns. Ensure selective survival during crisis events. Document everything.*

*4. The Black Death was NOT natural. ENGINEERED release along network-controlled trade routes. Purpose: Cull European population while concentrating beneficial genetic alleles. Estimated 30-40% population reduction, 200%+ increase in target trait frequencies.*

*5. Modern phase (1950-present): Seven corporations descended from original cipher families. Methods: Fertility treatments select specific embryos. Vaccine programs ensure targeted survival. Genetic screening tracks bloodlines globally. Population movements guided through economic incentives.*

*6. ENDGAME (partially decoded): Trigger next collapse event when sufficient global population carries target genetic markers. Estimated critical mass threshold: 15% of global population. Current estimate (2023): 12.8%. Approaching trigger point.*

*7. Post-collapse survival: Remaining population will be genetically optimized for [UNKNOWN - encoding I cannot crack]. References to "pattern completion" and "activation sequence" but meaning unclear.*

*8. CRITICAL WARNING: Evidence suggests original protocol intent was DIFFERENT from current implementation. Possible corruption or deliberate alteration of knowledge over generations. Original creators may have intended something else entirely.*

Sarah's hands shook so hard she nearly dropped the laptop.

They were at 12.8% global coverage. Moving toward 15%. And when they hit that threshold, they were going to trigger a collapse event that would kill billions.

She opened the CIPHER KEYS folder. Marsh's research showed references to three:

*Key 1: Iron. Last documented location: Mainz, Germany, 1347. Described as "the eye that sees." Current status: UNKNOWN.*

*Key 2: Silver. Last documented: Strasbourg, France, 1349. Described as "the hand that guides." Current status: UNKNOWN.*

*Key 3: Living Key. Description: "The flesh made cipher, memory in blood." Last mentioned: Prague, 1355. Nature unclear - possibly a person rather than object. Status: UNKNOWN.*

*Keys 4-7: No reliable historical references found. May be deliberately obscured in later cipher versions or destroyed.*

Sarah pulled out Marsh's camera and spent the next hour photographing everything. Every document, every note, every piece of evidence.

She needed help. She needed David.

Sarah pulled out the burner phone Aris had given her—cheap prepaid, untraceable. Dialed the number David had made her memorize.

Four rings. Then: "Hello? Who's calling from this number?"

"David, it's me. Don't say my name."

"Jesus Christ, where ARE you? The news is saying—wait, you know what, don't tell me where you are. Are you okay?"

"Physically, yes. Everything else, no." Sarah's voice cracked. "What are they saying on the news?"

"GenVault announced an internal investigation into stolen research data. Corporate espionage. They're being vague about details but—" He lowered his voice. "Sarah, they're saying someone stole proprietary genetic databases worth hundreds of millions. They're getting federal law enforcement involved. FBI. The whole thing."

Sarah closed her eyes. "Did you get into their backup servers? Did you get my research files?"

"I'm working on it. Their security is... it's really sophisticated. Multiple layers. I need maybe six more hours, possibly twelve."

"You have four hours maximum," Sarah said urgently. "After that, they'll realize someone's in the system and purge everything. It'll be gone forever."

"I'm going as fast as I can—"

"I know. I know you are." Sarah looked at the laptop full of Marsh's research. "David, I'm going to send you something through an encrypted dead drop. Historical documents that prove GenVault isn't just doing corporate espionage prevention. They're continuing a seventy-year genetic engineering program that goes back seven centuries."

Silence on the line. Then: "Sarah, that sounds completely insane."

"I know how it sounds. But it's true. Dr. Evelyn Marsh spent forty years documenting it. And now she's dead because of it."

"Fuck." More silence. "Okay. Okay. Send me everything you have. When I pull your data from GenVault, I'll cross-reference it with the historical stuff. If we can show a pattern connecting medieval manuscripts to modern genetic databases—that's harder to dismiss as fabrication."

"Thank you," Sarah whispered. "Thank you for believing me."

"Thank you for trusting me with this." Keyboard sounds. "Where are you going?"

"Somewhere remote. Somewhere I can work without being found." She looked at the pile of manuscripts. "I need to write this story properly. Document everything. Medieval cipher, modern corporations, the planned collapse, all of it. Then we release everything at once."

"That's going to take days."

"I know."

"They're going to be hunting you."

"I know that too."

David was quiet for a long moment. "Aunt Lin always said you were the most stubborn person in the family. I'm starting to see what she meant."

Sarah almost smiled. "Tell her I love her. And David?"

"Yeah?"

"If something happens to me—if they catch me before I finish the exposé—"

"I'll make sure the story gets out anyway. I promise."

"Thank you."

She hung up.

The morning sun was burning through the clouds now, making everything sharp and clear and painfully normal. Somewhere nearby, people were living ordinary lives. Going to work. Making breakfast. Complaining about traffic. Completely unaware.

Sarah gathered everything, stood up on shaky legs, and started walking.

She had work to do.

## Scene 3: David's Call / The Trap

### Four Days Later - Vermont Cabin

The cabin smelled like instant coffee, unwashed human, and single-minded obsession.

Sarah hadn't left in ninety-six hours. Hadn't showered. Had barely eaten except for protein bars and whatever she could make with the cabin's limited kitchen supplies. She'd written 40,000 words, cross-referencing Marsh's forty years of research with David's data dumps from GenVault's servers, building what she hoped was an absolutely airtight case.

Medieval manuscripts linked directly to modern genetic databases through mathematical analysis. Historical collapse events mapped onto current crisis indicators with statistical precision. Fertility clinic records showing deliberate genetic selection across three generations. Corporate documents proving coordination between seven supposedly independent companies.

Everything.

Her hands shook from too much caffeine and too little sleep, but the document was DONE.

Sarah read through it one final time, checking every citation, every connection, every logical link. This wasn't just investigative journalism—it was evidence. It was scholarship. It was whistleblowing. It was her entire PhD training compressed into a weapon.

She saved it. Encrypted it with the strongest protocols she knew. Created six backup copies on separate thumb drives. Hid three of them in different locations around the cabin.

Then she picked up the burner phone and dialed David.

He answered on the first ring. "Please tell me you're done."

"I'm done." Sarah's voice was hoarse from lack of use. "Forty thousand words. Fully sourced. Every claim backed by multiple pieces of evidence. Ready to release."

"How are you holding up?"

"Tired. Paranoid. Pretty sure I'm going crazy from isolation." She rubbed her eyes. They felt like sandpaper. "How are you?"

"Same, honestly. I've been running counter-surveillance for four days straight. Pretty sure someone tried to hack my home network yesterday but I routed them through like fifty proxy servers and they gave up." He sounded wired, running on the same exhausted adrenaline Sarah felt. "But I got everything. Your GenVault files, the restricted databases, the corporate communications—all of it."

"The distribution network?"

"Set up and tested. WikiLeaks, ProPublica, The Guardian, New York Times, Washington Post, plus about two hundred independent journalists and every major whistleblowing platform. When we release, it hits everywhere simultaneously. No way to suppress it all."

"When?"

"Tonight. Midnight Eastern. That gives major outlets eight hours to review before morning news cycles start." Typing sounds. "I'm sending you the encryption keys now. Upload your document to the dead drop location, I'll package everything together, and at midnight, it all releases automatically."

Sarah's hands were shaking so badly she had to set down her coffee mug. "This is actually happening."

"This is actually happening." David's voice softened. "Sarah, you did it. You're about to expose seventy years of genetic manipulation and stop a planned genocide. You're a fucking hero."

"I'm terrified."

"Heroes usually are." More typing. "Okay, encryption keys uploading now. Get your document ready. At midnight, we change everything."

"David?"

"Yeah?"

"Thank you. For believing me when I sounded insane. For risking your career and possibly your life. For—"

"Family, right?" His voice was warm despite the fear. "Aunt Lin raised us both to do the right thing even when it's hard. This is the right thing."

"It is."

"Okay, keys sent. Upload your document and then we just—" He stopped abruptly.

"What?" Sarah's pulse spiked.

"Someone's at my door."

Every muscle in Sarah's body went rigid. "Don't answer it."

"It's 10 PM. Nobody should be here. I didn't order anything, I'm not expecting—" Sound of movement. "Checking the security camera—"

Then David's voice changed completely. Tight with fear. "Oh shit."

"What? What do you see?"

"Two men. Suits. Not police—they're not identifying themselves, not announcing, they're just—" His breathing got faster. "They're picking my lock. Sarah, they're picking my fucking lock."

"Run! Back door, fire escape, window, whatever—RUN!"

"There's already someone at the back door. I can see them on the camera. I'm trapped." Sounds of David moving fast, grabbing things. "Sarah, listen to me. I'm uploading everything RIGHT NOW. Even if they take me, it'll still release at midnight. The dead man's switch is active."

"David, no, you need to—"

"If I don't log in every twelve hours, the files auto-release anyway. So even if they—" His voice cut off.

A crash. The sound of a door being kicked in. Shouting—multiple voices, professional, commanding. David yelling: "I already uploaded it! You're too late!"

Then the line went dead.

Sarah sat in the silent Vermont cabin, phone pressed to her ear, listening to nothing.

They had David.

She stared at the phone. At the laptop. At everything she'd built over four sleepless days.

The encryption keys were on her screen. David had sent them seconds before they broke in. The document was ready. All she had to do was upload it.

In—she checked the time—ninety-four minutes, at midnight, the truth would flood across the world.

Even without David managing it. He'd said the automatic release was active.

But they had David. Her cousin. Her family. They would hurt him. Torture him for information. Maybe kill him.

Because of her. Because she'd asked for his help.

Sarah's hand hovered over the laptop keyboard.

Upload the document and condemn David to whatever GenVault would do to extract information.

Or don't upload it and let seventy years of conspiracy roll toward its planned genocide.

There was a knock at the cabin door.

Sarah froze.

Another knock. Calm. Patient. Then a woman's voice, professional and controlled: "Dr. Chen. I know you're in there. I'm not here to hurt you. I just want to have a conversation."

Sarah didn't move. Didn't breathe.

"My name is Dr. Catherine Wells. I'm on the Board of Directors at GenVault Corporation." A pause. "I'm also a direct descendant of one of the original cipher families. I know exactly what you've discovered. And I know exactly what you're about to do."

Sarah's hands moved to the laptop. Started the upload process.

The progress bar appeared: 0%... 1%... 2%...

"Dr. Chen, we have your cousin David in custody. He's currently unharmed. But that status is... conditional." Wells's voice remained calm, reasonable. "Let me inside so we can discuss your options. You release that document, David dies. You talk to me, he lives. It's really very simple."

3%... 4%... 5%...

Sarah looked at the door. At the progress bar. At the impossible choice.

*Make this matter. Make my life's work mean something.* Marsh's last words.

*The others who found the pattern are dead.* Aris's warning.

The 14.7% of global population carrying the genetic markers. Six months until they hit the threshold.

*You're a hero.* David's voice on the phone, believing in her.

8%... 9%... 10%...

"Dr. Chen, this is your last opportunity for a reasonable conversation," Wells continued. "I should mention—you've already been compromised. Your document contains code I helped design. The moment it's distributed, every journalist who downloads it will receive a version subtly altered to make you appear to be a conspiracy theorist. A disturbed scientist fabricating evidence. It won't expose anything except your own mental instability."

Sarah's hands froze over the keyboard.

No. That couldn't be true. David had been careful. He'd checked everything—

But what if Wells wasn't bluffing?

15%... 16%... 17%...

"We've been preparing for this exact scenario for seventy years, Dr. Chen. You're the twenty-third person to try to expose us. The twenty-third. And every single one before you either joined our program or was successfully discredited. Do you really think you're smarter than all of them?"

20%... 21%... 22%...

Sarah's mind raced. Was the document actually compromised? Had they somehow infected it?

Or was this just psychological warfare? Trying to make her stop the upload through fear and doubt?

She looked at the progress bar. At the door. At the phone where David had last spoken to her.

*I already uploaded it! You're too late!*

Had he actually uploaded it? Or had they stopped him in time and this was part of the lie?

25%... 26%... 27%...

Sarah made her choice.

She grabbed the laptop and ran to the bathroom. Locked the door—flimsy barrier but it might buy her thirty seconds. That was all she needed.

Just needed the upload to finish.

28%... 30%... 35%...

Behind her, the cabin's front door crashed open with the sound of splintering wood.

"Dr. Chen!" Wells's voice, louder now, no longer patient. "You're making this significantly more difficult than necessary."

40%... 45%... 50%...

Footsteps in the cabin. Multiple people. Searching room by room. Professional. Thorough.

60%... 65%...

The bathroom door handle rattled. "Dr. Chen. It's over. Come out and we can discuss this rationally."

70%... 75%...

Sarah pressed her back against the door, watching the progress bar crawl forward. Come on. Come on.

"You've won," she called through the door, making her voice shake. Sound defeated. "Just... promise you won't hurt David. Please."

"Open the door and we can negotiate his release."

80%... 85%...

"I'm scared."

"I understand your fear. But we're not here to harm you. We're here to help you understand the bigger picture." Wells's voice was gentle now, persuasive, the voice of a patient teacher. "The collapse is coming whether you like it or not, Dr. Chen. It's already in motion. The only question is whether you're positioned to survive it. To be part of rebuilding afterward."

90%... 95%...

"What happens to David if I cooperate?"

"He goes home. Returns to his normal life. This all becomes a bad dream that fades with time."

99%...

Sarah took a deep breath.

100%. Upload complete.

She opened the door.

Dr. Catherine Wells stood there, exactly as Sarah had imagined her. Sixty-something, professionally dressed in an expensive blazer, gray hair styled perfectly despite the late hour. Eyes intelligent and utterly cold. Behind her, two large men in dark suits—private security, not police.

Wells smiled. "Smart decision. Now, let's have that conversation about your future."

Sarah handed over the laptop. "The document is already uploaded. In—" she checked her watch, "—eighty-seven minutes, it auto-releases to two hundred journalists worldwide. David set up a dead man's switch. Even if you kill both of us, the truth comes out at midnight."

Wells's smile didn't falter. She took the laptop, opened it, checked the upload confirmation.

Her expression didn't change at all.

"Impressive," she said finally. "You actually did it. Against significant odds." She closed the laptop with a quiet click. "But you've made one critical strategic error, Dr. Chen."

"What's that?"

"You're assuming the journalists will actually publish it." Wells gestured to her security team. "Take her. Gently. She's too valuable to damage."

The men moved forward.

Sarah backed up. "What do you mean they won't publish?"

"Because we've spent seventy years preparing for exactly this scenario." Wells's smile was sad now, almost sympathetic. "We have board members at every major publication. Editors who owe us favors. Lawyers who can tie material up in litigation for decades. And most importantly, we have the resources to make any journalist who tries to publish this story unemployable within their industry."

Sarah's world collapsed.

They'd thought of everything. Accounted for every variable.

The security guards grabbed her arms—not violent, but absolutely firm.

Wells stepped closer. "I meant what I said earlier. You're brilliant, Dr. Chen. Truly exceptional. The kind of mind that's been carefully cultivated over thirty generations. You'd make an outstanding addition to our program. In six months, when the collapse event begins, you could be positioned to not just survive, but to help guide what comes after. To be part of the next phase of human evolution."

"And if I refuse?"

"Then you become another cautionary tale. Another conspiracy theorist who stole corporate data and descended into paranoid delusions." Wells nodded to the guards. "Take her to the northern facility. We'll continue our conversation there in a more comfortable setting."

They dragged Sarah toward the door.

She'd failed.

Failed Marsh. Failed David. Failed Aris. Failed everyone who'd died trying to stop this.

The truth was uploaded, but it would never see daylight.

Wells had won.

The cipher network had won.

They always won.

They'd been winning for seven hundred years.

Sarah was shoved into a black van waiting outside the cabin. Hands zip-tied behind her back. Door slamming shut.

Through the tinted window, she watched the Vermont woods disappear into darkness.

And somewhere, a clock ticked toward midnight.

When truth would try to break free.

And fail.

Again.

Just like it always had.
