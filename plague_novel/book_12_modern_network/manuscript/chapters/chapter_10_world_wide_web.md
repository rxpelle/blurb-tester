# Chapter 10: The World Wide Web (1989-1990)

## Part I: The Proposal

### CERN, Geneva — March 1989

Tim Berners-Lee sat in his office at the European Organization for Nuclear Research and stared at the proposal he'd just finished typing:

**Information Management: A Proposal**

The document described a system for linking documents across the global network of computers—a "web" of information nodes connected through hypertext, accessible to anyone with a computer and network connection.

Universal. Distributed. Uncontrollable.

His supervisor, Mike Sendall, scrawled two words across the top of the proposal: **Vague but exciting.**

What neither Berners-Lee nor Sendall realized was that two observers had been tracking this development for months—one from GenVault's technology monitoring division, one from the defensive network's digital infrastructure team.

Both understood immediately: the web would change everything.

## Part II: The Genome

James Watson stood before the assembled press and announced the launch of the Human Genome Project—the most ambitious biological research program in history.

"We will map every gene in the human genome. All three billion base pairs. Complete genetic blueprint of humanity. Fifteen years, three billion dollars. By 2005, we'll understand human genetics at molecular precision."

The journalists took notes. The scientists applauded. The future of genetic medicine had officially begun.

In the back of the auditorium, two observers watched with opposite reactions.

Dr. James Chen from GenVault felt quiet satisfaction. This was what the Order had been building toward for ninety years. Complete genetic maps would enable designer genetics within a decade. The three-thousand-year optimization program approaching fulfillment.

Dr. Sarah Chen from the defensive network felt blood-memory activate with crushing intensity—not historical knowledge now, but forward-projecting pattern recognition showing her exactly when this concentration would collapse.

She gripped the bronze key in her pocket. Today. The Pattern Eye was transferring today.

She needed to get to New York immediately.

## Part III: The Launch

### CERN, Geneva — August 6, 1991

Tim Berners-Lee posted the first publicly accessible web page to the alt.hypertext newsgroup:

**WorldWideWeb: Summary**

*The WorldWideWeb (W3) is a wide-area hypermedia information retrieval initiative aiming to give universal access to a large universe of documents.*

Universal access. No gatekeepers. No central control.

The web was public.

Within months, the first websites appeared. Within a year, web browsers proliferated. Within five years, millions of people would have access to information that previously required institutional permission to obtain.

Sarah Chen posted defensive network content within the first week:

- Complete history of eugenics movement (ERO through GenVault)
