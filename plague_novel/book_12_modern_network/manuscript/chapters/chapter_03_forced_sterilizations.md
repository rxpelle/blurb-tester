## Lynchburg, Virginia — September 1924

Carrie Buck sat in the waiting room of the Virginia Colony for Epileptics and Feebleminded and tried not to cry.

She was eighteen years old. They'd taken her daughter—little Vivian, seven months old—and put her in foster care. Said Carrie was an unfit mother. Said she was feeble-minded, just like her mother Emma. Said the baby showed signs of deficiency too.

It was all lies.

Carrie had been raped by her foster family's nephew. When she got pregnant, they'd had her committed to the Colony to hide the shame. Tested her with questions she couldn't answer—what's the capital of Pennsylvania, what does "apple" rhyme with, name the presidents in order—and when she failed, declared her mentally defective.

Now they wanted to sterilize her.

"Miss Buck?" A nurse appeared in the doorway. "Dr. Priddy will see you now."

Carrie followed her down a long hallway to an office where Dr. Albert Priddy—the Colony's superintendent—sat behind a massive oak desk reviewing paperwork.

"Sit down, Carrie." His voice was kind, almost fatherly. That somehow made it worse.

She sat.

"I've reviewed your case thoroughly," Priddy said. "Your mother Emma has been institutionalized for immorality and feeble-mindedness. You yourself have demonstrated similar deficiencies—promiscuity resulting in illegitimate birth, inability to function in normal society, test scores indicating mental age of nine years."

"I was raped," Carrie said quietly.

Priddy continued as if she hadn't spoken. "Your daughter Vivian shows early signs of the same hereditary defects. Three generations of imbeciles. The Virginia Sterilization Act of 1924 was designed specifically for cases like yours."

He pulled out a legal document. "This authorizes the Colony to perform a salpingectomy—a surgical procedure to prevent you from having more children. It's for your own good, Carrie. And for society's protection."

"I don't want—"

"The procedure has been approved by the Virginia courts. We have legal authority." Priddy's voice remained kind, gentle, implacable. "You can consent voluntarily, or we can proceed without your consent. Either way, the operation will happen."

Carrie looked at the document. At the signatures. At the legal language that reduced her to a statistical problem requiring surgical solution.

"What if I fight it?" she asked.

"Then your case will go to the Supreme Court, and you'll lose. The law is clear. The science is settled. People like you—people with hereditary defects—shouldn't be allowed to reproduce. It's a kindness, really. Preventing the suffering of children who would inherit your condition."

He pushed the consent form across the desk. "Sign, Carrie. Make this easy on everyone."

She thought about Vivian. About her mother Emma, locked away in this same institution. About the rape that no one believed happened, the shame that everyone assumed was her fault, the life that had been decided for her by men in offices reviewing charts and test scores.

She picked up the pen.

Then she set it down.

"No," Carrie said. "I want a lawyer. I want to fight this. Because I'm not feeble-minded, and neither is my daughter, and what you're doing is wrong."

Priddy's expression didn't change. "Very well. We'll schedule the Supreme Court case. But understand, Carrie—you're going to lose. Harry Laughlin from the Eugenics Record Office has already submitted expert testimony confirming your hereditary deficiency. The justices believe in eugenics. They'll rule in our favor."

"Maybe. But at least people will know what you're doing. At least there will be a record."

Priddy gathered his papers. "The procedure is scheduled for October, pending the Court's decision. I'll see you then, Miss Buck."

After Carrie left, Priddy pulled out a file labeled "Test Case—Buck v. Bell." Inside were letters from Harry Laughlin, legal briefs from the Virginia Attorney General, and a note from Charles Davenport congratulating him on selecting the perfect candidate to establish legal precedent.

Three generations of imbeciles, the file said. Perfect for demonstrating the necessity of eugenic sterilization.

Priddy smiled.

The Supreme Court case would be a formality.

By 1927, forced sterilization would be law across all forty-eight states.

"Let them document Nazi crimes. Let them prove eugenics enabled genocide. By the time Nuremberg trials happen, we'll have already rebranded. GenVault won't be connected to Nazi eugenics. It'll be a forward-looking genetic research foundation, unfortunate about the past, focused on medical progress."

Laughlin looked skeptical. "Will it work?"

"It has to," Sterling said. "The alternative is total exposure. If the defensive network proves American eugenics directly enabled the Holocaust, the entire Order apparatus in the United States gets dismantled. We lose seventy years of bloodline data, three generations of breeding records, everything we've built since 1900."

"But if we rebrand successfully—separate the methodology from the genocide—we preserve the core mission while appearing to learn from history's mistakes."

Davenport walked to the window one final time.

Thirty-three years since Mendel's rediscovery.

Twenty-three years since founding the ERO.

Six years until the Holocaust.

Twelve years until GenVault.

"Do it," he said quietly. "Prepare the transition. Close the Eugenics Record Office in 1945. Launch GenVault in 1952. Preserve the methodology through the collapse."

Sterling gathered the documents. "The Order has survived three thousand years by adapting. This is just another adaptation."

After he left, Davenport and Laughlin sat in silence.

"He's right," Laughlin finally said. "We can't stop Germany. We can't prevent the Holocaust. But we can survive it."

"At what cost?" Davenport asked.

"The cost we've always paid. Some casualties are necessary for long-term optimization."

Six million casualties, as it turned out.

But the methodology survived.

GenVault would preserve it.

The Order's three-thousand-year program would continue.

Different name.

Same mission.
