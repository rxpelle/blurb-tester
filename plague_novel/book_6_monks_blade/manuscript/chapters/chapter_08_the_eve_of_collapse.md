# CHAPTER 8: THE EVE OF COLLAPSE
## 1300-1347 CE - The Final Generation

The fourteenth century opened with prosperity and closed with death.

Europe in 1300 CE was at its medieval peak: population growing, cities expanding, universities flourishing, trade networks spanning from Iceland to India. The climate was mild—the Medieval Warm Period allowing agriculture to push further north, supporting more people, building more cathedrals, copying more manuscripts.

The defensive network had never been stronger. Or more corrupted.

And deep in the pattern—visible only to those who knew how to look—the signs of coming collapse were already present.

---

**1275 CE - Wilhelm of Mainz**

Wilhelm was born in winter, 1275 CE, in the city of Mainz on the Rhine. His father was a goldsmith, his mother a midwife. Both were network carriers, Generation 80, their marriage arranged by the Blood Registry.

They never told Wilhelm this. Children weren't informed until age fourteen—the network's rule, ensuring carriers developed naturally before being burdened with their inheritance.

But Wilhelm knew something was different about him from age six.

He saw patterns everywhere. In the way frost formed on windows (hexagonal symmetry). In the rhythm of church bells (mathematical intervals). In the illuminated manuscripts his father sometimes brought home from the cathedral (colored dots arranged in triangles, squares, circles—too precise to be random decoration).

"Why do you stare at things so long?" his mother asked when he was eight, watching him examine a page from a Gospel of Mark.

"The dots aren't random," he said. "They're organized. See? Red dots form triangles pointing toward specific words. Blue dots create squares around margin notes. Yellow dots mark circles at verse endings. It's a system."

His mother's face went pale. She glanced at his father.

"You're not supposed to see that yet," his father said quietly. "You're too young."

"See what?"

"The pattern. The encoding. You're... you have strong genetic memory, Wilhelm. Stronger than we expected."

They told him everything that day. About the network. The dot notation system. The breeding programs that had arranged their marriage to produce him. The Blood Registry predicting he'd be a powerful carrier.

"We're sorry," his mother said. "We wanted you to have a childhood before—"

"Before what?" Wilhelm interrupted. "Before I learned I was bred like a horse? That my existence is a network project?"

His parents had no answer.

Wilhelm's childhood ended at age eight. He began formal training at nine. By age fourteen, he could decode basic manuscripts using the Iron Eye layer. By age twenty, he was one of the network's most skilled analysts.

By age thirty, he suspected the network was engineering the coming collapse.

---

**1320 CE - The Climate Shifts**

The Medieval Warm Period ended around 1310 CE. Temperatures dropped. Growing seasons shortened. Harvests failed more frequently.

By 1320 CE, the pattern was undeniable: Europe was entering a cooling cycle. The Little Ice Age beginning its centuries-long grip.

For the general population, this meant harder winters, crop failures, increasing hunger. For the defensive network, it meant something else: the conditions that preceded every historical collapse.

Brother Heinrich of Erfurt—a network coordinator and climatologist, Generation 82—wrote in his encoded journal (1321 CE):

*The climate is shifting. I've analyzed tree rings, harvest records, ice cores from Alpine glaciers. The warming that supported three centuries of growth is ending.*

*This pattern matches historical collapses:*
*- Bronze Age collapse (~1200 BCE): Preceded by climate shift*
*- Roman collapse (~400 CE): Accompanied by cooling, crop failures*
*- Our current era: Same signals appearing*

*I presented these findings to the network council. Their response was... unsettling.*

*"Collapse creates opportunity for genetic optimization," they said. "Population reduction removes weak genetic lines. Survivors carry stronger resilience markers."*

*I asked: "Are you saying we should ALLOW collapse? Let millions die for 'genetic optimization'?"*

*"We don't cause collapses," they replied. "We simply recognize their role in strengthening humanity. The Genesis Protocol teaches this: catastrophe is nature's method of refining the genetic pool. We preserve knowledge so survivors rebuild stronger."*

*But their faces... they weren't horrified by coming famine. They were ANTICIPATING it.*

*I fear we've crossed a line from preservation into something darker. We're not just documenting collapses anymore. We're treating them as necessary. Even desirable.*

*God forgive us.*

Heinrich died in 1324 CE, three years after writing that entry. The network called it plague. But Wilhelm of Mainz—who was forty-nine when Heinrich died—always suspected otherwise.

Carriers who questioned the corruption had a way of dying young.

---

**1295 CE - Wilhelm's Son**

Wilhelm married in 1294 CE at age nineteen, under intense network pressure. His wife was Anna of Worms, Generation 81, a carrier with moderate genetic memory but strong fertility markers.

Their marriage was arranged by the Blood Registry. Wilhelm resented it but complied—he'd learned that outright resistance brought persecution, while strategic compliance bought freedom to conduct research.

Their son Ernst was born in 1295 CE—making Wilhelm a young father at twenty, which the network preferred for optimal genetic transmission timing.

Ernst inherited strong genetic memory through both parents' lines—Wilhelm's analytical gift and Anna's stable transmission markers. The network was pleased. The bloodline was producing exactly what the breeding programs predicted.

But Wilhelm made sure Ernst's upbringing was different. He taught his son to question. To think independently. To see the network's corruption clearly even while participating in it outwardly.

"You'll have to choose someday," Wilhelm told Ernst when the boy was twelve. "Whether to continue the pattern or resist it. I can't make that choice for you. But I can make sure you see clearly enough to choose well."

Ernst grew up watching his father conduct secret research, watching him document the network's corruption, watching him plan for a future generation that might choose differently.

When Ernst married in 1320—again, by Blood Registry arrangement—he chose Gisela of Heidelberg, Generation 82, descended from Roger Bacon. Their marriage was as much a network optimization as Wilhelm's had been.

But Ernst and Gisela had learned from Wilhelm: comply outwardly, resist inwardly, prepare the next generation.

Their son Thomas was born in 1321 CE.

Wilhelm's grandson.

The one who might finally choose to end the pattern Wilhelm could only document.

---

**1321 CE - Thomas of Eltville**

Thomas was born in spring, 1321 CE, in the village of Eltville on the Rhine, ten miles south of Mainz.

His parents were network carriers, Generation 82, married by arrangement. His mother Gisela was descended from Roger Bacon through female lines. His father Ernst carried bloodlines back to Alcuin of York.

Thomas's genetic memory was strong. Not as powerful as Wilhelm's, but substantial. He could access encoded information intuitively, feel patterns before consciously recognizing them, sometimes experience full memory downloads of information his ancestors had encoded.

But unlike Wilhelm—whose genius was analytical, mathematical, systematic—Thomas's gift was synthesis. He saw connections between disparate information. Understood how individual pieces formed complete patterns. Could hold contradictions in mind without resolving them prematurely.

The network recognized this immediately.

"Thomas has the Pattern Eye," the evaluators told his parents when he was twelve. "He can perceive the seventh layer—the synthesis understanding that emerges when all six other layers unite. This is exceptionally rare. Perhaps one carrier per generation develops it fully."

"What does that mean for him?" Gisela asked.

"It means he'll either save the network or destroy it," the evaluator said. "Carriers with Pattern Eye see not just what we preserve, but WHY we preserve it. They understand purpose. And sometimes they conclude our purpose has corrupted."

"Should we... limit his training?" Ernst asked nervously.

"No. Train him completely. Give him access to everything. Because if collapse comes—and it will—we'll need someone who can see the whole pattern. Who can make decisions about whether the network should continue."

Thomas didn't hear this conversation. But he felt its weight throughout his childhood. The way network elders watched him. The intensive training. The access to encoded manuscripts most young carriers never saw.

He was being prepared for something.

He didn't know what.

Not yet.

**1315-1317 CE - The Great Famine**

The climate's cruelty arrived ahead of schedule.

Spring 1315 CE brought endless rain. Crops rotted in fields. Wheat and rye couldn't be harvested. Seed grain for next year's planting was eaten out of desperation.

The rain continued through 1316. And 1317.

By 1318, an estimated 10-15% of Europe's population had died from starvation and associated diseases. Entire villages abandoned. Cities overwhelmed with refugees. Social order fraying.

The defensive network watched, documented, preserved.

And did nothing to help.

Wilhelm of Mainz, age forty-two when the famine ended, remembered teaching his son Ernst (then age twenty-three) about the bodies in the streets of Mainz. Remembered his mother's face when a starving woman begged for food and his parents—prosperous goldsmith family, well-fed, secure—turned her away.

"We have responsibilities," his father explained later. "Network duties. We can't risk drawing attention by being too charitable during famine. People might wonder why we have resources when others starve."

"So we let them die? To protect our cover?"

"We preserve knowledge for future generations. That's more important than individual lives in the present."

Wilhelm was forty-two. Old enough to have lived with this logic his entire life. Still young enough to rage against it. And he made sure his son Ernst understood both the network's reasoning and its horror.

Ernst passed that lesson to his son Thomas, born six years later.

The network's response to the Great Famine crystallized something in Wilhelm's family line: three generations who saw clearly that the network had become what it fought. Controllers rather than preservers. Valuers of abstract knowledge over present humanity.

The corruption was complete.

**1330 CE - The Blood Registry Reaches Its Peak**

By 1330 CE, the Blood Registry contained genealogical records for 5,000+ carriers across fourteen generations. The network could trace bloodlines back to the 540 CE consecration ceremony. Could predict genetic memory strength with mathematical precision. Could arrange marriages to optimize specific traits.

Sister Agnes of Strasbourg—a network geneticist, Generation 82—maintained the master copy in an encoded tome disguised as a Book of Hours. Her work was meticulous, scientific, horrifying.

An excerpt (decoded):

*Bloodline Analysis - Generation 83 Pairings:*

*Thomas of Eltville (b. 1321, Pattern Eye extremely strong, synthesis gift remarkable) - optimal carrier for Generation 83. Grandson of Wilhelm of Mainz (Generation 80), son of Ernst of Mainz (Generation 81).*

*Problem: Three-generation pattern of resistance in this bloodline. Wilhelm refused network directives from age thirty onward. Ernst married as required but taught his son to question. Now Thomas shows same independent thinking.*

*Bloodline value: Exceptional. Wilhelm's analytical gifts + Roger Bacon mathematics + Alcuin synthesis all concentrate in Thomas.*

*Bloodline risk: Extreme. Three generations of carriers who see the corruption clearly. Thomas has strongest Pattern Eye in two centuries—he will see EVERYTHING when given access to all seven layers.*

*Recommended action: Assign Thomas optimal marriage partner immediately (age 12 evaluation suggests fertility window opens at 16). Produce offspring before he fully develops Pattern Eye synthesis and potentially opposes network.*

*Alternate scenario: If Thomas refuses breeding programs like his grandfather, network may need to eliminate him despite genetic value. Cannot risk Pattern Eye carrier actively opposing corruption.*

Agnes died in 1333 CE, three years after writing this assessment. The network attributed her death to plague.

But the encoded addendum to her journal—found decades later—told a different story:

*I can't continue this work. I've spent twenty years optimizing bloodlines, arranging marriages, treating humans as breeding stock. I've become an architect of cruelty disguised as preservation.*

*I'm ending my life before I cause more harm. May God forgive what I've done. May future carriers recognize the horror we've become.*

*Don't continue the Blood Registry. Please. Let carriers marry for love. Let genetic memory distribute naturally. Let humanity return to the network.*

*The pattern should preserve life, not control it.*

Her plea was ignored. The Blood Registry continued. The breeding programs intensified.

The corruption deepened.

**1340 CE - Wilhelm's Research**

Wilhelm was sixty-five years old in 1340 CE. His wife Anna had died in 1335, his son Ernst was settled in Eltville with grandson Thomas (now nineteen), and Wilhelm had dedicated his later years entirely to research. Increasingly isolated from the network hierarchy after decades of questioning their methods.

But his analytical gift was undeniable. He was the best decoder alive. Could extract information from manuscripts that other carriers couldn't access. Could synthesize patterns across centuries of encoded data.

So the network tolerated his resistance. Used his skills while knowing his bloodline had already produced the next generation—Ernst and Thomas both carried his gifts forward.

Wilhelm spent 1340-1345 researching the network's history. He decoded Alcuin's 801 CE letter. Found Hrotsvitha's 1001 CE play. Discovered Heinrich's 1321 CE journal. Traced the corruption's development across five centuries.

And he found something that terrified him.

In a 1290 CE encoded medical text, buried in seven layers, was a recipe. Instructions for creating a disease. Specific combinations of symptoms, transmission vectors, mortality rates.

The text claimed it was theoretical research. "Understanding plague vectors to better prevent epidemics."

But Wilhelm recognized the real purpose: weaponized disease. A controlled collapse. Genetic optimization through engineered pandemic.

*Optimal plague characteristics for genetic refinement:*
*- Mortality rate: 30-50% (removes weak, spares strong)*
*- Transmission: efficient but not instant (allows network preservation)*
*- Symptoms: dramatic enough to create social breakdown*
*- Selectivity: targets specific genetic weaknesses we've identified*

*With such a plague, we could optimize European genetic pool within one generation. Eliminate weak bloodlines. Strengthen carrier genetics. Reset social systems to allow network reorganization.*

*This aligns with Genesis Protocol principles: catastrophe refines humanity. We shouldn't fear collapses. We should engineer them.*

Wilhelm brought his findings to network council in 1342 CE.

Their response: "This research is theoretical. We would never actually implement it."

But their eyes said otherwise.

Wilhelm left the meeting knowing the truth: the network was planning to release a plague. The Great Famine of 1315-1317 had been natural. The coming pandemic would be engineered.

He had to find someone to stop it.

Someone with Pattern Eye who could see the complete corruption and choose to end the pattern rather than continue it.

Someone like Thomas of Eltville.

**1345 CE - Thomas's Training Completed**

Thomas completed his network training in 1345 CE, age twenty-four. He'd spent twelve years learning the encoding system, practicing with all seven layers, developing his Pattern Eye gift.

He was ready to receive a key.

The network offered him the Bronze Hand—the second key, currently held by an aging carrier in Strasbourg who was preparing to pass it on.

Thomas refused.

"Why?" the council asked, confused. Refusing a key was unprecedented. Keys were the highest honor, the ultimate responsibility.

"Because I've decoded the letters," Thomas said quietly. "Alcuin's warning. Hrotsvitha's play. Heinrich's journal. I know what we've become. And I won't participate in it."

"You're refusing to serve the network that created you?"

"I'm refusing to serve corruption disguised as preservation. You're not preserving humanity. You're controlling it. Breeding it. Planning to kill millions for 'genetic optimization.'"

The council went silent.

"You've seen too much," one elder said finally. "This is why Pattern Eye carriers are dangerous. You see the whole pattern—including our necessary compromises."

"Necessary?" Thomas's voice shook. "You're planning to release a plague. To kill one-third of Europe. To call it 'genetic refinement.' That's not preservation. That's genocide."

"It's survival. Humanity strengthens through catastrophe. The Genesis Protocol teaches—"

"The Genesis Protocol was a WARNING," Thomas interrupted. "I've decoded the original. I have Pattern Eye. I can see what it meant before you corrupted it. It wasn't instructions to CAUSE collapses. It was instructions to SURVIVE them. You've twisted preservation into murder."

"Then you're a threat to the network."

"No," Thomas said. "I'm the only one who sees clearly enough to save it. Or end it. Whichever is necessary."

He walked out of the council meeting knowing he'd become an enemy of the network he was bred to serve.

And knowing collapse was coming whether he stopped it or not.

**1346 CE - Wilhelm and His Grandson**

Wilhelm summoned Thomas to Mainz in autumn 1346 CE. Not a casual visit—an urgent summons.

Wilhelm was seventy-one, white-haired, exhausted from decades of research and resistance. Thomas was twenty-five, still young, but carrying the weight of Pattern Eye synthesis understanding.

They met in Wilhelm's study, surrounded by encoded manuscripts and research notes.

"Grandfather," Thomas said. "Your message said it was urgent."

"You refused the Bronze Hand," Wilhelm said without preamble. "The network offered you a key and you turned it down."

"As you once refused their directives." Thomas smiled slightly. "We share more than bloodline."

"We share the ability to see clearly. Which makes us threats." Wilhelm leaned forward. "I've spent fifty years researching. I know what they're planning. The plague is coming. Within a year. Maybe two. It's already spreading in the East—reports from Crimea describe massive die-offs. That's not natural pandemic. That's engineered bioweapon reaching Europe."

"Can we stop it?"

"No. It's too widespread already. Even if we could convince authorities—which we can't, they'd think we're insane—the disease is beyond containment."

"Then what do we do?"

Wilhelm pulled a wrapped bundle from his desk drawer. Set it on the table between them.

"This is the Iron Eye. First key. I've carried it for fifty years—received it when I was twenty-one, the same year your father was born." He pushed the bundle toward Thomas. "I was supposed to pass it to Ernst, your father. But he understood—this burden shouldn't fall on him. It should pass to you."

"Why me?"

"Because you have Pattern Eye. Stronger than mine, stronger than Ernst's, stronger than any carrier in a century. Because you see the corruption clearly—more clearly than I ever could. Because when the plague comes—and it will—you'll need to decide: gather all seven keys and expose what the network has become, or let the pattern continue."

Thomas unwrapped the bundle. The Iron Eye gleamed dully in his grandfather's study firelight—six inches of worn iron, shaped like a bookmark, with seven-pointed star on one end and stylized eye on the other. Simple. Functional. Eight hundred years old, passed carrier to carrier, generation to generation.

"I can't take this," Thomas whispered. "I'm not ready."

"No one's ready. I wasn't ready at twenty-one. Your father wasn't ready when I first showed it to him. But you're the only one in our bloodline—perhaps the only one alive—who might choose to end the pattern rather than continue it. That makes you the right person."

"What if I choose wrong?"

"Then millions die because we preserved a system that should have been destroyed. But at least you'll have CHOSEN. The network took choice from us—arranged our marriages, planned our lives, bred us like animals. Three generations of our family have resisted, but we never broke free. I'm giving you the chance to choose. Whatever you decide, it will be yours."

Thomas held the Iron Eye, feeling the weight of forty generations, eight hundred years, countless carriers who'd lived and died preserving the pattern.

And knowing he might be the one to end it.

"The plague is coming," Wilhelm said. "Within months. When it arrives, people will die. The network will use it for genetic optimization. They'll preserve their bloodlines, let others perish, call it refinement. And you'll have to choose whether to expose them or preserve them."

"What will you do, Grandfather?"

"I'm seventy-one. The plague will likely kill me—I'm too old to survive it. But I'll stay in Mainz. Continue decoding. Document the corruption. And when it reaches me—because it will—I'll die knowing I passed the key to my grandson, someone who sees more clearly than I ever could."

"The other keys. Where do I find them?"

"The genetic carrier—a girl named Maria, she's fourteen now, lives with her mother Adela here in Mainz. The Bronze Hand is held by Johannes the Alchemist in Strasbourg. The Copper Heart is hidden somewhere in Prague—my notes have clues. The others... you'll have to search. But the Iron Eye will guide you. First layer reveals network connections."

Wilhelm gripped Thomas's shoulder. "Three generations. Your great-grandfather started this resistance. I continued it. Your father taught you to see clearly. Now it falls to you to choose whether our bloodline's resistance ends the pattern or we all die having changed nothing."

They embraced. Grandfather and grandson. Two carriers bred for preservation, three generations of a family choosing resistance instead.

The pattern continuing.
Until Thomas decided it shouldn't.

**Winter 1347 CE - The Plague Arrives**

The disease reached Sicily in October 1347 CE, carried by merchant ships from Crimea. By November, it was in Marseille. By December, it touched Avignon.

The symptoms were horrific: fever, chills, buboes in groin and armpits, black patches on skin, death within days. The mortality rate approached 50% in affected cities.

It was called the Great Mortality. The Pestilence. Later generations would name it the Black Death.

The defensive network watched its arrival with mixture of horror and anticipation.

Some carriers—the ones who still remembered preservation's original purpose—were appalled. They tried to help. Organized medical care. Documented symptoms. Sought treatments.

Others—the ones who'd fully embraced the corruption—saw opportunity. Genetic optimization through mass death. Refinement of carrier bloodlines. Social reset allowing network reorganization.

And in Mainz, Wilhelm of Mainz grew sick.

January 1347 CE. The plague had been in Europe only three months but was already spreading rapidly. Mainz's position on the Rhine made it a major trade center—and perfect vector for disease transmission.

Wilhelm felt the fever first. Then the chills. Then the buboes forming in his armpits.

He had four days, maybe five.

He sent for Thomas.

**January 1347 CE - Wilhelm's Final Days**

The plague reached Mainz in early January 1347 CE.

Wilhelm felt the fever first. Then the chills. Then the buboes forming in his armpits.

He had perhaps four days, maybe five.

He sent for Brother Hamo—a network scribe at the Mainz cathedral scriptorium, Generation 82, one of the few carriers Wilhelm trusted completely. Hamo had spent twenty years copying manuscripts and had never fully accepted the network's corruption.

"The Iron Eye," Wilhelm said when Hamo arrived. "I gave it to Thomas in autumn. But if something happens before he returns to Mainz, if the network tries to reclaim it, you need to know: Thomas is the rightful bearer. Not because of bloodline—though he's my grandson—but because he has Pattern Eye and the wisdom to choose clearly."

"I understand," Hamo said.

"The plague. I found records suggesting... the network may have tried to engineer it. Or perhaps they simply predicted a natural outbreak and positioned families to exploit it. The encoded texts are ambiguous—theoretical research on plague vectors, modification techniques, predicted mortality rates. Whether they actually created a bioweapon or just studied how one might work... I can't be certain. But either way, they KNEW it was coming. They PLANNED to use it for genetic optimization."

Hamo's face went pale. "They engineered the Black Death?"

"I don't know. Maybe they attempted it and lost control. Maybe the actual plague reaching Europe is natural and they're simply exploiting it. Maybe their engineered version never left the laboratory and what we're seeing is coincidental timing." Wilhelm coughed, blood speckling his lips. "The records show they understood how to create such a disease. Whether they did... that's the horror. Even the possibility means they've corrupted completely. When I die—and I will, within days—the network will come for my research. They'll try to suppress what I found. You need to protect the manuscripts. Hide them. Get them to Thomas."

"I'll guard them with my life."

"Don't. Guard them with your cleverness. Stay alive. Thomas will need allies when he returns to gather the other keys."

Wilhelm died three days later, January 15, 1347 CE. He was seventy-two years old—ancient by fourteenth-century standards, especially during plague.

Hamo attended the funeral. Watched as network elders ransacked Wilhelm's study, confiscating research notes, burning documents they deemed too dangerous. They didn't find everything—Hamo had already hidden the most damning manuscripts in the cathedral's crypt.

And he waited for Thomas to return.

**Late January 1347 CE - The Passing of the Key**

Thomas returned to Mainz three weeks after Wilhelm's death. He'd been in Eltville, watching the plague spread, trying to decide whether to follow his grandfather's instruction or flee Europe entirely.

He went to the cathedral scriptorium looking for information about Wilhelm's research.

Instead, he found Brother Hamo copying a Boethius commentary. And found the Gray Robes breaking in.

The Order had tracked Thomas to Mainz. Knew he was Wilhelm's grandson. Suspected he had the Iron Eye.

Hamo saw them first. "Run!" he shouted to Thomas.

But Thomas froze. Watched as three Gray Robes surrounded Hamo. Watched as they demanded he reveal where Wilhelm's research was hidden.

"I don't know what you're talking about," Hamo said.

They tortured him. Right there in the scriptorium. Thomas watched from behind a bookshelf, paralyzed by horror and cowardice, as they broke Hamo's fingers, burned his hands, demanded answers.

"Wilhelm... hid nothing from me..." Hamo gasped. "But I'll tell you... nothing."

He looked past his torturers. Made eye contact with Thomas. Mouthed two words: *Take it.*

Then he reached into his robe—the Gray Robes thought he was reaching for a weapon, moved to stop him—and pulled out a cloth-wrapped bundle. Threw it toward the bookshelf where Thomas hid.

The Iron Eye clattered across the floor.

Thomas grabbed it. Fled as the Gray Robes killed Hamo. Ran through plague-infested streets with the key clutched in his hand and his grandfather's trusted ally's death on his conscience.

He had the first key.
Bought with Hamo's life.
Inherited from Wilhelm's resistance.
Part of three generations of his family's choice to see clearly.

And now the choice was his: gather the remaining six keys and decide whether to end the pattern or continue it.

He chose to gather them.

The pattern continuing.
Until he decided it shouldn't.

**February 1347 CE - Thomas's Decision**

Thomas returned to Eltville knowing everything had changed.

The plague was spreading. Wilhelm was dead. The network had engineered genocide. And Thomas carried the first key of a system that had corrupted across eight hundred years.

He could:
1. **Join the network fully** - Accept the corruption as necessary, participate in genetic optimization, continue the pattern
2. **Ignore it entirely** - Destroy the Iron Eye, abandon the network, live as normal person during plague
3. **Gather the keys** - Follow Wilhelm's instruction, collect all seven, decode the Genesis Protocol, make informed choice

He chose the third option.

Not because he knew what he'd decide when he had all seven keys. But because choosing ignorance when knowledge was available felt like cowardice. And choosing to continue corruption without understanding its full extent felt like complicity.

He would gather the keys. He would see the pattern completely. And then he would choose.

Whether to preserve the network despite its corruption.
Or destroy it and end the pattern forever.

Both choices defensible.
Neither choice easy.
But the choice would be his.

Wilhelm was right: the network had taken choice from carriers for eight hundred years. Arranged their marriages. Planned their lives. Bred them like animals. Used them as tools.

Thomas would have choice. And he would use it.

He began planning the quest.

Seven keys:
1. **Iron Eye** - Already possessed
2. **Bronze Hand** - Held by Johannes the Alchemist in Strasbourg (Wilhelm's notes)
3. **Blood Carrier (Maria)** - Living in Mainz with her mother Adela
4. **Copper Heart** - Hidden in Prague (location encoded in Wilhelm's research)
5. **Lead Shield** - Constantinople archives (Wilhelm's notes unclear)
6. **Tin Voice** - England or Wales (Wilhelm spent twenty years researching, never found exact location)
7. **Genesis Protocol** - Synthesis understanding achieved when all six unite

He would need help. Wilhelm mentioned Margarethe in Strasbourg—physician's daughter, not network, brilliant and practical. He would find her first.

Then Maria. Then the physical keys. Then the synthesis understanding.

Then the choice.

The pattern continuing.
Until he decided it shouldn't.

**March 1347 CE - The Eve of Quest**

Thomas left Eltville in early March 1347 CE, as spring thaw began and plague continued spreading.

He carried:
- The Iron Eye (wrapped, hidden in his pack)
- Wilhelm's encoded research notes (twenty years of documentation)
- A letter of introduction to Margarethe (forged using network contacts)
- Minimal supplies (travel light, move fast)
- Pattern Eye synthesis gift (the most important tool, carried in his mind)

He didn't know if he'd succeed. Didn't know if gathering all seven keys was even possible during a pandemic. Didn't know what he'd choose when he saw the complete pattern.

But he knew he had to try.

Because eight hundred years of preservation had become corruption. Eight hundred years of knowledge transmission had become control. Eight hundred years of genetic memory had become breeding programs and engineered collapse.

And someone—one carrier with Pattern Eye, one person who could see the whole system clearly—had to decide whether that was worth continuing.

Whether the pattern justified its costs.
Whether preservation at this price was preservation at all.
Whether the network had become the collapse it was meant to survive.

Thomas walked north toward Strasbourg, toward Margarethe, toward the beginning of a quest that would take him across plague-ravaged Europe.

Eight hundred years after Ciarán created the dot notation system.
Eight hundred years after five illuminators forged five keys.
Eight hundred years of preservation and corruption.

The pattern continuing.

Until Thomas decided it shouldn't.
