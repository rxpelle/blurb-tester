# CHAPTER 1: THE COLLAPSE
## 476 CE - Ravenna, Italia

The last emperor of Rome was a child.

Ciarán knew this because his father had told him, whispering it in the dark space beneath the villa's floorboards where they hid now, listening to boots thunder overhead. Four-year-old Ciarán didn't understand what "emperor" meant, but he understood *last*. Last meant nothing came after. Last meant the end.

"Stay silent," his father breathed. Marcus Corvinus pressed his hand over Ciarán's mouth—gentle, but firm. The gesture felt wrong. His father never touched him this way, as if Ciarán might scream. As if fear could make him forget everything he'd been taught since he could speak.

*A carrier never reveals the network.*

Above them, wood splintered. A woman's voice rose in protest—Aunt Livia, who wasn't really his aunt but who let Ciarán help in the villa's kitchen. Her Latin came fast, defensive: "This is a Christian household! We shelter no fugitives!"

"We're not looking for fugitives." The responding voice was calm, educated, speaking Latin with a Greek accent. "We're looking for documents. Manuscripts. Your master is known to possess certain... unusual texts."

"My master is a merchant! He deals in grain, not—"

The sound of something heavy striking flesh. Aunt Livia's voice cut off mid-word.

Ciarán felt his father's hand tighten. Not over his mouth now—over his heart, as if checking it still beat. In the darkness beneath the floorboards, Marcus Corvinus's eyes reflected the thin line of lamplight seeping through the cracks above. Those eyes held something Ciarán had never seen in them before.

Terror.

"The household staff knows nothing," the Greek voice said calmly. "Search thoroughly. The baptistery first—they often hide manuscripts in sacred spaces. Check for false walls, concealed compartments. The Defensive Network has become quite clever about these things."

Footsteps scattered in multiple directions. More splintering wood. The crash of pottery shattering.

In the crawlspace, Marcus Corvinus slowly released his son. His hands moved to the leather satchel he'd been carrying when they fled into this space—how long ago? An hour? Less? Ciarán had lost track of time in the darkness.

His father opened the satchel without sound and withdrew a codex. Even in the dimness, Ciarán could see it was old, the kind of old that made people speak in hushed voices. The leather binding was worn smooth, the pages inside visible at the edges—vellum, not papyrus. Expensive. Important.

Marcus Corvinus held the codex close to the light-crack, examining something on its cover. His lips moved, forming silent words. Then he looked at Ciarán, and his father's face did something strange. It seemed to fold inward, like watching a building collapse from the inside out.

"I'm going to give you something," Marcus whispered. "And you're going to run."

Ciarán shook his head—small, controlled. *A carrier never abandons their duty.* His father had taught him that.

"Listen to me." Marcus grabbed his shoulders. "Your mother is waiting at the docks. The ship for Massilia leaves at dawn. This—" He pressed something small and hard into Ciarán's palm. "—will get you on board. The captain is network. Show him this, say 'the pattern continues,' and he'll take you to her."

Ciarán looked down. In his hand lay a bronze medallion, no bigger than a walnut. One side showed a seven-pointed star. The other showed an eye.

"I don't understand," Ciarán breathed.

"You will." His father's hands were shaking now. "When you're older. When you activate. But right now, you need to—"

Above them, the cultured Greek voice spoke again, much closer: "Marcus Corvinus. We know you're here. We know what you carry. Come out peacefully, and your family will be spared."

A lie. Ciarán knew it was a lie the same way he knew when rain was coming or when Aunt Livia had added honey to the porridge—something he couldn't explain but couldn't doubt. The knowing came from somewhere deep inside, older than his four years, older than his father's forty.

Marcus Corvinus knew it too. His face confirmed it.

"The crawlspace," the Greek voice said thoughtfully. "Ingenious, really. Most villas don't have them. But then, most villas aren't owned by network carriers, are they?"

Footsteps approached their hiding place. Multiple pairs. Something scraped across the floor above—furniture being moved.

"When I open this," Marcus whispered rapidly, pressing the codex into Ciarán's hands alongside the medallion, "you run. Through the hypocaust system—you know the way. Out to the street, down to the harbor. Don't stop. Don't look back. Tell your mother: *The Archive of Chains is compromised. Activate Protocol Thirteen.*"

"Father—"

"Repeat it."

"The Archive of Chains is compromised. Activate Protocol Thirteen."

"Good boy." Marcus kissed his forehead, and Ciarán felt something wet on his father's cheeks. "The pattern continues, little one. Always. Remember that."

The floorboards directly above them began to lift. Someone had found the concealed latch.

Marcus Corvinus exploded upward.

The suddenness of it shocked even the men above. In the chaos of the moment—his father's roar, boots scrambling backward, someone shouting orders—Ciarán did exactly what he'd been trained to do since birth: he moved.

The hypocaust tunnel was narrow, designed for slaves to crawl through while maintaining the villa's heating system. Ciarán had played in these tunnels despite being forbidden, knew them like he knew the pattern of freckles on his own hands. He scrambled through the darkness, the codex clutched to his chest, the bronze medallion burning cold in his fist.

Behind him, he heard his father fighting. Not screaming—Marcus Corvinus made no sound except for the controlled exhalations of someone who had trained for this moment. The clash of bodies against walls. Something breakable shattering. A grunt, deep and masculine, that might have been his father or might have been someone else.

Then: "Hold him. Hold him!"

The Greek voice again, no longer calm: "Where is it? Where's the boy?"

Silence.

"Marcus Corvinus. Last chance. Tell us where—"

"*Custodes memoriae sumus.*"

His father's voice. Latin. Formal. The words network carriers used when they transmitted the Pattern Eye to their successors: *We are the guardians of memory.*

The response was a wet, heavy sound. Metal entering flesh.

Ciarán kept crawling.

The hypocaust tunnel led to the street through a concealed grate beneath the villa's front portico. Ciarán emerged into the gray hour before dawn, into a Ravenna that looked like it had been chewed up and spit out by something vast and hungry.

Smoke rose from three separate fires. In the Forum Augusti, a statue of Romulus Augustulus—the boy emperor, the last emperor—lay toppled, its marble head cracked open on the stones. A group of soldiers in Germanic dress laughed as they urinated on the fragments.

No one noticed a small boy covered in soot, clutching a codex, running toward the harbor.

The docks were chaos.

Ships were leaving—not departing on schedule, but *fleeing*, overloaded with cargo and passengers who had paid bribes or threatened violence to secure passage. The maritime prefecture had collapsed. No one checked manifests. No one collected harbor taxes. The mechanisms of empire were simply... stopping.

Ciarán searched the crowds with desperate eyes, looking for his mother's face, her honey-colored hair, her height—she was tall for a woman, towered over most men.

"*Puer.*"

The word came from behind him. Ciarán spun.

The man was old, perhaps sixty, with the weathered face of someone who had spent decades staring at horizons. He wore a ship captain's cloak, stained with salt and tar. His eyes fixed on the codex in Ciarán's arms, then dropped to the bronze medallion visible in the boy's clenched fist.

"The password," the captain said quietly.

Ciarán's mind went blank. Password? His father had told him to say something, but the memory scattered like smoke, replaced by the wet sound of metal entering flesh, his father's last formal words—

"*Boy.*" The captain's voice sharpened. "If you're who I think you are, we're both in danger standing here. The offensive network has eyes at every dock. Give me the password, or I leave without you."

"The pattern continues." The words came automatically, pulled from the same deep place that had told him the Greek man was lying.

The captain's face changed—some tension releasing from around his eyes. "Come."

He didn't ask about Ciarán's father. Didn't ask why a four-year-old boy was alone at the docks at dawn clutching network materials. He simply turned and walked toward a mid-sized merchant vessel, the kind that hauled grain and olive oil up and down Italia's coast.

Ciarán followed.

The ship was called *Memoria.* Ciarán could read the name painted on its hull in fading red letters, though he shouldn't have been able to—his mother had only just started teaching him Latin script. But he looked at the letters and *knew* them, the same way he'd known the Greek man was lying, the same way he knew something terrible had just happened that would change everything forever.

"Your mother isn't here," the captain said as they climbed the boarding ramp. "She sailed yesterday on the *Fortuna*, bound for Burdigala. I'm taking you to Massilia—you'll meet her overland from there."

"But my father said—"

"Your father gave you old information." The captain's voice was gentle now. "The network is fragmenting. Communication breaks down. We adapt." He paused, looking down at Ciarán with something like pity. "He didn't make it out, did he?"

Ciarán shook his head.

"Then he died protecting what we all protect." The captain laid a hand on Ciarán's shoulder. "And now you carry it forward. That's how it works. That's how it's always worked."

The *Memoria* cast off as the sun rose over Ravenna. Standing at the rail, codex clutched to his chest, Ciarán watched the city recede. Smoke still rose from the fires. The broken emperor still lay in pieces in the forum.

The last emperor of Rome was a child, and Rome itself was dying.

But Ciarán wasn't thinking about emperors. He was thinking about the wet sound of metal entering flesh. About his father's last words: *Custodes memoriae sumus.* About the codex in his arms, heavy with something he didn't yet understand.

About the fact that he could read Latin script he'd never been taught. About the knowing that came from somewhere deeper than memory.

He looked down at the codex. Its leather cover was unmarked except for a small symbol pressed into the binding—a seven-pointed star. The same symbol on the medallion in his pocket.

Ciarán opened the codex carefully.

The first page showed text in Latin, beautifully scribed in a confident hand: *Confessiones Augustini Hipponensis.* The Confessions of Augustine of Hippo. Ciarán had heard his father speak that name with reverence. A great theologian. A defender of the faith.

But written in the margin, in a different hand, much smaller: *Generation 53 to Generation 54. The method is semantic. Read with open eyes.*

Ciarán didn't understand the words, but he understood their weight. This book was more than a book. These pages carried something invisible, something that had gotten his father killed.

Something worth dying for.

He was four years old, and Rome was falling, and he was a carrier now—whether he understood what that meant or not. The bronze medallion in his pocket seemed to pulse like a second heartbeat. The Pattern Eye. His father's legacy. His burden now.

*The pattern continues.*

Ciarán closed the codex and watched Ravenna disappear behind the curve of the earth.

## **SCENE BREAK**

**Ten days later - Massilia docks**

His mother found him before he found her.

"Ciarán."

The voice came from behind him as he stood at the harbor market, trying to use one of the few copper coins the captain had given him to buy bread. Ciarán turned, and there she was: Lucia Corvina, tall and honey-haired, wearing a widow's dark cloak though she couldn't have known yet that she was a widow.

Except she did know. Ciarán saw it in her face.

"Mother—"

She dropped to her knees on the dock and pulled him into her arms, the kind of embrace that wasn't about comfort but about confirmation—checking that he was solid, present, alive. Her hands ran over his shoulders, his back, his head, as if inventorying for damage.

"The codex?" she whispered against his hair.

"Safe."

"The medallion?"

"Safe."

She pulled back, holding him at arm's length. Her eyes were red-rimmed but dry. Network carriers didn't weep in public. "Your father?"

Ciarán shook his head.

Lucia's face did something complicated—grief and acceptance and fury all at once. She pulled him close again, and this time Ciarán felt her whole body shaking, though she made no sound.

"The Archive of Chains is compromised," Ciarán recited into her shoulder. "Activate Protocol Thirteen."

His mother went still. Then she stood, pulling Ciarán up with her, her hand locked around his wrist. "Come."

She led him through Massilia's narrow streets at a pace just short of running. They passed fish markets and wine shops, turned down an alley that smelled of urine and rotting vegetables, emerged into a small courtyard where a fountain burbled. A church stood at the courtyard's far end—small, made of local stone, unremarkable except for the symbol carved above its door.

A seven-pointed star.

Inside, the church was empty except for an old priest arranging candles. He looked up as they entered, saw Lucia's face, and immediately moved to bar the door behind them.

"How bad?" he asked in accented Latin.

"Ravenna cell is gone," Lucia said flatly. "Marcus is gone. They have the Archive location."

The priest closed his eyes. "Mother of God."

"Protocol Thirteen," Lucia continued. "We need to activate immediately. How many can we evacuate before—"

"Lucia." The priest's voice was gentle. "Protocol Thirteen assumes we still have coordinated communication. We don't. The network has lost contact with Britannia, Hispania, and North Africa. Rome is occupied. Constantinople refuses to acknowledge the crisis. We're not fragmenting—we've already fragmented."

Ciarán watched his mother's face as she processed this. Her jaw tightened, but she didn't break. Network carriers didn't break.

"Then we adapt," she said. "Like we always do."

"To what?" The priest spread his hands. "The empire is falling. The infrastructure we've used for four hundred years is collapsing. The roads are unsafe. The postal system is defunct. The monasteries are being burned by barbarians. Our encoding methods assume stable transmission chains—"

"Then we develop new methods." Lucia's voice was sharp. "Marcus didn't die so we could surrender."

The priest looked at her for a long moment, then nodded slowly. "Where will you go?"

"North. Britannia first, then Hibernia if Britannia's lost. The Irish monasteries are independent—they don't answer to Rome, so Rome's fall won't destroy them. If we can reach them, we can rebuild."

"It's a long journey. Dangerous. The boy—"

"The boy is a carrier." Lucia's hand tightened on Ciarán's shoulder. "Aren't you, love?"

Ciarán nodded. He didn't fully understand what that meant yet, but he understood enough. His father had died protecting these books, these secrets, this invisible pattern that connected people across the empire. Now it was his turn to protect them.

The priest knelt to Ciarán's level. "Can you read?"

"Some."

"Do you know what your father was carrying?"

Ciarán thought about the codex, about the marginal note. "A book by someone named Augustine. But not just the book—something hidden in it."

The priest smiled, though it was a sad expression. "Smart boy. Your father trained you well." He reached into his robes and withdrew a small object wrapped in oiled cloth. "This was going to be delivered to Ravenna, but..." He unwrapped it carefully.

Another medallion. Bronze, like the one Ciarán carried, but different. Where his showed a seven-pointed star on one side and an eye on the other, this one showed a seven-pointed star and an open hand.

"The Pattern Eye and the Pattern Hand," the priest said. "Two halves of the same key. Your father carried the Eye—the ability to see connections, to recognize patterns across generations. This is the Hand—the ability to create new patterns, to encode new meanings."

He pressed it into Lucia's palm. "You'll need both in Hibernia. The Irish have their own encoding traditions—older than ours, some say. If we're going to survive Rome's collapse, we'll need to learn from them."

Lucia closed her fingers around the medallion. "How do we contact you? Once we're established?"

"You don't." The priest stood. "Protocol Thirteen is correct about one thing: We need to scatter. Complete disconnection. Let the offensive network think we've been destroyed. Then, in a generation or two, when the chaos settles, we rebuild the connections carefully."

"A generation or two?" Lucia's voice rose. "That's fifty years! How do we—"

"The same way we always have." The priest gestured to the codex Ciarán carried. "We encode it. In books, in teachings, in the memories we pass to our children. The method is what matters, not the speed. Augustine understood that. He built encoding systems that could survive centuries without active coordination."

He looked at Ciarán. "Your father's death was not in vain, boy. He bought you time to escape. To carry what he carried. To continue the pattern." The priest placed a hand on Ciarán's head. "Someday, you'll understand what that means. Someday, you'll activate the Eye, and you'll see what your father saw—the whole network, stretching back generations, forward into possibilities. And you'll know: The pattern continues. Always. Even through collapse."

## **SCENE BREAK**

**Four weeks later - Aboard a merchant vessel bound for Britannia**

Ciarán had learned to read at sea.

Not ordinary reading—his mother had been teaching him that since he was three. But *network reading*. The kind that saw double meanings, hidden structures, semantic layers built into innocent-seeming text.

Every evening, while other passengers dozed or gambled or fought off seasickness, Lucia would open Augustine's *Confessions* and read aloud. At first, Ciarán heard only the surface: a man confessing his sins, praising God, describing his spiritual journey.

But then his mother would stop and say: "Again. But this time, listen for the pattern."

And she would read the same passage, except now certain words were emphasized. Certain phrases connected to earlier passages. And suddenly the text wasn't about Augustine's personal faith at all—it was about *memory systems*. About how to structure information for long-term preservation. About recognizing patterns across generations.

"Do you see?" Lucia would whisper. "He's teaching methodology. The prayer is real—Augustine was genuinely devout. But he was also encoding. Every word serves two purposes."

Ciarán saw. And more than that, he *felt* something shifting inside him, like a door opening in a room he hadn't known existed. The knowing that had helped him escape Ravenna grew stronger. Sometimes he would look at a passage and understand its hidden meaning before his mother explained it.

"You're close to activation," Lucia said one evening, studying his face. "I can see it. Your father activated at six—you might be even earlier."

"What's it like?" Ciarán asked. "Activation?"

His mother was quiet for a long time, watching the sun set over the water. Finally: "Like remembering something you never forgot. Like opening your eyes and realizing you've been seeing all along, just not understanding what you saw."

She pulled him close. "Your father used to say it was like hearing a song you've been humming your whole life, and suddenly someone tells you the words."

"Did it hurt?"

"No. It's... overwhelming. Beautiful and terrible at once. Because once you activate, you understand what we are. What we carry. Why it matters." She kissed the top of his head. "And you can't ever unknow it."

That night, Ciarán dreamed of his father.

Not a memory—though at first it seemed like one. They were in the villa's courtyard, and Marcus Corvinus was teaching him to count using colored stones. Red stones, blue stones, yellow stones, arranging them in geometric patterns.

"See how they connect?" Dream-Marcus said. "Three reds make a triangle. Four blues make a square. The pattern tells you what they mean."

"But they're just stones," Dream-Ciarán protested.

"Now they are." His father smiled. "But what if they weren't? What if you could put them on a page instead of a courtyard? What if you could hide them in decoration, in illumination, in the margins of books where everyone expects to see beautiful things?"

Dream-Marcus picked up a red stone. As Ciarán watched, it transformed into a dot of red ink on parchment. Then another. Then another, forming a triangle in the margin of a manuscript.

"The pattern continues," his father said. "Not in my time. But in yours. You'll see, little one. You'll see how to adapt what we have to what the world is becoming."

"I don't understand," Ciarán said.

"You will." His father placed a hand over Ciarán's heart—the same gesture he'd used in the crawlspace, checking that it still beat. "When you activate. When you see what I see. You'll understand that Rome's collapse isn't an ending. It's an opportunity."

Ciarán woke gasping.

The ship creaked around him. Other passengers snored in the darkness. Beside him, his mother slept fitfully, one hand resting on the bundled codex.

But Ciarán's mind was racing.

The dream hadn't felt like a dream. It felt like a memory from someone else's life, slipping sideways into his consciousness. And the idea his father had shown him—colored dots in manuscript margins, encoding information in decoration—it felt *possible*. Real. Like something that could actually work.

He closed his eyes and tried to hold onto the feeling, but it slipped away like water through fingers.

Not yet. He wasn't ready yet.

But soon.

## **SCENE BREAK**

**Three months later - Coastal Britannia**

The island was drowning in fog.

Ciarán stood at the ship's rail beside his mother, watching the white cliffs of Britannia emerge from the mist like the bones of something vast and dead. The crossing had been rough—autumn storms turning the Oceanus Britannicus into a furious beast—but the *Fortuna* (not the same ship his mother had taken from Ravenna, but another vessel with the same name, operated by the same network-aligned merchant family) had brought them through.

"It's so gray," Ciarán said.

"You'll get used to it." Lucia's voice was steady, but Ciarán could hear the exhaustion underneath. Three months of constant movement, sleeping in different network safe houses every few nights, always checking over their shoulders for offensive network agents. It had worn her down to something sharp and desperate.

They docked at Dubris, where Roman fortifications still stood but Roman soldiers had long since departed. The locals spoke a Latin so degraded it was barely recognizable, mixed with a Celtic language Ciarán had never heard. His mother navigated the chaos with practiced ease, finding their contact—a wool merchant who said the network password so smoothly it sounded like ordinary conversation.

"Weather's turning," the merchant said, loading their packs onto his wagon. "Pattern suggests rain by evening."

"The pattern continues," Lucia replied.

"As it always does." The merchant smiled. "Come. We'll have you safe by nightfall."

But they weren't going to a safe house in Dubris. The wagon headed north, into the hills, following Roman roads that were beginning to crumble from disuse. They passed abandoned villas, empty milestones, the stone markers of a civilization that had simply... stopped.

"Where are we going?" Ciarán asked.

"Londinium," his mother said. "Or what's left of it. There's someone there we need to see."

---

Londinium was dying.

Not dead yet—there were still people in the streets, still merchants selling goods, still some semblance of urban life. But the decay was obvious. Buildings with collapsed roofs, never repaired. Streets beginning to choke with refuse. The amphitheater was abandoned, its gates broken.

The wool merchant led them to a compound near the old forum. Once, it had been a wealthy Roman's townhouse. Now it served as a monastery—one of the new Christian establishments that were sprouting up across the empire's corpse like mushrooms on a rotting log.

A tall man in monk's robes greeted them at the gate. He had the bearing of someone who had once been a soldier, and his Latin was educated, precise. "Lucia Corvina. We weren't sure you'd make it."

"Neither were we." She embraced him carefully. "This is my son, Ciarán."

The monk looked down at Ciarán with intense dark eyes. "Marcus's boy?"

"Yes."

"I'm sorry for your loss. Your husband was a good man. A brave carrier." He knelt to Ciarán's level. "My name is Brother Ambrosius. I knew your father. We trained together, many years ago, in Roma before the troubles started."

Ciarán studied the monk's face and saw something familiar there—the same quality he'd seen in his father, in the ship captains, in his mother. The weight of carrying something invisible but infinitely heavy.

"Do you still have it?" Ambrosius asked Lucia quietly.

She withdrew the codex from her pack. Augustine's *Confessions*, looking more worn now from months of travel but intact. Ambrosius took it with the kind of reverence usually reserved for holy relics.

"This is one of the last copies," he murmured, opening it carefully. "Most were destroyed when Ravenna fell. The offensive network has been systematically targeting Augustine's works—they recognize the encoding, even if they can't always decode it."

"That's why we need to move it further," Lucia said. "Britannia isn't safe. If the Saxons push much further west, if the Irish raid the coasts—"

"Agreed." Ambrosius closed the codex. "Which is why we're sending you to Hibernia."

"Ireland?" Ciarán looked up at his mother. "The island west of here?"

"The island where learning still flourishes," Ambrosius corrected. "While Europe collapses, the Irish monasteries are thriving. They're producing books, training scribes, preserving knowledge. And they're not part of the Roman system—they have their own traditions, their own encoding methods that predate even ours."

He led them inside, through corridors lined with more books than Ciarán had ever seen in one place. The monastery had become an archive, he realized. Manuscripts stacked on shelves, copied and recopied by scribes working in the scriptorium they passed.

"We're preserving what we can," Ambrosius explained. "But preservation isn't enough. We need innovation. The old methods—semantic encoding in theological texts—work beautifully for transmitting philosophy and methodology. But they're too slow for practical information. Too vulnerable to translation errors. We need something faster, more precise, more adaptable to the new world we're living in."

They entered a private study. Ambrosius closed the door and gestured for them to sit.

"The Irish have something," he said. "A tradition of visual encoding in their manuscript illumination. Beautiful geometric patterns, interlaced designs, colored inks. It's decorative—purely artistic, as far as most people can tell. But I've seen their master illuminators at work, and I wonder..."

He paused, studying Ciarán's face. "What if decoration could carry meaning? What if those geometric patterns could encode information? What if we could hide our knowledge in beauty itself?"

Ciarán felt something ignite in his chest. The dream. His father's dream of colored dots arranged in patterns, hidden in plain sight.

"It would have to be systematic," Lucia said slowly. "Reproducible. Not just artistic intuition, but a method others could learn."

"Exactly." Ambrosius leaned forward. "And that's why you're going to Clonmacnoise. It's one of the greatest Irish monasteries—a center of learning, manuscript production, metalwork. If anyone can develop a new encoding system using illumination as cover, it's the scribes there."

He looked at Ciarán. "And they'll train your son. By the time he's grown, the world will need new methods. New ways of preserving what we protect. The semantic encoding will always have value—but we need something that can preserve precise information. Coordinates. Dates. Network connections. Things Augustine's method can't handle."

Ciarán felt the weight of the man's gaze, felt something being passed to him—not physically, but deeper. A responsibility. A purpose.

"I had a dream," Ciarán heard himself say. "My father showed me colored dots. On manuscript pages. Forming patterns."

The room went silent.

Ambrosius and Lucia exchanged a look that Ciarán couldn't quite read.

"When?" his mother asked quietly.

"On the ship. A month ago. Right after you told me about activation."

"Mother of God," Ambrosius breathed. "He's already starting to see it. At four years old."

"He takes after Marcus." There was pride and grief in Lucia's voice. "His father activated at six. I activated at eight. But Ciarán—"

"—is showing signs at four." Ambrosius stood, pacing. "This is extraordinary. The genetic memory must be unusually strong in your bloodline."

"What does that mean?" Ciarán asked.

His mother pulled him close. "It means you're going to be important, love. It means the pattern is already working through you. You're not just going to learn the new methods—you're going to help create them."

---

They left for Hibernia two weeks later, after Lucia had met with other network members hiding throughout Britannia, passing messages, coordinating what little coordination was still possible. The network was fracturing, yes—but it was fragmenting *intentionally* now, creating isolated cells that could survive even if others were destroyed.

"Like seeds," Ambrosius explained as he blessed their departure. "Scatter them widely enough, and some will take root no matter how harsh the winter."

The ship that carried them across the Irish Sea was small, sturdy, built for rough waters. The crossing took two days. Ciarán spent most of it at the rail, watching the coast of Britannia disappear and the coast of Hibernia emerge—green where Britannia had been gray, wild where Britannia had been cultivated.

"It's beautiful," he said to his mother.

"It is." Lucia stood beside him, one hand on his shoulder. "And it's going to be your home now. Our home. Where we rebuild."

"Will the offensive network find us here?"

"Eventually. They always do." Her hand tightened. "But by then, we'll have developed new methods. New encoding. New ways of protecting what we protect. That's how we survive—by staying one step ahead. Your father knew that. Ambrosius knows that. And someday, you'll know it too."

The ship docked at a place called Áth Cliath—a small settlement, barely more than a few buildings clustered around a ford. But there were monks there, Irish monks with tonsures shaved in a different style than Roman monks, speaking a Latin flavored with their own tongue.

One of them approached—a young man, perhaps twenty, with intelligent eyes and hands stained with ink.

"Lucia Corvina?" he asked in accented Latin.

"Yes."

"Welcome to Hibernia. I'm Brother Finnian. Ambrosius sent word you were coming." He smiled at Ciarán. "And you must be the boy who dreams of colored dots."

Ciarán stared. "How did you—"

"We have our own communication methods." Finnian's smile widened. "Faster than you'd expect. Come—I'll take you to Clonmacnoise. It's a few days' journey, but the roads are safe. Well, safer than what you've been traveling through."

As they walked away from the dock, Ciarán looked back one last time at the sea, at the horizon where Britannia and the collapsing empire lay behind them.

His father was dead. His home was gone. The world he'd been born into was ending.

But somewhere ahead, in a monastery he'd never seen, among monks who illuminated manuscripts with geometric precision, a new method was waiting to be born.

*The pattern continues.*

Always.
