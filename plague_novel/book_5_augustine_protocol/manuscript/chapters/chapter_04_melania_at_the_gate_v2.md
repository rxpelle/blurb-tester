# Chapter 7 — Melania at the Gate (v2, thriller)

*Hippo Regius, the third week of November 411 CE.*

The autumn rains had come. They had come early this year, in the second week of October instead of the third, and the harbour road had been mud since. Augustine stood at the half-shuttered window of the writing room and looked at the sky. The sky was the colour of the sea on a winter morning. The sea, which he could see in a strip between the tile of the basilica roof and the line of the fig trees in the cloister, was the colour of the sky. The two had been the same colour for nine days.

He turned back to the desk.

The first paragraph of the work was in its tenth revision and would have a tenth-and-final form by vespers. He had been at it since the third hour and had taken bread and water at the noon table without leaving the room. The wax tablet was on the corner of the desk where the boy from the scriptorium would see it in the morning. The parchment was on the desk in front of him. The bone stylus was in his hand.

The work had been going for fourteen months now — since the night the courier had been brought in by the carter Lucius and laid on the table behind him in a position the table had since been scrubbed of in three separate cleanings — and it would go for another decade if his body permitted. Eraclius had begun to complain that the bishop was at the desk too long for a man of his age. Augustine had pointed out that he was fifty-seven and not yet dead. Eraclius had not answered. Eraclius's silence, on the matter of the bishop's age and the bishop's hours at the desk, was the kind of silence that gathered weight for years and was eventually expressed in a small written note left on the corner of the desk for the bishop to find. Augustine had been finding such notes from Eraclius for nine years.

He set the stylus down.

A knock at the door. Three short.

*Father.*

*Come.*

Stephen came in and closed the door behind him. He had aged in the fourteen months since the night of the courier in the way men of his age age when they have been the hands of a thing they had not asked to be the hands of for the second time in their lives. The aging was visible in the corners of the mouth and in the way he held his shoulders. Augustine had been watching it and had not spoken of it.

*Father. A letter from Carthage.*

*From Aurelius?*

*From a senator in Aurelius's house. The seal is the senator's. The address is in his own hand.*

Stephen handed him the letter. Augustine cut the seal with his thumbnail.

The letter was three lines long, in the formal Latin of an educated layman writing to a bishop who was also an intellectual figure of the province. It said:

*Bishop, I am Rufius Antonius Agrypnius Volusianus, formerly proconsul of Africa under the late emperor and presently a guest in the house of the Christian primate at Carthage. I will be at Hippo in the third week of November on the business of the imperial grain office. I would like to hear you preach. I would like to speak with you afterwards, on a matter of philosophy.*

Augustine read it twice. He set it on the desk.

*Stephen.*

*Father.*

*This is the man who has been writing the dispatches from Carthage that the refugees have been bringing south.*

Stephen waited.

*He is the head of the household that is currently arguing, in Aurelius's parlour and in three private salons in the city, that the empire fell because the empire became Christian. He is a clever man and a polished one. He is coming to Hippo to debate me in public, which is why he has chosen the day he has chosen and why he has written that he wishes to hear me preach. He will hear me preach on the day of the African martyrs. He will then ask his question in front of whatever crowd has come to the basilica that day, and the question will be the question I have been answering by letter for fourteen months.*

*What will you do?*

*I will preach. Then I will answer his question. The answer is the answer I have been writing in the work.*

Stephen looked at the parchment on the desk.

*The work is not finished.*

*The work is not finished. The work will do for tonight.*

Stephen nodded. He did not leave.

*Father.*

*Yes.*

*Is there a reply to send to Carthage?*

Augustine thought.

*Yes. Tell the messenger I will receive the senator at the basilica on the day he names. Tell him there will be a meal in the small parlour after the homily if the senator wishes to take one. Tell him that the bishop of Hippo looks forward to a question in philosophy and will do his small best with it.*

Stephen committed the words to memory. He went out.

Augustine sat at the desk a long while after the door closed. The first paragraph of the work was on the parchment in front of him. He had not, in fourteen months of revision, changed its essential shape. He had changed only the words. The shape was the shape it had been when he had written it on the night of the courier, by the same lamp on the same desk in the same room.

The senator was coming to Hippo to question the shape.

He took up the stylus. He did not write the answer. He would not write the answer until he had heard the question put. He wrote, instead, on the wax tablet at his elbow: *Volusianus arrives in the third week. The question will be put in public on the day of the martyrs. Prepare nothing. Speak from the work.*

He put the stylus back in its tray. He went to vespers. The rain had stopped.

---

*The day of the martyrs of Africa, at the cathedral of Hippo Regius, the eleventh hour of the morning.*

The basilica was full. It had not been this full since the dedication of the new font, fourteen years before. Word that Volusianus would attend had moved through the city by the river of the wine-shops and the harbour warehouses since first light, and the lay congregation had filled the nave to the back wall by the second hour. The educated men of Hippo — three of the magistrates, two retired professors of rhetoric, the deacon of the synagogue who came to the cathedral on the days when the bishop would preach on a matter of philosophy — sat in the rows before the altar. Volusianus and his small Carthaginian retinue sat in the third row, in the place reserved for visiting dignitaries.

He was sixty, perhaps a little more. He wore the senatorial toga with the broad purple stripe, which on a man of his bearing in a North African basilica in November was a small instrument of theatre. The toga was immaculate; he had had it laundered before the service. His hair was grey and combed. His face was the face of a man who had been telling other men what to do for forty years and had not yet been wrong.

Augustine preached the homily he had prepared the night before. He preached it on the seven martyrs of Scillium, whose memory the African church kept on this day, and whose answers under interrogation by the proconsul Saturninus in the year 180 — *I am a Christian* — had been the founding text of the African church's understanding of what the office of the believer required. He preached for half an hour. He preached without polemic. He did not look at the third row.

When the homily was done and the dismissal pronounced, Augustine came down from the bishop's chair. The stone of the nave was cold through his sandals; he had been on the chair two hours. The smell of the full basilica — tallow, wet wool, the warm breath of a crowd that had been standing — came up at him as he crossed to the rail. He could feel the bodies packed to the west door behind him in the same way he could feel a difficult sentence before he had written it. The senator rose from the third row and approached. The crowd did not disperse. The crowd had been waiting for this and was not going to miss it.

*Bishop.*

*Senator.*

Volusianus inclined his head — the inclination of a man greeting an equal who is also, by the conventions of imperial protocol, a degree his junior in office. Augustine returned the inclination. The two men stood at the rail.

*Bishop, I have heard you preach. I have heard what your African church considers the foundation of its life. I would like, with your permission, to put my question.*

*Put it.*

The senator turned half-toward the crowd, which was the move he had practised in the senate at Rome for thirty years and which would have worked in any forum of the empire. He was a man at home in front of a crowd. The bishop of Hippo, who had spent thirty years at the front of one congregation or another, was also at home in front of a crowd. The two men knew this about each other.

*Bishop, the eternal city of Rome stood unconquered for eight hundred years while she worshipped the gods of her fathers. Within a century of embracing the Galilean, she has fallen to the Visigoths. Refugees arrive in this very city by the shipload. The temples of the old gods stand empty. The city of stones lies open. I ask you, before this congregation: is the fall of Rome the judgment of the old gods upon a city that abandoned them?*

Some in the crowd nodded. Some made the sign of the cross. The deacon of the synagogue did not move.

Augustine waited. He did not begin to speak immediately. He let the question sit in the chamber of the basilica for the count of three, the way he let a difficult sentence sit on the parchment before he answered it. Then he said:

*Senator. Permit me a question of my own before I answer yours.*

*Permit it.*

*Brennus the Gaul sacked Rome in the year three hundred and ninety before the birth of our Lord. Hannibal and the Carthaginians ravaged Italy for sixteen years before the gates of Rome itself. Sulla butchered his fellow citizens in the Forum within living memory of those who had seen it. Caesar crossed the Rubicon and made the city the prize of his ambition. The civil wars of the late republic — Marius and Sulla, Caesar and Pompey, Octavian and Antony — emptied the streets of Rome of their citizens and filled them with the citizens' blood. All of these things happened, senator, while the gods of your fathers had the watch.*

*They happened.*

*Were the gods asleep at those times? Were they indifferent? Were they powerless? Or are we to say that the gods who permitted Brennus to enter the city in the consulship of Camillus punished the city for some sin we have not yet identified, and that the gods who permitted Sulla to butcher its citizens punished Rome for a different sin in a different generation, and that the gods who permitted Caesar to take the city in the year of his crossing were punishing yet a third sin? If so, the gods of Rome have been punishing the city continuously for the better part of four hundred years. Forgive me, senator, but a god who has been punishing a city continuously for four hundred years has not been protecting the city, except in the sense in which a man who beats his wife continuously has not, when he stops beating her, become her enemy.*

A small laugh ran through the second row. The deacon of the synagogue smiled into his hand. Volusianus's face did not change.

*Bishop. The earlier sacks were tests. The present sack is a judgment.*

*By what method do you distinguish?*

*By the outcome.*

*Which is to say: the sacks Rome survived were tests, and the sack Rome did not survive is a judgment. Senator, your argument proves only itself. It tells us that Rome survived until it did not — and nothing about the gods.*

*The Galilean has weakened the city's virtue.*

*The Visigoths who took the city are also Christian, senator. They are Arian Christians; they hold the Son to be of a substance like the Father's but not of the same substance. But they are Christian. They prayed before they breached the Salarian gate. They spared the basilicas of the apostles. They protected the women who took refuge in the great churches. They are imperfect Christians but they are Christians, and your gods raised them up, on your account of things, to punish a city that had embraced their faith. We are asked to believe that the gods of Rome care so deeply about the precise definition of the relation between the Father and the Son that they will turn one party of Christians upon another to punish the empire for choosing the wrong party. If your gods take an interest in this question, senator, they have been holding their tongues remarkably well at every council where the question has been debated.*

The crowd laughed again. This time more openly. Volusianus's face still did not change, but his hand at his side closed and opened.

*Bishop, you reduce a serious question to mockery.*

*Senator, I take a serious question with great seriousness. The question is whether the gods of Rome punished Rome for becoming Christian. The answer is that Rome was not a city protected by the gods — Rome was a city like other cities, that prospered when its institutions held and fell when its institutions failed. The institutions of Rome have been failing for two centuries. The Visigoths are the visible sign of a failure that has been invisible for a long time. They are not the cause of the fall. They are the proof that the fall has occurred.*

*Then your god did not protect Rome either.*

*Senator, the city of God is not the city of Rome. It has never been the city of Rome. The city of Rome is the city of stones. The city of God is a different kind of city. The city of stones falls. The city of God does not fall, because the city of God is not made of stones. It is made of the love of God and the love of one another. It is made wherever those loves are found. It is made in this basilica today and it was made in the Aventine houses during the sack and it is made in the warehouses by the harbour where the women from Rome are sleeping on the straw with their daughters. When the city of stones falls, the city of God continues. This is what the church teaches. This is what the homily this morning was about. This is what I have been writing for fourteen months in a work that, when finished, will say it more carefully and more at length, but which will not say it differently. The fall of Rome did not refute the Christian church. The fall of Rome demonstrated what the Christian church has always taught — that the city of stones is not the city of God, and that the love of God is not bound up with the fortunes of any empire.*

Volusianus stood very still. His hand at his side had stopped its small clenching. The senator had come to Hippo expecting the bishop to defend Rome. The bishop had not defended Rome. The bishop had given Rome away.

After a moment Volusianus said:

*Bishop, this is a clever speech.*

*It is not a speech, senator. It is a description of what is.*

*It will not feed the refugees in the warehouses by the harbour.*

*No. The Christian church will feed the refugees in the warehouses by the harbour. The Christian church is feeding them now. It has been feeding them for fourteen months. While the educated men of the empire have been debating in salons whose gods are punishing whose city, the Christian church has been distributing grain. We are doing the work the city of God does in the city of stones, which is to take care of those whom the city of stones has cast into the street. This is not a new work. We have been doing it for three hundred years. We will continue to do it after Rome and after Hippo and after Carthage and after every other city of stones has fallen. This is what the city of God is.*

The crowd was silent. Some men had begun to nod. The educated men in the front rows were looking at each other with the small look that men with educated friends use when they have just witnessed an argument decided. The deacon of the synagogue's smile had become very deep.

Volusianus inclined his head. The inclination this time was the inclination of a man who has lost the round and is acknowledging it without theatrically acknowledging it. He said:

*Bishop, I will think on what you have said.*

*Senator, I would not ask more than that.*

The two men stood at the rail a moment longer. Volusianus turned and went back through the nave and out through the great west door of the basilica with his small retinue. Most of the crowd went with him; not all. The educated men in the front rows stayed behind to take the meal in the small parlour Augustine had mentioned in his letter, and several of them — the deacon of the synagogue among them — came up to the rail to thank him and to ask, with the particular phrasing that men of education use when they wish to take a teaching home with them, whether he would be willing to write the substance of his answer down for circulation.

*I am writing it down,* he said. *It will take some years.*

He went into the small parlour and ate.

---

*The basilica gate, after vespers.*

She came at the hour of the lamp. He was at the gate to receive the small delegation of fishermen who had brought a basket of cured tunny for the basilica's relief work and who could not, on principle, be received by Eraclius because the principle of giving in their part of the harbour required the receiving to be done by the bishop himself. The fishermen handed over the tunny. Augustine blessed them. They went away. He turned to go back into the cloister.

She was standing under the arch of the gate, in the half-light of the gate-keeper's lamp, in a plain wool cloak the colour of the rain. She was perhaps thirty. Her face was not the face of a woman who had been weeping. Her face was the face of a woman who had been listening, all afternoon, to people weeping, and was now done.

*Bishop.*

*Daughter.*

*I would ask a few minutes of your time.*

*Take them.*

*Privately, if it can be managed.*

He looked at her. The accent of her Latin was the accent of an educated Roman woman of the senatorial class. The cloak she was wearing was not a cloak a woman of that class would normally wear in public. She had chosen the cloak and chosen the hour. She was a woman of careful calculation.

*Come into the small parlour. Eraclius will bring water.*

She followed him through the cloister to the small parlour. He told the gate-keeper to send Eraclius with water and bread and to disturb them for nothing else for the next half-hour. Eraclius brought the water and the bread and went out without speaking. The two of them sat across the table.

*Bishop, my name is Melania. My husband Pinianus and I crossed from Italy in the autumn of last year. We have property in Tagaste, in your home town, which is where we have been staying. I have come to Hippo for two reasons. The first is to hear you preach today, which I have done. The second is to speak with you, if you will allow it, about a project I wish to undertake in this province.*

He looked at her again. The name was a name he knew. The Melania who had founded the monastery on the Mount of Olives twenty-five years ago was famous in Christian circles across the empire; the family had been giving away its wealth, in a slow and careful manner, for a generation.

*You are the granddaughter.*

*I am.*

*You are the one who married Pinianus.*

*Pinianus. Yes.*

*And you are now thirty.*

*Twenty-eight, Bishop. I have been giving away the estates for eight years.*

*Why are you in Africa.*

She did not answer immediately. She picked up the cup of water and held it in both hands. She did not drink.

*Bishop, my grandmother taught me before she died that there were certain matters which were best understood by those who had been brought up to understand them and which it was not always profitable to discuss with those who had not. She said that the working of these matters was carried in households, generation by generation, and that the households which carried them were now few and were becoming fewer. She said that the work of carrying them was a work that the persons doing the carrying did not always discuss, even with each other, because the discussion attracted attention that the work could not bear. She said that I would, in my life, meet two or three men who would understand without my having to say very much, and that with those men I might say a little more, and that with the rest of the world I might say nothing.*

*She said this to you when?*

*The year before she died. She died at Jerusalem in the spring of 410, six weeks after the news of the sack reached the monastery. She was eighty-two.*

*And you have come to Hippo because you have heard that I am one of the two or three.*

*I have come to Hippo because my grandmother named you, by name, before she died. She said: the bishop in Hippo is one. The priest in Bethlehem was another, in his way — the old scholar who has given his life to the scriptures there — but he will not last long now, and the work is moving south. She said the work would move south through Africa for the next century, and that I should follow it.*

She set the cup down. She had not drunk from it.

Augustine sat with what she had said. He sat with it for a long count. The lamp on the parlour table guttered once. He could feel, for the first time in his life, the bronze disc warm against the parchment in the upper shelf of the writing room three corridors away. He had felt it warm in his hand twice — once at Ostia twenty-four years ago, once on the night of the courier fourteen months ago. He had not, on either of those occasions, felt it from across rooms. He felt it from across rooms now. He did not let his face change.

*Daughter. What is the project?*

*A monastery, here in this province, funded from what remains of my husband's and my estates. The estates have been substantial; they will fund a community of perhaps forty for a long generation. The community will keep the daily round and the prayers and the works of charity that any monastery keeps. It will also, with your permission and your guidance, train a small number of those who join it in the practices my grandmother taught me. The training will be private. It will not appear in any rule. It will not be visible to the visitors from Carthage or the inspectors from Rome. The community will be, on the surface, a wholly orthodox foundation. Beneath the surface, it will be what I have come to ask you to help me make it.*

*What practices.*

*The reading of texts that have meaning at more than one level, with attention to the level at which the meaning is carried for those trained to read it. The writing of texts of the same kind. The keeping of records, by methods my grandmother taught me, that survive the loss of any one person. The cultivation of the small number of persons who, in each generation, can be trusted to do this work. The carrying-forward of what my grandmother said could not be carried forward by institutions, because institutions in our age are not lasting.*

*The institutions of our age are not lasting.*

*No, Bishop. They are not. You said it this morning at the rail. You said it with greater eloquence than I have just said it.*

She looked at him across the table. Her face was the face of a woman who had been giving away wealth for eight years and who had not, in the giving, lost the steadiness of someone who knew what the wealth was for. Augustine held her look. He held it for the count he might have held a look in a cathedral with a man about to be ordained as deacon. He did not let his face change.

*Daughter.*

*Bishop.*

*I will help you make it.*

*I had hoped you would.*

*There will be conditions.*

*Name them.*

*The first. The community will be your community. Not mine, not the diocese's, not Carthage's. You will hold it in your name and your husband's, and you will appoint the abbess and the rule. I will be available to you for guidance and for the training of the few you describe. I will not be the founder of this community in any document or in any record. The community will be your foundation. This is for your protection and for mine and for the community's.*

*Agreed.*

*The second. The training of the few will be in your hands and in the hands of those you appoint. I will teach the few directly when I am asked and when my health permits. I will not, however, write down the practices in any document. The transmission will be person to person. This is also for everyone's protection.*

*Agreed.*

*The third. Your husband must agree to all of this in advance and in writing, on the basis of his own conviction and not on the basis of mine or yours. He must understand what he is signing. The husband of an eight-year giver of estates has a right to understand what he is putting his name to.*

*He understands. He has been waiting for this for two years.*

*Then he will sign.*

*He will sign.*

*The fourth. There is a man in this diocese named Possidius, who is the bishop of Calama and was once my student. He will need to know enough of the project to be your protector if I am no longer alive to be it. He will not know everything. He will know what he needs to know. I will write to him this week.*

*Agreed.*

*The fifth. There is a moment that will come in the work — it may come next year, it may come in twenty — when the community will be approached by men who will seek what we are protecting, who will offer money or threaten violence in the hope of acquiring it. When that moment comes, you will not negotiate. You will withdraw. You will burn what cannot be moved and you will move what cannot be burned and you will go further south into the desert if the desert is what is left to you. The community is replaceable. The work is not. You will tell the abbess this on the day you appoint her, and you will tell the abbess after her on the day she is appointed, and you will tell every abbess after that until the moment comes.*

*Agreed.*

*Then we have an agreement.*

She extended her hand across the table. He took it. Her hand was small and cool and steady.

*Bishop. May I ask one further thing.*

*Ask.*

*My grandmother told me there were certain physical objects that were, in this work, of some importance. She did not tell me what they were. She did not tell me where they were. She said that in this generation there were three of them remaining in the world that she knew of. She said that one was at Jerusalem with her, that one had been with the priest in Bethlehem for thirty years, and that one had been, since the year of your baptism, with you.*

He held her look. He had heard the first sentence and the second sentence and the third sentence, and there was a moment — brief, not visible — in which thirty years of carrying a thing alone became, in the space of those three sentences, no longer alone. He did not let his face change. He held her look.

*Daughter. What I have or do not have is not a matter for the parlour at this hour.*

*No, Bishop. It is not. I do not ask. I tell you only that I know that something is. My grandmother told me, before she died, that what is in your keeping should not, on her opinion, ever leave your keeping until the man it is for arrives, and that if the man it is for had not arrived in your lifetime, you should pass it to a man whom you trusted in the next generation to wait for the man it was for, who might be in the third generation or the fourth, and that the wait might be very long. She said this with very great seriousness. She said it as the most serious thing she said to me.*

*I am grateful for the message.*

*Yes, Bishop.*

*I will not speak of the matter further tonight, nor will I speak of it any other night unless I judge that the speaking is required. You will not raise the matter again. We are clear?*

*We are clear.*

*Good. Drink the water.*

She drank the water.

When the cup was set down they spoke for some further time about the practical arrangements — the location of the foundation (Augustine recommended a small valley three miles from Tagaste that the family already owned), the timing (Pinianus to come north for the formal acts in the spring), the men to be approached for the work of building. By the time Eraclius knocked at the door at the end of the half-hour they had the shape of the project on a single sheet of wax, in her hand and in his alternately, in the careful Latin of two people who had both been doing administrative work for years.

She rose. She bowed. She drew the cloak around her and went out into the cloister.

Augustine sat at the table with the wax tablet in front of him and the cup of water she had drunk from at her elbow. He did not move for some minutes. Then he stood, took the wax tablet, blew out the parlour lamp, and went back to the writing room.

---

The bronze disc was warm.

He had not gone to it. He had felt it from across the cloister, from across the parlour, from the moment in the parlour when she had said *the priest in Bethlehem will not last long now and the work is moving south.* He had felt it warm against the parchment on the upper shelf the way a man feels a fire he is not looking at. He had not gone to it. He had finished the conversation and walked the cloister and come back to the writing room and put his hand on the door before he opened it, the way a man puts his hand on a door before he goes into a room where he has been told a thing has changed.

He opened the door. He went in. He went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box.

The disc was warm.

He set the box on the desk. He sat. He did not open the box. He did not need to. The first warmth, at Ostia, had been a question. The second, the night of the courier, had been an answer. This warmth he had no word for yet. He sat with his hand on the lid of the box and waited to see if a word came.

He put his hand on the box.

There was, in the empire, one man who could wrap a document of the kind he was wrapping. There were now, in the empire, two who knew the wrapping was going on. The number had been one for fourteen months. The second one had walked through the gate of the basilica three hours ago in the colour of the rain and had sat across the table from him and had drunk his water and had, in the careful sentences of the senatorial class, told him that her grandmother had named him. Her grandmother had named him. The priest in Bethlehem would not last long. The work was moving south.

He sat with his hand on the box.

After some time he wrote, on the wax tablet from the parlour, beneath the shape of the foundation: *Melania. Granddaughter. To be trusted to the limit of what she has been told. To be told no further.*

He underlined it once.

He covered the parchment of the work. He blew out the lamp. He went to compline, the last prayers of the night.

The autumn rain had begun again at the cathedral door. He stood in it for the count of three before he remembered which way the cell was, and then he remembered, and he went.
