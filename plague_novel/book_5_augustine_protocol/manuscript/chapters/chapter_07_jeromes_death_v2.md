# Chapter 9 — Jerome's Death (v2, thriller)

*Bethlehem, the third week of September 420 CE.*

The ink had dried on the last page of the Vulgate three months ago — his life's work: a single Latin Bible meant to replace every competing translation and become the one scripture the Latin Church would read for centuries. Jerome's hand cramped now when he held a stylus for more than a few minutes — the joints had been swelling since the spring, with the particular arthritis that came to scribes who had spent seven decades bent over parchment. The September heat in Bethlehem pressed against the stone walls of his cell, and he kept the window shuttered against it. Light hurt his eyes these days. The sounds from the courtyard — young monks reciting the morning psalms, the scrape of sandals on stone — reminded him too acutely of what he had once been.

Seventy-three years. Thirty-four of them in this monastery. The numbers felt abstract until he looked at his hands. The hands were spotted with liver marks. The hands tremored when he reached for the water cup beside his bed. His body was cataloguing its failures with the methodical precision he had once applied to manuscript variants.

On the small table beside him lay the papal edict of 397, the document that had made him rich in a currency he was only now learning to count. Twenty-three years since Pope Siricius had commissioned him to compile the definitive list of texts the Latin Church would not read. The Gospel of Mary. The Gospel of Thomas. The Secret Book of John. Thirty-seven texts in total, each one a thread he had cut from the tapestry of Christian thought.

He had argued for their setting-aside not from malice but from fear. The barbarians were at the gates — literally, as it had turned out. Unity of doctrine, he had argued at the time, would preserve truth when the empire fell. A single, authoritative scripture in the language of power. One Church, one text, one voice speaking with imperial authority.

The taste in his mouth, when he thought of it now, was metallic. Rome had fallen anyway, ten years ago. The Goths held southern Gaul and the Visigoths held Hispania and the imperial provinces of the west were being administered by men whose grandfathers had not spoken Latin. Britain had been let go. The empire he had helped the Church bind itself to was failing.

Jerome pushed himself upright against the stone wall. The cold of the wall came through the wool of his outer robe and into his shoulder blades. His breathing had grown shallow over the summer; each inhalation was a conscious effort.

The walk to the scriptorium required more effort than it should have. His feet shuffled against the stone floor; twice he had to stop and rest his hand against the wall. The monastery's corridors were carved into the hillside, following the natural contours of the rock, and the uneven floor had never bothered him before. Each slight rise felt now like a mountain.

Brother Marcus looked up from his copying as Jerome entered the scriptorium, but said nothing. The young monk had learned not to offer assistance unless directly asked.

*Leave me.*

Marcus gathered his materials and went out. The sound of his sandals faded into the deeper silence of the monastery at rest.

Jerome moved to the lectern where his personal manuscripts were kept. Beneath a cloth cover, untouched for months, lay his annotated copy of the Septuagint — the Greek Old Testament the early Church had used for three centuries before Jerome argued it was an inferior substitute for the original Hebrew. The Hebrew was older, more authentic, closer to the original revelation; that had been his position in every scholarly debate, every letter to Constantinople, every defence of his Latin translation.

He lifted the cloth and opened the manuscript to a random page. The margins were dense with his own handwriting — arguments and counter-arguments in the careful script he had perfected as a young man in Rome. Here, a note challenging the Greek translation of a particular Hebrew word. There, a cross-reference to a variant reading that supported his Latin rendering. Page after page of intellectual combat, the written record of a war he had fought for fifty years.

Looking at those margins now, he felt something shift in his understanding the way a damaged passage of text suddenly resolves into meaning. The arguments were still valid. The scholarship was still sound. But the purpose for which he had built the arguments — the reason he had fought so hard for Hebrew over Greek and for Latin over everything else — that purpose had been built on an assumption that was now proving catastrophically wrong.

He had assumed, when he took up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would last. He had assumed it into his fifties, into his seventies, with the certainty of a man who had never watched an institution die. He had now watched one dying for ten years. He set his hand flat on the open page. The parchment was cold. What he had built had been captured by the institution; the institution was not what he had built it for.

He sat with this. The chair was too hard. His back was cold against the wood. He turned a few more pages of the Septuagint and stopped. The dust of the lectern was on his hand.

A knock at the scriptorium door. Brother Marcus came in with a leather satchel — the kind couriers used for long-distance correspondence.

*Reports from Gaul and Africa. The prior thought you should see them.*

Jerome accepted the satchel without comment. Marcus withdrew. The silence returned like water flowing back into a depression.

The first scroll was from Marseilles. The Visigothic king Wallia had completed the consolidation of his hold on south-western Gaul; Theodoric was now contending for primacy among the Visigothic nobility and the federate treaties with the Romans were under renegotiation. Two monastic communities in the lower Rhône valley had been abandoned in the spring. The libraries of those communities had been carried, by such brothers as had remained, into Lérins and into the bishop's house at Arles.

The second was from Tarraco, in the eastern part of Hispania, where what was left of the imperial administration was managing the Vandal kingdom in the south of the peninsula by treaty and by silence. The Vandal galleys were ranging the western Mediterranean now; the merchants of Carthage were doubling their insurance premiums on cargo bound for Italy and Gaul. There was, the Tarraconian writer noted, a question of how long the Vandal kingdom would remain content with the southern part of the peninsula and how soon it would look across the strait.

The third scroll was from Carthage. The senior bishop of Africa wrote that the African church remained intact, that the harvest of the previous year had been good, that the work against the Pelagian teachers — who held that men could save themselves without God's grace, a position both Jerome and Augustine had condemned — was being managed jointly by the diocese of Hippo and the diocese of Carthage with the cooperation of the priests at Rome. The bishop of Hippo, the writer noted, was sixty-six and was at present completing the seventeenth of an intended twenty-two books of a long work on the city of God. He was well. He was working. He sent his greetings to the priest of Bethlehem and would, he had asked the writer to say, be glad of the priest of Bethlehem's prayers.

Jerome set the three scrolls down beside the Septuagint.

He sat with them.

The work of the bishop of Hippo had been going on for ten years. Jerome had read the first ten books, in a fair copy sent to him in Holy Week, and had been reading the eleventh and twelfth — sent in a lesser copy by a Carthaginian bookseller in the spring — across the slow weeks of the summer. He had read them as a dying man read, which was to say with the attention of a man who had nothing left in his life that was more important than the reading. He had recognised, as he read, what the bishop of Hippo had been building: an argument that the Christian world survived not because Rome preserved it, not because one authoritative Church controlled it, but because the idea itself was too distributed, too alive in too many people, for any single catastrophe to kill. The recognition had come in installments. It was complete now.

The bishop of Hippo had been right. Not in every detail — there were places, in Books seven and eight, where the historical scholarship was thin and where Jerome would have written marginal corrections in the years when he had still been the man who wrote marginal corrections — but in the essential thing he had been right. The institutions on which the Church had built the last hundred years were not lasting, and were not going to last, and the only Church that would survive what was coming was the Church that had not been built upon them.

Jerome had spent seventy years building upon them.

He sat at the lectern with his hands flat on the wood. He did not weep. He had not wept in some years; the tear ducts of an old man are not what the tear ducts of a younger man were, and the things that would once have made him weep made him, now, only sit very still. He sat very still. The September heat pressed against the shutters. He could hear, very faintly through the stone, the brothers at sext, the midday office.

After some time he called for Marcus to bring him writing materials.

The letter took him most of the afternoon. He wrote in his own hand rather than dictating, with frequent rests when his hand grew too unsteady to form legible words. Some communications were too personal for witnesses, even trusted ones.

*Augustine,*

*I have read the seventeen books of the work that have come to me, and I am writing what I should have written ten years ago and did not write because I was not yet ready to write it.*

*You have been right that the Church's survival does not depend on controlling a single authoritative text locked inside a single institution. You have been right that meaning, distributed across a thousand communities reading in their own tongues, outlasts empire. I built a fortress. You have been building a river. The fortress will fall with Rome. The river will find new ground. I thought you were naive when you began to say these things in the year of the sack. I thought you were dangerous when you continued to say them after. I have, over the seven years between then and now, gone from thinking you naive to thinking you mistaken to thinking you incomplete to thinking you correct, and the going has been the going of a man who was watching the world describe itself to him in the words you had been using.*

*The letter I sent you in the year after the sack was a tactical letter. I wrote it to keep you cooperative on other matters. It contained sentences I did not, when I wrote them, believe. I am writing now to tell you that I have come to believe them, and that the believing has cost me what little I had left to lose, and that I am not sorry for the costing.*

He paused. His hand was shaking. He set the stylus down for a count of perhaps thirty and waited for the hand to be steady. The hand was not entirely steady when he picked the stylus back up but it was steady enough.

*I have done what I have done. I do not name the men with whom I have done it; you know them or will. They are not evil men. They are men of my disposition who made the calculation I made and who have not, at their ages, the strength to unmake the calculation. They will continue. The work they have done with me will continue. It will continue in the form it has taken, which is the form of an institution making the choices an institution makes. You will recognise its work. You will not need a list from me to recognise it.*

*I am sending you, with this letter, my personal copy of the Septuagint. The margins of the Septuagint contain my arguments — fifty years of them, in my own hand — for the Hebrew over the Greek and for the institutional preservation of scripture over the distributed preservation of meaning. The arguments are sound on their own ground. The ground is the wrong ground. I am giving them to you as primary material. Use my errors as evidence of the position you have been taking. Let them serve, in the long copying that will follow you, the work you are doing.*

*I have, in the second-last week of my life, finally said what I should have said twenty years ago. I have said it to one man. I do not have the strength to say it to more, and I do not think it would be useful to say it to more. The man I have said it to will know what to do with it.*

*I do not ask forgiveness. I am too old to claim that intention excuses consequence and too tired to maintain the fiction that I did not understand what I was choosing. I ask only that you use what I have learned, and that you finish what I could not bring myself to begin.*

*In the Christ we both serve, who is perhaps not the Christ I argued for in my forties, but is, I think, the Christ both of us have been arriving at across these forty years,*

*Jerome of Stridon.*

He read the letter through once. He did not change a word. He folded it and sealed it with his own seal, in wax he had to warm against the lamp because his hands were too cool now to soften wax by holding it. He wrote on the outside, in a hand that was almost the hand he had used as a young man and almost the hand he had used in the last year and was, in fact, neither: *To Aurelius Augustinus, Bishop of Hippo Regius.*

He called for Marcus.

*Three copies of this letter. By three different routes. The Septuagint with the principal copy. Pack it in oiled leather. If the ship goes down between here and Africa, the leather will float for some hours. If it washes ashore somewhere, it may be found by someone who will know what it is.*

Marcus took the package. He took it without speaking. He had, in the nineteen years he had been with Jerome, learned when not to speak.

*Marcus.*

*Father.*

*I will not see your face again.*

Marcus was silent. After a moment he inclined his head.

*Father. The brothers will be at vespers in an hour.*

*Yes. Go to vespers. Pray for me. I will pray for you in such time as I have.*

Marcus went out with the package. The door closed.

Jerome returned to his cell as the sun was setting behind the hills of Judaea. The light at this hour, through the small high window, was the colour of dried blood on the stone of the western wall. He had watched this light from this cell for fifty-six Septembers. He watched it now.

---

Sleep came in fragments over the following days. He would drift into unconsciousness for an hour or two and then wake with his heart hammering and his breath shallow. Brother Luke, the monastery's physician, offered poppy extract for the pain. He refused. He wanted to remain conscious for whatever time remained.

On the thirtieth of September the pain in his chest grew sharp and constant. He dismissed his attendants and lay alone on his narrow bed. He could hear the brothers at evening prayers through the stone of the wall. The Latin phrases — some of them his own translations — sounded strange to him now, like echoes from a conversation he could no longer quite remember having.

He did not feel forgiveness. He did not feel the peace that the deathbed accounts of the Christian dead always claimed. He felt the weight of pattern: the sense of himself as one life inside the longer shape of a movement that had been unfolding for three thousand years and would continue unfolding long after his name was forgotten. The thought brought neither comfort nor terror. It was simply what was, like the stone walls of the cell or the steady rhythm of his failing heart.

He looked at his hands.

The ink stains under his fingernails were the ink stains of forty years of copying. They would not, when his body was washed for burial, come out. He had once, in his fifties, tried to scrub them out for a formal occasion at Constantinople; the stains had not gone. They were now, he supposed, what would mark his hands for the seven days of the public lying-in-state and for the centuries of his portrait being copied in monastic chronicles. The portraits would have the ink stains. The copying-monks would put them in.

His last coherent thought was a question. Whether the Greek text he had fought against for fifty years would arrive safely at Hippo. Whether the bishop of Hippo would be able to read his margin notes as something other than the stubborn arguments of a dead opponent.

Then the thought went and there was no further thought.

*Hippo Regius, the third week of November 420.*

The package came on a Wednesday in the second post of the day. The courier had crossed from Caesarea Maritima to Carthage and from Carthage by coastal ship to the harbour at Hippo, and the package had been on his person for fifty-three days. Possidius brought it to the writing room without knocking, which had not been his practice in twenty years.

*Aurelius. From Bethlehem.*

Augustine looked up from the desk. He had been on the third draft of the chapter on Cain in Book fifteen and had been, for the better part of the morning, in the wrong frame of mind for it. He looked at the package in Possidius's hands. The package was wrapped in oiled leather and was sealed with Jerome's personal seal in red wax — a simple cross inscribed with a stylus the way Jerome had always inscribed it.

He set down the bone stylus.

*Bring it.*

Possidius set the package on the desk. He did not leave the room. Augustine cut the leather wrap with his thumbnail. Inside was the principal copy of the letter, sealed separately, and the codex of the Septuagint, packed in a second layer of oiled leather to protect against sea-water. Augustine cut the seal of the letter and read it through once. He read it through a second time. Then he set the letter on the desk and put both hands flat on the desk on either side of it for a long count.

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*Jerome is dead.*

*Jerome is dead.*

He had known it before he opened the letter. The package had told him; the way a man knows, before he reads a particular letter, that the letter is a particular kind of letter. Jerome had been ill since the spring. The reports through the post had been getting darker since June. He had been writing replies that ignored what the reports were saying and that talked instead about the work, because the work had been the conversation between them for twenty years and the work was the conversation he wished to continue having. Jerome had been answering in the same voice. Jerome had been answering until the spring.

Augustine sat with the letter on the desk in front of him.

He had, in his sixty-six years, lost a great many people. He had lost Monnica. He had lost Adeodatus. He had lost the boy in the night of the courier whose name he had not, in ten years, spoken aloud. He had lost Pelagius, who was not dead but was in a desert in which he would die without further attention from the world. He had lost Atticus and the silence at Sketis and the man at Lérins who had, in the end, narrowed what he taught. He had lost the senior bishop at Carthage who had ordained him. He had lost, two years ago, the deacon of the synagogue at Hippo who had smiled into his hand at the rail of the basilica nine years before. He had lost his right knee, in some functional sense, three years ago. He was an old man, and old men lose, and the losing had become, in his sixties, a thing he had stopped counting.

He had not known how he would feel when Jerome went.

He felt, sitting at the desk with Possidius standing across from him, neither vindicated nor bereaved nor surprised. He felt, instead, the small particular silence that arrived when a man one had been speaking to for forty years stopped speaking, and the shape of the silence was the shape of forty years of conversation, and the silence was not a hole but a presence — the presence of everything that had been said sitting now on the desk in his own writing room with no further sentence behind it.

He picked up the codex of the Septuagint. He opened it.

It was Jerome's own copy. The margins had been densely annotated in Jerome's own hand. Augustine knew the hand at the first letter. He had been reading Jerome's hand for thirty years. The hand had aged across the codex — the early notes were in the small precise script of Jerome at fifty, the later ones in the slightly larger and slightly less steady hand of Jerome at seventy. The marginal arguments were the marginal arguments of a man who had been arguing one position for fifty years.

He turned to a page at random. Psalm 137. The song of exile. Jerome's first marginal note, in the precise script: *Hebrew "remembers" carries the connotation of active memorial, not passive nostalgia. The Greek translation weakens the imperative. We must not merely remember Jerusalem. We must rebuild it.*

Below that, in the larger and less steady hand, in a darker ink, written some years later: *Or perhaps the remembering is the rebuilding. Perhaps the act of transmission preserves more than the institution ever could.*

Augustine touched the second note with the pad of his finger. The ink had been laid down hard; he could feel the small indentation of the stylus through the parchment. Jerome had pressed the stylus the way a man presses a stylus when he is writing something he has not before said.

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

Possidius looked at the page over his shoulder.

*He knew it was the most useful thing he could have done.*

*He knew.*

They sat for some time. The brazier was unlit because the November fog this year was warm. The cathedral bell, two cloisters away, began to ring for the half-hour of prayer before vespers, and the bell sounded the way the bell had sounded for nine years, and the bell would sound that way after Augustine and would sound that way after the basilica had gone, in the hands of whatever men in whatever century took up the work of replacing the bell when the bell wore out.

Augustine closed the codex. He rested his hand on the cover for a count.

*I should feel vindicated,* he said. *Jerome spent seventy years building the Christian world the wrong way. He helped to build the very thing we have been trying to keep the work from being captured by. Now he has confessed it, in his own hand, with the working materials for what we will need. I should feel vindicated.*

Possidius waited.

*I do not feel vindicated. I feel the want of him. He was wrong about almost everything that mattered, and he was wrong for the reasons of a man who wanted what we wanted and saw the means to it differently and chose the means he chose with his whole heart. I would rather have him in Bethlehem, wrong, writing me letters I would have to answer with effort, than have his confession on my desk. The confession is the more useful gift. The wanting is the wanting.*

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*It is permitted to feel both.*

*It is permitted.*

He sat with his hand on the cover of the codex. After some further time he took down a clean sheet of parchment and set it in front of him. He took up the bone stylus. He wrote, at the head of the sheet: *To Jerome of Stridon, priest at Bethlehem.*

He had begun a letter to a dead man before. He had not, in that case, finished it. He thought he would finish this one. He thought he would not send it. He thought he would put it, when it was finished, into the chest with the iron lock on the upper shelf, where the wax under-drafts of the books were, and that he would leave it there for whoever, in some century, would open the chest and read it and would know that the bishop of Hippo had written to the priest of Bethlehem after the priest of Bethlehem was dead and would understand, from the writing, what kind of friendship that had been.

He began the letter.

He had not written more than three lines when the bell rang at the cathedral for vespers. He set the stylus down. He covered the parchment. He rose. Possidius rose with him. They walked together along the cloister in the warm November fog to the side door of the cathedral, where Stephen was waiting at the door with the censer, and they went in.

In the writing room, on the desk, the codex of the Septuagint sat where Augustine had set it down. The wax of Jerome's seal, broken now, lay on the linen beside the leather wrap. The bone stylus lay on the parchment of the unfinished letter. The lamp burned low and burned even and did not flicker.
