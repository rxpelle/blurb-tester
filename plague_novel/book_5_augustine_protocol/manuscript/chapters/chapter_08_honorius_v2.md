# Chapter — Honorius (v2, thriller)

*Hippo Regius, the third week of April 422 CE.*

The work that morning was the work it had been all month, which was the third book of the long polemic against Julian of Eclanum. Augustine had begun the polemic in 419 and had been on it, on and off, for two and a half years. The book on the desk was the third of an intended four. The fourth would, on his present rate of attention to it, take him into the late autumn at minimum and possibly into the winter. He did not, on the morning of the third week of April, expect to finish the fourth book before the year's end.

Possidius was at the side table.

He had come down from Calama on the Monday for the regional council that would be held at Hippo at the end of the week. He had been expected on the Sunday and had come a day late, on a road that had been bad in the previous week's rain, with a small dispatch box of correspondence Augustine had been waiting for since February. He had set the dispatch box on the side table on his arrival and had not, in three days, opened it; the council preparation had taken the morning hours and the polemic had taken the rest. Augustine had not asked about the dispatch box. He had assumed that whatever it contained had been judged by Possidius to be the kind of thing a bishop took up in a quiet half-hour and not the kind of thing a bishop interrupted his anti-Julian work to deal with on the morning of arrival.

Stephen knocked at the door at the half-hour after the second hour. Three short.

*Father.*

*Come.*

Stephen came in. He had a small wax tablet in his hand. He had aged into the master of novices in the last six years; he was fifty-three now and looked it; the hair at his temples had begun to grey in the year after the Donatist business had finally settled at Calama, and the greying had progressed since at the slow rate of the greying of a man whose health was good and whose disposition did not produce the kind of tension that grey men's hair fast.

*Father. The novice from the lower-town school is at the door. He has the question about the office of compline.*

*The question we discussed on Tuesday.*

*The same.*

*Tell him to come back at the third hour. I will have the answer for him then.*

*Yes, father.*

Stephen looked at Possidius at the side table. Possidius looked up from the dispatch box, which he had at last opened and which he had been sorting on the table. The two of them held a look for the count of two. The look was the look the two of them held in Augustine's presence now, which was the look of two old friends acknowledging each other across the room without occupying any further attention either of them might owe to each other in the room. Stephen inclined his head. Possidius inclined his back. Stephen went out.

Augustine watched the door close. He took up the iron pen.

He had, in the nineteen years since the Calama summer, watched the look between them across many rooms at many half-hours. He had watched it become, in the first three or four years after the summer, the careful look of two men who had decided not to look at each other in the way they had begun, in the summer at Hippo, to look at each other. He had watched it become, in the years after that, the steadier look of two men who had built, in whatever rooms in whatever houses the work brought them both to, an arrangement that was not a thing he was permitted to ask about. He had long since stopped requiring himself not to know it. He had not, in the nineteen years, raised the matter with either of them. He had not, in the nineteen years, raised it with himself except at long intervals. The intervals had grown longer. He had stopped, in the last three or four years, raising it at all. He had room in the work for the look.

He went back to the polemic.

Possidius, at the side table, said:

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*The dispatch box has a letter from the senior bishop's office about the council that was not in the original dispatch I had at Calama. He wishes to add the question of the appointments at Cirta to the agenda.*

*The third day.*

*He does not specify.*

*Tell him the third day. The first two days are full. We will put the appointments at Cirta in the morning of the third day before the financial reconciliation.*

*I will write it.*

Possidius wrote it. The dictation took a quarter of an hour, in the careful manner of a bishop drafting a letter to the senior bishop on a matter of council protocol. Augustine listened to the dictation with one ear and continued the paragraph of the polemic with the other. The paragraph was a paragraph on the matter of infant baptism that Julian had raised in the second book of his own treatise, and the paragraph was the kind of paragraph that required Augustine's full attention every fourth or fifth sentence and could be done in the background of other work for the sentences in between.

When the dictation was finished, Possidius set the iron pen down. He stood. He came to the desk.

*Aurelius. I will go down to the chapter house for the morning office. I will be back at the noon meal.*

*Yes.*

*The polemic.*

*Yes.*

*Is it going.*

*It is going as the polemic of a man of sixty-eight against the polemic of a man of forty-six goes. The man of forty-six has more energy. The man of sixty-eight has more time, in which to be patient with the man of forty-six. It is, on the whole, a fair fight.*

Possidius smiled. He had been smiling at things Augustine said in the writing room for twenty-five years and the smile had not changed greatly across those years; it was, when it appeared, the smile of a man who recognised the cadence of a particular kind of dry remark and had found, in the course of long acquaintance, that the dry remark was usually preceded by a particular small adjustment of the eyebrow that he had learned to watch for.

*I will see you at the noon meal,* he said.

He went out.

Augustine wrote on for the rest of the morning. The cough that had begun in the previous winter and that he had been intending to mention to the physician at some quiet hour was not, on this morning, much in evidence; the morning was warm and the shutters were open to the courtyard. From below came the sound of water being drawn at the well, and the smell of bread from the harbour quarter that the lay sister had begun buying in the last week, and the light on the desk was the flat white light of a North African April at the second hour, which made the iron letters on the parchment look very black. The right knee that had begun to be bad in the wet weather of the autumn was, on this morning, manageable. He worked. The polemic accumulated.

At the noon meal he ate with Possidius and Eraclius at the small table in the small parlour, which was the arrangement when no other guests were present. Eraclius was thirty-eight. He had been at the basilica for nineteen years, since he had come up from the lower-town school as a junior at nineteen and had been taken into the household at the recommendation of the master of the school, who had identified the boy as the kind of boy who would, if given to a bishop's house at the right age, become the kind of administrator a bishop needed. The master had been right. Eraclius had become the kind of administrator a bishop needed, and had, in the last year, begun to become the kind of administrator who would, on the present trajectory of Augustine's age and Eraclius's competence, be the bishop himself when Augustine was no longer in the chair. Augustine had been thinking about this trajectory for some months and had not, in those months, said anything about it to Eraclius. Eraclius had, on his own evidence at the small table over the noon meals, been thinking about it too and had not, in his own months, said anything to Augustine.

The three of them ate. They spoke about the council. They did not speak about the polemic. They did not speak about what was coming when Augustine was no longer at the desk in the writing room. They spoke about the bread, which had been better in the last week than it had been in the previous month because the lay sister who managed the kitchen had begun buying from a different baker in the harbour quarter; they spoke about the second of the council's procedural questions; they spoke about the rain that had been on the road from Calama. Possidius asked Eraclius about a young brother in the household who had been ill in the spring. Eraclius reported that the young brother was now well.

The meal ended. Possidius went down to the cathedral for the noon office. Eraclius went to the lay parlour to deal with the day's correspondence. Augustine went back to the writing room.

He worked for the rest of the afternoon on the polemic. He did not, that afternoon or any afternoon of that month or the next, take down the bronze box from the upper shelf.

*Hippo Regius, the second week of September 423 CE.*

The dispatch came up from the harbour at the third hour of the afternoon by a regular post-courier from Carthage who had ridden the coast road in nineteen days and who carried, in his satchel, a sealed packet from the senior bishop's office that had been forwarded from the imperial post at Carthage with the additional fee of the priority handling that had been on the senior bishop's standing instruction since the spring. The courier was tired. The mule was tired. Eraclius received them at the gate at the third hour and brought the packet up to the writing room without delay. He did not knock. He came through the door with the packet in his hand and set it on the corner of the desk and stood at the desk with his hands at his sides.

*Aurelius. From the senior bishop's office. The seal is the imperial.*

Augustine set the iron pen down. He looked at the packet. The seal at the join of the leather was the seal of the western imperial chancery — a smaller seal than the eastern, in the dark wax the western chancery had been using since the year of the consulship of Asclepiodotus, and pressed by the right hand of an under-clerk and not by the chancellor himself. The seal told him, before the seal was broken, that the news was the kind of news that the chancery wished sent to the African church in its formal channel and not by the slower route of common dispatch.

He cut the seal with his thumbnail.

The letter was three lines in Latin and one line in Greek. The Latin was: *To the senior bishop of Africa, with the concurrence of the imperial vicar at Sirmium and the consistory at Ravenna, greeting. The Augustus Honorius died on the fifteenth day of August in the present year, of an obstruction of the chest, in the imperial palace at Ravenna. The succession proceeds as the imperial council shall determine. The provinces are to continue in their administrations.* The line in Greek was the formal acknowledgment to be returned to the chancery by the receiving bishop.

Augustine read the letter through twice.

Honorius had been emperor for twenty-eight years. He had been emperor since Augustine's first year as bishop. He had been the emperor under whom Augustine's two greatest fights — against the Donatists, who had split the African church for a century, and against Pelagius, whose doctrine that men could earn their way to God without grace had consumed twenty years of Augustine's writing — had been settled by imperial force, not persuasion. He had been a weak emperor. He had been a weak emperor for twenty-eight years. A strong emperor in the western provinces between 395 and 423 would have done more harm than the weak one had done.

*Eraclius.*

*Yes.*

*The senior bishop will need a formal acknowledgment by return. I will write it tonight. The courier may stay in the lay quarters and leave at first light. Pay him the standard. Tell him there will be a small purse for the speed of the return.*

*Yes.*

*The acknowledgment will go with the standard formula. There will be no comment on the succession in our letter. The succession is none of the African church's business until the consistory has settled it.*

*Yes.*

*Eraclius.*

*Yes.*

*Send word to Possidius at Calama. He will know already by the end of the week through his own channels, but he should hear it from us first. A short letter. I will write it tonight with the senior bishop's.*

*Yes.*

Eraclius went out. Augustine sat at the desk with the imperial letter in his hand. The afternoon light was the light of mid-September, which was a good light at Hippo, and the writing-room shutters were half-closed against the warmth of the day, which was the warmth of an autumn that had not yet committed to being autumn. He read the letter a third time. He set it on the desk. He did not, for a count of perhaps three hundred, take up the iron pen.

He thought about what Honorius's death meant for the African provinces.

He thought it would mean, in the immediate term, very little. The imperial machinery in Africa was the machinery of the Numidian and Carthaginian administrations and it would, on the present competence of the proconsuls, continue to function whether the imperial succession at Ravenna took six months or three years to settle. The African church was, on the practical accounting of its day-to-day work, not particularly dependent on the imperial succession; the African church had its own rhythms and would continue them. The Pelagian proceedings would continue. The Donatist watchfulness would continue. The daily round would continue. The polemic against Julian would continue. The City of God, on which Augustine had finished the seventeenth book in the spring and was now on the eighteenth, would continue.

He thought it would mean, in the longer term, something else.

The longer term was the term in which a weak emperor's death created a vacuum and the vacuum drew in whoever had the strength to fill it. The candidates with the strength were not, on the present mapping of the western provinces, men whose strength was the kind of strength a province under their authority would benefit from. There were the Visigothic federates in southern Gaul. There was the rump of the western army under whatever generalissimo the consistory would settle on. There were the various imperial relations in the east who had some claim. There were the Vandal kingdom in southern Hispania, which had been consolidating since the previous decade and which would, on the present rate of its consolidation, look across the strait at Mauretania within ten years.

He sat with this.

He took up the iron pen at the seventh hour. He wrote the formal acknowledgment to the senior bishop's office in the Greek line and in the Latin paragraph the chancery required. He wrote the short letter to Possidius. He sealed both letters and set them in the tray for the morning post.

He stood. The right knee was bad in the late afternoon. He went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box.

He had taken it down, in the eighteen years since the Calama summer, on three or four occasions a year on no particular reason; the count had not changed. He had not, on any of those occasions, found the disc warm. He opened the box now in the slow way of a man performing a ritual whose meaning he was not certain of and whose discontinuance he had not, on any of the occasions, considered. The hinges sounded.

The disc was cold.

He held it for a count of perhaps fifty. He thought of his mother at Ostia thirty-six years before with her hand on the rim of the well. He thought of the words she had given him about the work: *I have been the watcher. You will be the carrier.* He had carried the box for thirty-six years. He had been the watcher and the carrier both. He had been, for thirty-six years, a man waiting for a thing that had not, in those years, come.

He set the disc back. He closed the box. He returned the box to the shelf.

He went to compline, the last office of the night.

*Hippo Regius, the third week of May 424 CE.*

The letter from Jerusalem came up from the harbour with a returning grain ship that had touched at Caesarea Maritima in the third week of April and at Carthage at the beginning of May. It had been six months on the road and at sea. The seal was Melania's own, which she had used since she had become the head of the foundation on the Mount of Olives the year before, in succession to her grandmother's second deputy, who had died in the autumn at the age of seventy-three. The seal was a simple cross inscribed in wax by the side of a thumb. The address was in her own hand.

Augustine cut the seal at the desk in the late morning, with the spring light coming in through the half-opened shutters and the brazier in the corner unlit because the May at Hippo had been warm.

The letter was nine pages.

Melania wrote rarely now. She had written four times since 416, which was the year she had left Africa for Jerusalem with her dying husband who had not, in the end, died for years. She had written on her grandmother's death; on the establishment of her own administration of the foundation; on the news of the priest at Bethlehem in the autumn of his death; on a small matter of a young Gaulish woman who had come to her foundation at Jerusalem with a letter of introduction from the bishop at Lérins. The letters had been brief. The letter on the desk in front of Augustine was not brief. He had not, since 416, had a long letter from her.

He read it through.

She wrote about Pinianus. Pinianus was sixty-one and was failing. The illness that had been with him on and off since the Carthage years had returned in the autumn and had not, in the spring, gone again. The physicians at the foundation gave him perhaps two years. Melania, in the ninth page of the letter, wrote that she had begun to make the arrangements that would be necessary at the foundation when she was no longer the wife of a senator with the senator's name to invoke at the imperial chancery in Constantinople, which she had been invoking, with care, since the consolidation of her administration at the foundation; and that she had been thinking, in the long evenings she had been spending at his bedside, of the conversation she and Augustine had had in the small parlour at Hippo on the wet evening of the second week of November in the year of the Pelagius work, which had been thirteen years past now and which she still kept, on her honest accounting of what she had been given in her life, among the four or five hours that had mattered to her most.

Augustine set the letter on the desk. He did not, for a long count, turn the page. He thought of the small parlour. He thought of the wet evening. He had not known, on that evening, that he was giving her something she would carry for thirteen years. He had been giving himself something. He had not, in the thirteen years, known what to do with the knowledge that the exchange had been equal.

She wrote, in the page after that, about the work in the east. The foundation continued. Two new young women had come up from the bishop at Caesarea in the spring. The Gaulish woman from Lérins had become the mistress of the small infirmary in the year previous and was, on Melania's account, the kind of woman whose hands the work would be safe in for the next thirty years if the foundation lasted that long. The man at Constantinople whom Melania's grandmother had named, who had been one of the four when Melania had left Africa, had died in the autumn of 422; she had had the news in the spring. The number of those who knew — who had been told of the disc, the instruction, and the man it was carried for — was now three, on her present mapping. There was Augustine. There was Possidius. There was Melania. The man Augustine had spoken of at the gate at Hippo thirteen years before — the man with the small mark in his Latin, who would arrive at the foundation if he came at all and would ask after the priest at Bethlehem and after the bishop at Hippo in that order — had not come. The priest at Bethlehem being now four years dead made the first question a test in a different way; only someone who did not know the priest was dead would fail it.

Augustine read the page twice.

She wrote, in the second-last page, about a thing she had heard, in March, from a merchant of Carthage who was in business with a freedman of her household and who had come up to Caesarea on the spring trade and had taken a meal at the foundation. The merchant had said that a Vandal force out of southern Hispania had taken a small coastal town in northern Mauretania Tingitana in the previous October and had held it through the winter and was, on the report that had reached Carthage in February, fortifying it as a base. The merchant had mentioned the matter without weight; he had been talking about trade conditions in the western Mediterranean and had brought the Vandal town up as one of several small developments that had affected his year's accounts. Melania had not, in March, attached weight to the matter either. She had, in the weeks since, found that she could not stop thinking about it.

She wrote, in the last paragraph, that she did not know when she would write again. She closed the letter with her grandmother's blessing in her grandmother's words. She signed with her own name and not the title she had assumed at the foundation.

Augustine read the letter through again from the beginning.

He sat with it for a long count.

The Vandal town in Mauretania Tingitana was the first specific Vandal action on the African coast that had reached him by any channel. He had known, since the year of Honorius's death, that the Vandal kingdom in Hispania would eventually look across the strait. He had not known they had begun to look. The taking of the small town in October, on the casual report of a Carthaginian merchant in March, was not yet the beginning of the crossing; it was the small specific signal that the crossing had become, on the Vandal calculation, a thing worth scouting for. The full crossing, on Augustine's reading of how migrating peoples managed these things, would come not in the next year or the year after; it would come when the conditions in Hispania required it, which would be in five years or ten or fifteen, on the present rate of the imperial chaos. He could not, on the morning of the third week of May 424, know which year. He could know only that the crossing was now coming.

He took up the iron pen.

He drafted the reply to Melania across the afternoon. He wrote about the polemic and about the City of God and about the daily round at Hippo and about Possidius, who was well. He wrote about Eraclius, whom Melania had met once on her last visit to Hippo before going to Jerusalem and who was now thirty-nine and was, on Augustine's careful eye, the man who would be the bishop after Augustine. He wrote about Pinianus in the careful sentences a man wrote about another man's dying husband whom he had never met. He wrote about Melania's grandmother. He did not write about the Vandal town. He had decided, at some point in the slow drafting, that the matter of the Vandal town would be carried in the corner of Melania's letter where she had written it and did not need to be carried also in his reply; he wrote, in the closing paragraph, that he was glad of her letter and that he would, with God's permission, write again.

He sealed the letter.

He set it in the tray for the next eastward post, which would be the first ship of June.

He sat at the desk with the empty inkwell at his elbow and the lamp not yet lit and the late afternoon light slanting across the shutter. He thought of the Vandal town. He thought of Melania at Jerusalem with her dying husband. He thought of the man at Constantinople who had died in 422 and whom he had never met and whom he had, in the eight years between Melania's departure and the man's death, never written to. The number was three. The number had been eight when his mother had given him the box at Ostia in 387 and had been four since the summer of 416 and was now three.

He went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box.

He opened it. The hinges sounded. He took out the disc.

The disc was cold.

He held it for a count of perhaps fifty. He thought, sitting at the desk in the late afternoon of the third week of May 424 with the cold bronze in his hand, that the disc had been cold for thirty-five years now and that the man it was for had not, in those thirty-five years, arrived in the household at Hippo or at any other house Augustine had been in or had heard of. He thought that the man might not, in the years remaining to him, arrive. He thought that the man might arrive in the year remaining to him in such a way that Augustine would not recognise him. He thought that the man might arrive in some house Augustine would never see in some century that would not be Augustine's. He thought that the carrying was the carrying.

He set the disc back in the box. The hinges sounded again. He closed the lid. He returned the box to the upper shelf behind the rolls of accounts.

He took up the iron pen. He continued the eighteenth book of the City of God. He worked until the lamp went out.
