# Chapter — After Calama (v2, thriller)

*Hippo Regius, the second week of August 403 CE.*

Augustine was at the gate with Stephen. The gate arch held the morning's heat the way Hippo stone held it in August, which was to give it back through the soles of the feet. The harbour road was white.

In seven years as bishop of Hippo he had received a great many carts on that road. He had not received one carrying Possidius.

The gate-keeper had been at the door of the bishop's residence at the noon hour after the junior officer had come up from the gate; Eraclius — who was nineteen now and had been at the basilica for nine months as a junior in the household — had received the news in the corridor and had brought it directly to the writing room, knocking three times in the way he had been told to knock when news could not wait. Augustine had set down the stylus. He had asked Eraclius two questions and had received the answers and had stood. He had walked to the door without speaking. Stephen, who had been at the small side table copying out a letter to the priest at Tubursicu, had stood with him and had walked to the door behind him.

That had been the second hour. The two of them had been at the gate since.

The cart came up the harbour road at the second hour of the afternoon, in the slow methodical way a cart with a wounded man on the floor of it came up a road that had not been mended in three seasons. It had travelled the lower coast road through the night and the morning under armed escort — four mounted men in the colours of the Numidian garrison and a junior officer who had ridden ahead at the noon hour to give Augustine the time to prepare. The escort had been provided, grudgingly, by the proconsul's office at Carthage after the senior bishop had pressed for three days. Possidius was on a pallet of straw under the canvas roof, with two of his own clergy from Calama at his head and feet, and with a freedman of the Calama diocese walking at the off-side of the mule — a man who had been a stretcher-bearer in the army in his earlier life and had been hired by the deacon at Calama to keep the pallet steady on the road. The mule was tired. The freedman at the off-side was steadying the cart at every dip. The junior officer rode ahead of the mule with one hand on the bridle of his own horse and the other on the hilt of his sword, which was not unsheathed but was being kept ready, in the manner of a man who had been told to escort a bishop on the African coast in the year of the council of Carthage and was not yet certain the road was over.

Augustine watched the cart come.

He had watched many carts come up that road in seven years as bishop — refugees, grain, dispatches, corpses for the burying-ground beyond the western wall — and had received them with the steadiness of a bishop receiving the work the office sent him. The cart on this afternoon was a different kind of cart. It carried a man who had been, until the second day of the previous week, the bishop of Calama and the friend of the bishop of Hippo and a name in the daily round of the basilica's prayers, and who was now, on the report Eraclius had brought up at the noon hour, alive but not entirely alive: a wound to the head, a wound to the right shoulder, and further wounds the report had not particularised.

Augustine's hands had been folded at his belt for two hours. He had not noticed them folded until now. He let them drop.

Stephen stood beside him. He had not, in the two hours at the gate, said a word. He had stood the way Stephen stood, which was with his hands folded in front of him at the level of his belt, and he had watched the road. He had been watching it longer than Augustine had been watching it because Stephen had, at the first hour, walked down the road for a quarter of a mile in the direction the cart would come from, and had walked back, and had taken his place at the gate, and had not moved since.

The cart came through the gate.

The junior officer dismounted. The smell of the cart came with him — road dust, animal sweat, the iron-flat smell of used bandage linen. He inclined his head to Augustine in the small specific inclination of a Numidian officer to a bishop he had been instructed by his proconsul to be polite to. Augustine returned the inclination. The freedman at the cart's off-side stopped the mule. The two clergy from Calama stepped down from their places at the head and foot of the pallet and stood beside the cart.

Augustine went to the cart.

Something turned in his chest before he reached the side of it. Possidius was on the pallet under the canvas. He was awake. His eyes were open and were tracking and were the eyes of a man who had been on a cart on a bad road for thirteen hours after having spent six days on the floor of a basilica at Calama before the diocese had judged him strong enough to be moved. The wound on the head was bandaged in linen that had been changed at the third hour of the morning at the post-house at the river crossing; the linen was clean now and would be changed again before the evening. The wound on the right shoulder was under the tunic; Augustine could see the swelling at the line of the collar. The face under the bandage looked at him from a distance it had not previously held.

*Possidius.*

*Aurelius.*

*You are at Hippo.*

*I am at Hippo. I am, I think, going to be at Hippo for some weeks.*

*You will be at Hippo for as many weeks as the wounds require. The wounds will require what they require. Do not make a calendar for them. The body will tell us.*

*Yes.*

Augustine put his hand on Possidius's hand at the side of the pallet. The hand was cool. The hand had been on a cart for thirteen hours and the cart had been jolted and the hand had been holding the rim of the pallet for the whole of those hours and the holding had taken what strength the hand had to give. Augustine held the hand for the count of three. Then he stepped back and turned to the junior officer.

*Captain. The bishop will be carried into the residence on the pallet. Your men may help. Stephen will direct. After the bishop is in the residence I will see you in the lay parlour. There will be wine. I will write the letter to the proconsul this afternoon for you to carry back at first light.*

*Bishop.*

The junior officer turned to his men. His men dismounted. The freedman at the off-side of the mule and one of the clergy from Calama lifted the front of the pallet; the second clergyman and one of the Numidian soldiers lifted the foot. They carried the pallet through the basilica gate and across the courtyard and through the cloister door and along the corridor of the bishop's residence to the guest cell that had been prepared at the second hour and where Stephen had set out the small things a wounded man would need at the bedside before going down to the gate.

Augustine walked beside the pallet to the door of the cell. He did not go in. He turned to Stephen.

*You will stay with him.*

*Yes.*

*Whatever he requires. Whatever the physician requires. Whatever the brothers from Calama require. I will be in the writing room and at the lay parlour and at the small parlour as the work of the afternoon requires me. Eraclius will bring me word at the half-hour through the rest of the day. If the bishop's condition changes between half-hours, send Eraclius for me at once.*

*Yes.*

*Stephen.*

*Yes.*

*The wounds are what they are. You have seen wounds before. You will not, I think, find this one beyond what you have seen. Do not let the face of him concern you more than the wounds concern you. The face will go back to being the face it was. The wounds are what we manage today.*

*Yes, father.*

Augustine looked at him. Stephen had not used the address *father* to him in private speech in nine years. The address came out of him now in the way an old habit came back to a man whose mind was on a thing he could not entirely manage.

Augustine did not, at the door, mention the address. He nodded once to Stephen. He went down the corridor to the writing room.

---

The afternoon went the way an afternoon went when a bishop had to write four letters of consequence in three hours. One to the proconsul at Carthage, to go out with the junior officer at first light. One to the senior bishop at Carthage, to go out with a regular post-courier at the same hour. One to the imperial vicar at Sirmium, drafted now and locked in the desk until the senior bishop's response came back — the vicar would, in the spring, hear Augustine's case for state action against the Donatists, the African schism that held the catholic bishops to be traitors who had handed over the Scriptures under Roman persecution and were, on Donatist reading, no longer the church. One to Possidius's deputy at Calama, instructing him in what the diocese would require in the weeks Possidius was at Hippo.

The four letters took the afternoon.

Eraclius brought word at the half-hour through the four hours. The reports were the reports a junior brought to the bishop in such cases: the wounds had been re-bandaged; the bishop had taken a small quantity of broth; the bishop had slept for half an hour and had woken without distress; the physician from the harbour quarter had come at the third hour and had said the wound on the shoulder was not, on present examination, going to require the deeper cutting the diocese at Calama had feared; the wound on the head was the wound that would require the longer time. Eraclius reported in the small clean voice of a junior who had been instructed in how to report and was doing it well. Augustine took each report without lifting his eyes from the parchment and gave the small acknowledgements a bishop gave at such reports, and Eraclius went out, and Augustine wrote on.

At the seventh hour he set the iron pen down. The letters were finished. He sealed them and set them in the tray for the morning. He stood. He walked through the cloister to the guest cell.

Stephen was at the bedside on the small stool the cell kept by the cot. He had a cloth in his hand and a basin of water at his foot and the cloth was damp. He was not, when Augustine came through the door, applying the cloth; he was sitting with the cloth folded in his hand the way a man sat when the work he had been doing had been done for the present moment and the next moment of it had not yet come.

Possidius was asleep.

Augustine came to the foot of the cot. He looked at his old student. The face under the bandage was not the colour of a man at the beginning of a fever, which was what Augustine had been intending, in the back of his mind, to check for at the seventh-hour visit. The face was the face of a man whose body had decided the present hour was a good hour to rest. His breath was shallow and even and would, on the present trajectory, deepen into sleep for some further hours.

*Stephen.*

Stephen looked up.

*The bishop is sleeping.*

*Yes.*

*How long.*

*An hour and a half. He took the broth at the fifth hour and the broth stayed. The physician was here at the sixth and was satisfied. The brother from Calama who is the deacon of the basilica there has gone to the lay quarters to sleep. The other clergyman is at the kitchen to eat. The freedman is with the mule.*

*Good.*

*Father.*

*Yes.*

*You should eat.*

*Yes.*

*I will eat with you in the small parlour if you wish.*

*No. Stay here. He may wake. I will eat in the writing room with whatever Eraclius brings me. You eat when the next watch arrives. Who is the next watch.*

*The deacon from Calama at the third hour of the night. I will eat then.*

*Good. Stephen.*

*Yes.*

*The face will go back to being the face it was.*

*I know, father. I am all right.*

He was not, on Augustine's reading of him at the foot of the cot, all right in the sense the words usually carried. He was managing in the way Stephen had managed across the years he had been in the household, which was the way of a man who had decided some long time ago that the management was the work and that the management did not require permission to feel less than it appeared to feel. Augustine had been watching him manage for nine years and had not, in those nine years, once broken the management by asking what was beneath it. He did not break it now. He inclined his head. He went out.

He ate at the writing-room desk. Eraclius brought bread and a piece of cheese and a small jug of watered wine. Augustine ate the bread without paying attention to it. He drank the wine for the warmth and not for the taste. He sent the jug back half-finished. He sat at the desk in the lamp-light.

After some time he went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box.

He had not taken the box down in some years. He had not, on his own honest count, taken it down since the autumn the boy at the basilica school in the lower town had been brought up dying and had been the dying-boy whose death had been on Augustine's hands without any one of his intentions having been part of the dying. That had been four years ago. He had held the box that night in the writing room without opening it and had set it back. He had not opened the box since the night his son Adeodatus had died at Tagaste in the late summer of 389, which was now fourteen years past.

He opened the box.

The hinges gave their small specific sound. He had heard the sound twice in the last sixteen years — the night his mother died at Ostia in 387, the night his son died at Tagaste in 389 — and the sound had carried both nights with it on the third hearing. He set the lid back on the felt of the desk.

The disc was inside.

He took it out. He held it in both hands.

The disc was cold.

It had been cold the night the boy from the lower-town school had died, four years before. It had been cold every evening of the year of the dying-boy and the year after that. It had been cold on the night of the Crispus drowning in the new font in 397, which Augustine had checked the box for after the burial, in some superstition he had not analysed and could not now name. The disc had been cold on every occasion he had checked it in the fourteen years since Adeodatus.

It was cold now.

He held the disc for the count of perhaps a hundred. The counting was old in him. The cold of it sat in his palm and did not move. Possidius was two cells away on the cot, under the canvas of the pallet that had not yet been moved out. The wound on the shoulder. The wound on the head. Stephen at the foot of the cot with the cloth folded in his hand. The four letters on the tray for the morning post. And under all of it, quietly, his mother's hand on his at Ostia in November 387. The bronze had been warm that hour she held it. It had not been warm since. Not once in sixteen years.

*Carry it,* Monnica had said. *The man it is for will know it. He will not be you. The bronze will tell you when he is near — it will warm in his presence the way it warmed in mine. If he does not come in your lifetime, you will pass it to a man you have judged worthy of carrying it after you.*

He had been judging men, in the back of his attention, for sixteen years. He had not, in those sixteen years, judged any of them worthy. He did not, at this particular hour, judge the man two cells away, or the man on the small stool beside him. He thought, sitting at the desk with the cold bronze in his hands and the lamp at his elbow, that the man he would pass the box to had not yet come and would, when he came, come from a direction Augustine had not been watching.

He set the disc back in the box. The hinges sounded. He closed the lid. He returned the box to the upper shelf behind the rolls of accounts.

He went to compline — the last office of the day, the prayer that closed the dark.

---

The first week passed.

Possidius's wounds did the work wounds did in the first week. The fever Augustine had been intending to check for at the seventh hour of the first day did not, in the end, come; the wound on the shoulder remained the swelling it had been on arrival but did not progress to the deeper rot the diocese at Calama had feared; the wound on the head began the slow process of closing in the way a head wound closes when the bone underneath has not been broken. The physician from the harbour quarter came in every morning and every evening for the first three days and after the third day every morning. The deacon from Calama and the other clergyman traded the night watches with Stephen, who took the day watches and the second of the two night watches when he could. The freedman from Calama walked the mule down to the harbour livery on the second day and arranged the mule's stabling for the duration and then took the post-coach back to Calama on the fourth day with letters from Possidius to the deputy at the diocese which Possidius dictated in the slow careful voice of a wounded bishop and which Stephen wrote out in his clean hand on the small writing-tablet at the bedside.

Augustine came to the cell three times a day. The morning visit was at the second hour after first prayers; the noon visit was after the noon meal; the evening visit was at the half-hour after compline. He sat on the second stool the cell now had at the bedside and he asked Possidius the questions about the wounds and about the broth and about the sleep that the physician had told him to ask, and he gave Possidius what news he had of the diocese at Calama from the dispatches the regular post had brought up that day, and he held Possidius's hand a moment when he came in and a moment when he went out. The conversation was the conversation between two old friends whose friendship had been built across ten years on long letters and short visits and shared work, and the conversation in the cell was not different in its substance from the conversations the two of them had had at long meals at the bishop's residence on Possidius's previous visits to Hippo, except that the conversation was now had with one of them sitting up against the pillows and the other at the bedside with his hands on his knees.

Stephen was in the room for every one of the visits. He sat on the smaller stool against the wall by the door. He did not, in any of the visits, speak unless Augustine put a question to him directly about the bandages or the broth or the sleep. The cloth stayed in his hand, the basin at his foot. He kept the watch.

Augustine watched him keep it.

By the fourth day Augustine had begun to notice a thing about the watch. It was small. When Possidius slept in the moments Augustine was in the cell, Stephen looked at the sleeping face. The looking was not the looking of a man at the face of a wounded brother. The looking was a stillness in the upper part of Stephen's body — his shoulders down a fraction, his hands gone quiet on the cloth in his lap, his mouth softened at the corners — and his eyes resting on Possidius's face for the count of three before he turned them away to the wall or to his hands. After the next count he would look back. He did not know he was doing it, on Augustine's reading of him. He had been doing it without noticing for the four days he had been at the bedside.

Augustine saw it on the fourth day at the edge of the thing he was looking at, which was the linen on Possidius's chest. On the fifth day he saw it again and held it in his attention for a count before he looked back at the linen. He did not, on the fifth day, name what he was holding.

On the sixth day, at the noon visit, Possidius was asleep. Augustine had been on the bedside stool for some time. The looking had happened twice. On the second of the two — Stephen's eyes on Possidius for three counts, the quiet hand in the lap, the soft corner of the mouth — Augustine recognised what he was seeing. He had seen it before, in his own life, in the years before his conversion. He had called it by its name then. He did not call it by its name now.

The recognition arrived in his chest as a small heat, low and discrete, the way a coal comes alive under ash before it shows light. It was on him before he could keep it from being on him. He sat with it on the stool. He did not let it travel into his face. He did not look at Stephen and he did not look at Possidius. He looked at the linen on Possidius's chest and watched it rise and fall until it had done so twice, and the heat in his chest settled into itself and did not go anywhere.

He stood from the bedside stool. He inclined his head to Stephen. He went out.

He went to the writing room. He sat at the desk. He sat at the desk for some time without taking up the iron pen. Then he took it up and continued the letter to the priest at Tubursicu that he had begun the day of Possidius's arrival and that had been on the corner of the desk since.

He did not, in the days that followed, attach further meaning to what he had recognised on the sixth day. He had recognised it. To name it would be to require a thing to be done with it; and there was nothing, on any reading of the household and the friends and the cot in the guest cell, that doing anything with it would improve. He did not name it. He carried it. He had been carrying things without naming them since Ostia. The carrying had a shape his hands knew.

The disc, when he checked it that night after compline, was cold.

---

The second week began.

Possidius could sit up by the second day of the second week. By the fourth day he could take the stool by the high window for an hour at the noon. By the seventh day of the second week he could walk, with one hand on Stephen's arm, the length of the corridor to the chapter house and back, which was the longest unbroken walking he had done since the morning before the attack. The walking left him more tired than the sitting left him. The tiredness was the tiredness of a body that was the body of a man of thirty-three who had been hit hard and was now, in the slow way bodies of thirty-three did this, putting itself back together.

By the third week he was at the writing room with Augustine at the noon hour, in a chair Eraclius had carried in from the guest cell and set at the side of Augustine's desk. They read the dispatches together. They drafted replies. Possidius could write for a quarter of an hour at a time before the right hand began to shake, and Stephen, who was in the writing room with them by the second visit and after, took the dictation when Possidius's hand reached the shaking. The three of them worked through the late mornings of the second half of the third week in a routine that, to anyone coming through the writing-room door without the news that the bishop of Calama had been injured on the road three weeks before, would have looked like the routine of a senior bishop and his old student and the senior bishop's senior deacon doing the daily work of a busy diocese.

Augustine looked up, on the morning of the third day of the third week, from the parchment to the three of them at the desk. Possidius in Eraclius's chair to his left, the right hand closed loose around the stylus on the table, the bandage at the head smaller now and clean. Stephen at the side table with the small writing-tablet across his knees, taking the dictation. Eraclius at the doorway with a fresh inkpot. The morning light through the high window fell across the writing-tablet in Stephen's lap and across Possidius's hand resting on the stylus. The room had the sound of men at work in a room they had been in for weeks.

*This is the household I have made,* he thought, and was surprised by the thinking of it. He had been the desk the household had organised itself around. He had been a bishop for eight years and at Hippo for twelve, and the men at this hour were the men the eight years had given him. He looked at them a count longer than he had meant to look. Then he went back to the letter.

He went on with the letter.

---

Possidius left for Calama on the fourth day of the fourth week.

The deacon from Calama and the other clergyman had returned to the diocese in the second week to manage the daily round; Possidius would now travel back with a single attendant — the freedman who had brought him down on the cart, who had been at the harbour livery for three weeks and had been doing odd work for the basilica's lay-sisters in his off hours — and with the post-courier the senior bishop at Carthage had assigned to escort Calama-bound letters for the next month. The escort would be courier-level rather than military; the proconsul's office had judged the immediate threat to the bishop of Calama to be reduced after the legal proceedings against the Donatist priests at the next valley had begun in the second week.

The cart was at the gate at the third hour of the morning. The mule was the same mule. The pallet had been replaced with a proper bench and a small canvas top. Possidius could ride sitting up.

Augustine was at the gate. Stephen was not.

They embraced.

*Aurelius.*

*Possidius.*

*Three weeks have been three weeks. I will not, on the present trajectory of the diocese, get back to Hippo before the spring.*

*I will be at Calama before the spring. The senior bishop wants the conference on the African councils to be at Hippo in February. I will need a fortnight at Calama with you in January for the preparation. I will come.*

*Good.*

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*Travel safely.*

*I will travel as safely as the road permits. Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*Stephen.*

He had said the name without preamble. It was the only time he had said the name, in Augustine's hearing, since he had been at Hippo. Augustine had been listening for it.

*Stephen has been at the desk this morning at my asking,* Augustine said. *He is finishing the schedule of the council preparation. He sends you his blessing.*

*The blessing is taken.*

Possidius looked at Augustine. The look held. Augustine returned it. Possidius's mouth set a fraction at the corner — the kind of small set the face makes when it is acknowledging a thing the rest of the body is not going to acknowledge. Augustine inclined his head a fraction more than he had been inclining it. The exchange took no longer than the saying of a name. Both of them knew what had been said in it. Neither of them said it.

The look broke. The small heat that had arrived on the sixth day was in his chest again, and he let it be there, and then they embraced again, briefly, in the way of two bishops at a public gate. Possidius was helped up onto the bench of the cart by the freedman. The post-courier mounted his horse. The cart went out through the gate. Augustine watched it until it had gone past the cypress trees at the second mile-stone and was no longer a cart but a small mark on the harbour road.

He went back through the gate.

He went to the writing room.

Stephen was at the desk. The schedule of the council preparation was on the desk in front of him, half-finished. Stephen looked up when Augustine came through the door. Stephen looked at him the way a man looked at a man who had been at the gate to see off a third man; the look was the look of a man who had, in not coming to the gate himself, made a decision he was prepared to be asked to defend and was not prepared to defend without difficulty.

Augustine did not ask.

He came to the desk. He sat in his own chair across from Stephen. He took up the iron pen. He read what Stephen had written so far on the schedule.

*The conference,* Augustine said.

*The conference, father.*

*We will set the morning of the second day for the question of episcopal jurisdiction. The morning of the third day for the matter of the Donatist proceedings. The afternoon of the third day for the financial reconciliation between the dioceses. Possidius will preside at the first two; the senior bishop at the third. I will speak at the close of each day in my place as host.*

*Yes, father.*

*Stephen.*

*Yes.*

He looked at his deacon. Stephen looked back. His hands were folded at the edge of the schedule in the way he folded them when he was finished with a thing, not the way he folded them when he was waiting. Augustine had been intending, as he had walked back from the gate, to say a thing that he had decided, in the corridor, not to say. He did not, in the chair across the desk from Stephen, say it.

He said instead:

*The schedule is good. Continue it. I will be at the cathedral at the fourth hour for the prayers.*

*Yes, father.*

He got up. He went out of the writing room. He went to the chapter house and stood in the chapter house alone for the count of perhaps three hundred. The chapter house was empty at this hour. The small high window gave on the courtyard, and the light through it laid a single bar across the marble of the floor and onto the foot of the lectern.

His mother came up in him.

She came up the way she had come up in him in the basilica at Hippo on the day of his ordination — in his ribs and behind his face, not as memory but as presence. She had said to him at Ostia, in the second-last sentence of her life: *the watching was the work I did in your house for thirty-three years.* He had been carrying the words for sixteen years. He had been thinking he understood them. He understood now, in the chapter house with the bar of light on the floor, that he had been watching the men of his household for those sixteen years without seeing what was in front of him in any of them.

He would, in the years to come, watch better. The thought arrived with something else behind it — a question he did not form fully, because to form it would be to name what he had been watching at the bedside, and he was not prepared today to name it. The disc was for a man he had not yet recognised. He had been standing in rooms with his men for sixteen years and had not, until this morning, seen them. The thought stayed in the chapter house with him. He did not take it to the writing room.

He went to the cathedral for the prayers.

---

In the autumn the proceedings concluded. Two priests were convicted. A third was exiled to Caesarea. In his October letter Possidius wrote only of the roof which the rains had made acute and of the council preparation. He did not write of the men who had commissioned the priests, who had not been found. Augustine wrote back about the roof.

In November Augustine took the disc down on an evening when no other particular event of the day required him to. The hinges sounded. The disc was cold, as he had known it would be. He understood, as he set the box back, that he had been the kind of man who took the disc down on no particular occasion to check whether the man it was for had arrived in the household. The man had not arrived. He held the understanding the way he had held the disc — at arm's length, weighing what it weighed. The bronze was bronze.
