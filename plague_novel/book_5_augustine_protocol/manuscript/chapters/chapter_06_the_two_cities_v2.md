# Chapter 8 — The Two Cities (v2, thriller)

*Hippo Regius, the second week of February 414 CE.*

The brazier was lit. The cold of February had come in through the writing-room shutters in the second hour of the morning, and Eraclius had brought in the small charcoal brazier from the chamber behind the cathedral altar without being asked. The charcoal had a smell of vine-stock; the lay brother who managed the basilica's small stores had been buying, for the last eight months, charcoal made from the vine-prunings of the local farmers, which was cheaper than the oak charcoal Eraclius preferred but which gave a smoke the brazier-pipe could not entirely carry off. The room smelled of vine-charcoal and of ink and of the parchment that had been on the desk in fresh sheets since the new year.

Augustine sat at the desk. His right knee, which had been bad in damp weather since the autumn the Goths took Rome, was bad in cold weather too, and the cold of February in the writing-room at the third hour of the morning was a particular kind of cold. He had wrapped a piece of wool around the knee under his outer tunic. The wool helped. The wool did not help enough.

In front of him on the desk were five fair copies, each in a different scribal hand, and each marked at the head with a Roman numeral in his own hand: I, II, III, IV, V. Books one through five of the work. They were finished. They were finished in the sense that they were on parchment, in the form he intended them to be read in, and in the sense that they had been on parchment for some months and he had not, in those months, found any place where the wax under-draft needed to be brought back. They had been read by Possidius and by no one else.

Beside the five fair copies on the desk was a single sheet of parchment, blank, on which he had not yet begun to write.

The single sheet was the architecture.

He had been thinking about the architecture since the night of the courier, three years and five months earlier, when he had written on the wax tablet by the lamp: *Books one through ten: refutation. Books eleven through twenty-two: the city itself.* He had had the shape since that night. He had not, until this winter, sat down to set the shape on parchment in the form in which it would have to live.

He took up the bone stylus.

He wrote, in a small clean hand at the head of the blank sheet: *XI. Quod sit duae civitates — That there be two cities.*

He set the stylus down. He sat with the line. He had sat with it, in his head, for the better part of a year. The line was the line on which the second half of the work would turn.

A knock at the door. Three short.

*Father.*

*Come.*

Possidius came in. He was forty-six now and looked it. The hair had gone grey at the temples in the year after his return from his last journey to Carthage; he had carried back from that journey, as well as the news he had gone north to gather, a fever that had taken him to bed for nineteen days and had left him thinner. He was thinner still, this winter. He had his outer cloak on, the brown wool one he had worn since his ordination, and the cloak had been re-darned at the shoulder in a colour that did not quite match.

*Aurelius.*

*Possidius.*

Possidius sat in the chair across from the desk, which had been his chair for nine years. He looked at the five fair copies. He looked at the single blank sheet beside them with the line at its head. He read the line.

*Quod sit duae civitates.*

*Yes.*

*You are at the second half.*

*I am.*

Possidius nodded. He did not speak for a small period. He had a way, when he was thinking, of looking at a point three inches above the table-top, and he was looking at that point now.

*Aurelius. May I ask the shape of it.*

*That is why I asked you to come down.*

He drew the blank sheet toward him on the desk and turned it so that Possidius could read it from the chair. He took the stylus and began to write under the heading.

*Book eleven — the foundation. The two loves. The two cities not as places but as habits of love.*

*Book twelve — the angels. The fall of one part of the heavenly city before the fall of the earthly.*

*Book thirteen — the death of the soul before the death of the body.*

*Book fourteen — the two loves at length. The longest of the books. The hinge.*

*Books fifteen through eighteen — the two cities in history. From Cain through Babylon through the prophets through the apostles. Each book a stage.*

*Books nineteen through twenty-two — the present and the end. Politics, time, judgment, and the city's revealing.*

He set the stylus down. Possidius read the outline. He read it twice. He looked up.

*This will take you ten years.*

*It may take twelve.*

*You are sixty.*

*I am.*

*If you do not finish.*

*If I do not finish, you finish. The shape is the shape. The wax under each book will be in this room. Anyone who has read the first ten books and the outline can finish the work, in some form. You are the only one who has read the first ten books and will read the outline.*

Possidius was silent.

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*You are giving me the work.*

*I am giving you the possibility of the work, if it should fall to you. I am not giving you the work. The work is mine. I will, with God's permission, finish it. But the work cannot be lost because of the failure of one man's body, and I am one man and I have a body, and the body is sixty.*

*The body is sixty.*

*I would not, in any case, write the rest of it without you reading it as I write it. I have not written the first ten without you. You have read every book at the wax-and-parchment stage. You will read every book to come at the same stage. That has not changed and will not change. It is the architecture you have been seeing for three years. I am setting it on a single sheet because the time has come to set it on a sheet, and because if it is on a sheet it can be carried by a single hand if it must be.*

*Whose hand.*

*Yours.*

Possidius nodded again. He sat with it. The brazier in the corner gave a small crack as a piece of vine-stock went. The room smelled, for a moment, more strongly of the vine-stock and then less.

*Aurelius. There is something I would say.*

*Say it.*

*You are not, on this work, in any place where you are alone. You have been alone on the work, in the writing of the wax. You have not been alone on the carrying of the work. I have been carrying it for three years. I will carry it for the rest of my life, whether you finish it before I die or whether I finish it after you. It is not a thing you have given me by writing the outline on this sheet. It is a thing I took on the night I sat in the chair you have me sitting in now, three years ago, when you told me what the courier had brought. I have not put it down since. I will not put it down.*

Augustine looked at his old student. He held the look for the count of three. Then he said:

*Possidius.*

*Aurelius.*

*Thank you.*

Possidius inclined his head. He did not answer in words. He had not expected to be thanked and was not sure what to do with the thanking. After a moment he said:

*The vine-charcoal smokes.*

*It does.*

*I will tell Eraclius.*

*Eraclius knows. Eraclius is making a point.*

*What is the point.*

*That a basilica that buys its charcoal from the local farmers is a basilica that does not have to buy its charcoal from the imperial supplier in Carthage, and that a basilica that does not have to buy its charcoal from the imperial supplier in Carthage is a basilica that can manage without the imperial supplier in Carthage if the imperial supplier in Carthage should, at some point in the next decade, cease to be available.*

*Eraclius is reading the work.*

*Eraclius is reading the work.*

They sat for a small time longer. Possidius rose.

*I will be back at the chair after compline.*

*I will have the outline of book eleven on the wax by then.*

Possidius went out. Augustine drew the blank sheet back toward him on the desk. He took up the stylus.

He began.

*Hippo Regius, the last week of August 416 CE.*

Melania came to the gate at the hour of the lamp, as she had almost five years before, in the same plain wool cloak the colour of the rain. The cloak was not new. The hem had been let down at some point in the intervening period — there was a darker line where the wool had been folded under for some months and then released — and the cloak was now the right length for a woman who had been walking, in the years since he had seen her last, in places that required a cloak that touched the ankle.

She was thirty-three. Her face had aged in a way her face had not aged when he had first seen it. She had been giving away wealth for thirteen years and was still giving, and she was, from her bearing, near the point at which there would be no further wealth to give and the giving would have to take a different form.

*Bishop.*

*Daughter.*

*I have come to say a thing that should be said in person.*

*Sit. There is wine. There is bread.*

She sat in the chair across the table in the small parlour where they had sat the first time. Eraclius brought the wine and the bread and went out.

*Bishop. Pinianus and I are leaving Africa.*

He had known this since the spring. He had heard from the deacon of Tagaste that the household had been preparing — that the books were being packed, that the freedmen were being given their portions, that the household servants who were going to Jerusalem with the family were being given the choice. He had heard it without surprise. He had been waiting for her to say it in person.

*When.*

*The first week of October. We sail from Carthage. We will be at Alexandria by the feast of St Demetrius and at Jerusalem by Christmas, with God's permission and the weather's.*

*Pinianus is well enough.*

*He is well enough for now. He has been losing weight since the spring. The physician at Tagaste says it is not a fever, it is the other thing, the thing that takes some men in their forties and that does not have a name and that does not, when it has begun, generally stop. He may have two years. He may have ten. We do not know. We have decided not to spend whatever years he has on the wrong continent.*

*The community at Tagaste.*

*The community at Tagaste continues. The deputy abbess is Sophia, who has been with us six years and who I have been training in the things we agreed on. She knows what she needs to know. She does not know more. There is a man at Carthage in the household of the senior bishop who has agreed to be her contact for the things that go beyond the diocese. He knows what he needs to know. He does not know more.*

*Good.*

She picked up the cup of wine. This time she drank from it. He noticed her drinking and did not let his face change.

*Bishop. There is a second thing.*

*Say it.*

*The names you and I agreed, three years ago, were the names of those who would carry the work after my grandmother and after the priest in Bethlehem. We agreed there were seven names then. There were eight, with you. The number was small.*

*The number was small.*

*The number is now four.*

He did not speak immediately. He set the stylus down flat on the desk.

He sat with the four.

*Who.*

*The man at Constantinople, Atticus, died in the summer. The fever. I had a letter from his deacon, who is not in the work and who does not know what he was writing, who said only that the bishop had passed and had asked, in his last week, that a particular cup be sent to me. The cup arrived. The man at Sketis is silent — the elder in the Egyptian desert where my grandmother first learned the practices she taught me. The courier we sent in March did not return. We do not know whether he was killed or whether the community has dispersed or whether the silence is something else. The desert does not always tell us. The man at Lérins — Cassian — is alive and is teaching, but he has, in the last year, narrowed what he teaches. He will teach the daily round and the practices of the desert fathers. He will not, he has written me, teach the practices my grandmother taught. He has come to the conclusion that the time for those practices is past. He may be right. He may be wrong. I do not, on this point, hold an opinion.*

*Four.*

*You. Possidius. Sophia at Tagaste, who knows enough. Me, who is going to Jerusalem and who will be at the foundation my grandmother built and who will, with God's permission, find any of the others who are still there. There may be others I do not know of. My grandmother said there might be. She said the work had been carried in households we had no record of and that we should not assume the silence in any quarter meant absence. I hold to her in that.*

*Four whom we name.*

*Four whom we name.*

*And the man it is for.*

She did not answer immediately. She drank again from the cup of wine. She set the cup down.

*Bishop. My grandmother told me, in the year before she died, that she did not believe the man it was for would arrive in our generation. She said she had thought, when she was younger, that he might. She said she had stopped thinking it in her sixties. She said the work would be carried by those who carried it for him, in the trust that he would come, and that the trust was the work, and that the carrying was the trust.*

*Yes.*

*She said: the bishop in Hippo carries it because the bishop in Hippo can carry it, not because he expects to put it down to the man it is for. He may put it down to no one. He may put it down to a man in the next generation, who may put it down to a man in the generation after, who may put it down. The putting-down is not the question. The carrying is the question.*

*Yes.*

*That is the second thing I came to say.*

He looked at her across the table.

*Daughter.*

*Bishop.*

*You will be at Jerusalem in December.*

*With God's permission.*

*If the man it is for arrives at the foundation on the Mount of Olives and he is not the man for what is in your grandmother's keeping, you will know him by a small mark in his Latin. He will speak Latin in the manner of a man who has been taught it by a Greek who learned it in Alexandria. He will not have a particular name to give you. He will ask, if he is the man, after the priest in Bethlehem and after the bishop in Hippo, in that order, and he will ask in that order without my having told you he will. If he asks in any other order, he is not the man.*

*Bishop.*

*Yes.*

*That is more than my grandmother told me to expect.*

*It is more than I was told. It is what I have reasoned out from what Simplicianus gave me and from what the logic of the chain requires. I may be wrong on a particular. I am not wrong on the order. The order is a function of what the chain is, not of who is carrying it.*

She nodded. She sat with it. She finished the wine in the cup.

*Bishop. May I bless you before I go.*

She had never, in their almost five years of association, asked anything of the kind. He looked at her. He understood, while he looked at her, that the request was the kind a woman makes when she does not expect to see a man again and when she has, in the long calculations of her household and her work, recognised that the man before her is one of the things she will not see again. He was sixty-two. She was thirty-three. She would, on the actuarial expectations of senatorial Roman women who had access to Greek physicians and who lived in houses with stone floors and clean water, outlive him by twenty years.

*I would be grateful.*

She rose. She came around the table. The sound of her sandals on the stone stopped behind him. Her hand came down on his head — lighter than he had expected, dry and cool, the hand of a woman who had been giving things away for thirteen years. She said, in the formula of the household blessing her grandmother had taught her, in Latin she had not used in his presence before:

*The God of your fathers and the God of your mothers and the God of the carrying keep you, Aurelius, and keep what you are carrying, and bring you, in the time that is given you, to the rest that is prepared for you.*

He bowed his head under her hand. He did not weep, although his throat had closed in a way it had not closed in a long time. When she lifted her hand he looked at her. She was already turning to the door.

*Daughter.*

*Bishop.*

*Write me from Jerusalem.*

*I will.*

She went out. He heard her sandals along the cloister. He heard the gate-keeper at the basilica gate and then the sound of her cart on the stones outside the wall and then the sound of the cart going down the road toward the harbour where, in some week of October, she would board the ship that would take her, with her dying husband, to the Mount of Olives.

He sat at the table with the empty cup. He could still hear the cart on the stones. Then he could not. The cloister was quiet in the way the cloister was quiet when there was no wind and compline was still two hours away.

He stood. He went back to the writing room. The fourteenth book of the work was on the desk in its third draft. The hinge passage was the passage he had been on for two months. He took up the bone stylus. He scraped the wax of the tablet clean. He set the stylus to the wax.

*Fecerunt itaque civitates duas amores duo: terrenam scilicet amor sui usque ad contemptum Dei, caelestem vero amor Dei usque ad contemptum sui.*

*Two cities, then, have been formed by two loves: the earthly by the love of self, even to the contempt of God; the heavenly by the love of God, even to the contempt of self.*

He read what he had written. He had written this sentence on the wax six times. Each of the previous writings he had scraped clean. The seventh writing was the writing he kept. The sentence would turn the chapter.

He covered the parchment of the work. He went to vespers. The sound of the cart down the harbour road had been gone an hour. He could not hear it now. He could not hear, in fact, anything from beyond the cloister. The autumn evening was very still.

---

*Hippo Regius, the third week of October 420 CE.*

The letter came on a Wednesday in the second post of the day. Eraclius brought it without comment. It was the seal of the monastery at Bethlehem. The address was in a hand Augustine did not know.

He cut the seal. He read the letter through.

It was brief. It was from the assistant Eusebius, who had been with Jerome for nineteen years and who had now to write the letters Jerome had used to write himself. The letter said that Jerome had died on the thirtieth of September, of the long failing of his lungs and of the short fever that had come in the second week of the month. He had been at peace, although the end had come hard. He had received the consolations of the Church. He had asked, in his last week, that his personal copy of the Septuagint — the great Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures, the text the two of them had been arguing about for ten years — annotated in his own hand, be sent to the bishop of Hippo, who would understand what to do with it. He had asked also that the bishop of Hippo be told that he had read the first ten books of the work and that the work was the work, and that the bishop of Hippo should continue.

That was all the letter said.

Augustine sat at the desk. The first ten books had been finished and copied for distribution by the Easter just past. He had sent a fair copy of the first ten to Bethlehem in Holy Week with a Carthaginian bookseller who had been carrying mail to the eastern provinces for thirty years. Jerome had had the books for six months. He had read them in those six months. He had read them as a dying man reads, which is to say with the attention of a man who knows what he is reading and who has nothing left in his life that is more important than the reading. He had said, before he died, that the work was the work.

Augustine sat with the letter on the desk in front of him for some time. The shutters were half-closed against an October that had been warmer than usual. The fig tree in the cloister had been bearing for three weeks past its season.

He had been weeping in installments since the news of Rome. He did not, now, have a great deal of weeping left for the man in Bethlehem. He had wept for Jerome in a small way ten years before, in a private sense, when the news of Rome had reached Hippo and he had understood what the fall of Rome would do to Jerome's life-long project and to the man himself. He did not weep now.

He thought of the things Jerome had been to him. Jerome had been the man who had read the *Confessions* in 401 and had written the letter from Bethlehem that had, on its first reading, made Augustine furious; on its second reading, made him think; on its third reading, made him grateful. Jerome had been the man who had argued with him for ten years about the Septuagint and the Hebrew, and who had taught him, in those ten years of arguing, more about the practice of textual scholarship than any other man Augustine had encountered. Jerome had been the man who had translated the scriptures into the Latin that the African church and every other Latin church now used and would, on Augustine's quiet calculation, use for the next thousand years.

Jerome had also been one of the names. Melania's grandmother had named him. The number had been eight. The number had been four since the summer of 416. The number was now three. There was Augustine, who was sixty-six. There was Possidius, who was fifty-two. There was Melania, who was thirty-seven and who was at Jerusalem with her husband who had not, after all, died in two years but had survived to see her finish the establishment of the foundation she had inherited from her grandmother.

Three. The number had once been many.

He took down a piece of clean parchment and wrote a brief reply to Eusebius. He thanked him for the letter. He asked that the Septuagint be sent to Hippo by the next reliable courier. He said that the bishop of Hippo would, with God's permission, continue.

He sealed the letter. He set it on the corner of the desk for the second post.

He took up the wax tablet. The work was on Book twenty-one, which was the book on judgment and on the things that came at the end of all things. He had been on the third chapter of the book for two weeks. The third chapter had been resisting him. He had been writing it and scraping it and writing it and scraping it. He set the stylus to the wax now.

He wrote a short paragraph that had not been in the outline he had given to Possidius six years before in this room.

*Many have served the city of God without recognising it. They have built institutions in the belief that the institutions were eternal, not knowing that institutions are not eternal. They have preserved texts for one reason, not knowing that the texts they preserved would serve another. They have defended an orthodoxy in good faith, not understanding that the truth they defended is larger than any orthodoxy. These servants are not lost. Their work is not wasted. The city of God uses what is offered to it, and the offering it uses is sometimes the offering that was made for another use, and the using is, on God's accounting, the using that was meant.*

He read what he had written. He had written it for one man. The one man would not read it; the one man was buried beside the door of the basilica at Bethlehem in a stone tomb under a marker that named him in Greek and in Latin and in Hebrew. Other men, in some century not yet his, would read it and would not know they were reading a particular man's eulogy. He thought that was correct. He thought Jerome would have thought it was correct. He set the stylus down.

He went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box. He opened it. The hinges gave their small specific sound. The disc was inside. The disc was cold. He put the disc back. He closed the box. He put the box back on the upper shelf.

He went to vespers.

*Hippo Regius, the third week of August 426 CE.*

The garden was the garden where Monnica had taught him to read his Greek, fifty-eight years before, when he had been seven and she had been thirty-three. The garden had not changed greatly in fifty-eight years. The fig tree under which Monnica had sat had died in the cold winter of 415 and had been replaced by a fig tree of the same kind from the same root-stock, which had been bearing for nine years now. The bench was the same bench. The wall behind the bench was the same wall, although the wall had been re-pointed in the lime mortar of the new century in a small section near the eastern corner where the old mortar had failed in the rain of the autumn of 421.

Augustine sat on the bench in the late afternoon of the third week of August. He was seventy-one. The pain in his right knee had stopped being a thing he noticed about three years ago, in the way that pain stops being a thing one notices when it has been continuous for long enough that the body has incorporated it into the baseline of being a body. The pain in his right knee was now what his right knee was, and would be that until he had no right knee, which would be soon enough.

In his lap was the parchment of the last paragraph of the twenty-second book.

He had finished writing it in the second hour after vespers the previous evening, by the lamp in the writing room, with the November rain — there was no rain; it was August; he had been thinking, in his head, of an evening in the writing room three years before when he had, in fact, been writing by lamp in November rain, and the memory of that evening had attached itself to the memory of last night's evening in a way the body of an old man permitted. He had read the paragraph last night and had taken it to his cell and had slept. He had read it again at the third hour this morning, by daylight, at the desk, and had judged it the paragraph the work was to end on. He had carried it down to the garden to read it once more in the garden because it was the last paragraph of the work, and the work had been started in his head in this garden when his mother had lived and the work, having been finished, ought to have its last paragraph read in the garden where its first sentence had been formed.

He read it now.

*Let us therefore — the word is *agamus*, let us go forward — let us therefore, in faith, in hope, in love, build the city that has no end and that is built always. Of which there is no need to say more, for what could be said of the rest of which has not been said in the body of this work; and of which, if more were said, the saying would not finish.*

It was an ending of the kind a man arrived at when he had written for thirteen years and had finally, in the writing, understood that the writing had to end and that the ending was a small act of faith in the reader who would, at some future date, read the closing line and turn the parchment over and find no further parchment, and who would, at that moment, take up the burden of doing what the book had been written to provoke: of building, of carrying, of going forward.

He set the parchment on his knee.

He had not, in the writing of the twenty-two books across the thirteen years, seen the bronze disc warm in the box on the upper shelf. He had checked the box at intervals — at the end of each book, in some private superstition he had not analysed and had not abandoned. He had taken down the box. He had opened it. The hinges had given their small specific sound, twenty-two times, between 414 and last night. The disc had been cold each time. Each time he had put the disc back and had closed the box and had returned the box to the upper shelf and had gone to the next book.

Last night, after the final paragraph was on the parchment and the lamp was guttering and Possidius — back from Calama for the week of the completion — was asleep in the guest cell across the cloister, he had taken down the box one last time. He had opened it. The disc had been warm.

It had been warm in the way that bronze warms when it has been against another warm thing, and not in the way the disc had warmed three times in his hand in his life. He could not, now, distinguish the two warmths reliably; they had become, in his old age, less distinct from each other. He had held the disc for the count of perhaps thirty. He had set it back in the box. He had put the box on the upper shelf. He had blown out the lamp. He had gone to bed.

He did not know what the warmth meant. He had finished the work last night. The disc had been cold for thirty years. Last night it was warm. He had carried the box for thirty years — through the writing of the *Confessions* and the founding of the second monastery, through the long war against Pelagius and the argument that a man could earn his way to God without grace, through the nights of the courier and the cuts and the burials and the long thirteen years of the present work — and he would, with God's permission, carry it for some short period more, and then he would, by an act he had been planning for some time, not carry it any longer.

He looked up from the parchment.

Possidius was coming through the garden from the cloister door.

He was fifty-eight now. He had aged at the rate of a man of his disposition under the conditions that had obtained for the last thirteen years, which was to say faster than the years should have aged him. The brown wool cloak had been re-darned twice further. He carried, under his arm, the leather satchel he had carried into this garden on every visit since 416.

*Aurelius.*

*Possidius.*

He sat on the bench. He set the satchel on his knees. He looked at the parchment in Augustine's lap.

*That is the last paragraph.*

*That is the last paragraph.*

*May I read it.*

Augustine handed him the parchment. Possidius read it. He read it twice. He set it back in Augustine's lap.

*It is the right ending.*

*It is the ending I had.*

*It is the right ending.*

They sat for some time without speaking. The August afternoon was warm and the air over the Mediterranean below them moved a little and did not quite move. The basilica behind them gave the small sounds a working basilica gave — a deacon at the office in the side chapel, a brother carrying water across the cloister, the gate at the harbour-end opening and closing for someone whose business was small enough not to come up the road.

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*The fair copies.*

*The fair copies will be five. Carthage, Constantinople, Lérins, Rome, Jerusalem. The senior bishop at Carthage will have the master and will provide for the further copying as the demand arises. Eraclius is to have the working copy. The wax under-drafts of the twenty-two books are in the chest with the iron lock on the upper shelf in the writing room. They are not to be destroyed in your lifetime. After your lifetime, they may be burned or kept as the next holder thinks proper. I do not require them to be kept. I do require that they not be destroyed in your lifetime, because there are passages in them which were taken out of the parchment and which someone, in some century that will not be ours, may need to know were once in the work.*

*I will not destroy them.*

*Good.*

*And the box.*

He had been waiting for the question. He had known Possidius would put it; he had not, when he came down to the garden with the parchment, known whether Possidius would put it today or later in the week.

*The box stays where it is for now. When the time comes, you will take it. You will know when the time has come. When you take it, you will not open it again until you have left Hippo and crossed the sea. You will not show it to anyone in this diocese. You will write to Sophia at Tagaste once you have crossed; she will receive the letter through the senior bishop at Carthage, and she will know what to do with the news. After that the box is yours to keep until you can no longer keep it. When you can no longer keep it, you will give it to a man whom you trust to carry it the way I have carried it, and the way you will have carried it, and the way Simplicianus carried it before me. The man you give it to will know nothing about what is in the box beyond what I have told you. He will know that the box must be carried. He will know that the carrying is the carrying. He will not, in his lifetime, hand the box to the man it is for, because the man it is for will not, on every prediction, arrive in his lifetime. He will, like you, hand the box to a man whom he trusts to carry it. The chain will continue. It may continue for centuries. That is what has been laid on us. We did not choose it. We did not need to. We were the men who could carry it and so we carried it. The next man will be the same.*

Possidius listened. He listened with the listening of a man who had heard the substance of all of this in installments over thirteen years and who was now hearing it set out in one place for the first time. He nodded once when Augustine was finished. He did not speak for a long count.

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*I will do it.*

*I knew you would.*

*One question.*

*Ask.*

*The man you give it to.*

*I do not know him. I have never met him. He may not yet have been born. I would think that he is currently in the second decade of his life somewhere in the empire and that the thing in him that will permit him to take the box has not yet ripened. You will recognise him when you have him in front of you. The recognition is the recognition. There is no further sign.*

*And if I am wrong about him.*

*If you are wrong about him, you will know it within the first year of him having the box, and you will, at that point, take the box back from him. You will not be wrong. The recognition is not a thing that lies.*

Possidius nodded once more. He sat with what had been said. He set his hand briefly on Augustine's knee — the right knee, the bad one, which had not been touched by another hand in some years — and removed it.

The sun moved. The sea below them changed colour by a small degree.

*Aurelius. The Vandals.*

*I know.*

*The reports from Mauretania this summer.*

*I have been reading them. Eraclius has been bringing them to the desk.*

*They will be in this province within five years.*

*They will be in this province within three.*

Possidius looked at him.

*Three.*

*Three. Possibly four. They are moving faster than the previous reports suggested. The harvest in Mauretania this year was better than they expected and the Vandals fed well. They will winter in the western province and they will move east in the spring of next year, and they will cross into Numidia by the year after, and they will be at the gates of Hippo by the year after that or the year after that. I do not know whether I will be alive to see it. I suspect I will be alive to see it. In any case the question is not whether I will be alive but whether the work will be safe when the gates are at the city. The fair copies will not be safe in this diocese under the Vandals. The fair copies must be out of this diocese before the Vandals are at the gates. That is what the conversation about the five copies is for. That is what the conversation about the box is for.*

*I had not seen it as clearly as that.*

*I did not say it as clearly as that until today.*

They sat for some further time. The sun moved further. A small wind came off the sea and brought with it the particular smell of the harbour at the end of the working day — fish-oil and pitch and wet rope. Augustine had been smelling that smell from this garden for thirty years.

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*Take me back to the writing room. The parchment should be in the chest before the evening damp.*

Possidius rose. He helped Augustine to his feet — the right knee, getting up off the bench, was the bad knee, and Augustine took the help without comment. They walked back through the garden and through the cloister door and along the cloister to the writing room. Augustine put the last paragraph of the twenty-second book in the chest with the iron lock, on top of the wax tablet of the closing draft, and closed the chest, and turned the key.

The work was done.

He did not blow out the lamp until compline. He sat at the desk through vespers and through the small space between vespers and compline, with the chest on the upper shelf and the box on the upper shelf above it and the lamp on the desk and his hands flat on the desk on either side of where the parchment had been for thirteen years.

He thought: *The City of God is real. Build it.*

He thought it without having written it.

He went to compline — the last office of the night, the prayer that closed the day.
