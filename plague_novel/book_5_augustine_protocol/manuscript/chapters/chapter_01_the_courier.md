# Chapter 1 — The Courier

*Hippo Regius, August 410 CE.*

The fog was off the sea and stayed past the third hour. Augustine had walked the cloister twice in it that morning, and none of the small things going wrong with his body had been true the last time this city had heard news from Italy that mattered.

The community met at the third hour in the small house off the cloister. There were nine of them. Possidius's absence was noted; he was on church business in Calama and not expected back until Wednesday. The reading was from Cassian, on patience. They were in the third paragraph when the gate-keeper came.

He opened the door without knocking, which had not been his practice in twenty years at the gate. The reader, a deacon named Stephen who in his younger days had assisted at a difficult baptism Augustine no longer named aloud, broke off mid-sentence.

The gate-keeper said: *Father, a man at the gate from Italy.*

Augustine waited for more. The old man could not provide more, because his throat had closed.

*Bring him to the small parlour. Feed him first. I will come at the third hour.*

Stephen, after a glance that found nothing in Augustine's face that asked accommodation, returned to the reading. They finished the paragraph. They settled the disciplinary matters. They reviewed the liturgy. Augustine ended the meeting at the customary hour and went to the small parlour.

The man at the gate was a wine-merchant from Ostia named Marcus. He had eaten nothing because he had reached the bread, looked at it, and begun to weep. Augustine sat across from him and let it run. Weeping that did not wish to end could not be hurried.

After some minutes Marcus wiped his face on his sleeve.

*Bishop.*

*Tell me.*

*Rome is taken. The Goths came in by the north gate before midnight on the twenty-fourth of August. They were inside three days. They burned. They took. They killed where they were resisted and many places where they were not. There are Christians among them who spared the basilicas of the apostles, but there were many who did not spare. My cousin saw a procession of women carrying boys' bodies out of the Aventine quarter on the second day. He saw the Forum on fire. He says the eternal city is no longer.*

Augustine kept his hands on his knees.

*Father, what does it mean?*

The honest answer would have taken half an hour and would have helped no one. The pious answer would help this man for a quarter of an hour and then fail him. He gave the pious answer because the man in front of him would die without it.

*It means God has permitted what He has permitted. It means the city of stones may fall, and the city which is not of stones cannot be reached by the Goths. It means we will mourn, and we will continue, and we will give shelter to those who come.*

Marcus nodded the way a man nods when he has been told the thing he came to be told and now must do something else with his life. Augustine tore bread, put it into his hand, and watched a small piece of life come back into his face with the chewing.

*You will sleep here. Tomorrow we will find you a passage onward, or a place if you wish to remain.*

Marcus caught his hand and kissed it. Augustine let his hand be kissed, and went out into the fog.

He did not break the prayers. He went to the cathedral for the third-hour service and sang the psalms without alteration. The chant had been written for the burning of Jerusalem, centuries before, and it would outlast Hippo as it had outlasted Jerusalem. *I am thinking this in order not to think the other thing yet,* he thought, and stopped thinking it.

After the third-hour service he heard confession.

The bench was against the south wall, the curtain drawn for those who wished it. There were six. A steward who had cheated his master of grain. An apprentice fuller who had struck his sister. A soldier on his way to Carthage who had taken the wife of his cousin. A woman of thirty whose husband had returned from a campaign two years older than he had left and was beating her in the new manner. A deacon who had spoken sharply to his bishop, which was Augustine, and wished to be heard saying so. He gave penances and did not let his thinking elsewhere shorten the hearing.

The sixth was a girl of nine.

She had been in Hippo eleven days, sleeping in the warehouses by the harbour with her mother and the other Romans who had crossed in July. The grandmother had not come south with them. She had had property in the Aventine and had insisted to the end that flight was for the impious. Yesterday a man in the warehouses had told the girl's mother what had been done in the smaller of the grandmother's two houses, and the mother had told the girl that the bishop knew what God did and did not do.

She put it more briefly. *Father, my grandmother was burned alive in her own house. They put her on her own table and they made the table burn. The Phrygian woman saw and got out by the wall. Father, I want to know if my grandmother is in heaven.*

Her hair was unwashed and braided badly. Her hands on her knees were small and clean.

*Tell me your grandmother's name.*

*Maria.*

*Maria is in the hands of God, who is more merciful than any of us can imagine, and who knows the soul that He made.*

*Father, did God let it happen?*

He looked at the girl who was waiting for the answer that would let her sleep tonight in the warehouse on the straw.

*Yes. God permitted it.*

She nodded slowly. She had already known it; she had needed to hear it said.

*Then God did not love her.*

*He loved her. He loves her now.*

*Then I do not understand love.*

*Neither do I.*

She did not weep. She thanked him in the formula her mother had taught her — *Bless me, father, for what is sweeter than honey* — and he gave her the blessing. He laid his hand on her braided head. The hair under his hand was cold. He told her to come to the basilica on Sunday and to eat at the door any day she was hungry. She got up and went out into the fog.

The bench was empty. He stayed at it. Stephen came at length and asked whether there were further confessions; there were not. The deacon left. Augustine went to his cell.

He did not eat at the noon meal. The shutters were half-closed against the fog, which had thinned to a clean light, and the cell was cool. He sat at the desk a long time. The wax was in front of him. The stylus was in his hand. He did not move.

Then he began the work he had been carrying without name for some years.

The wax took two attempts. The third held, and he transferred the sentence to the parchment with the iron pen:

*Gloriosissimam civitatem Dei sive in hoc temporum cursu, cum inter impios peregrinatur ex fide vivens, sive in illa stabilitate sedis aeternae, quam nunc expectat per patientiam, quoadusque iustitia convertatur in iudicium, deinceps adeptura per excellentiam victoria ultima et pace perfecta, hoc opere instituto et mea ad te promissione debito defendere adversus eos qui conditori eius deos suos praeferunt, fili carissime Marcelline, suscepi.*

He addressed it, by name, to Marcellinus — his friend the imperial tribune now in Carthage, who would be glad to receive a personal letter and would not yet know that the letter was also a book and that the book was also other things. The most glorious city of God, whether in this passing course of time when she lives by faith as a stranger among the impious, or in that stability of her eternal seat which she now awaits patiently — this city he had undertaken to defend against those who preferred their gods to her founder.

Augustine sat with the sentence. He had defended a city he could not see against accusations from a city that no longer stood. Whether he believed the sentence tonight was a smaller question than whether the woman in the warehouse with her hand on her daughter's head needed it written. The parchment he covered. He went to vespers.

He took bread and water at the evening table in silence and went to his cell early. Sleep was the intention. He lay on the straw mattress and closed his eyes, and the seventy-eighth psalm ran through his head until the second hour of night, when the bell at the gate rang.

The bell at the gate did not ring at the second hour of night except for emergency. He sat up.

Footsteps in the cloister, two voices, then Stephen's knock at the cell door, three short.

*Father.*

*Yes.*

*A man at the gate has brought a body. He says the body is not yet a body. He says it is a courier from Italy.*

Augustine pulled on his outer tunic and took up the small lamp.

*Bring them to the writing room. Both of them. Wake no one else. Send the gate-keeper back to the gate. Tell Eraclius nothing.*

The cloister was dark. He carried the lamp through it to the writing room — a small chamber between his cell and the library where he received correspondence and dictated to his secretary, with a long pine table, two stools, a shelf of writing materials, and a brazier in the corner kept lit on cold nights and unlit tonight because the August fog was warm.

The lamp went down on the table. He cleared the day's correspondence onto the shelf, spread a folded piece of clean linen across the table, and laid beside it the iron paring knife he used for trimming pens. The room was small and badly suited to what was about to be done in it. From the first lamp he lit a second.

The men came through the door. The man at the gate was a Numidian carter named Lucius, whom Augustine knew slightly — honest in his trade, not particularly devout. He had found the courier in a ditch beside a still-living mule three miles outside the western wall, and had recognized that the man on the mule had been put on the mule, not riding it. He had brought both to the basilica because the basilica was the nearest place that did not ask money for taking in a dying stranger.

Stephen took the shoulders, Lucius the feet. They laid the man on the linen.

He was perhaps thirty, in the dust-coloured tunic of an imperial courier, belted in plain leather. His face was the colour of wet ash. His eyes were open and tracking and not yet entirely empty. There was a thin pink foam at the corner of his mouth. Around his belly under the tunic was a long bandage wrapped tight in clean linen and over the linen a stained ligature.

Augustine put his hand on the forehead. Cold. He bent close.

*Brother. I am the bishop here. I am going to look. Do you understand?*

The eyes moved. The lips moved. No sound came.

*I will be quick. Then we will speak.*

He brought the lamp closer and lifted the tunic. The bandage went around the belly twice, neatly, the way a soldier-medic would have wrapped it; the ligature over it was tight and competent. He cut the ligature, unwrapped the bandage in long turns, and saw what had been done.

The wound ran horizontally a hand's breadth above the navel from the right flank to the left, perhaps a span and a half long, closed with ten or twelve coarse sutures of waxed linen thread set wide and quickly — the way a man stitches a sack he means to open again. The skin around it was red and warm where the rest of the body was cold. There was a smell from it he had known once before, in his first year as a presbyter, burying plague dead, that he did not wish to name.

He looked at Stephen. *Take the carter to the kitchen. Give him bread and wine. Pay him. Tell him to sleep in the lay quarters and leave at first light. Ask him no questions. Then come back and stand outside this door and let no one in until I open it.*

Stephen took Lucius out. The carter looked once at the man on the table and then did not look again. The bolt slid. Footsteps went, returned, and stopped outside.

The courier's eyes had closed. His chest rose and fell unevenly. Augustine put two fingers under the angle of the jaw. The pulse was thin and fast.

*Brother. I am going to open the wound. There is a thing inside you. I am going to take it out. Then I will give you absolution. Do you understand?*

The lips moved. A sound came that was not a word.

He set the iron knife to the line of stitches at the right end of the wound and cut three. The wound parted like a mouth. Bad-smelling fluid soaked the linen and a small amount of fresh blood came with it. He cut the next three and the next, and when the wound was open from end to end he set the knife down and put his fingers into the body of the man.

His fingers found, inside the cavity, something not of the man.

It was small and flat, the size of his palm, wrapped in oiled leather and knotted, pressed up between the loops of the gut and the inner wall of the abdomen. It came out cleanly. The leather was wet but the knot was still tight. He laid it on the linen beside the wound and looked once more into the cavity for a second object. There was none. There was only the gut, cut once and badly, that had been bleeding into the body for the eleven days since. With the cavity open to the lamp, the smell that had been faint a moment before became the smell of a man who had been dying for a week and a half from the inside.

The courier had been kept alive on purpose. The cut had been small enough not to kill quickly. The stitching had been competent enough to keep the cargo in place. The men who had done it had wanted the courier to be found alive, or nearly alive, by whoever would be required to open him.

The courier's eyes opened.

*It is done. I have it. Brother, can you speak?*

The lips moved. Augustine put his ear to them. The breath was foul. The lips shaped a sound, then another. He waited. The third was a syllable.

*Cy —*

*Cyprian?*

*Pri —*

*Cyprian. I have heard you. I have it.*

The eyes met his. There was nothing in them but the syllable and the wish for the syllable to be heard. The mouth shaped something smaller.

*Wri —*

*Write it down. I will write it down.*

The courier closed his eyes. Augustine went to the shelf, took down a small wax tablet, and scratched into the wax: *Cyprianus — courier — Ostia — eleven days — cavity sewn closed — Aug. 410.* He set the tablet on the shelf face down and returned to the table.

Augustine sat on the stool beside the courier's head and took the man's hand. The hand had no strength.

*I will give you absolution. Hear the words and do not try to answer.*

He spoke the form. He made the cross over the brow with his thumb. He said the dismissal. The lips moved through the form along with him for the first three words and then stopped. The pulse under his fingers thinned. The breath in the chest paused, started again, paused, started again, paused, and did not start again. Augustine sat for some minutes with his hand on the man's hand.

The linen went up over the body to the chin. The eyes he did not close; they closed cleaner after a quarter of an hour.

The leather packet was on the linen where he had set it down. He untied it.

Inside was a folded piece of papyrus, stiff from carriage but undamaged. The leather had kept the fluids of the body off it. He spread it on the shelf in the light of the second lamp.

It was written in late common Greek in a clerk's hand he did not know, on papyrus he judged from the fibre and the laid-down lines to be Egyptian and not less than ten years old. In the margins, in a small Latin glossing hand which was a different hand from the Greek, were short translations and notes. There was no salutation, no signature, no city of origin. There was only the body of the document, which was, on the first reading, a list.

It was a list of forty-one entries grouped under seven headings. Each heading was the name of a place. *Akkad. Ur. Ugarit. Mukenai. Tyros. Persis. Karchēdōn.* Akkad, Ur, Ugarit, Mycenae, Tyre, Persia, Carthage. He knew Carthage in the way an African bishop knew Carthage, intimately and without illusion. He knew Persia and Mycenae as a man knew them who had read Herodotus once and Homer many times. He knew Tyre from scripture. He had heard of Ugarit. He had not heard of Akkad or of Ur as cities, having met the latter only as the place from which Abraham was called.

The list was an extraction. Each section contained between four and seven entries. The entries described, briefly and without rhetoric, a thing that had been observable in the place named in the years before the place ceased. *The magistrates appointed without examination by the magistrates of the previous year. The temple incomes diverted to the household of the king. The granaries reporting full and being empty. The frontier garrisons unpaid for two seasons together. The coinage debased to two parts in five. The countryside emptied and not returning. The army of the city composed in greater part of those whose fathers had not been of the city. The festivals retained and the meaning of the festivals not retained. The chief magistrates buying their offices and the office-buying entered in the registers without comment. The aqueducts left to silt. The corn ration extended at the cost of the farmers who had been required to bring corn to the city without payment. The ambassadors of the city ignored by the cities that had once attended to them.*

He read on. The Latin glosses, set in a hand that knew Latin but had not been native to it, gave the phrase in plain Latin and occasionally added a word: *cf. Polybius vi*, or *cf. Sallust*, or *non in Romae historicis sed in archivis ipsis*. The glossator had read in the archives themselves of the cities he glossed, and wrote a Latin Augustine judged to be of Alexandria.

At the end of the seven sections, in the same Greek hand as the body, was a closing paragraph of perhaps eight lines. He translated as he read.

*From the seven cities here named, forty-one conditions are set down. These conditions were observable, by men who watched, in each city in the years before that city ceased to be the city it had been. Of these forty-one conditions, the city of Rome in the consulship of Arcadius and Honorius, in the year that is now eight years past from the writing of this document, exhibited not less than thirty-one. The remaining ten were not absent. They were not yet observable to the writer of this document. They will be observable shortly. He who reads this document is to do with it what he can. The writer is no one of consequence. The matter is of consequence.*

There was no signature.

The second reading took the better part of an hour. By its end he understood not only what the document was but what it was for. It was an instrument — the working notebook of an analyst, distilled into a form that could be carried by a single courier in the inside of a man's body across the sea. The analyst had not written it for posterity. The analyst had written it for whoever would receive it. The analyst had no name and offered none, because a document of this kind does not survive if its author can be traced.

At the shelf, with the courier's body cooling on the table behind him and the lamp burning low in front, he saw the shape of the problem.

Papyrus does not survive. No one in Africa would copy this on its own merits. No one in Africa, if it were not copied, would see it. If he died in his sleep tonight, the packet would be in Eraclius's hands by morning and Eraclius would not know what to do with it. No document of this kind survived the next century unless the institution of the next century had already decided to copy it. The institution of the next century in this part of the world would be the Church or it would be no one, and the Church would not need this papyrus on its own merits; it would need it only if it could not be separated from a thing the Church could not afford to lose.

He had a paragraph already.

Back at the desk, he took up the wax tablet, turned it over, and wrote under the line about the courier:

*The City of God to be in twenty-two books. Books one through ten: refutation. Books eleven through twenty-two: the city itself. Within the eleven later books to be set down, beneath the proper teaching, the matter of the document received this night. Method to be worked out. Method must work for a reader four hundred years from this writing. Method must not depend on me to be alive to explain it.*

The wax went smooth under the stylus. He rewrote the note more compactly. He had the shape, not yet the method. The shape would do for the night.

Augustine folded the papyrus, wrapped it again in the leather, and took down from the upper shelf a small bronze box in which he had once kept Monnica's letters, until those letters had been boxed differently and the box emptied. The leather packet went inside. He placed the box at the back of the upper shelf behind two unrelated rolls of accounts, closed the cupboard shutter, and set the wax tablet face down on the desk.

He turned to the courier.

The linen came back from the chin. He closed the eyes — which had been open half an hour now and were ready to close — and drew the linen up over the face. The folding went as the plague dead had been folded in his first year as a priest: three folds along the body, two across. The order had not left him.

The bolt slid back. He opened the door. Stephen was outside.

*The carter is gone?*

*To the lay quarters. He sleeps. He asked nothing.*

*Get the spades. Both. Wake no one else. Bring them to the small east door of the cloister.*

The two of them carried the courier wrapped in linen out by the small east door at the third hour of night, the same door by which Crispus had been carried thirteen years and four months earlier. They went to the same corner of the burying-ground beyond the western wall — the corner used in the previous century for plague dead, no longer consecrated, to which Augustine had since the consecration of the new font returned more than once. They dug a trench narrower than was usual and deeper than was usual. They put the courier in. Augustine said the form for a man whose name he would not record. Stephen said the responses. They filled the trench. They tamped. The work took until the seventh hour of night, as it had taken thirteen years before. Augustine noticed that he was measuring it, and that he had not noticed himself measuring the first one until he was measuring this one.

They returned to the writing room and burned the soaked linen in the brazier, which took until the brazier glowed and there was a smell Augustine would have to explain in the morning by saying it had been used for medical refuse, which was nominally true. They scrubbed the table with vinegar and salt. They scrubbed the floor where one drop had fallen. They did not speak through any of it except for the necessary words.

*Go and sleep. You are at first prayers.*

*Father.* Stephen hesitated.

Augustine waited.

*Will there be others?*

The deacon was forty-one. He had been a senior reader for nine years. At twenty-eight he had been the hands of a thing his bishop had asked of him in a baptistery, and he had not asked since to be told of any work of that kind that did not require his hands. He was asking now whether there would be other couriers, not another Crispus.

*I do not know. The man I have buried tonight was killed at Ostia. Whoever killed him knew he was carrying. They did not retrieve. They sent. We will know more later. Tell me at once if any unfamiliar man comes to the gate, in any clothes, on any pretext, in the next month. Tell me before you tell any other.*

*Father.* He went.

Augustine sat at the desk. The lamp guttered. He took down the bronze box, looked at it once, put it back. He took up the parchment with the first paragraph and read it again. The sentence was not less beautiful for being also, now, the wrapping of a thing that had come tonight in a wallet in the inside of a man.

He covered the parchment, blew out one lamp, took the other, and went to his cell. He lay on the straw mattress and closed his eyes, and this time he slept. The sleep took perhaps two hours. The bell rang for first prayers and he was there.

---

Possidius came back from Calama on the Wednesday, two days early, having heard the news of Rome on the road. He arrived in the late morning, dust on him, the small mule he rode tied at the gate. He embraced Augustine in the cloister. He had not known what he would find. He found his bishop alive, in the daily round, the prayers sung, the cathedral functioning, the refugee work organized. He embraced him a second time without speaking, then stepped back.

*Aurelius. You look badly.*

*The week has been bad.*

*The week has been bad for everyone.*

*Yes.*

They walked together to the writing room because Possidius wished to see the work and because Augustine wished him to. They sat at the table — the same table — and Augustine showed him the first paragraph. Possidius read it twice and set it down.

*This will be very long.*

*Yes.*

*This will be the principal work.*

*Yes.*

*You have been carrying it for years.*

*I have.*

Possidius nodded. He looked around the room by habit, the way a long friend looks at the workplace of another long friend after an absence. His eye paused at the upper shelf, where the bronze box was now where it had not been before. His eye paused on the brazier, which had a residue that had not been there when he last sat at the table. His eye paused on the corner of the floor near the leg of the table where the salt and vinegar had dried into a whitish bloom which had been wiped twice but not entirely. His eye returned to Augustine.

He did not look at the upper shelf again. He did not look at the brazier again. He did not look at the floor again. He looked at Augustine. He held the look for the count of three. Augustine did not look away and did not offer a sentence.

*I will be with those preparing for baptism this afternoon. I will see you at vespers. You should eat at the noon table today.*

*I will eat.*

*Promise me.*

*I promise.*

Possidius went out. Augustine sat alone for some minutes. The eye had paused three times and had returned to him. The silence had been particular. There were two of them in the work now, and one had been told nothing, and that one would continue not to be told. The not-telling was now a thing between them.

Covering the parchment, he went to the noon table and ate.

In the afternoon, while Possidius was with the baptism candidates, he returned to the writing room, took down the bronze box and the parchment together, and began the second paragraph. The shelf had room.
