# Chapter 2 — Una, and the Living Wound

*Hippo Regius, November 410 CE.*

He had not known that Adeodatus was buried in the western cemetery outside Carthage.

The letter that told him this came down by the regular post on a Wednesday morning, in a bundle with two from the senior bishop at Carthage and a small sheaf of grain receipts that the basilica was expected to forward. Eraclius brought the bundle to the writing room at the second hour and set it on the table without remark. Augustine was at the desk, on the second draft of the chapter on Brennus and the Gauls, and was not pleased with the second draft. He pushed the parchment aside and took up the bundle.

He read the senior bishop's two letters first, because the seal asked for that — a query about the council date, a request to host a delegation in February. He set them on the right side of the desk for replies and the grain receipts on the left for Eraclius's clerk. The third letter was addressed in a hand he did not know, careful and clerkly: *to Aurelius Augustinus, Bishop of Hippo Regius, with the courtesy of the church.* The seal was a plain wax disc, no signet, pressed by the side of a thumb. He cut it with his thumbnail.

Four pages. The hand was the same clerk's hand throughout, which meant the writer had dictated rather than written; in the Latin of the African coast one read a man's condition from whether he wrote his own letters. A freedman, then, or a man of the working middling sort who had paid a few coppers for a clerk's hour. The letter ran:

*To the most reverend Bishop of Hippo Regius, from Anthony, freedman of the late household of Maximus and overseer of the lower properties on the Way of the Cothon, greeting in our Lord.*

*Bishop, I write to inform you of a death. My sister, who in the records of our late master's household appeared by the name Una, died on the twenty-sixth day of October in the small house she had kept for nineteen years on the lower street behind the harbour, of a fever which had been with her since the spring and which the physicians of the lower town judged in October to be incurable. She had received the consolations of the Church from the priest of the basilica of the Two Martyrs, who attended her in her last week. She was buried on the day after her death in the western cemetery outside the city, beside the small marker she had set up there twenty-one years ago for the boy Adeodatus, who as you will remember was also buried in that ground.*

Augustine, who had reached the second page, set the letter down on the desk and closed his eyes for a count of perhaps thirty.

He had not known.

Adeodatus had died at Tagaste in the late summer of 389. The boy had been seventeen years and four months. The fever had taken him in five days. Augustine had buried him in the small Catholic cemetery at Tagaste, beside the family plot, and had composed the inscription himself — a small Latin elegy in a meter the boy had loved and that Augustine had thought, at the time, perhaps too literary; he had let it stand because the boy, if he could read it, would have laughed at his father for trying. He had not known that, in the same autumn, somewhere in Carthage, she had also been at a graveside. She had paid a stoneworker for a marker. She had visited it. She had been buried beside it in the November before this morning, and the marker was the only stone in any cemetery in the world that named the boy in her hand. In twenty-one years no person who could have told him had told him, because he had not given any person reason to believe he would have wished to be told.

He opened his eyes and read on.

*She left no testament because she had little to leave. The small house was held in lease from the master's estate; the lease passes back to the estate on her death and we will let it again before Christmas, to a Greek widow whose name need not concern you. The household goods are few and of small value. The women who lodged in the house at the time of her death — six of them, of various conditions — have been given two weeks to find other lodging, and we are placing them as we can; one of them, a Mauretanian girl of seventeen, has been received into the converts' instruction at the Two Martyrs and may be useful to the Church there in time.*

*Of my sister herself. I will say to you what I will say to no other man, because she asked me, in the second-last week of her life, to send to you on her death the letter you wrote her in the spring of 383, which she had kept folded inside the chest at the foot of her bed since she received it, and which I now enclose with this letter — she did not ask me to write to you of anything else, but the priest of the Two Martyrs has counselled me to write what is in this letter, judging that some of it would matter to you for reasons of conscience that he did not invite me to inquire into and which I have not inquired into.*

*She kept the small house for nineteen years. She did not marry. She did not, in her conduct as I knew it across all those years, give any man cause to remark on her in the lower town. She ran the house as a place of lodging for women in difficulty, who could pay what they could pay and who in the months when they could not pay were allowed to stay and to make up the rent later, and many of them, as you may suppose, made the rent in the manner that women in the harbour quarter make the rent when the fish-trade is poor and the textile work has dried up. My sister did not enquire into the manner of the rent. She fed the women who came in. She nursed those who fell ill. She buried two with her own hands in the western cemetery in the years when the plague was bad and the city would not pay for the burials of harbour women.*

*She was, as you will no doubt remember, of a quiet and stubborn character, much given to silence and not at all given to complaint, and in this also she was steady to the end. She did not at any time in nineteen years send to you for help, although she had your address and could have written, and although in the bad year after the riots she came near to selling the household plate in order to feed the women in the house and could have taken a letter to you with the plate. She did not send. She did not write. She did not, in my hearing, ever speak your name. She referred to you, when she had occasion to refer to you, as the bishop in Numidia, as if you were a public man whose office was the only thing of you that survived for her.*

*The chest at the foot of her bed I have not yet opened. The priest of the Two Martyrs has it under seal in his sacristy, awaiting your instruction. The contents, my sister told me before the fever took her speech, are: the letter you wrote her in 383, which I now enclose; a wooden cup that you once sent her by a courier with a small sum of money in the year that Adeodatus was born, which she had kept though she had never used; a hair-comb of bone of no special value; and certain papers of accounts from the lodging-house, which are of no interest to you and which I will hold for the estate. There is also a small bronze hand-mirror, which she said you had given her in the year of your meeting in Carthage, and which she said might be returned to you if you wished.*

*Bishop, write to me by return what your wish is. The chest weighs perhaps three pounds. The estate will pay the carriage. I do not require an answer for the rest of what is in this letter, and I do not believe my sister required one either, though the priest of the Two Martyrs has hinted to me that he would not be displeased to know that a Mass had been said at Hippo for the soul of his late parishioner, and I leave that to your judgment as a fellow servant of the Church.*

*I am your servant in our Lord,*
*Anthony.*

He read the letter through a second time, then a third. On the third reading he found that he was reading not the letter but the spaces between the lines of the letter, and he set it down. The second sheet was the enclosure: his own letter to her of 383, in his own hand, twenty-seven years gone.

He recognised it at the first letter. He had been twenty-eight when he wrote it. It had been written from Rome in the spring after he had crossed from Carthage, before he met Ambrose, before he knew what would happen. Eight lines in a hand that had been still partly the hand of a young man — not a love letter as he had written her in the early years, but a domestic letter of small things. The wine at Rome was bad. He had taken a room near the Caelian Hill. His teaching was going badly. He had had a tooth pulled by a man in the Subura who had hurt him. Adeodatus was to be told that the small wooden horse with the broken leg was at the bottom of his father's writing chest if he had been looking for it. The letter ended: *Mind yourself in the heat. The heat in Carthage in May has always been too much for you. I will write again at the next post.* He had not signed his first name. He had signed: *Aurelius, who loves you.*

He had not, as far as he could remember, written to her again from Rome. He had not written from Milan. He had had her sent back to Carthage from Milan in the spring of the year that followed this letter, and he had not written then. He had not written after. The letter on the desk in front of him was the last letter he had written to her, and she had kept it.

He sat with it.

He did not weep. He did not pray. He watched the small piece of his own younger handwriting on the desk as one watches a fire. After a quarter-hour he heard, or thought he heard, the bell for the third hour. He let the bell ring or not ring.

After some further minutes he reached out and folded the two letters together along the brother's fold and set them on the upper shelf at the back, behind two unrelated rolls of accounts. The bronze box from the previous summer was already on that shelf. He set the folded letters beside it. The two of them rested there together — the letters and the box — and he turned away from the shelf without looking again at where he had placed them.

The parchment of the chapter on Brennus and the Gauls was still where he had pushed it. He took it up. He read what he had drafted that morning. He could not enter it. He set it aside.

He took up wax and stylus.

He had been preparing, that month, a clean exemplar of the *Confessions* for Paulinus of Nola, who had asked some months earlier for a fair copy, the older copy in his possession having grown stained and partly illegible from heavy use in his community. The fair copy was being made by a junior scribe in the scriptorium, working from Augustine's own working copy — annotated in Augustine's hand, kept on the lower shelf of his cell when he was not consulting it. Augustine had told the boy to bring him the working copy whenever a passage was about to be transcribed that Augustine wished to amend. The boy was careful. He brought the working copy three or four times a week. He had brought it that morning, before the meeting, with a small wax tablet on which he had written the next passage to be transcribed and a query about a punctuation mark.

The next passage was Book Six, chapter fifteen.

Augustine took up the working copy, opened it, found the chapter, and read it through.

The chapter, as he had written it ten years ago, in the unhurried Latin of the man he had been at forty-six, ran in its essential sentences as follows:

*Meanwhile my sins were multiplied; and she with whom I had been wont to share my bed, having been torn from my side because she was an obstacle to the marriage I was preparing to make, my heart, which clave to her, was broken and wounded and shed blood. And she returned to Africa, vowing to Thee never to know any other man, leaving with me the natural son I had had by her. But I, unhappy man, no imitator of a woman, unable to bear the delay of two years until she whom I sought should be of age to marry, and being not so much a lover of marriage as a slave of lust, took to me another woman, not a wife, by which the disease of my soul was sustained and prolonged, either as it was or in greater force, under the protection of habit, into the kingdom of marriage. Nor was that wound of mine which had been made by the cutting away of the former one healed; but after fervour and the most acute pain, it festered, and the pain became colder but more desperate.*

He had written it ten years ago. He read it now. The wound that festered was not metaphor. It was the wound, and was still festering, and would still be festering on the day he died. The prose of ten years past had gone exactly as far as a man could go in writing about a wound of that kind without doing the further thing.

The further thing was naming her.

She was *quaedam* in the working copy — *a certain woman.* She was *quaedam* in the published copies that had been going out across the Mediterranean for ten years, and *quaedam* in the copy the boy in the scriptorium was making for Paulinus. She had been *quaedam* on the day he had written the passage at this same desk in this same writing room, with the lamp on the same shelf, having decided in the moment of writing — he remembered the moment — to leave the name out. He had told himself at the time that to name her would be to give the reader access to a person who did not belong to the reader. He had told Possidius the same thing, the one time Possidius had asked. He had told the bishop of Stridon, who had not asked.

Sitting at the desk now, he was not certain that the reason had been the reason.

He took up the wax tablet, scraped it clean of the boy's punctuation query, and set the stylus to the wax. He thought: I will write what I would have written if I had been writing the passage today, knowing what came of her, and I will see what I write.

He wrote: *Et illa in Africam redierat, vovens tibi alium se virum nescituram, relicto apud me naturali ex illa filio meo* — *And she returned to Africa, vowing to Thee never to know any other man, leaving with me the natural son I had had by her*. He wrote it as he had written it ten years ago. The hand was older.

Then he wrote her name.

He wrote her name in the Latin form she had been baptised in, which he knew though he had not spoken it aloud in twenty-five years, and which he wrote now in clean small letters of the kind a man uses for the names of the dead in church registers. He looked at it. He saw his own hand having written it. He saw, at the same moment, how he might let the name stand, write the rest of the passage around it — *And [her name] returned to Africa* — and send the amended passage to the boy in the morning, and have the boy transcribe it into the fair copy for Paulinus, and have the fair copy go out to Nola, and have the name go out from there into the copies that Paulinus's community would copy, and into the copies that those copies would generate, and into the *Confessions* as it would be read in the next century and the century after, and into the historical record of the Latin Church for as long as the Latin Church should make copies. The move was one stroke of his pen. The move was the doing of justice to a woman who had been alive on the twenty-sixth of October and was now dead.

A minute passed.

Then he drew the flat blade of the stylus through the wax across her name, hard enough that the wax buckled, three short strokes, the way one strikes through a clerk's error. The name was no longer legible.

In the cleared space above the strike-through he wrote: *quaedam.*

He read what he had written. *Et quaedam in Africam redierat.* *And a certain woman returned to Africa.*

After some time he set the stylus down. He had been doing this for twenty-five years, and it was no longer the kind of thing for which a person of his age supplied a reason. The wax would be re-melted by the boy in the scriptorium tomorrow morning. The fair copy would say *quaedam.* The published *Confessions* would continue to say *quaedam.* The historical record of the Latin Church would, in the matter of this woman, continue to be silent, and the silence would continue to be the silence he had made.

*Forgive me.*

He could not finish the sentence. He did not know to whom the sentence was addressed. He did not write it down.

He capped the wax tablet by reflex, so that it would not be marred by anything that fell on it before the boy came in the morning, and set it on the corner of the desk where the boy would see it. The stylus went back in its place.

His mind moved, without his asking it to, to the chest in the sacristy at Carthage. The bronze hand-mirror. The wooden cup. The bone hair-comb. He thought, after these, of the wooden horse with the broken leg, which had not been in the chest in Carthage and which he had not seen in twenty-seven years and which was at the bottom of a writing chest at Tagaste he had not opened in fifteen, and the broken leg of which he could now feel under his thumb as if his thumb had touched it that morning. After the horse, Adeodatus — going down in five days of fever, who had asked for his mother on the third day and had been told she could not come because of the distance, and who had asked again on the fourth and had been told she had been sent for, and who had died on the fifth without further asking. The mother had been told by what messenger Augustine did not now remember, and had set up a marker in the western cemetery outside Carthage in the autumn of 389, and had visited the marker, alone, in every November between then and this — twenty-one Novembers in a row — and had, on the twenty-sixth of October in the present year, been laid down beside the marker she had paid for. *She went without ever having had from me the thing she could have had from a man who had not been me.*

He sat. The bell for the noon prayers rang. He did not move. The bell stopped. Eraclius's clerk knocked at the door and did not enter and went away. The boy from the scriptorium came at the door at the half-hour and did not enter and went away. He sat through the noon meal. By mid-afternoon he stood up, went to the cupboard, took out a flask of vinegar-water and drank, ate one of the small honey-cakes Stephen kept on the upper shelf for fast days, and went back to the desk.

The clerk of the procurator's office arrived in the late afternoon, having ridden up from the harbour and presented himself first at the gate and then at the writing room with the gate-keeper's introduction. He was a small man of perhaps thirty-five in a clerk's tunic with the procurator's seal pinned at the shoulder. His name was Julian. He had been to the writing room twice before, in the previous year, with similar business; he and Augustine had a working acquaintance.

*Bishop.*

*Julian. Sit. Wine?*

*Thank you, no, Bishop, I am to be back at the harbour within the hour. I have one writ for your countersignature.*

He took a folded parchment from his satchel and laid it on the desk, set in the middle, and sat back. The seal was the procurator's seal in red wax, and below it the smaller seal of the Numidian treasury official. The fold was the standard imperial fold. Augustine reached out and unfolded the writ.

It was a writ of confiscation under the Edict of Unity of February 405 — for the seizure of the grain stores held in the storehouse attached to the Donatist place of worship at the village of Tibilis Minor in the territory of Calama. The grain in question was the seed-grain set aside for spring sowing and the winter-grain held against the lean months of February and March. The village was a Donatist congregation of fifteen households, as enumerated in the procurator's last visitation in the third month of the present year. The writ required the countersignature of the metropolitan or of the bishop assigned to the case, to confirm that the said congregation was Donatist and not Catholic and that the grain stores were therefore liable to confiscation under the constitution and its subsequent amendments.

Augustine had been the bishop assigned to Donatist cases in the territory of Calama since 408, when Possidius had asked, on his own initiative, that the work of countersignature be transferred to Hippo because the work was painful to him and would be done less painfully by Augustine. Augustine had agreed. He had been countersigning writs of this kind, on average, two a month for two years and four months. He had countersigned, by his own count, fifty-six.

He read the writ through. He knew the village.

He had been to Tibilis Minor in the year of his consecration of the new font, in the spring after Easter, on a routine visit to the Catholic minority — six families of fishermen and one family of stoneworkers, who met in a converted stable on the western edge of the settlement and who had been petitioning the diocese for funds to put a roof on the converted stable that would not leak in the rains. He had granted the funds. He remembered the elder of the Catholic congregation, a man named John, freedman of the local stoneworking estate, who had wept when Augustine gave the funds and had insisted Augustine stay to supper at his own house, where Augustine had eaten fish stew and a kind of barley bread that had had a small quantity of oil pressed through it before baking and had been very good. He remembered also the Donatist elder of the village, with whom he had had a brief and not unfriendly conversation in the small market square — a man whose name he did not now recall, who had been polite, who had offered him water from the village well, and who had said, at the end of the conversation, *Bishop, our grandfathers parted from your grandfathers. Let us not part further than they did.* Augustine had said: *That is in our hands.* The Donatist elder had nodded and walked away.

He read the writ a second time. The writ was correctly drawn. The grain quantities were specified — seventeen of seed-grain, forty-three of winter-grain, given in the standard measure. The procurator's enumeration of the fifteen households was attached as a schedule. The schedule listed only the names of the heads of household and the count of dependents per household, not the names of the dependents. The dependents totalled fifty-eight.

He understood, with the part of his mind that understood the operations of late-summer accounts, that fifteen households of fifty-eight dependents at Tibilis Minor without seed-grain and without winter-grain would not, between them and through their own labour and any charity their Donatist co-religionists at Calama could afford them, get all fifty-eight dependents through to the next harvest. Some would be carried by neighbours. Some would walk to Calama and be taken in by relations. Some would walk to Hippo. Some would not walk to anywhere. He had been doing the arithmetic of these confiscations long enough to have a working figure: of the dependents in a confiscated village, in a year that was not particularly hard, the loss between November and April was usually between one in eight and one in ten. The small children went first, then the very old, then the women who had nursed too recently. Of fifty-eight dependents, the loss would be between six and eight.

He did not put the figure into words. He put it into the part of his mind that did not require words. He took up the iron pen and dipped it.

He countersigned the writ on the line indicated, in the standard formula:

*Aurelius Augustinus, Episcopus Hipponensis Regius, secundum misericordiam confirmavit. According to mercy, confirmed.*

He blotted the signature with sand, tapped the sand off, folded the writ along the standard fold, and passed it across the desk to Julian.

Julian took the writ, glanced once at the signature, nodded, and put the writ in his satchel.

*Bishop. The procurator thanks you.*

*The procurator is welcome.*

*The visitation of the village is to be effected by the cohort under Quintus on December the third. The grain will move to the procurator's stores at Calama. The procurator will, as is customary, allocate a portion to the basilica's relief work at Calama as a gift in kind, which will appear in your monthly accounts under the usual head.*

*Thank you. Eraclius will reconcile.*

*Bishop, by your leave.*

*Go in peace.*

Julian rose, bowed slightly, and went out. The door closed. Augustine heard his sandals along the cloister and then on the stones of the courtyard and then, after a pause for the gate-keeper, the sound of his horse on the stones outside the gate, and then the sound of the horse going away down the road to the harbour.

He sat at the desk.

He did not ask the clerk, at any point in the conversation, the question of how many. He did not ask the procurator, who was not present to be asked. He did not ask himself. He did not name the figure. He did not write it down. He did not, when he had finished signing, go to the upper shelf and add a line to the wax tablet of the previous summer to mark the village or the grain or the dependents. He did not write a note to remind himself, in the spring, to inquire after the village and learn what number had in fact been lost. He did not, when he stood up to go to vespers, take out the wax tablet on which he had earlier written *quaedam* over the strike-through and place the writ alongside it as objects of a single morning's work. The wax tablet sat at the corner of the desk where the boy from the scriptorium would see it in the morning. The writ was on its way to the harbour. The two objects were not on the desk together. He did not arrange for them to be on the desk together. He went to vespers.

He went by the cloister in the late afternoon light, the thin North African winter sun laying the shadow of the columns long across the stones. Stephen was lighting the lamps in the small chapel as he passed; the door of the meeting-room was dark. At the cathedral's side door the subdeacon held out the chasuble. He put it on, walked down the centre of the nave as the brethren took their places in the choir stalls and the people of Hippo took their places along the side aisles, and came to the bishop's chair. The prayers began.

The vespers cycle that day contained the canticle of the Virgin. *Magnificat anima mea Dominum.* *My soul magnifies the Lord, and my spirit hath rejoiced in God my Saviour, because He hath regarded the lowliness of His handmaid; for behold, from henceforth all generations shall call me blessed.* He sang it in his proper place, in the second voice as the cantor took the first. The verse came round: *He hath put down the mighty from their thrones, and hath exalted the lowly. He hath filled the hungry with good things, and the rich He hath sent empty away.* He sang it without alteration. His eyes went to the small flame of the sanctuary lamp and stayed there. The discipline of thinking of nothing held. He pronounced the dismissal, took off the chasuble in the sacristy, washed his hands in the basin, dried them on the linen the subdeacon held for him, and went to his cell. He lay down on the straw mattress with his sandals still on, closed his eyes, and slept until the bell for the night prayers.

He did not dream.

He woke at the bell, went to the night prayers, sang, came back to his cell, blew out the lamp, and slept.

In the morning the boy from the scriptorium came to the writing room at the second hour to take the corrections from the wax tablet for the next passage of the fair copy. Augustine was at the meeting. The boy found the tablet on the corner of the desk where Augustine had left it. He read the correction. He copied it carefully into the working notes for the fair copy: *quaedam* in the place where the working copy of Augustine's hand had also written *quaedam* and where, the boy noticed without remarking on it, three deep stylus-strokes had been drawn through whatever had been written before. The boy was a careful boy and did not inquire. He took the tablet to the warm-corner of the scriptorium where the wax was re-melted on a small brazier for re-use, and set it in the queue with the other tablets to be cleared. The wax was re-melted that afternoon. The tablet was used the next morning for a list of converts under instruction and for a letter to a Spanish bishop on the question of the date of Easter. Of what had been written on the wax in the writing room the day before, nothing remained.

The fair copy of the *Confessions* went out to Paulinus of Nola in the week before Christmas. The fair copy said *quaedam.* Paulinus's community copied the fair copy. Their copies were copied in turn. The published *Confessions* continued, in every century in which it was copied, to say *quaedam.*

In the second week of February, in the year following the writ, a small report from the cohort at Calama reached the procurator's office and was forwarded, in summary, to the basilica at Hippo as part of the customary monthly accounts. The report noted that the grain confiscation at Tibilis Minor had been effected without disturbance on the appointed day; that the village had not resisted; that the Donatist elder of the village had wept but had not impeded; and that there had been, in the period from the date of confiscation to the date of the report, six deaths in the village among dependents under fourteen and over sixty, of causes consistent with the season. The report did not give the names of the six. The summary in the basilica's monthly accounts gave the figure six and not the cause. Augustine, reviewing the monthly accounts at the end of February in the writing room, read the figure six and the figure for the Calama relief allocation and the figure for the basilica's grain stores, signed the bottom of the page in the formula he used for the monthly accounts, and turned the page.

He did not, in his work that month or in any month afterwards, return to the figure. The figure entered the long ledger of figures he had signed and ceased to be a figure in particular. The names of the six, which had not been recorded, were not recorded by anyone, then or later.

Of the six, four were children. The youngest was a girl of eleven months who had been weaned in the autumn because the mother's milk had failed under the bad nutrition of the November after the confiscation, and who died in the second week of January of the dehydration that followed a dysentery the mother could not treat for want of broth. The mother was a Donatist woman of twenty-six named Anna. She had not given her daughter a name; she had been waiting until the spring, in accordance with the custom of her family, which named children only after the first winter. The daughter of Anna was buried, on the day after she died, in the small ground beside the converted stable where the Donatist congregation of Tibilis Minor met for worship. There was no marker. The grave did not appear in any record.

The mother did not survive the spring. She died of the same dysentery in March. The father had died in the first cohort raid against a Donatist village in 408 and had not been recorded either. The brother of the daughter, a boy of seven, was taken in by relations at Calama and raised in the Catholic communion under a baptismal name not his own. He grew up. He died of an illness in his thirty-second year in Carthage. His name was not in any document signed by Aurelius Augustinus.
