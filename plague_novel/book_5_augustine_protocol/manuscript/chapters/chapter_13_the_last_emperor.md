# CHAPTER 13: THE LAST EMPEROR (476 CE)

## I. RAVENNA — Late August 476 CE

The ink had barely dried on the last line of *De Doctrina Christiana* when Severina heard the horns. Not the palace trumpets announcing dawn prayers or the evening watch — these came from the north, where the Via Flaminia met the marsh roads. Barbarian horns, she knew without lifting her head from the copying desk. She had heard them before, during the Gothic troubles fifteen years past.

Her hand remained steady as she set down the reed pen. The final word was *sapientia* — wisdom. Augustine would have found that fitting. She blotted the line with sand, then brushed the grains into her palm. Such small gestures. Such ordinary motions at the end of the world.

The manuscript spread before her comprised three months' work: a clean Italian recension of Augustine's treatise on Christian teaching, copied from the only complete exemplar in the palace library. Bishop Neon had commissioned it before the summer fevers took him. Now there was only Bishop-elect Ursus, and he had troubles enough without worrying about dead men's copying projects.

Severina rose from her stool and walked to the archive's eastern window. Dust rose from the north road — not the thin ribbon that marked a messenger or merchant train, but the wide brown cloud that meant an army on the march. She counted the time by watching birds lift from the marshland and wheel away south. The cloud grew broader.

She had been copying in this room for seven years, since the African deacon Fulgentius brought her north as a refugee from the Vandal persecutions. Fulgentius had taught her the *nous* discipline — the old African way of stilling the mind until it could hold complex thought without strain. Augustine's lineage, he called it, though he spoke of the bishop of Hippo as if the man were still alive, still directing students from his cell in the burning city. The discipline served copying well. Hours could pass like minutes when the mind learned to rest inside the work.

Now the discipline served other purposes. Fear came and went like weather. She acknowledged it, set it aside, turned back to her desk.

The Augustine manuscript could not remain here. Whatever Odoacer wanted from the palace — tribute, supplies, the boy-emperor's formal submission — he would quarter his troops in any large building that could house them. The archives would become a barracks within days. Soldiers had little reverence for vellum, and less for Augustine's careful distinctions between literal and figurative interpretation of scripture.

Severina lifted the manuscript and felt its weight: forty-three sheets of good Umbrian vellum, sewn in eight gatherings. The stitching was her own work, done by candlelight after the day's copying finished. She had learned bookbinding from her mother, back when Carthage still had Christian scriptoriums and the Vandals seemed like someone else's problem.

Three other manuscripts lay ready for binding on the side table: Jerome's commentary on Matthew, a portion of the *City of God*, and Ambrose's hymns. All commissioned work, all paid for by the bishop's dwindling treasury. All likely to be used as fuel or bedding if left for Odoacer's men to find.

The horns sounded closer now. She could hear individual notes — the short blast that meant *halt*, the longer call that meant *prepare camp*. They would be at the gates by evening.

Severina gathered the four manuscripts and carried them to the oak chest where Bishop Neon had kept his personal books. The lock was simple — a bronze mechanism that required only pressing two raised circles simultaneously. Inside: Neon's breviary, his annotated copy of the *Moralia*, and three volumes of Origen that the deacon Maximus had smuggled from Alexandria years before. Space remained for her additions.

She arranged the new manuscripts carefully, wrapping each in oiled silk before laying it in the chest. Augustine's *De Doctrina* settled next to Origen's commentary on Genesis. Strange companions, perhaps — the African bishop who had spent his career arguing against allegory nestled beside the Alexandrian master who found allegory everywhere. But they would survive together or not at all.

The chest's lid closed with a soft thud. Severina turned the key and hung it on the bronze chain at her waist, next to the smaller key for her private writing box and the ivory stylus that had belonged to her mother.

Someone was shouting in the courtyard below. Not panic — not yet — but the raised voices of men receiving unexpected orders. She returned to the window and saw palace guards moving in formation toward the main gates. Their movements looked resigned rather than eager. Everyone knew how this would end.

A last task remained. Severina took up her pen and a small piece of papyrus — the cheap Egyptian reed-paper the archives used for temporary notes. She wrote quickly, in the abbreviated Latin that scribes used for inventory records:

*A. D. VIII Kal. Sept. Rav. In arca N. episcopi: Aug. doct. christ. IV lib., Hier. comm. Matt., Aug. civ. Dei XIV-XVIII, Ambr. hymn. Custodiet S.*

August 25th, Ravenna. In Bishop Neon's chest: Augustine's *De Doctrina Christiana* in four books, Jerome's commentary on Matthew, Augustine's *City of God* books 14-18, Ambrose's hymns. Severina will guard them.

She folded the papyrus and slipped it inside her sleeve. If she lived through whatever came next, she would hide the note where another scribe might find it. If she died, perhaps someone would search the body and wonder why a freedwoman carried a library inventory written in her own hand.

The afternoon light was beginning to slant through the archive's western windows. Time to go. Severina gathered her personal items — pen case, wax tablets, the small bronze stylus she used for ruling lines — and packed them in the leather satchel Fulgentius had given her seven years ago. The satchel had carried her few belongings from Africa to Italy. It could carry them from this room to whatever refuge she might find in the city.

She stood for a moment at the copying desk where she had spent so many hours. The afternoon light fell across the wooden surface, illuminating the small stains and scratches that marked years of careful work. Tomorrow someone else would sit here, if anyone sat here at all. But the words she had copied would survive elsewhere, in the bishop's locked chest, waiting for another scribe in another city to discover them and copy them again.

Augustine had written that God's city continued regardless of what happened to human cities. The sentiment had seemed abstract when she first encountered it in the African deacon's teachings. Now it felt as concrete as the weight of the satchel on her shoulder, as immediate as the sound of barbarian horns growing louder beyond the palace walls.

Severina walked to the archive door and turned the bronze key one final time. The click echoed in the empty corridor. She pocketed the key — a small theft, but one that might keep someone else from opening the room too quickly — and made her way toward the servant's stairs that led to the palace's rear courtyard.

The emperor's city was ending. The work would continue elsewhere.

## II. RAVENNA — September 4, 476 CE, morning

The purple cloak hung too large on the boy's shoulders. Romulus Augustulus — the name itself had become a mockery, though no one in the basilica dared smile — stood before the altar with the diadem slipping slightly over his brow. Sixteen years old, perhaps seventeen. Old enough to understand what was happening, young enough that his face still held the soft uncertainty of adolescence.

Odoacer watched from twenty paces away, flanked by his commanders. The Herulian captain Onulf stood to his right, the Rugian prince Tufa to his left. Behind them, a dozen lesser officers whose scarred hands and weather-darkened faces told of twenty years' campaigning along the Danube frontier. Good men, most of them. Men who had earned their ambitions.

The boy-emperor lifted his head and looked directly at Odoacer. No tears, which was something. His father Orestes had wept when they brought him the ultimatum six months ago. Land for the foederati, or war. Orestes had chosen war and lost his head for the choice. The son possessed either more wisdom or less pride.

"Caesar," Odoacer said, using the formal address. The word felt strange in his mouth. He had been born in a timber hall beside the Danube, in a settlement where the only Latin came from deserting legionaries and the occasional tax collector. Yet here he stood, accepting the submission of the last Western emperor in the language of Cicero and Augustine.

Romulus stepped forward. The scepter in his left hand trembled slightly — not fear, Odoacer judged, but the simple weight of holding ceremony-grade gold for an extended period. The boy began the formula of abdication, his voice carrying clearly through the basilica's marble spaces:

"To Odoacer, most excellent patrician and defender of Italy, I resign the purple that was placed upon me by the will of the army. I acknowledge that the dignity of the Western emperors is no longer required for the governance of the Roman people."

Each word had been negotiated. Odoacer's advisors — particularly the presbyter Faustinus, whose theological training extended to matters of constitutional law — had insisted on specific phrasing. *Resigned*, not *stolen*. *No longer required*, not *ended by force*. Future historians would read these words and understand that the Western crown had been surrendered voluntarily, that Odoacer ruled by invitation rather than conquest.

The boy finished speaking. He lifted the diadem from his head with both hands and extended it toward Odoacer.

Odoacer did not move to take it. This was the moment his advisors had prepared him for. He gestured to Onulf, who stepped forward with an open wooden casket lined in purple silk.

"Place the crown in the box, Caesar. It travels east to Emperor Zeno, along with the scepter and your father's cloak. The Eastern Augustus will receive them as tribute from the kingdom of Italy."

*Kingdom of Italy.* The phrase had weight. Not *the Western Empire under Odoacer's regency*. Not *the Roman territories administered by the foederati*. A kingdom. Germanic in nature, Roman in culture, Christian in law. Something new.

Romulus lowered the diadem into the casket. The gold settled against the silk with a soft whisper. He followed it with the scepter, then reached up to unfasten the purple cloak. The garment pooled around his feet before he lifted it and laid it carefully over the imperial regalia.

The boy now wore simple white tunic and brown belt — clothes that would have suited a senator's younger son or a provincial administrator. He looked smaller without the imperial trappings, but also more real. More like someone who might survive the next decade.

"You are no longer Caesar," Odoacer said. "You are Romulus, son of Orestes, citizen of Rome. You will retire to the estates in Campania that belonged to your family. The treasury will provide six hundred *solidi* annually for your maintenance."

A generous pension. Odoacer had killed emperors in the past — necessary deaths, but deaths nonetheless. This one would live quietly in the countryside, raise horses perhaps, maybe take a wife and produce children who would tell their grandchildren that their great-grandfather had once worn the purple. A curious footnote for future scholars to debate.

Romulus nodded once. "I thank you for your mercy, patrician."

The boy turned and walked from the basilica without looking back. His footsteps echoed against the marble columns — a sound that would haunt this building for as long as it stood. The last Western emperor, walking away from his throne as simply as a clerk leaving his office at the end of a long day.

Odoacer lifted the casket's lid and examined the imperial regalia one final time. The diadem caught the morning light streaming through the basilica's eastern windows. Beautiful workmanship — Roman goldsmithery at its finest, probably crafted during the reign of Theodosius the Great, when the empire still possessed the wealth and confidence to adorn its emperors properly.

Now it would journey to Constantinople, where Zeno would receive it with appropriate ceremony and lock it in the imperial treasury. The Eastern emperor would understand the message: the West acknowledged his supremacy, paid him tribute, requested his recognition of Odoacer's authority over the Italian peninsula. Diplomacy wrapped in submission wrapped in threat.

Onulf closed the casket and gestured to the guards. They would escort it to the harbor, where a Genoan merchant had already agreed to carry it east for the appropriate fee. The sea route was faster than the mountain passes, and less likely to attract bandits who might not recognize the difference between ordinary tribute and the actual crown of the West.

The presbyter Faustinus approached as the guards departed. A thin man in his forties, with the pale complexion of someone who spent his days reading rather than soldiering. One of the few Romans who had supported Odoacer's rise from the beginning, recognizing that barbarian rule might preserve more of Roman civilization than continued civil war.

"It is finished, patrician."

"The beginning is finished," Odoacer corrected. "Now we discover whether Italians will accept a Gothic king who speaks better Latin than most of their bishops."

Faustinus smiled at that. It was true — Odoacer's Latin carried the precision of someone who had learned it from books rather than nursery songs. His Gothic accent colored certain vowels, but his grammar surpassed that of half the senators who had fled to Constantinople over the past decade.

"The people want peace and reliable harvests," Faustinus said. "They care little whether their king was born beside the Danube or the Tiber, provided he keeps the grain ships arriving and the tax collectors honest."

Simple enough in theory. More complex in practice, when every decision had to balance the expectations of his Germanic soldiers against the traditions of Roman civilians. The soldiers wanted land grants and regular pay. The civilians wanted familiar laws and intact churches. Both groups wanted to believe their interests came first.

Odoacer walked to the basilica's main entrance and looked out across the palace courtyard. His men were already establishing guard rotations, organizing supply trains, converting a Roman imperial residence into the seat of a barbarian kingdom. The transition would take months, but the hardest part was finished. No one had died who did not need to die. No buildings had burned that could not be rebuilt.

*So this is how it stops being an empire*, he thought. Not with siege warfare or dynastic crisis, but with a boy-emperor walking quietly away from a throne no one wanted to defend. The Western crown had been crumbling for decades — a hollow shell maintained by armies who could no longer afford to pay themselves, administered by bureaucrats who governed territories they could not protect.

Better to end it cleanly. Better to build something new from the materials that remained, than to prop up institutions that had already died in all but name.

The morning sun climbed higher over Ravenna's marshlands. Somewhere in the city, scribes were copying the day's events into their chronicles. They would write that the Western Roman Empire ended on this date, in this place, with these words. Future historians would debate whether Odoacer was conqueror or liberator, whether Romulus Augustulus was victim or beneficiary of circumstance.

The debates would continue long after everyone in this courtyard had died. But the throne itself was empty now, and would remain so. That much was finished.

## III. RAVENNA — September 4, 476 CE, evening

Faustinus walked slowly from the basilica toward his lodgings in the episcopal quarter, his mind already composing the letter he would write to Pope Simplicius before sleep. The evening air carried the scent of cooking fires and horse sweat — Odoacer's men were making themselves at home in the palace's outer buildings, transforming the seat of Western empire into a military camp with remarkable efficiency.

The deposition had proceeded exactly as planned. No blood, no theatrics, no martyrdom to complicate future relations between the Church and Odoacer's government. The boy-emperor had acquitted himself well — better than his father, certainly, who had spent his final months raging against the Gothic barbarians as if superior birth could substitute for superior strategy.

Faustinus paused at the fountain in the episcopal courtyard and splashed water on his face. The day's heat was finally breaking, and stars were beginning to appear over the Adriatic marshes. Tomorrow he would need to meet with Odoacer's quartermaster about lodging arrangements for the Germanic officers. Tonight, however, belonged to more important work.

His chamber occupied the second floor of the guest house where visiting clerics normally stayed during episcopal councils. Modest accommodations — a narrow bed, a writing desk, a wooden cross mounted above the single window — but adequate for a man whose ambitions transcended personal comfort. Faustinus lit two oil lamps and arranged his writing materials on the desk: papyrus, reed pens, a small pot of iron-gall ink that he had learned to prepare during his years studying canon law in Milan.

The letter to Rome would require careful phrasing. Simplicius was an intelligent man, but also a cautious one. He needed to understand the opportunity that Odoacer's victory represented without feeling that the Church was rushing toward unseemly collaboration with barbarian conquerors.

Faustinus began writing:

*To his Holiness Simplicius, Bishop of Rome, servant of the servants of God, from Faustinus, presbyter of Ravenna, greetings in Christ.*

*I write to inform Your Holiness that the Western imperial dignity has been dissolved by the voluntary submission of Romulus Augustulus to the patrician Odoacer. The regalia travel east to Emperor Zeno. Italy is now governed as a kingdom under nominal Eastern authority.*

*Odoacer professes the Arian faith, but governs according to Roman law and maintains cordial relations with the Catholic episcopate. He seeks legitimacy through continuity with imperial institutions rather than through religious innovation. This presents opportunities that Your Holiness will recognize.*

He paused, considering how directly to state what they both understood. The end of Western imperial authority meant the end of imperial interference in papal prerogatives. No more edicts defining theological doctrine. No more secular appointees to episcopal sees. No more competition between Constantinople and Ravenna for influence over the Roman bishop.

The Church now stood as the only institution that could claim unbroken continuity with the old Roman order. Diocesan boundaries matched the empire's administrative divisions. Canon law preserved Roman legal principles. The episcopal hierarchy maintained communications networks that spanned from Britain to Egypt. Most importantly, the papacy possessed the theoretical authority to speak for all Western Christians — an authority that no barbarian king could credibly challenge.

*The dissolution of the Western crown removes certain impediments to the exercise of papal authority throughout the former imperial territories. Where secular administration has failed, ecclesiastical administration remains intact. Where royal authority is contested, spiritual authority offers stability.*

*I believe Your Holiness will find that barbarian kings, lacking the constitutional traditions of Roman emperors, prove more amenable to recognizing papal supremacy in matters of law and custom that touch upon Christian doctrine — which is to say, in matters of law and custom generally.*

This was the crucial insight that had taken Faustinus years to develop. Roman emperors had possessed their own claims to divine authorization. They ruled by grace of God, administered sacred law, called themselves *pontifex maximus*. Their authority competed directly with papal authority, creating the endless jurisdictional disputes that had plagued Church-state relations since Constantine.

Gothic and Vandal and Frankish kings lacked such pretensions. They ruled by right of conquest, by tribal consensus, by personal charisma — but not by divine mandate in the Roman sense. This left ecclesiastical space that could be filled by papal claims to temporal authority over Christian territories.

Odoacer's victory was not a setback for the Church. It was the precondition for papal monarchy.

*I recommend that Your Holiness consider dispatching legates to the various barbarian courts to establish formal relations and define the Church's role in the governance of Christian populations. The kings seek legitimacy. The Church can provide it, in exchange for recognition of papal prerogatives.*

*The empire has dissolved. The opportunity begins.*

Faustinus signed the letter and sealed it with the wax seal he carried for official correspondence. Tomorrow he would find a messenger traveling to Rome — one of the Church's own couriers, who could be trusted to deliver sensitive material without sharing its contents with Odoacer's administrators.

He extinguished one of the oil lamps and carried the other to his bed. Outside, he could hear Germanic voices singing what sounded like a victory song, though the melody was unfamiliar and the words incomprehensible. The sound did not trouble him. Conquerors always sang. The question was who would teach their children to read.

Twenty years from now, perhaps less, Odoacer's son would need tutors. The Frankish princes were already sending their boys to episcopal schools to learn Latin grammar and Roman law. The Visigothic court employed Christian scholars as diplomatic advisors. Arian or not, barbarian kings understood that governance required literacy, and literacy in their territories meant literacy in a Christian context.

The Church would educate their children, draft their laws, manage their correspondence with other kingdoms. Step by step, decision by decision, the ecclesiastical hierarchy would become indispensable to barbarian administration. And indispensable institutions eventually became dominant institutions, provided their leaders possessed sufficient patience and strategic vision.

Faustinus closed his eyes and began the evening prayers that marked the end of each day's work. But his mind remained focused on the future rather than the formulas of devotion. The Western empire was dead. The papal monarchy was ready to be born. Tonight marked the beginning of its gestation.

## IV. CONSTANTINOPLE — Early October 476 CE

The casket arrived on a Genoan merchant ship that docked at the Golden Horn just after dawn. Theodosia watched from the patriarchal palace's harbor-facing window as imperial guards carried the wooden box up the stone steps toward the Great Palace. Even at this distance, she could see the purple silk lining that marked the container's contents as worthy of ceremonial treatment.

Word had already reached Constantinople through the empire's intelligence networks. The Western crown was empty. Odoacer ruled Italy as king. The regalia were coming home to their Eastern Augustus, along with the barbarian's formal acknowledgment of imperial supremacy over his territories.

Emperor Zeno would accept the submission, of course. He lacked the military resources to restore Western imperial authority, but he possessed sufficient diplomatic wisdom to receive tribute graciously and maintain the fiction that Odoacer governed as his appointed representative. Face would be preserved on all sides.

Theodosia turned from the window and walked to her writing desk in the scriptorium's eastern corner. As the patriarch's senior lay reader, she maintained correspondence with monasteries throughout the empire, tracking the flow of manuscripts and coordinating copying projects that required multiple scriptoriums working in parallel. The work brought her into contact with information that never appeared in official dispatches — rumors from traveling monks, observations from merchants who carried books alongside more conventional cargo, fragments of news from regions where imperial administration had grown thin.

She had learned of Odoacer's march on Ravenna a full month before the court received its first official reports. A monk traveling from Milan to Athens had mentioned, almost in passing, that Germanic soldiers were demanding land grants throughout northern Italy, that the boy-emperor's father was refusing to negotiate, that civil war seemed inevitable. Such details rarely interested emperors, but they mattered greatly to anyone trying to understand how Christian communities would survive the empire's dissolution.

The Pattern Eye lay wrapped in silk on the shelf above her desk. It had come to her three years earlier through the long chain of custody that bound her to a woman she had never met — Kassia, the patriarchal scribe who had held the Eye in the decade of the 440s and trained her own successor, who in turn had trained the reader who had placed the wrapped bronze in Theodosia's palm one evening in an unlit corridor and said only: *It chooses, now. Carry it well.* The training had taken months — learning to still the mind until genetic memory could surface without overwhelming present consciousness, understanding how to interpret the cascades of image and sensation that emerged during full contact with the device's bronze surface.

Theodosia had used it sparingly since then. The visions were difficult to control and harder to interpret. But today's news seemed to demand the longer perspective that only the Eye could provide.

She unwrapped the device and placed it on her desk. Bronze, certainly Roman in manufacture, inscribed with symbols that matched no alphabet in the patriarchal library. Her teacher had called it a meditation aid created by Augustine's circle in North Africa, though she had never explained how such a tool might facilitate genetic memory recall. The mechanisms were less important than the results.

Theodosia placed her right palm flat against the Eye's central disk and closed her eyes. The bronze felt warm — not heated by sunlight, but warm with some internal energy that seemed to respond to direct contact. She slowed her breathing and emptied her mind of immediate concerns, following the disciplines that had been passed down to her.

The cascade began slowly. First came fragments of her own childhood — the convent school in Anatolia where she had learned to read Greek and Latin, the elderly nun who had taught her the principles of manuscript illumination, the morning when raiders from across the Danube had burned the village and sent the survivors fleeing toward Constantinople's walls.

Then deeper memories, inherited rather than lived. She saw Augustine in his study at Hippo, bent over a writing tablet as Vandal catapults shook dust from the ceiling beams. She saw African deacons carrying books and bronze artifacts north through Numidia toward the Mediterranean ports. She saw a British monk copying by candlelight in a stone cell where rain drummed against tiny windows.

The visions jumped forward as well as backward. She saw figures she did not recognize — a woman in strange clothes examining books in what appeared to be a vast library, a man with careful hands repairing an ancient manuscript in a workshop filled with tools she could not identify. The Eye made no distinction between past and future; both were equally present in the genetic memory it helped unlock.

But the clearest vision was of the moment itself, seen from a perspective that encompassed centuries rather than years. The Western empire's collapse was not an ending but a *decongestion*. The pattern of preservation and transmission that Augustine's circle had established was designed to survive the failure of centralized institutions. Scribes in isolated monasteries could maintain intellectual traditions that required no imperial funding, no administrative oversight, no military protection beyond what local communities could provide.

The empire had become a bottleneck. Knowledge flowed through official channels, was copied in official scriptoriums, was preserved in libraries that depended on imperial patronage. When the empire weakened, the flow of knowledge weakened with it. But when the empire collapsed entirely, knowledge could find other channels — monastic ones, primarily, but also aristocratic households, episcopal schools, even the courts of barbarian kings who needed literate administrators.

Theodosia lifted her palm from the Eye and opened her eyes. The vision faded, leaving only the bronze disk on her desk and the memory of what she had seen. The pattern was not breaking. It was spreading.

She took up her pen and opened the leather-bound notebook where she recorded observations that were too sensitive for official correspondence. At the top of a fresh page, she wrote the date: *Kal. Oct. A.D. CCCCLXXVI*. Below it, a single line of analysis:

*The empire has finished dying. The copying has only just begun.*

A knock at the scriptorium door interrupted her writing. Brother Maximos, the patriarch's secretary, entered without waiting for permission — a liberty that indicated urgent business.

"The emperor has accepted the Western regalia," he said without preamble. "There will be a ceremony at the Hagia Sophia tomorrow. The patriarch requests your attendance to take notes for the archives."

Theodosia nodded and closed her notebook. The ceremony would be purely diplomatic — Zeno accepting tribute from a barbarian king, demonstrating that imperial dignity continued in the East even as it disappeared in the West. But the real significance lay in what would happen after the ceremony, when the regalia were locked away and Odoacer's messengers returned to Italy with Zeno's formal recognition of their master's authority.

The Western throne would remain empty. The Eastern throne would govern from a distance. And in the space between them, new institutions would emerge to fill the vacuum that imperial administration left behind. Christian institutions, primarily. Monastic scriptoriums. Episcopal schools. Papal legates carrying canon law to barbarian courts.

The pattern was adapting to new circumstances. Augustine's vision of the City of God — a spiritual community that transcended political boundaries — was becoming practical reality as political boundaries themselves grew fluid and unstable.

Theodosia wrapped the Pattern Eye in silk and returned it to its shelf. Tomorrow she would attend the ceremony and record the formal end of Western imperial ambitions. Tonight she would write letters to the monasteries in her network, informing them that their copying work had gained new urgency. The empire was no longer competing with the Church for scribal resources. For the first time in centuries, Christian scholars could focus entirely on preserving and transmitting the intellectual traditions that would outlive whatever kingdoms emerged from the empire's ruins.

The collapse that Rome's enemies celebrated was the liberation that Rome's teachers had been waiting for.

## V. THE BRITISH FRONTIER — Late 477 CE

The news reached the monk twenty-nine weeks after the event, carried by a traveling brother who had walked from the coast with a satchel of letters and a bad cough.

"Rome has ended," the brother said, sitting on a bench in the cloister and warming his hands at a small brazier. "Truly this time. Not a sack, not a siege. A boy gave up the crown and the crown went east and now there is no emperor in the West at all."

The monk set down the quill he had been cutting and considered this. Outside the cloister wall, rain was moving across the hills in the slow grey sheets that made everything beyond ten paces look like a charcoal sketch.

"When?"

"Late summer. The fourth day of September, they said. In a city called Ravenna."

The monk nodded. He had heard of Ravenna. Augustine had written a letter to someone there, or perhaps about someone there — he could not remember exactly; it was in one of the volumes that had not yet reached the abbey's small collection.

"What does it mean," he asked, "for us?"

The traveling brother shrugged. "The abbot says it means nothing. The abbot says Augustine already told us it meant nothing. The City of God continues. The city of men has always been passing."

The monk accepted this as he accepted most of what the abbot said: with the assumption that a man who had spent thirty years reading Augustine by candlelight had probably understood something the monk himself had not yet understood. He returned to cutting his quill. The barb of the goose feather had split on the last downstroke of the morning's work, and replacing it was tedious.

After a while the traveling brother drank the small beer the kitchen had brought him and walked out into the rain to seek the abbot. The monk remained on the bench, the newly cut quill between his fingers, looking at the brazier.

Rome had ended. Somewhere a long way south a boy had taken off a cloak too big for him. Somewhere further south still, men he would never meet were burning oil on manuscripts, or not burning it, depending on which men. None of it touched the flagstones of the cloister, which were wet. None of it touched the abbey's single cow, which was in calf. None of it touched the quill in his hand, which was now ready for another six hours of work.

He stood, put the quill in its case, and walked back to the scriptorium to light a new candle. The psalter he was copying was open at the sixteenth psalm. *Conserva me, Domine, quoniam speravi in te.* He dipped the pen and continued.

## VI. CODA

A boy-emperor named Romulus Augustulus retired that autumn to a villa in Campania and began, according to the records of the treasury, to receive six hundred *solidi* a year for his maintenance. He lived obscurely into the 490s, if he lived that long; nobody thought it important enough to write down the date of his death.

A king named Odoacer settled in Ravenna and began the slow work of turning an empire into a kingdom. He ruled for seventeen years. He was murdered at a banquet in 493 by a Gothic king named Theodoric, who ruled after him.

An emperor named Zeno received the Western regalia in Constantinople in October of 476 and locked them in the imperial treasury, where they remained until they did not. He died in 491 of epilepsy, possibly while still alive in his tomb, according to a rumor that no one troubled to suppress.

A pope named Simplicius received a letter from a presbyter in Ravenna and, after due reflection, began dispatching legates to barbarian courts. The process he initiated took three centuries to complete. When it was complete, there was a papal monarchy in the West, which was what the presbyter had predicted.

None of this is the point.

The point is that in the winter of 476 and 477, in places whose names have mostly not been preserved, men and women who would have recognized none of these personages cut vellum and ruled it, mixed gallnut ink in clay pots, sharpened quills from local geese, and wrote out, letter by letter, the sentences that other men and women had written before them. They did this in Ravenna and in Constantinople and in a hill house in Samnium and in an unnamed abbey on a British coast. They did it in rooms where the ceiling leaked. They did it in rooms where the only heat came from one brazier and the only light from one candle. They did it for reasons that most of them could not have articulated clearly if asked, and that most of them were not asked.

The defensive discipline had not won. The Order had not won. No one had won, because winning was not a category the work admitted. The work was the thing that continued when winning and losing had both stopped being interesting.

It was a bad year for emperors. It was an ordinary year for scribes.
