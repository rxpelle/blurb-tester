# Chapter 1 — The Courier (v2, thriller)

*The road from Rome to the harbour at Ostia, the thirty-first of August, 410 CE.*

The wayside inn had two rooms, a stable for four mules, and the kind of clientele who came in carrying nothing they could not carry out. The smell of the place came up through the floorboards from the stable below — mule and dung and old straw — and across this rode the smell of the wine, which was a vintage the keep had watered twice. The man who would be called Cyprian for the night had arrived an hour before sundown and taken the corner table by the unshuttered window.

His back was against the pine of the wall, which was badly cured and still a little wet at the joins. He had asked for the inn's wine and bread and a bowl of olives. The bread had gone hard at the crust. The olives sat untouched in their bowl. The cup of the inn's wine sat at his elbow; he had drunk none of it. In his right hand was a different cup, smaller, of his own — a Falernian he had been warming for an hour against his palm. Warm wine was a kindness offered to a tired man. He intended to offer one.

The book on his knee was open at a page of the Aeneid he had been reading for an hour. He was not reading it. The light through the unshuttered window had gone copper a long while ago and was now a deeper colour and would shortly be gone. Two miles south of the inn was a wooden bridge with a bowed plank that had not been replaced this season and which gave a particular sound when a cart crossed it. He had counted four crossings since he sat down. None had been imperial. He listened for the fifth.

The courier came in at the second hour of the lamp. He brought the road in with him — dust on his face, the particular sweat of a man who had been riding hard since dawn — and unbuckled his belt with the practised gesture of a messenger, because a messenger took his belt off to eat. He was thirty, perhaps a little under, in the dust-coloured tunic of an imperial courier. He sat at the table by the door. He drank water before he drank wine, which is what a man who has been riding does first.

The man at the corner table closed his book.

When the courier had eaten half a loaf and a piece of cured pork, the man stood, took up his own cup, and crossed the room. He did not hurry. There were four other men in the room — a carter at the long table, two drovers near the door, the keep behind the bar — and none of them looked up. None had looked up since he came in.

*Friend. From Rome?*

The courier looked up. *From Ostia. Today.*

*You will reach the south coast tomorrow. The road past Lavinium is bad. The bridge at the mile-stone before Ardea has gone again.*

The courier put down the bread. Lavinium was the recognition phrase. The bridge had not gone. The man at his table was using the network and was using it with the right words.

*You are?*

*Cyprian.*

The courier sat very still.

*Drink with me. Then we will talk in my room.*

The man set a fresh cup at the courier's elbow. Sweet Falernian, not the rough vintage the inn served. The courier took it. The cup was warm because the man had been holding it.

They drank. The courier finished his bread. They went up the stair to the small room above the stable, lit by a single oil lamp, with one bed and one window, the window shuttered and barred. The man closed the door.

He turned the bolt.

The courier was already sitting on the bed because the room had begun to lean. *You. The wine.*

*Yes.*

*Network does not.*

*This is not the network.*

The courier reached for the knife at the back of his belt and the knife was not there. The man held it up, briefly. He had taken it on the stair. The courier did not remember the stair.

*Lie down, friend. The cut is small. You will live as long as you can give. There is no use in moving.*

The courier did not lie down. He stood, which the body was no longer good for. His legs were going. He could feel them going. The man took his shoulder and pressed and the courier was on the bed before he knew which way the bed had been.

The man went to the corner where his pack had been from the beginning. He laid the contents on the floor beside the bed in the order he would need them — a roll of waxed linen thread, a bronze probe, a small set of bone-handled blades wrapped in oiled silk, a clay flask of vinegar, a copper bowl. He counted the spool. There was thread for thirty sutures. He would use ten. The lamp on the small table burned without flicker; the room was that still. He worked by it.

He cut the courier's tunic up the front, from the navel to the chest, with the courier's own knife. The skin of the belly was tight and warm. He pressed two fingers into the soft place above the right hip and felt what he had been told he would feel — a small hardness, the size of a hen's egg, not bone, not viscera. The cargo was where the network sewed it. He had been told it would be.

He marked the line of the cut in soot from the lamp, a hand's breadth above the navel, flank to flank, a span and a half. He chose the smaller of the two blades.

The courier said something. The man did not hear it. He was past hearing.

The cut was steady and not deep. He went through the second layer of the belly at the second pass and into the cavity at the third. The courier's body convulsed once on the bed and then did not. A smell came up out of the cavity that was not yet the smell it would be in eleven days but was the start of it. The drug had taken the spine before the nerves; the man had used it for this purpose before, in Antioch.

His fingers found the packet at the second sweep. Oiled leather, a span across, knotted at the corner the way the network knotted, sewn in place between the loops of the gut and the inner wall by four short threads that he cut. The packet came out with a sound. He set it on the linen at the head of the bed.

He did not stitch the courier yet.

He untied the packet at the lamp. Inside, a single sheet of papyrus, folded twice. He spread it on the small table by the window. He read in the lamp.

He read for the better part of a quarter of an hour. The second reading was slower than the first. At the end of it he refolded the papyrus in the original folds, replaced it in the leather, and re-knotted the corner in the network's knot, exactly as he had untied it.

He returned to the bed.

He set the packet back into the cavity at the same angle it had come out. He closed the four short network threads with four short network threads of his own, in the same waxed linen, sewn from the same spool. He drew the lips of the wound together and stitched ten coarse sutures, set wide and quickly — the way a man stitches a sack he means to open again. He wiped the line of the cut with vinegar. He wrapped a clean bandage twice around the body and over the bandage tied a stained ligature he had carried in the pack for the purpose.

The courier was alive. The pulse at the throat was thin and would thin further. The cut had been small enough not to kill quickly. The gut had been opened only as much as it needed to be opened. The man would die in eleven or twelve days, on the ship and on the mule, with the cargo intact in his body, in pain and not delirious enough to speak.

The man dressed the courier in a clean tunic from his own pack. He carried him down the back stair to the stable. He laid him across the back of the smallest of the four mules and tied his wrists to the saddle horn with linen, so that the body would ride.

He gave the stable-boy three silver pieces and a destination — the harbour at Portus, the brown ship that would sail at first light for Carthage by way of Hippo. At Hippo the courier would be found. The finding had been seen to. He told the boy the messenger was sick with the African fever and was going home to die at his mother's. The boy crossed himself and nodded.

The man went back up the stair and washed in the copper bowl. He burned the soot mark from the lamp. He folded the bone-handled blades back into the oiled silk and wrapped them in the spare tunic. He blew out the lamp.

At the corner table downstairs he asked for a fresh cup of the Falernian and another bowl of olives. He read his book until the second hour past midnight. He paid in copper. He went out into the road.

He walked north, toward Rome, where the gates were open and the smoke was four days settling. The wind off the Tyrrhenian came at his back and the smoke came toward him on the road; he smelled it before he had walked a mile. He had work to do there. The mule would do its work without him. The bishop in Africa would do his work without knowing it was being done.

He had read what was in the packet. He understood now why he had been sent and not killed himself.

The cargo would wait.

The man who had been called Cyprian for the night walked north in the dark with his small pack on his shoulder and his small book under his arm. The Falernian was good in his throat. The road to Rome was empty.

*Hippo Regius. Eleven days later.*

The fog was off the sea and stayed past the third hour. Augustine had walked the cloister twice in it that morning. His right knee was worse in damp weather; it was worse this morning than it had been all season. The headache that lived behind his left eye when he had not slept was beginning to live there now. None of the small things going wrong with his body had been true the last time this city had heard news from Italy that mattered.

The community met at the third hour in the small house off the cloister. There were nine of them. Possidius's absence was noted; he was on church business in Calama and not expected back until the middle of the week. The reading was from Cassian, on patience. They were in the third paragraph when the gate-keeper opened the door without knocking, which had not been his practice in twenty years at the gate.

The gate-keeper said: *Father, a man at the gate from Italy.*

Augustine waited for more. The old man could not provide more, because his throat had closed.

*Bring him to the small parlour. Feed him first. I will come when we have closed.*

Stephen, who was reading and who in his younger days had assisted at a difficult baptism Augustine no longer named aloud, finished the paragraph without inflection. They settled the disciplinary matters. They reviewed the liturgy. Augustine ended the meeting at the customary hour and went to the small parlour.

The man at the gate was a wine-merchant from Ostia named Marcus. He had eaten nothing because he had reached the bread, looked at it, and begun to weep. Augustine sat across from him and let it run.

After some minutes Marcus wiped his face on his sleeve.

*Bishop.*

*Tell me.*

*Rome is taken. The Goths came in by the north gate before midnight on the twenty-fourth of August. They were inside three days. They burned. They took. They killed where they were resisted and many places where they were not. There are Christians among them who spared the basilicas of the apostles, and there were many who did not spare. My cousin — he saw a procession of women on the second day. Boys' bodies. Out of the Aventine quarter. He saw the Forum on fire. He says the eternal city is no longer.*

Augustine kept his hands on his knees. They wanted to do other things. They wanted to lift to his face. They wanted to find the wood of the bench and grip. He kept them on his knees, because Marcus was watching them, and a man who has come from a sacked city watches the bishop's hands for what those hands tell him about whether the news will be carried.

*Father, what does it mean?*

The honest answer would have taken half an hour and would have helped no one. The pious answer would help this man for a quarter of an hour and then fail him. He gave the pious answer because the man in front of him would die without it. It came out of him in his bishop's voice, which was a voice he had built over twenty years for moments like this one and which he was not, by tonight, sure he still believed.

*It means God has permitted what He has permitted. It means the city of stones may fall, and the city which is not of stones cannot be reached by the Goths. It means we will mourn, and we will continue, and we will give shelter to those who come.*

Marcus nodded. Augustine tore bread, put it into his hand, and watched a small piece of life come back into his face with the chewing.

*You will sleep here. Tomorrow we will find you a passage onward, or a place if you wish to remain.*

*Father.* Marcus held his cup with both hands. He looked at the cup. *There is one other thing. I do not know if it is for you. A man at Portus told me to tell you. He said the bishop in Hippo had once received a letter from a brother named Anthony, and would know the hand. He said: there is a boy at the harbour at Ostia, ten years old or eleven, selling small bronze crosses. The boy says his grandmother gave him the crosses. The crosses are old. They are made in a way that is not made any longer. He sells them for a copper piece each, to anyone who will take one. He has sold seven. He is not yet sold out.*

Augustine kept his hands on his knees.

*Did the man at Portus give a name?*

*No. He said the bishop would know.*

*Did he say anything else?*

*He said: the houses on the Aventine are empty. He said: not all the burning was done by the Goths.*

Something moved in Augustine's chest that he did not have time to name. He put his hand briefly on Marcus's shoulder. *You have done me a kindness. Eat. Sleep. We will speak in the morning.*

Marcus caught his hand and kissed it. Augustine let his hand be kissed, and went out into the fog. The fog was warm and smelled of harbour, and he stood in it for a count of three before he remembered which way the cathedral was.

He did not break the prayers. He went to the cathedral for the third-hour service and sang the psalms without alteration. The chant had been written for the burning of Jerusalem, centuries before, and it would outlast Hippo as it had outlasted Jerusalem. He thought about the boy at the harbour with the bronze crosses, and stopped thinking about him, and continued the psalm.

After the third-hour service he heard confession.

---

The bench was against the south wall, the curtain drawn for those who wished it. There were six. A steward who had cheated his master of grain. An apprentice fuller who had struck his sister. A soldier on his way to Carthage who had taken the wife of his cousin. A woman of thirty whose husband had returned from a campaign two years older than he had left and was beating her in the new manner. A deacon who had spoken sharply to his bishop, which was Augustine, and wished to be heard saying so. He gave penances and did not let his thinking elsewhere shorten the hearing.

The sixth was a girl of nine.

She had been in Hippo eleven days, sleeping in the warehouses by the harbour with her mother and the other Romans who had crossed in July. The grandmother had not come south with them. She had had property in the Aventine and had insisted to the end that flight was for the impious. Yesterday a man in the warehouses had told the girl's mother what had been done in the smaller of the grandmother's two houses, and the mother had told the girl that the bishop knew what God did and did not do.

She put it more briefly. *Father, my grandmother was burned alive in her own house. They put her on her own table and they made the table burn. The Phrygian woman saw and got out by the wall. Father, I want to know if my grandmother is in heaven.*

He kept his eyes on her face. Her hair was unwashed and badly braided — by her own hand, probably; her mother had other children and there had been no time. Her hands on her knees were small and clean. She had found water somewhere in the warehouse before coming and had washed them, and the thought of that — a child finding water in a refugee warehouse and washing her hands before asking a bishop whether God had permitted her grandmother to be burned alive on her own table — closed something in his throat.

*Tell me your grandmother's name.*

*Maria.*

*Maria* — he said the name first, because the dead were owed their names — *is in the hands of God, who is more merciful than any of us can imagine, and who knows the soul that He made.*

He had said this sentence two hundred times. He had not, before today, said it to a child whose grandmother's mark was on a leather cord against her chest under her tunic.

*Father, did God let it happen?*

The bench was hard under him. There was the breath in his chest going in and going out. There was the answer that would let her go back to the straw and sleep, and there was the answer that was true, and these were not the same answer. He had been a bishop for fourteen years and he had given the first answer to nine adults out of every ten. He had given it to a child four times. The shape of it sat ready in his mouth.

*Yes. God permitted it.*

She nodded. The nod was slow and grave and not at all surprised. She had already known. She had needed to hear it said by a man whose office permitted him to say it.

*Then God did not love her.*

He had a sentence for this also. It was smooth from use. He felt it open in his mouth and come out.

*He loved her. He loves her now.*

It came out the way a coin from his hand to a beggar came out — necessary, sincere, insufficient. He felt it land. He felt it not be enough.

*Then I do not understand love.*

She said it the way one reports a result to oneself. Some answer was forming in him. He waited to see what it would be.

*Neither do I.*

It was out of him before he could check it. Two words. He did not move. The room did not move. A gull was complaining about something somewhere above the cloister in its specific gull voice, and the sound came down through the small high window and went past.

She did not weep. She reached into the front of her tunic and brought out, on a leather cord around her neck, a small bronze cross. She lifted it off and held it out to him on the open palm of her hand.

*Grandmother gave me this when we left. She said the boy from the boat would know the mark. We did not see the boat. The boy at the harbour at Ostia took one of the others. He gave me a copper piece. I did not want to take it. He said grandmother would have wanted me to take it.*

Augustine looked at the cross on her palm without moving his hand.

It was bronze. It was old — the patina was the patina of two centuries, perhaps three. The crossbar had been cast slightly long, a workshop signature, and at the foot of the upright was a small mark scratched into the metal: two short strokes, a circle, two short strokes. The mark was the mark of the Aventine house. The mark had been the mark of the Aventine house for as long as there had been an Aventine house. The grandmother had been the keeper of the Aventine house. The Aventine house had not been on the list of what the Goths had touched, because the Aventine house had not been in any list of any kind anywhere.

The grandmother had sent the marks out before she went into the kitchen to be made to sit at her own table. She had given one to the granddaughter and seven, or some other number, to the boy at the harbour, and the boy was selling them for copper pieces to whoever would take one. The boy was distributing the mark because the grandmother had told him to distribute the mark. The grandmother had known they were coming for her. She had sent the keepers' sign out to anyone who would carry it before she sat down.

He brought his hand up and closed it gently over the girl's hand and the cross.

*It is a beautiful cross. Will you keep it for me a little longer? I will come and find you in the basilica on Sunday and we will look at it together.*

*Yes, father.*

*Tell me where you sleep.*

*The third warehouse from the east mole. By the door.*

*I will know it. Come to me at the basilica door any day you are hungry. Eat first. The questions you have are good questions. We will come back to them.*

She closed her hand over the cross, put it back inside her tunic on the cord, and thanked him in the formula her mother had taught her. He gave her the blessing. He laid his hand on her braided head. The hair under his hand was cold.

She went out into the fog.

He sat with his hands open on his knees. The cold of her hair was on his palm and would not leave it. There was no sound in the south aisle but his own breath. He waited for the breath to slow. It did not slow for some time.

The bench was empty. He stayed at it. Stephen came at length and asked whether there were further confessions; there were not. The deacon left. Augustine went to his cell.

He did not eat at the noon meal. He did not eat at the evening meal either, but that was later.

---

He sat at the desk. The shutters were half-closed against the fog, which had thinned to a clean light. The wax tablet was in front of him; the stylus was in his hand. The stylus was the bone one Monnica had bought him in Carthage when he was a student and which he had used for thirty-eight years. It was warm where his fingers had been on it. The wax was cold. The wax was always cold this hour of the morning.

He did not move. Then he moved.

The first sentence took two attempts at the wax. The wax buckled at the first attempt — he had pressed too hard, the way a man presses when his hand is unwilling — and he scraped it smooth and tried again. The third held, and he transferred the sentence to the parchment with the iron pen. He addressed it, by name, to Marcellinus — his friend the imperial commissioner now in Carthage, who would be glad to receive a personal letter and would not yet know that the letter was also a book, and that the book was also other things.

The most glorious city of God, whether in this passing course of time when she lives by faith as a stranger among the impious, or in that stability of her eternal seat which she now awaits patiently, this city he had undertaken to defend against those who preferred their gods to her founder.

He sat with the sentence. The line was beautiful; he had a good ear for a Latin sentence and he knew this one would carry. He had defended a city he could not see against accusations from a city that no longer stood, and the question of whether he believed what he had just written was a smaller question, tonight, than the question of whether the woman in the warehouse with her hand on her daughter's head needed it written. The woman needed it written. That was sufficient. He covered the parchment. He went to vespers.

He took bread and water at the evening table in silence and went to his cell early. Sleep was the intention. He lay on the straw mattress and closed his eyes, and the seventy-eighth psalm ran through his head until the second hour of night, when the bell at the gate rang.

The bell at the gate did not ring at the second hour of night except for emergency. He was on his feet before he had decided to be on his feet, and his right knee took it badly.

Footsteps in the cloister, two voices, then Stephen's knock at the cell door. Three short.

*Father.*

*Yes.*

*A man at the gate has brought a body. He says the body is not yet a body. He says it is a courier from Italy.*

Augustine pulled on his outer tunic and took up the small lamp.

*Bring them to the writing room. Both of them. Wake no one else. Send the gate-keeper back to the gate. Tell Eraclius nothing.*

The cloister was dark. He carried the lamp through it to the writing room — a small chamber between his cell and the library where he received correspondence and dictated to his secretary, with a long pine table, two stools, a shelf of writing materials, and a brazier in the corner unlit because the August fog was warm. The room held the smell of old ink and under it, faint, the smell of the sea.

The lamp went down on the table. He cleared the day's correspondence onto the shelf, spread a folded piece of clean linen across the table, and laid beside it the iron paring knife he used for trimming pens. From the first lamp he lit a second.

The men came through the door. The man at the gate was a Numidian carter named Lucius, whom Augustine knew slightly — honest in his trade, not particularly devout. He had found the courier in a ditch beside a still-living mule three miles outside the western wall, and had recognized that the man on the mule had been put on the mule, not riding it. He had brought both to the basilica because the basilica was the nearest place that did not ask money for taking in a dying stranger.

Stephen took the shoulders, Lucius the feet. They laid the man on the linen. He weighed less than a man should weigh. He had been bleeding into himself for eleven days.

He was perhaps thirty, in the dust-coloured tunic of an imperial courier, belted in plain leather. His face was the colour of wet ash. His eyes were open and tracking and not yet entirely empty. There was a thin pink foam at the corner of his mouth. Around his belly under the tunic was a long bandage wrapped tight in clean linen and over the linen a stained ligature.

Augustine put his hand on the forehead. The skin was cold; it was the cold of a man who was already going. He bent close. The breath that came up out of the mouth was the breath of a man whose gut had been opened, and Augustine had smelled it before, long ago, in his first plague summer, and the smell had not waited to be remembered. It had already stepped into the room. He did not let his face change.

*Brother. I am the bishop here. I am going to look. Do you understand?*

The eyes moved. The lips moved. No sound came.

*I will be quick. Then we will speak.*

He brought the lamp closer and lifted the tunic. The bandage went around the belly twice, neatly, the way a soldier-medic would wrap it; the ligature over it was tight and competent. He cut the ligature, unwrapped the bandage in long turns, and saw what had been done.

The wound ran horizontally a hand's breadth above the navel from the right flank to the left, perhaps a span and a half long, closed with ten or twelve coarse sutures of waxed linen thread set wide and quickly — the way a man stitches a sack he means to open again. The skin around it was red and warm where the rest of the body was cold. There was a smell from it he had known once before, in his first year as a priest, burying plague dead, that he did not wish to name.

He looked at Stephen. *Take the carter to the kitchen. Give him bread and wine. Pay him. Tell him to sleep in the lay quarters and leave at first light. Ask him no questions. Then come back and stand outside this door and let no one in until I open it.*

Stephen took Lucius out. The carter looked once at the man on the table and then did not look again. The bolt slid. Footsteps went, returned, and stopped outside.

The courier's eyes had closed. His chest rose and fell unevenly. Augustine put two fingers under the angle of the jaw. The pulse was thin and fast.

*Brother. I am going to open the wound. There is a thing inside you. I am going to take it out. Then I will give you absolution. Do you understand?*

A sound came that was not a word.

He set the iron knife to the line of stitches at the right end of the wound and cut three. The wound parted like a mouth. Bad-smelling fluid soaked the linen. He cut the next three and the next, and when the wound was open from end to end he set the knife down and put his fingers into the body of the man.

The skin against the back of his hand was cold; the inside of the cavity was the temperature of a man. His fingers — which had not done this kind of work in thirteen years and which had assumed, all those years, that they had forgotten — moved along the underside of the gut without his asking. They had not forgotten. They found, inside the cavity, something not of the man.

It was small and flat, the size of his palm, wrapped in oiled leather and knotted, pressed up between the loops of the gut and the inner wall of the abdomen. It came out cleanly. The leather was wet but the knot was still tight. He laid it on the linen beside the wound.

He looked once more into the cavity for a second object. There was none. There was only the gut, cut once and badly, that had been bleeding into the body for the eleven days since.

The cut had been small enough not to kill quickly. The stitching had been competent enough to keep the cargo in place. The men who had done it had wanted the courier to be found alive, or nearly alive, by whoever would be required to open him.

The courier's eyes opened.

*It is done. I have it. Brother, can you speak?*

Augustine put his ear to the man's mouth. The breath was foul. The lips shaped a sound, then another. He waited. The third was a syllable.

*Cy —*

*Cyprian?*

*Pri —*

*Cyprian. I have heard you. I have it.*

The eyes met his. There was nothing in them but the syllable and the wish for the syllable to be heard. The mouth shaped something smaller.

*Wri —*

*Write it down. I will write it down.*

The courier closed his eyes. Augustine went to the shelf, took down a small wax tablet, and scratched into the wax: *Cyprianus — courier — Ostia — eleven days — cavity sewn closed — Aug. 410.* He set the tablet on the shelf face down and returned to the table.

He sat on the stool beside the courier's head and took the man's hand. The hand had no strength. The fingers were long, and there was an old callus at the base of the index finger that messengers got from carrying a wax stylus for years against the same place.

*I will give you absolution. Hear the words and do not try to answer.*

He spoke the form. He made the cross over the brow with his thumb; the brow was damp with the sweat of dying. He said the dismissal. The lips moved through the form along with him for the first three words and then stopped. The pulse under his fingers thinned and went thready and then was a thing he could not find. The breath in the chest paused, started again, paused, started again, paused, and did not start again. Augustine sat for some minutes with his hand on the man's hand. The room was very quiet. The lamp guttered once and held.

The linen went up over the body to the chin. The eyes he did not close; they closed cleaner after a quarter of an hour.

The leather packet was on the linen where he had set it down.

He untied it.

Inside was a folded piece of papyrus, stiff from carriage but undamaged. The leather had kept the fluids of the body off it. He spread it on the shelf in the light of the second lamp.

It was written in late common Greek in a clerk's hand he did not know, on papyrus he judged from the fibre and the laid-down lines to be Egyptian and not less than ten years old. In the margins, in a small Latin glossing hand, were short translations and notes. There was no salutation, no signature, no city of origin. There was only the body of the document, which was, on the first reading, a list.

Forty-one entries. Seven headings. Each heading the name of a place.

*Akkad. Ur. Ugarit. Mukenai. Tyros. Persis. Karchēdōn.*

Akkad, Ur, Ugarit, Mycenae, Tyre, Persia, Carthage. Seven cities. Not one of them still standing.

Each section gave between four and seven entries. The entries described, briefly and without rhetoric, a thing observable in the place named in the years before the place ceased. *The magistrates appointed without examination by the magistrates of the previous year. The granaries reporting full and being empty. The coinage debased to two parts in five. The army of the city composed in greater part of those whose fathers had not been of the city. The festivals retained and the meaning of the festivals not retained.* He read on. By the time he reached Carthage, the entries he was reading were the entries of the city he was sitting in, and he stopped reading them as Carthage and read them as Rome, and read them, after Rome, as Hippo.

The Latin glosses in the margins gave the phrase in plain Latin and occasionally added a word: *cf. Polybius vi*, or *cf. Sallust*, or *non in Romae historicis sed in archivis ipsis*. The glossator had read in the archives themselves of the cities he glossed, and wrote a Latin Augustine judged to be of Alexandria.

At the end of the seven sections, in the same Greek hand as the body, was a closing paragraph. He translated as he read.

*From the seven cities here named, forty-one conditions are set down. These conditions were observable, by men who watched, in each city in the years before that city ceased to be the city it had been. Of these forty-one conditions, the city of Rome in the consulship of Arcadius and Honorius, in the year that is now eight years past from the writing of this document, exhibited not less than thirty-one. The remaining ten were not absent. They were not yet observable to the writer of this document. They will be observable shortly. He who reads this document is to do with it what he can. The writer is no one of consequence. The matter is of consequence.*

There was no signature.

---

Augustine stood at the shelf with the document on the wood in front of him and the courier cooling on the table behind him.

The institution of the next century in this part of the world would be the Church or it would be no one. Papyrus did not survive. No one in Africa would copy this on its own merits. If he died in his sleep tonight, the packet would be in Eraclius's hands by morning, and Eraclius would not know what to do with it. No document of this kind survived the next century unless the institution of the next century had already decided to copy it. The Church would not copy this on its own merits. The Church would copy it only if it could not be separated from a thing the Church could not afford to lose.

He needed a wrapping.

He went to the upper shelf. Behind two unrelated rolls of accounts was a small bronze box that had once kept Monnica's letters. He took it down. The hinges gave their small specific sound when the lid lifted — he had heard that sound twice in the last thirty years, the night his mother died at Ostia and the night his son died at Hippo, and the sound carried both nights with it.

Inside was the thin disc of bronze the size of his palm, polished on one face, marked on the other with a pattern of small holes he had never been able to read. He had received the box from the hands of the priest Simplicianus in Milan the night he was baptized. The priest had said only: *Carry it. The man it is for will know it. He will not be you.* He had carried it for thirty years and had taken it out twice and on both occasions the disc had been warm in a way it should not have been.

It was warm now, the third time. The warmth was not the warmth of a thing that had been against another warm thing — the bronze had been on a high shelf in a cool room behind a closed lid for ten years. He had stopped trying to explain that twenty years ago. He set it on the desk beside the papyrus.

He read the papyrus a third time. The disc did not light, did not move, did nothing visible. The markers in the papyrus arranged themselves in his head differently with the disc on the desk than they had arranged themselves an hour before without it. The seven cities were one repeated city. The forty-one markers were not forty-one observations but the working pieces of a single mechanism. Rome was the thirty-one. The ten remaining were what Rome would be in eight years, or twelve, or twenty.

The recognition arrived in his body the way a fever arrives — first in the back of the throat, then in the chest, then everywhere — and after it came the man who had cut the courier and sent him.

The cut had been small enough not to kill quickly. The stitching had been competent enough to keep the cargo in place. The man who had done it had read the document and had returned it. The man who had done it had wanted Augustine to receive the document.

Augustine sat with the disc in one hand and the papyrus in the other.

He had not been sent the courier. He had been sent to be the archive. There was, in the empire, one man who could wrap a document of this kind in a wrapping the Church would copy for four hundred years, and that man had been chosen.

He set the disc down on the desk. His hands were not steady. He waited until they were. They took some time.

The archive would survive. In some century not yet his, the man who had cut the courier would return for it.

He could refuse. He could burn the papyrus, blow the brazier, and the document would die with the courier in the trench beyond the western wall. The men who had cut the courier would send another. They had eight years, or twelve, or twenty. They would send to Jerome. They would send to a man not yet born.

He sat with the choice. The choice did not get smaller. The brazier was at his elbow. He could feel the unlit charcoal in it. He could see the lamp in his hand putting it to the kindling.

He could not refuse.

The wax was in front of him. He took up the stylus.

He turned the tablet over and wrote under the line about the courier:

*The City of God to be in twenty-two books. Books one through ten: refutation. Books eleven through twenty-two: the city itself. Within the eleven later books to be set down, beneath the proper teaching, the matter of the document received this night. Method to be worked out. Method must work for a reader four hundred years from this writing. Method must not depend on me to be alive to explain it.*

The wax went smooth under the stylus. He rewrote the note more compactly. He had the shape, not yet the method. The shape would do for the night.

He folded the papyrus, wrapped it again in the leather, and put the leather and the disc together back into the bronze box. He returned the box to the upper shelf behind the rolls of accounts. He set the wax tablet face down on the desk.

He turned to the courier.

The linen came back from the chin. He closed the eyes — which had been open half an hour now and were ready to close — and drew the linen up over the face. The folding went as the plague dead had been folded in his first year as a priest: three folds along the body, two across. The order had not left him.

The bolt slid back. He opened the door.

*The carter is gone?*

*To the lay quarters. He sleeps. He asked nothing.*

*Get the spades. Both. Wake no one else. Bring them to the small east door of the cloister.*

The two of them carried the courier wrapped in linen out by the small east door at the third hour of night, the same door by which Crispus had been carried thirteen years and four months earlier. The door's iron pull had a particular cold; he had not put his hand on it since the last time, and his hand remembered the cold before his mind remembered the door. They went to the same corner of the burying-ground beyond the western wall. They dug a trench narrower than was usual and deeper than was usual. The ground in August was hard at the surface and giving below it; the spades went in shallow at first and then easier. He felt the easier giving go up his arms. He felt his right knee through the second hour of the digging. Stephen did not speak. Stephen knew not to.

They put the courier in. Augustine said the form for a man whose name he would not record. Stephen said the responses. They filled the trench. They tamped. The work took until the seventh hour of night, as it had taken thirteen years before. Augustine noticed that he was measuring it, and that he had not noticed himself measuring the first one until he was measuring this one.

They returned to the writing room and burned the soaked linen in the brazier. The brazier smoked badly; the linen was wet and the room filled with a smell that would still be in the room at first prayers and that the lay brother who came at sunrise to clean would put down to the brazier and not ask further. They scrubbed the table with vinegar and salt. They scrubbed the floor where one drop had fallen. They did not speak through any of it except for the necessary words.

*Go and sleep. You are at first prayers.*

*Father.* Stephen hesitated.

Augustine waited.

*Will there be others?*

The deacon was looking at the floor of the cloister. His right hand was a fist at his side and had been a fist for some minutes; he had not noticed. Stephen had been the hands of a thing in a baptistery thirteen years before. The hands had been a particular pair of hands, and he had not asked since to be told of any work of that kind that did not require them.

*I do not know. The man I have buried tonight was killed at Ostia. Whoever killed him knew he was carrying. They did not retrieve. They sent. Tell me at once if any unfamiliar man comes to the gate, in any clothes, on any pretext, in the next month. Tell me before you tell any other.*

*Father.* He went.

Augustine sat at the desk. The lamp guttered. The room was empty in a way that an empty room is not usually empty — there had been a body in it for the better part of three hours, and the absence of the body was a thing he could feel against his face. He took down the bronze box, looked at it once, put it back. He took up the parchment with the first paragraph and read it again. The sentence was not less beautiful for being also, now, the wrapping of a thing that had come tonight in a wallet in the inside of a man. He held the page in both hands. The parchment was cool. His hands were cold. The cold of his hands and the cold of the parchment were close to the same cold and that was a comfort of a small kind.

He covered the parchment, blew out one lamp, took the other, and went to his cell. He lay on the straw mattress and closed his eyes. The seventy-eighth psalm came back, the way it had come back when the bell rang the first time, and ran through his head until sleep took him. The sleep took perhaps two hours.

The bell rang for first prayers and he was there. The cold of a child's hair was on the palm of his hand through the canticle, the way a small physical sensation will outlast every effort to set it down. He sang the verses. He went on with the morning. The hand stayed cold.
