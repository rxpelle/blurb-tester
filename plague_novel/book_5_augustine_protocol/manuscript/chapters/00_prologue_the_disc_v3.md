# Prologue I — The Disc (v3, thriller)

*Ostia, the rented room above the harbour street, the morning of the seventeenth of November 387 CE.*
*The man in the room is Aurelius Augustinus — later called Augustine of Hippo, Bishop, Doctor of the Church, and one of the most consequential minds in the history of the Western world. He is thirty-three years old. His mother is dying.*

The household sent for him at the first hour. He was already awake. He had been awake for most of the night that had been all rain — rain on the shutters, rain on the tile of the courtyard below, rain in the corridors of his sleep when sleep had come at all in the last nineteen nights. Lartidianus's lamp moved along the corridor and stopped at his door, and the knock was the small soft knock a servant gives at the door of a man whose mother is dying in the next room.

*Aurelius. She is asking for you.*

*I am coming.*

He sat up. The cot was cold. The floor under his bare feet was the cold of November stone in a port the sea had begun to take its winter back from, and the cold went up through his soles into his ankles and lodged there the way the cold of these nineteen nights had lodged in him a week ago and not gone since. He pulled on his sandals. He took his lamp. The corridor was colder than the cot.

He went down.

The door of her room was open the width of a hand. He pushed it. The hinge made the small sound it had made all nineteen nights. He stepped in. The room smelled of lamp oil and something beneath it — faint, specific — that he had learned in nine nights not to name.

He saw her first. A long shape under the linen, the small head on the bolster, the grey braid pulled forward over one shoulder the way she had liked it for sleeping all his life. The rest of the room arranged itself at the edges of his sight. Navigius at the foot of the bed. Adeodatus on the low stool in the corner under the high window, hands flat on his knees, fifteen years old and tall for fifteen and looking at his hands. Evodius and Alypius on the bench against the wall, close enough to be present, far enough not to crowd the bed. The freedwoman the household had sent up — a woman of perhaps forty, a nurse out of nursing — at the small table at the bedside with a cup of water and a folded square of linen. The lamp on the table was the only lamp in the room. The light it gave was the colour of the inside of a closed eye when the eye is closed against a stronger light beyond it.

He came around the bed. He sent the freedwoman to the corner with a small look. He took her stool. The linen on his mother's chest moved with her breath. The breath was shallower than it had been the night before. He had not, in the night, expected it to be.

He took her hand.

The hand was cool. It was cool in the way her hand had never been cool in his life — not in the garden at Tagaste in his fourth year, when she had taken his small fingers in her larger ones and shown him the shape of a letter in dust; not in the kitchen at Carthage in his nineteenth year when she had taken the bowl from him and set it on the table and said the boy will be loved; not at Milan the previous Easter, when she had taken his hand at the door of the basilica after the rite and held it the way a woman holds a thing she has been waiting the length of her life to hold. The hand he held now was not those hands. The fever had taken something out of it that the hand was not going to get back.

*Mother.*

She opened her eyes.

*Aurelius.*

*I am here.*

*Yes.*

He held the cup to her lips. She took two small swallows and turned her head a small degree to the side, which was the way he had learned, in nine days, that she said *no more.* He set the cup back. He did not let go of her hand.

She was fifty-six. He was thirty-three. She had had what she had wanted from him for seven months — since the news had come from Milan that the rite had been performed, since the afternoon at Cassiciacum in May when she had stood at the rim of the well with her hand flat on the stone and said, *Aurelius. I have nothing left to want in this world.* He had believed her. She had been holding what she had said since May. She had held it, on his understanding of her now, until the autumn fevers had given her a door to walk through.

*Aurelius. The day is what day.*

*The seventeenth, mother. The morning. The ship is at the third hour — mid-morning. The ship will be without us.*

*Yes. Has Navigius made the arrangements.*

*He has made them. The household will hold the rooms for the burial. We will sail when the ship after the next has passage.*

*Good.* She closed her eyes a moment. Opened them. *The burial here. Not at Tagaste. Tagaste is for the boy in his time. I have walked enough of the African road. I will rest here.*

*Yes.*

*Aurelius.*

*Mother.*

*Bring me the box.*

He had been in the room perhaps the count of a hundred when she said it. He had not known she would speak the word *box.* He had not, on his own honest reckoning, known there was a box for her to know about — which was a lie, because he had been carrying the box since Easter, but the carrying had been the carrying of a man who had been told to carry a thing and had not, in seven months, lifted the lid of the thing he had been told to carry.

He looked at her.

She held his look. The holding was an exertion now. He saw what it cost her.

*Yes,* he said.

He let her hand go. He stood. The blood went out of his head a moment and came back. He went to the small chest at the foot of his cot in the next room. He opened it. The bronze box was where he had set it on the morning after his baptism, in the linen wrap Simplicianus had wrapped it in, beneath the second tunic he had been wearing on the road south. He took it out. The bronze was cold through the linen.

He brought it back. He set it on the small table at the bedside, beside the cup of water and the linen square. He sat. He took her hand again.

*Open it,* she said.

He looked at her.

*I have been instructed not to open it.*

*You have been instructed not to open it without the word of the woman who would tell you when.* A pause. She drew the next breath carefully. *The woman is telling you. Open it.*

He sat with that.

*Mother. You knew about the box.*

A small sound came out of her that might have been a laugh in a stronger body. *Aurelius. I have known about the box since before you were born. It was given to Simplicianus — your bishop at Milan, the man who finished your conversion — by my own teacher in the year before you went to Carthage. The arrangement was that I would tell you on the day I judged correct.* Another breath. *The day I judged correct was a day I had hoped would not come for some further years.*

*Today.*

*Today. Open it.*

He undid the small bronze hasp. The lid lifted. The hinges gave a small specific sound, and that sound went in past his ribs and lodged where things lodge that a man is going to carry. He would, on every occasion of opening this box for the rest of his life, hear this morning first.

Inside was a thin disc of bronze the size of his palm. It was polished on one face. It was marked on the other with a pattern of small holes arranged in a double arc that, at the angle the lamp gave, resolved almost into a letter he recognised — and then, as he tilted the disc a degree further, dissolved into a pattern he did not. He held it at three angles. It was not a letter he knew. It was not nothing, either.

*Take it out,* she said.

He took it out. The marked face was rough under his thumb where the holes had been worked — small, precise, not random. The polished face was smooth as river stone.

*Hold it.*

He held it.

The disc was warm.

It was warm in a way the bronze had no business being warm — not in an unheated room, not on the morning of the seventeenth of November, not after nineteen nights at the bottom of a closed chest. The bronze had been cold under his fingers when he had set the box on the table a moment before. The bronze in his hand was not cold now. The warmth was the warmth of skin. He felt it spread through his fingers and into the heel of his palm where the disc rested.

He looked at his mother.

She had closed her eyes. The closing was not the closing of a woman drifting; it was the closing of a woman gathering.

*The disc warms when it warms,* she said. Her voice had gone quieter. *Do not seek to make it warm. It will not be made to warm. Today the reason is that I am going and you are taking it.* Her chest rose. *That is reason enough.*

*Mother.*

*Aurelius.*

*What is it.*

She opened her eyes. She looked at him and she did not look away.

*I do not know. My teacher did not know. We have been carrying it.* A breath. *We have been carrying it in the trust that it is for a man, in some not-our-generation, who will know what it is. Until that man comes, the carrying is the work.* Another breath. *That is all of it. There is no creed that comes with it. There is no doctrine. There is no other person who will, in your lifetime, give you a further instruction. Hear me.*

*I am hearing.*

*Carry it. The man it is for will know it. He will not be you.* She paused on this. He felt her gather. *If he comes in your lifetime, you will recognise him. You will know him by a thing in the way he looks at you. I cannot describe it because no one has been able to describe it to me. If he does not come in your lifetime, you will, in the year before you die, give the box to a man you have judged worthy. He will be the next carrier. The carrying may go on for two more generations. It may go on for ten. You will not know.* She stopped and breathed. *You will do the carrying that is yours to do. Now the work begins.*

The disc was still warm in his hand. His throat had closed in the last minute and he had not noticed it closing. He noticed it now. The water in his eyes was hot. It was on his cheek before he had time to be embarrassed by it. He did not lift his hand to wipe it. The hand he might have used was the hand holding the disc, and the other hand was the hand holding hers, and he did not let either of them go.

*Mother.*

*Yes.*

*I will carry it.*

*I know you will. I have been watching you carry things you did not know you were carrying for thirty-three years. You will carry this one knowing.*

She closed her eyes. She rested. He did not move. He held the disc. He held her hand. He listened to her breath thin and start and thin and start and not, as he listened, not yet stop.

She opened her eyes again.

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*Adeodatus.*

He turned his head. The boy was where the boy had been, on the low stool under the high window. The boy's hands had come off his knees in the last minute. The boy's hands were folded in his lap now and the boy's eyes were on him and the boy's face was the face of a fifteen-year-old who has just heard something he is not going to forget and does not yet know what it is.

*Come, son.*

Adeodatus came. He stopped beside Augustine. Augustine did not look up at him; he felt the boy stop. The boy's hand brushed the edge of the bench by his hip and stayed there a moment for balance. The boy was breathing through his mouth.

Monnica looked at the boy. She held the look longer than she had held the look at Augustine.

*Adeodatus.*

*Grandmother.*

*The carrying is your father's. It is not yours. You will not, in your life, carry the box. I want you to hear me say it.* Her hand moved a small degree on the linen toward the boy. *The box is your father's burden. It is not a burden I am giving to you. You are a free man. Do you understand me.*

*Yes, grandmother.*

*Good. Stand with your uncle. I will see you again before I go.*

The boy went. His feet made a small sound on the floor that was the sound a boy's feet make when they are too big for him still and he has not learned yet to walk without making the floor know about it. He went to Navigius at the foot of the bed. He stood there. He did not know what to do with his hands. He folded them and unfolded them and folded them.

Monnica looked at Augustine.

*Aurelius.*

*Mother.*

*I have not been a particularly good carrier. I have carried the box in the back of a cabinet in three houses. I have not, in thirty years, opened it. My teacher did not ask me to open it. She asked me to carry the words she gave me, and to give them to you when the time came. The words are what I am giving you now. I have, in the last year, watched you become the man I have been waiting to give it to since the year before you went to Carthage.* She breathed. *The waiting was the work. The watching was the work I did in your house for thirty-three years. Do you understand what I am telling you.*

*I am beginning to.*

*Begin faster. I do not have the morning.*

He held her hand tighter. He could not speak. The thing in his throat had made a fist of itself and would not move.

She waited. She did not look away.

After some time he said:

*Mother. The watching was the work.*

*Yes.*

*The watching was what you were doing all the years I thought you were only being my mother.*

*Yes.*

*I did not know.*

*You were not meant to know. The box is not a thing one is meant to know about until one is the carrier. I have been the watcher. You will be the carrier.* Her hand tightened on his a small degree. *Whoever you give the box to in your time, you will watch them too. The watching is part of the work. The watching is the larger part. The carrying is the small part. The work is mostly watching.*

*Yes, mother.*

*Good. Now. Bring Adeodatus. I would have a word with him.*

He brought the boy.

She had words with the boy. The words were not the words she had had with Augustine. They were the words a grandmother has with a grandson at her death, about small things — the small wooden horse with the broken leg at the bottom of his father's writing chest at Tagaste, the bench by the third fig tree at his uncle's house that the boy had liked when he was small. The words were brief. The boy's eyes did not, through the words, leave her face. Augustine watched the boy's throat work between sentences and did not, watching, find words for what was in his own.

She blessed him. He bowed under her hand. The hand on the boy's head rested a moment longer than it might have. Then it lifted, and the boy went back to Navigius's side at the foot of the bed.

She turned her head a small degree on the pillow toward Augustine. He had taken her hand again.

*Aurelius. The wine.*

He held the cup. She took two small swallows. She turned her head away.

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*I am going.*

*Yes.*

*Sit with me.*

*I am sitting.*

*Hold the disc.*

*I am holding it.*

*Good.*

She closed her eyes.

She breathed for some further time. The breathing was the breathing of a woman whose body had begun to do its own work without her permission. The pulse under his thumb thinned. Each breath was further from the last. He held the disc in his right hand and her hand in his left and the disc was warm and her hand was warm still in the kind of warm a hand is when it is a body's last warm, which is not the same warm.

She opened her eyes one more time. She did not look at him. She looked at a point in the ceiling above the lamp where the rafter joined the wall and where, in the lamplight, the wood had a knot the colour of a fig.

*The work begins now,* she said.

Her hand went still in his hand. The breath in her chest paused, started again, paused, started again, paused, and did not start again.

He did not move for a long count.

The room did not move.

A sound came from the foot of the bed — a single caught breath, high and involuntary, the kind that surprises the throat that makes it. Augustine did not look at him. He did not have, in his face, the room for the boy yet. He held the disc in his right hand and his mother's hand in his left and the disc was warm and his mother's hand was already not warm in the way it had been a minute before.

Navigius came forward. Augustine raised one hand without looking up. Navigius stopped.

*An hour,* Augustine said.

*Aurelius.*

*An hour. Then send for the women. I will sit with her an hour. No one comes in. The boy can stay if he wishes. Anyone else goes.*

*Aurelius.*

*An hour.*

Navigius nodded. He drew the boy back to the bench. Evodius and Alypius rose; Augustine did not lift his eyes; they bowed and went out. The freedwoman went out behind them with her cup and her linen. Lartidianus went out last with the second lamp.

The door closed.

Augustine sat with his mother's body.

He did not weep. He had wept in the moment of the disc warming, and he did not weep now. He sat. He held her hand. He held the disc. The disc was warm in his right hand for the whole of the hour. He did not, in the hour, examine the warmth or attempt to account for it. He let it be the warmth.

He thought of small things.

He thought of Cassiciacum in May — her hand flat on the rim of the well, fingers slightly spread. She had said: *I have nothing left to want in this world.* He had heard it then as relief. He heard it now as permission she had been granting herself, slowly, since the moment of the saying.

He thought of the garden at Tagaste in his fourth year. She had taken a stick and drawn a letter in the dust with the end of it and put the stick in his hand and folded her hand around his and drawn the letter again. The letter was the letter A. The dust was the warm thin dust of late summer. Her hand around his had been the warm hand, the hand that the hand he was holding now would never be again.

He turned the disc in his right hand and brought the marked face into the light. He had seen it for the count of a minute before she had told him to hold it. He looked now with more care. The holes were small and precisely worked — twelve or perhaps fourteen; the light was not sufficient to be certain. Arranged in a double arc, they resolved, at one angle, into something that was almost a letter, or the near-half of a letter from his Greek or from some other alphabet he half-remembered — and then, as he tilted the disc a degree further, became only precision without legible intent. He held it at three angles. He could not read it. He was certain it was not nothing.

He thought of Una — sent back from Milan before his baptism, before he had become the man he had decided, in that year, to become. He would write to her brother. He would not write to her. The shape of what that meant was a thing the disc had nothing to do with, and he set it aside, and sat.

He thought of the boy at the foot of the bed. Adeodatus was fifteen. The boy would have, on the present trajectory of his health, some long set of years ahead of him in Africa. The boy would do well. The boy was bright and had been baptised at Milan with his father at the same Easter. The boy had heard, in this room, a thing about his father he had not previously known and had been told, in the same hour, that the thing was not for him to carry. Augustine did not know yet what that hearing was going to do to the boy. He suspected, sitting with the disc warm in his right hand and his mother's hand cooling in his left, that what it was going to do was a thing he was not going to be able to do anything about.

The hour passed.

He stood. The blood went out of his head and came back. He set the disc back in the box. He closed the lid. The hinges gave their small specific sound. He set the box on the small table beside the cup of water and the linen square. He laid his mother's hand on her chest beside her other hand. He folded the second hand over the first. He smoothed the hair on her forehead. He drew the linen up over her face. He went to the door.

He opened it. Navigius was in the corridor with Adeodatus and Lartidianus and the freedwoman and the women who had been called.

*She is ready,* he said. *I will be in the next room. Tell me when the women have done.*

He went into the next room. He sat on the cot. He held the bronze box on his knees. The disc inside the box was, on his hand on the box, no longer warm; the warmth had gone with the hour. He sat with the box for the count of perhaps three hundred and then he set it back in the chest at the foot of the cot, beneath the spare tunic, in the linen wrap Simplicianus had wrapped it in.

He went out to the corridor. He went down to the courtyard. The rain had stopped. The air in the courtyard was cold — a clean cold, the cold of rain and sea and stone, nothing in it of the room above. It smelled of wet stone and the kelp the tide had brought up against the breakwater in the night. He stood in it. He let it be cold. The sky over the courtyard wall was the colour of a knot in wet wood.

The morning was almost the third hour. The ship that would have carried his mother to Africa would, at the third hour, sail without her.

Navigius came down to the courtyard at the third hour and a quarter.

*Aurelius. The women are done. You may come up.*

*I will come up. Send word to the priest at the basilica of Saint Aurea. The burial will be tomorrow. I will pay for the place beside the eastern wall.*

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*Are you well.*

He looked at his brother. He had not, in twenty-eight years of being his brother's brother, been asked the question by Navigius in this form. He sat with the question. The disc had been warm in his hand the length of an hour. His mother's hand had cooled in the same hour. The two warmths and the one cooling were going to make some shape in him, in the years to come, that he was not yet able to see the shape of.

*I am beginning the work,* he said.
