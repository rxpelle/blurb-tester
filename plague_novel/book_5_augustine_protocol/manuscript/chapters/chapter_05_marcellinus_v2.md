# Chapter — Marcellinus (v2, thriller)

*Hippo Regius, the last week of January 412 CE.*

Mark came back on the Wednesday on a coastal ship out of Ostia that had touched at Carthage on the Sunday and had come down the harbour road in two days. He arrived at the basilica gate at the second hour of the afternoon, on foot, with the leather satchel under his outer tunic where it had been when he had left, and with a small additional cloth bag at his shoulder that he had not had when he had left. He was thinner than he had been in October. He was also smiling.

Eraclius received him at the gate. Eraclius brought him directly to the writing room. Augustine was at the desk on the third paragraph of the second book of the *City of God*, which he had been on for two weeks and which was not going. He set down the bone stylus when Eraclius opened the door without knocking.

*Father. The deacon Mark.*

Augustine stood. Mark came through the door behind Eraclius. He bowed, the way he had bowed at the end of October when he had left — a clean inclination of the head and shoulders that the diaconate at Carthage had drilled into him in his first months and that he had not, in three months on the road, lost. He did not, Augustine noted, bow with the small forward step that nervousness had given him in October. He bowed cleanly and stood.

*Father.*

*Mark. Sit. Eraclius, send for hot water and bread. The deacon will eat.*

*Father, with thanks, I have eaten. I took a meal at the harbour an hour ago.*

*Then sit and tell me. Eraclius will bring water.*

Mark sat in the chair across the desk. He took the leather satchel from under his tunic and laid it on the desk and undid the strap. From inside the satchel he drew, one at a time, seven small folded acknowledgments — receipts in seven different hands, in seven different inks, with seven different seals or marks. He laid them on the desk in the order Augustine had given him in October: Sixtus, Justin, Cornelius, the two from Carthage, the master's deputy, Ostia.

*Father. All seven delivered to the named recipients in person. The waiting was convenient at four of the seven; written acknowledgments are here. The other three I waited for an oral acknowledgment because the recipient was either not at the office on the day or was not, on the recipient's representation to me, in a position to write an acknowledgment that day. The oral acknowledgments I have repeated to myself daily on the return journey to be sure I have them in their precise wording. I will give them to you now if you wish.*

*Give them.*

Mark gave them. The three oral acknowledgments were brief — the kind of brief acknowledgments senior officials gave to a courier of a junior diocese on a routine delivery. Mark recited them in the small careful voice he had been carrying them in since November, with the precision of a young man who had been entrusted with a task and who had performed it in every particular as he had been instructed.

Augustine listened. He listened with the part of himself that was a bishop receiving a successful courier's report and he listened with the part of himself that was a man hearing the closing seal of an operation he had set in motion in October the consequences of which the courier did not know and would not, on Augustine's intention, ever know. The two parts of himself listened to the same sentences. The two parts heard the sentences differently. Mark, on the other side of the desk, heard nothing of the difference; Mark heard the sentences he was reciting and the bishop receiving them.

When Mark was finished, Augustine said:

*You have done well.*

*Father.*

*The journey was hard.*

*The journey was a journey, father. The crossing was rougher than I had been told to expect. The waiting at Ostia for the southbound ship was nine days longer than the bishop's vicar's schedule had estimated. I read the four books of Cicero's *De Officiis* — *On Duties* — I had taken with me twice through during the wait, which I had not done before, and which I have, in the doing of it, found has changed the way I think about the duties of a clerical office. I would, with your permission, like to discuss the *De Officiis* with you when there is a quiet hour.*

*There will be a quiet hour. We will discuss it.*

*Father.*

*Eraclius will see to your settling in. The diocese has work for you. We will return you to the regular round at the second hour after vespers. You should rest the afternoon.*

*Father.*

Mark stood. He bowed the clean bow. He turned to follow Eraclius out. At the door he paused and looked back.

*Father.*

*Yes.*

*The commission you gave me in October. Is there a further word for me on the substance of what the letters contained.*

Augustine looked at his deacon. Mark was twenty-four. He had grown half an inch in three months on the road, on the visible evidence at the door, and had thinned in the way young men of his age and disposition thinned on a long journey of carrying letters they were not permitted to open. He was looking at Augustine with the open look of a young man who had performed the task entrusted to him and was now hoping, in the small way clergy of his age hoped, to be told what the task had been about.

*The substance of the letters,* Augustine said, *is the kind of substance that a senior bishop carries and a junior deacon does not. The carrying of the letters by a deacon who does not know the substance is the proper division. You have carried in good faith. The substance has, on the present accounting of the matter, been received and acted on. There will not be a further word.*

*Father.*

*You have not been told because the not-being-told is what permits a deacon of your office to do this work without complication. Do you understand.*

*I understand, father.*

*Good.*

Mark inclined his head and went out behind Eraclius.

Augustine sat at the desk. He sat for a long count. The seven receipts on the desk were the seven receipts. The third paragraph of the second book of the work was on the parchment in front of him and had not been continued. The afternoon light was the light of January, which was a thin light at Hippo, and the writing room was cold; Eraclius had banked the brazier in the corner as he had been instructed to in this season but the brazier was not throwing what it threw in November.

He took up the wax tablet at his elbow. He scraped clean what had been on it. He wrote, in a shorthand only he and Possidius now used: *Mark returned. Seven delivered. Boy uninjured in his understanding. The matter of the desert has, on Justin's note in the autumn, completed without further complication. The man is in the cell at Sketis where he will finish. Sketis was three weeks' travel into the Egyptian desert; a man placed there was, for every practical purpose of the world he had been removed from, gone. Augustine had decided, in October, that this was the better of the two things that could have been done. He still believed it was.*

He scraped the tablet clean. He wrote it again, more compactly, in the way he wrote a thing he intended to remember without committing to the parchment. He scraped it clean a second time. He set the stylus down.

He went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box. He opened it. The hinges sounded. The disc was inside. He took it out. It was the size of a man's palm, heavier than bronze had any reason to be; he had stopped trying to explain the weight twenty-five years ago.

The disc was cold.

He held it for a count. He held it for a second count, which he had not done before, and then set it back. He could not have said what he had been checking for. He closed the box. He returned the box to the shelf.

The seven receipts on the desk would go into the chest with the iron lock at the end of the afternoon, where they would stay for the rest of his life, in the company of the seven copies he had kept of the seven letters he had not, on the morning he had sealed them in October, made copies of for his own files because the keeping of copies of those particular letters had not been a thing he had been prepared to do. He had made the copies in the third week of October, after Mark had sailed, in the slow honest accounting that a man performed when he had committed to a thing and recognised that the committing required the keeping of a record. The seven copies had been in the chest since. The seven receipts would join them by sundown.

He went back to the second book of the work. The third paragraph began to come.

*Tagaste, the third week of June 412 CE.*

Augustine travelled to Tagaste at the end of June by the inland road, with Possidius — the bishop at Calama, his oldest friend in the episcopate — and one of the household lay brothers and a small mule for the baggage, in the slow heat of an African summer that had begun three weeks early and was, on every farmer's report along the road, already harder than the previous summer had been. The journey took five days. He was fifty-seven and his right knee was, in this weather, the right knee of a man of seventy; he rode the small mule at the slow pace the small mule preferred and did not, in the five days, complain about the pace once.

They came to Tagaste on the Saturday afternoon.

The monastery was on a piece of land three miles east of the town, in the small valley the family had owned for two generations and that Pinianus had signed over to the foundation in March in a formal act before the senior bishop's deputy at Carthage. The buildings were not yet finished. The chapel was finished and the small refectory was finished and the four cells of the lay sisters' wing were finished; the larger residence for the choir sisters was a frame and a roof and three walls. Twenty workmen were on the site when Augustine and Possidius came up the cart-track from the road. The workmen stopped what they were doing when they saw the bishop's small party. The foreman — a man Augustine had known at Tagaste since both of them were boys, who had been the son of the carter who had owned the cart that had carried Monnica's body from Ostia to the harbour for the African ship she had not in the end taken — came forward and bowed.

*Aurelius. The lady is in the chapel.*

*The chapel is finished?*

*The chapel was finished on the Wednesday. The dedication is what you have come for. The lady has been in it since the second hour of the morning.*

*Pinianus.*

*The husband is in the small refectory. He is not, on the deputy abbess's word, well. He has been there since the first hour. The lady has had a chair brought to him. He is sitting.*

*Good. Take the mules. Have them watered. We will be at the chapel.*

The foreman took the mules. Augustine and Possidius walked across the dust of the building site to the small chapel, which was a single-naved building of cut stone with a tiled roof in the new style, with a small bell-tower at the west end that had not yet been hung with a bell. They went in through the west door.

Melania was at the altar. She was not praying; she was examining the work of the masons and the carpenters with the careful eye of a woman who had spent her family's wealth on every stone and was making sure the stones were what she had paid for. She turned when she heard their sandals on the threshold.

*Bishop.*

*Daughter.*

She came down the nave to them. She was twenty-nine. She was thinner than she had been seven months earlier. The line at the jaw had become the line of an older woman. She walked with the carriage of someone who had been on her feet for nine hours and had two more hours to be on them. Augustine had not been ready for any of it.

*Bishop. The chapel is what we agreed.*

*It is.*

*The choir wing is behind. It will be finished in October. The lay sisters' wing is finished. There are six women in residence; there will be ten by Christmas. The deputy abbess is from the senior bishop's house at Carthage; she has been at this kind of work for fourteen years. She is the woman I named to you in the letter.*

*Sophia.*

*Sophia. She is in the lay sisters' wing showing the young Numidian woman from Hippo where the linen is kept. She will come to the chapel for the consecration when the bell rings. There is no bell yet but she will come on the half-hour of the third hour, which is when we have agreed the consecration will begin.*

*Good. Pinianus.*

She held his look for a count.

*Pinianus is in the small refectory. He has been there since the first hour. He cannot stand for more than a count of ten without the chair, which he will not take into the chapel because he would not be seen with the chair at the consecration of the chapel he has signed the foundation papers for. He will walk to the chapel for the consecration if it kills him, and it may. I have not, in the last hour, been able to dissuade him. You will, perhaps, be able to.*

*I will speak to him.*

*Bishop.*

*Yes.*

*Thank you for coming. He had hoped you would.*

*I would not have missed it.*

She inclined her head. She turned back to the altar and to the work of looking at it that she had been at when they had come in. Augustine and Possidius went out of the chapel and across the dust to the small refectory.

Pinianus was in a chair against the eastern wall. He was sixty. He had the colour of a man whose body had been failing for some time and was now, in the heat of the summer, failing faster. He stood when Augustine came in. The standing was a slow operation that began with a hand on the arm of the chair and ended with a small adjustment of the second hand against the wall behind him for balance. He was perhaps an inch shorter than he had been when Augustine had last seen him at Carthage two years before. The shortening was the shortening of a body that had begun to fold under its own weight.

*Aurelius.*

*Pinianus. Sit.*

*I cannot walk to the chapel without sitting first. I will sit until the bell.*

*There is no bell.*

*Until the half-hour. Sit with me.*

Augustine sat in a second chair the lay sister had set on the other side of the wall. Possidius sat on a bench across from them. The three of them spoke for an hour about small things — the journey from Hippo, the news from Italy that Possidius had brought down from Calama in May, the foundation, the senior bishop at Carthage, a small matter of an inheritance dispute between two cousins of Pinianus's family that the family was managing through the senior bishop's office. The conversation was the conversation of three men who had known each other across long distances for some years and who had agreed, for the hour they had together in the small refectory at Tagaste in the heat of late June, not to speak about the death that was moving through the body of the one of them who would, in the foreseeable accounting of the next decade, not be at this kind of conversation for many further sittings.

At the half-hour the deputy abbess came to the door of the refectory and inclined her head. Augustine and Possidius stood. Pinianus stood. He stood without the chair this time, with his hand on the wall for the count of two and then without the wall. He walked across the dust to the chapel under the noon sun on his own legs. He went the slow careful pace of a sixty-year-old whose body had been failing for some time, and he did not, in the walking across the dust, let any of the three of them see his face.

Augustine performed it in the rite proper to a small monastic chapel at its first dedication. The deputy abbess and the six lay sisters in residence and the twenty workmen and Melania and Pinianus and Possidius and the household lay brother who had come with them from Hippo were the congregation. Augustine pronounced the formula. He blessed the altar with the holy oil he had brought from Hippo in the small bronze flask he had used for the consecration of the new font fifteen years before. He pronounced the dismissal.

Pinianus walked back to the small refectory under the noon sun without taking the chair Melania had offered him in the chapel. He sat in the refectory in the chair against the wall when he reached it. He did not stand again that afternoon.

Augustine sat with him in the refectory for the rest of the afternoon. They spoke of the foundation and of Tagaste and of the inheritance dispute. At the seventh hour Pinianus said:

*Aurelius. I will go to the cell now.*

*Yes.*

*I will not, on the present trajectory, see you again.*

*I will write.*

*Write to her also. She will need the letters more than she will say.*

*I will.*

Augustine stood. Pinianus did not rise this time. He inclined his head. Augustine inclined his back. The lay sister came to take Pinianus to the cell. He went on her arm at the slow pace of the morning's pace, and the slowness was the slowness of a man who had been walking the slowness for some weeks and who would, in some week not many weeks from now, not be walking at all. Augustine stood in the small refectory and listened to the footsteps go across the dust outside until he could not hear them anymore, and then stood for a moment longer, because the silence after was also something to stand in.

Augustine and Possidius and the household lay brother left Tagaste at the first hour of the next morning. They took the inland road back. They were at Hippo in five days.

Augustine, on the inland road back from Tagaste in the slow heat of June, thought he was going home from his last conversation with Pinianus. He carried the conversation with him on the road back.

*Hippo Regius, the third week of September 413 CE.*

The dispatch came up from the harbour on the Friday at the third hour of the afternoon, by a Carthage post-courier who had ridden the coast road in seven days. The seal was the senior bishop's. Eraclius brought it up to the writing room without knocking.

*Aurelius. From Carthage. The senior bishop.*

Augustine set the iron pen down. He cut the seal.

The letter was four lines.

*Aurelius. The imperial commissioner Marcellinus and his brother Apringius have been arrested at Carthage on the thirteenth on the warrant of the count of Africa, on charges arising from the late revolt of Heraclianus — the governor of Africa who had made a failed attempt on the throne in the spring, who had been killed for it, and whose death was now being used to settle every old account in the province. They are being held in the praetorian compound. The proceedings are being managed by men of the count's household, not by the proconsul — which is to say outside the ordinary procedure of the courts, and without appeal. The senior bishop has already written to Ravenna and to the consistory. We are doing what can be done. I write to you because Marcellinus has asked, as his one personal request to the senior bishop, that the bishop of Hippo be informed.*

Augustine read the letter through. He set it face-down on the desk, which he had not meant to do.

He had been corresponding with Marcellinus for two years and three months. He had been writing the City of God to him for two years and one month — the answer to the charge that Christianity had destroyed Rome, twenty-two books if he lived to finish them, the largest argument he had ever attempted, which he had not started until Marcellinus had asked the question that made it necessary. He had a letter from Marcellinus on his desk at this hour that was three weeks old and that he had been intending to reply to that afternoon. The letter was about a question Marcellinus had raised about the third book of the work, in which Augustine had argued a particular line about Roman history that Marcellinus had thought worth qualifying. The third paragraph began: *On the question of the Roman virtue you address in the third book — I have been thinking about the difference between a virtue the state pretends to and a virtue an individual performs under the state's pretense, and whether your argument applies equally to both. I suspect it does not. I suspect that is the argument of your fourth book, and that you have not yet told me so.* Augustine had been going to tell him so. He had written the sentence in draft four days ago.

He set the senior bishop's dispatch on the desk beside Marcellinus's letter.

He did not move for a long count.

He thought about Marcellinus. Marcellinus was forty-five. He had been the imperial commissioner who had chaired the conference at Carthage in 411 that had ended the long split in the North African church — a hundred years of Christians against Christians over the question of which side held the legitimate succession — on the terms favourable to the Catholic side. He had been a Christian of the careful steady kind that Augustine had recognised, the first time he had met him in the spring of 411, as the kind of Christian who would, in the long arithmetic of Augustine's correspondence with imperial officials over twenty years, become a friend rather than a contact. The recognition had been correct. Marcellinus had been a friend. The friendship had been, in the two years and three months of it, the kind of friendship Augustine had not had with any imperial official in the eighteen years before. The City of God had been written to him. The dedication of the work — which was now three books drafted and a fourth being drafted — was *to my dearest son Marcellinus*. The dedication was on the parchment of every fair copy of the first book that had gone out from the basilica scriptorium in the past year.

He stood. He went to the window. He pushed the shutter further open. The afternoon was the afternoon of the third week of September, which was the afternoon a North African coastal town gave at this hour of the year — the light coming off the harbour at the slant the harbour gave it in autumn, the small particular wind off the sea that started at this hour and ran until evening. He looked at the harbour for a count.

He went back to the desk.

He took up the iron pen. He drafted the reply to the senior bishop in the slow careful sentences a senior bishop drafted to another senior bishop on the matter of an arrest of a friend of both of them on the warrant of an imperial count. He thanked the senior bishop for the immediate notice. He committed Hippo to writing to the proconsul and to the consistory — the emperor's court at Ravenna, the only body whose authority sat above the count of Africa's — in support of the senior bishop's communications. He requested, in the closing paragraph, that he be kept informed of the developments by the fastest courier the senior bishop's office could spare. He sealed the letter and set it in the tray.

He took up a second sheet of parchment. He drafted the letter to the proconsul at Carthage. He wrote it in the careful Latin of a senior bishop addressing an imperial official on a matter of personal weight. He sealed it and set it in the tray.

He took up a third sheet. He drafted the letter to the consistory at Ravenna. He wrote it in the more formal Latin of an episcopal communication to the imperial chancery. He sealed it. He set it in the tray.

He took up a fourth sheet.

He drafted, in his own hand without dictation, the letter to Marcellinus in the praetorian compound at Carthage. He wrote it as a man wrote to a friend who was in a cell on a charge that might or might not, on the present indications, end in execution. He thanked him for the qualification on the third book; he committed to incorporating it into the fourth. He wrote about the small things of the household at Hippo that Marcellinus had asked after in his last letter. He wrote about his own knee, which had been bad in the autumn rain. He wrote, in the closing paragraph, that he was at the desk at this hour writing the fourth book of the work and that he would, with God's permission, continue the writing of it through whatever came in the next month and that the work was the work and that the dedication of the work was the dedication. He sealed the letter. He set it in the tray.

He sat back.

He thought, for a small period, about whether to take down the bronze box from the upper shelf and check the disc. He decided not to. He had taken the box down on no particular occasion four times in the year and the disc had been cold each time and he had no reason, on this afternoon, to take it down again. He left the box on the shelf.

He took up the parchment of the fourth book of the work. He had been on the seventeenth paragraph of the second chapter when Eraclius had come in with the senior bishop's dispatch. He read the seventeenth paragraph through. He continued the paragraph.

---

The four letters went out with the morning post.

Marcellinus and his brother Apringius were executed at Carthage on the thirteenth of September — the day before the senior bishop had written, on the date of the dispatch — by beheading at the praetorian compound, on the count of Africa's authority. The senior bishop had not, on the writing of his letter, known the executions had already been performed. The news of the executions reached Hippo nine days later by a different courier. Augustine received the news at the third hour of the morning on the second day of October. He read the dispatch through. He set it on the desk beside Marcellinus's letter — the one about the third book, the qualification about Roman virtue, the good careful thinking of a man who had been alive when he wrote it. The crease at the centre of the letter had softened to cloth from being folded and unfolded; the dispatch from Carthage lay flat and new beside it. He sat with both papers in front of him for a long count. Then he took up the iron pen. The hand that held it was steady. He made it steady.

He went back to the fourth book.

The dedication of the City of God was *to my dearest son Marcellinus*. He did not change it. He did not, in the fourteen years of further work on the City of God between the autumn of 413 and the autumn of 426, change it. The dedication remained the dedication. The work was finished and the work was copied and the fair copies went out across the empire and into the centuries and the dedication was the dedication on every copy. The man it was made to was dead before the second of the twenty-two books had been finished. The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years.

Augustine wrote on through the autumn. The fourth book accumulated. The work continued.

In the third week of October, on a Thursday afternoon when the writing room was cold and the brazier had been re-lit for the first time of the season and the fourth book had reached the closing paragraph of its sixth chapter, Augustine went to the upper shelf and took down the bronze box. He opened it. The hinges sounded. He took out the disc.

The disc was cold.

He held it for a count of perhaps fifty. He thought of Marcellinus — not the commissioner, not the dedicatee, but the man who had asked the question about the third book and had been right about it, and who had asked it in September, in the week before the warrant. He thought of Pinianus in the chair against the wall at Tagaste, inclining his head. He thought of Mark in the lay parlour. He thought of Stephen. He thought of the man at Sketis, who had been in the cell for a year and eight months and who had not, on the one letter that had come back from the abbot in February, spoken. He thought of his mother at Ostia and of what she had said in the second-last sentence of her life: *the watching was the work I did in your house for thirty-three years.* He had understood it correctly at the time and had still not been ready. He had been the carrier for twenty-six years — of the disc, and of the knowledge of what the disc was for, and of the silence around both. He would be the carrier for whatever years remained. There was no one else.

He set the disc back. He closed the box. He returned the box to the shelf.

He went back to the desk. He took up the iron pen. He continued the seventh chapter of the fourth book.

He worked until the lamp went out.
