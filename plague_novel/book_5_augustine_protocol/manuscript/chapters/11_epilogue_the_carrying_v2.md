# Epilogue — The Carrying (v2, thriller)

*Hippo Regius, the morning of the first of September 430 CE.*

The cart was at the basilica gate at the second hour of the morning. Eraclius had borrowed it from a freedman in the harbour quarter who had owed the basilica a kindness; the small mule that pulled it was a mule the freedman had not been particularly attached to and had let go for less than the mule was worth, on the understanding that the mule was being lent to a bishop who was not coming back. The cart had a wooden plank for a bench and a small canvas roof against the sun. The chest was on the floor of the cart between Possidius's feet and Stephen's.

Stephen was at the reins. He had been driving carts since he was eleven, before he had entered the church as a junior cleric or anything else; his father had been a free carter at Hippo and Stephen had grown up on the harbour road. Possidius had not, in his life, learned to drive a cart well. He had ridden mules and horses; he had been driven in carts. The driving of the cart, on this morning, was Stephen's.

They had said the goodbyes that needed to be said. Eraclius had embraced Possidius at the gate of the bishop's residence in the dark of the third hour of the night and had been weeping, in the brief discreet way Eraclius wept; he had not been trying to hide the weeping, but he had not been making a show of it either. He had said: *Father. Go.* Possidius had said: *Eraclius. Stay.* They had not said more. The basilica bells rang for the first hour. Eraclius went back to the residence. Possidius went to the cart.

Stephen had been at the cart for an hour by then, in the dark, with the mule. The chest had been loaded the previous evening in a quiet act between the two of them and Eraclius — three men carrying a chest down from the bishop's bedroom through the cloister to the cart at the gate, by lamp, with no one at the residence pretending the chest was anything other than what it was. Stephen had taken the back end. Possidius had taken the front. Eraclius had taken the side. They had not spoken. The chest had been set on the cart's floor between the bench-supports. Stephen had nailed two small wooden chocks against the chest's sides so that it would not slide on the road. The cart had been ready for an hour by the time Possidius came down from the residence in the dawn.

He climbed onto the bench beside Stephen. The bench was narrow. They sat with their thighs touching from hip to knee. They had sat that way on benches in the basilica and in the chapter house and at every refectory table for thirty years. The touching was not new. The touching, on this morning, was not nothing.

Stephen clicked his tongue at the mule. The mule started. The cart moved.

Hippo went past in the slow methodical way a city goes past a cart at the pace of a small mule on a harbour road. The basilica behind them. The lower monastery to the east where Helena was probably already at the morning duties of a sister who had a house full of refugees to manage. The houses of the harbour quarter where the people who had not yet fled were waking to a Sunday morning that would, by midday, have the Vandals at the western walls. The cart did not stop. The mule walked.

At the eastern gate of the city the gate-keeper, who was the gate-keeper who had been at the basilica gate on the night of the courier in 410 and who had moved to the city's eastern gate when his throat had finally given out for good in 423 and would no longer carry his voice through a closed door, was on duty. He saw the cart. He looked up. He did not speak. He raised one hand to the side of his head in an old soldier's salute that he had not been a soldier long enough as a young man to have entirely forgotten. The cart went past. Stephen did not look at him. Possidius did. He did not raise his own hand in answer. He did not need to. The looking was the answer. The gate-keeper put his hand back down.

The cart went out of the city onto the harbour road south.

Stephen drove for perhaps an hour without speaking. Possidius did not speak. The road went between the small farms that fed the harbour, most of them now abandoned or worked by smaller crews than they had been in the spring. The cypress trees along the road had not changed in the way the farms had; the cypress trees did not know there was a war. The cypress trees had been on the road since Possidius had first come down from Calama as a young man in his twenties on his way to study with a bishop he had not yet met. The cypress trees would be on the road after the Vandals had taken everything the farms had once held.

After perhaps an hour Stephen spoke.

*Aurelius.*

It was Augustine's first name. Stephen had used it three times in thirty years in Possidius's hearing; Augustine had not, in those thirty years, been called Aurelius by anyone living except his mother on the day of her death and Crispus in the lamplight of the new font and one or two letters from Melania the Younger in the years after she had gone to Jerusalem. Stephen, calling him Aurelius now, was not addressing the dead bishop. He was naming the absence.

*Yes,* Possidius said.

*He knew about us.*

Possidius had been preparing for this conversation for years. He had not, he understood in the cart on the morning of the first of September, been preparing for it to come up before the city of Hippo had quite gone over the horizon behind them. He sat with what Stephen had said. The mule walked. The road went past.

*Yes,* he said. *I think he did.*

*When did you know.*

*I do not know when I knew. I know I knew before he died. I do not think I knew before the Pelagius year. I am not sure of even that.*

*He never said.*

*He never said.*

Stephen drove. He did not look at Possidius.

*Why do you think he did not say.*

Possidius thought.

*Because if he had said, we would have had to do something. We would have had to deny it, or to leave, or to ask his forgiveness. We would not have been able to go on doing what we have been doing. He did not want us to have to do any of those things. He wanted us to go on doing what we have been doing. He did not say because the not-saying was the kindness.*

*Yes.*

*Stephen.*

*Yes.*

*I should have told you that I had known.*

*I should have told you that I knew you knew.*

They drove for some further distance without speaking. The sun was up over the eastern hills now. The mule was not in a hurry. The mule had its own pace, which was the pace at which the mule had decided, at some point in its earlier life, that the world should move past it. Possidius had been on enough carts to know that you did not, when a mule had made that decision, attempt to alter it.

After some time Stephen said:

*The chest.*

*Yes.*

*You have not opened it since the night.*

*No.*

*Will you open it on the road.*

*No.*

*At Carthage.*

*No.*

*Ever again.*

Possidius did not answer immediately. He thought about the question. The answer he had been carrying for ten weeks — since June, since Augustine had set the box on the desk between them in the writing room and said *take it* — had not been a finished answer. The question Stephen was asking now was the question Possidius had not, in those ten weeks, finished answering.

*I do not know,* he said. *I think I will open it twice more in my life. I do not know when.*

*You do not have to tell me what is in it.*

*I will not tell you what is in it.*

*Good.*

*Stephen.*

*Yes.*

*I am sorry.*

*Do not be sorry. I have known for thirty years that you carry something I do not. I have not minded. I would not have wanted to carry the thing you carry. Some things in a life are better not to be carried by both. The carrying you have done was the carrying you were given. The not-being-told was the not-being-told I was given. Both have been ours.*

*Yes.*

*Drive on.*

*Stephen.*

*Yes.*

*You are driving.*

He was. He had not stopped. He clicked his tongue at the mule. The mule, which had not in fact slowed, did not change pace. The cart went on. Hippo went over the horizon behind them at the third hour of the morning and was gone.

---

*Carthage, the senior bishop's residence, the spring of 431 and after.*

They were given two cells in the senior bishop's residence at Carthage. The cells were on the eastern side of the building, on the second floor, with a small window each looking down on the herb garden the lay brothers tended. There was a door between the cells that did not lock. The door had been hung that way by some bishop or bishop's deputy at some point in the previous century who had thought the connection useful and had not foreseen the use to which Possidius and Stephen would, by the second week of their residence, put it.

In the first week they did not use the door. They were tired from the road and from the four nights they had spent in inn-stables on the way down and from the small ceremonies at every African town they had passed through in which the local clergy had wished to mark, in one way or another, that the bishop carrying the body of Augustine's life-work was passing through. Possidius slept the sleep of an exhausted old man. Stephen slept in the cell next door, on the other side of the door, on a narrow cot.

In the second week they began the work.

The senior bishop at Carthage, who was Capreolus and who had been senior bishop for two years and who had known Augustine by correspondence for nineteen, gave them a small room on the ground floor with a desk and two stools and a bookcase already half-filled with reference works that Capreolus had taken the trouble to assemble. The senior bishop did not, in their presence or out of it, mention either the chest in Possidius's cell or the door between the two cells. He had been senior bishop for two years. He had been a bishop for thirty. He understood the things a bishop did not mention.

Possidius set the chest at the foot of his cot. He did not open it.

The *Vita Augustini* began with a single sentence. *Aurelius Augustinus, Bishop of Hippo Regius, died on the twenty-eighth day of August, in the year of our Lord 430, in the seventy-sixth year of his age, and the fortieth year of his episcopate.* He had written that sentence in the night of the death; he had carried the sentence with him in his head down the road and through the inns and into the small room Capreolus had given them, and the sentence had been the place from which the whole of the rest of the work would unfold.

Stephen sat across the desk from him with his own stylus and his own wax tablet. Stephen could not write the work; he had not been with Augustine in the years before 397 and could not testify to the years before he had been there. But Stephen could remember, in the way only a man who had been in a particular room for thirty-three years could remember, the small specific things that the work needed: the wax that had been on the wick of a particular lamp on a particular night, the cadence of a particular sermon, the way Augustine had folded letters in his sixties differently from the way he had folded them in his fifties. Stephen would remember a thing while Possidius was writing about a different thing, and Stephen would say it, and Possidius would write it down on a separate sheet, and the sheet would, at the end of the morning's work, go into the pile from which Possidius would draw the small specific facts that the *Vita* required to be true.

They worked four hours each morning and three hours each afternoon, with the noon meal and the office between. The sentences accumulated. The first book was finished in the second month. The second was begun.

In the evenings they walked in Capreolus's garden. The garden was small. There was a pomegranate tree in one corner that had been bearing for forty years and had begun, this autumn, to bear less than it had the previous year; there were three rows of vines along the wall that the lay brothers were pruning for the spring; there was a bench under a fig tree that had been the bench Augustine had sat on in the year of his consecration. They walked for a quarter of an hour each evening before vespers. They did not, on these walks, speak about the work. They spoke about small things — the pomegranate, the weather, the names of the lay brothers, the news of which port had been taken and which had not. They were old men taking the air after a long day at a desk. To anyone watching from a window they would have looked like exactly that.

In the cells, in the evenings after compline, the door was open more often than not.

They had, in thirty years, never had the door open before. They had had a few brief encounters in particular places that the work had brought them both to — a guest cell at Calama on three or four occasions, a borrowed room in the basilica at Hippo twice, a small house at Tubursicu in the year Possidius had come down for a visitation and they had taken a meal together that had run late. They had not, in thirty years, had a nightly proximity. They had now, in their cells in Carthage, a nightly proximity. The door was open. Possidius slept on his cot. Stephen slept on his. They did not, particularly, share a bed; they were old men and the cots were narrow and the careful habits of thirty years did not, in their seventh decade, fall away. They slept in their separate cots in separate cells. The door between was open. Each could hear the other's breath. Each could hear, on a cold night, the other's brazier crackling. Each could speak across the threshold when one of them woke from a dream, and the other would answer, and the speaking and the answering would be the things they had not had in thirty years.

Capreolus did not, on his weekly visits to the small room with the desk, ever look at the door of the cells when he passed it in the corridor. He had been a bishop for thirty years. He understood doors.

The *Vita* reached the year of Augustine's consecration in the autumn of 432. Possidius wrote about the night Augustine had been called from his monastic cell to the basilica to be consecrated against his will. Stephen, who had not been at Hippo then but who had heard Augustine tell the story twice — once in the early years and once in the late — corrected a small detail. The detail was that Augustine had been in tears not in the basilica, as Augustine had told it, but in his own cell, before the deacons came to take him to the basilica. Augustine had not been able, in the public telling, to admit the tears. Stephen had heard the private version of the story from Augustine himself in the monastery garden in the year of the courier, when Augustine had been drinking watered wine and had been more truthful than he had usually allowed himself to be. Possidius wrote it down both ways. He used the public version in the *Vita*. He kept the private version in a separate sheet which he placed in the chest at the foot of his cot.

The chest, in the seven years they were at Carthage, accumulated forty-three such sheets. After Stephen's death it accumulated no further sheets, because the sheets had been the things Stephen had remembered, and there was no one else who remembered them, and Possidius's own memory was not the kind of memory that produced such sheets.

---

*Carthage, the second week of October 432.*

The fever came in the evening, after compline, when Stephen had been up at the desk later than usual on a passage about the Donatist suppression that Possidius had been finding difficult to write. Stephen had said, at the end of the evening: *I am cold. I am going to bed.* Possidius had not, on the evening, made anything of it. The cell was cold. October at Carthage was cold. They had banked the small braziers in both cells. Stephen had gone to his cot through the door.

In the night Possidius woke to the sound of Stephen's voice. The voice was not speaking words. It was the kind of murmuring sound a man made in a fever-dream when the fever had begun to take him into the country a fever takes a man into. Possidius rose. He went through the door.

Stephen was in his cot, the blanket pulled to his chin, his face damp with sweat. The fever was high. Possidius put the back of his hand on Stephen's forehead. The forehead was the temperature of a man whose fever had been working in him for an hour or two before it had woken him. Possidius had felt fevers like this on his own forehead and on his nieces' foreheads and on the foreheads of dying parishioners across forty years. He knew the temperature.

He went to wake the lay brother who slept in the corridor. The lay brother went for the physician. The physician came at the third hour of the night and looked at Stephen and said the things physicians said when they had nothing to do and did not wish to admit that they had nothing to do. He left the standard medicines. He said he would come back in the morning. He went.

Possidius sat with Stephen until dawn.

Stephen did not, in that night, become particularly lucid. He spoke once. He said: *Aurelius.* Possidius did not know whether the Aurelius was Augustine or whether it was an Aurelius from earlier in Stephen's life or whether it was no one at all and the word had only been a sound the dream had produced. He sat. He held Stephen's hand. The hand was the hand he had been holding, on and off, for thirty years, in particular rooms in particular houses on particular nights when the work had brought them both to the same place. The hand was thinner now than it had been at thirty.

The fever broke at the third hour of the morning. Stephen woke briefly, looked at Possidius, did not seem entirely to recognise him, and slept again. The sleeping this time was the sleeping of a man whose body had decided to rest. The fever stayed broken until the second hour of the afternoon, when it returned higher than it had been in the night.

It was the second-week-of-October fever that came up the African coast every autumn and that took, in the harbour wards of Carthage, between thirty and fifty old men a season. Stephen was sixty-three. He had been driving carts and carrying bodies and reading badly-trimmed lamps for fifty years. His body had been, for some time, the body of a man whose body had begun to decide that it had carried enough.

The fever stayed for three days. On the morning of the fourth day Stephen woke clear. He looked at Possidius. He said:

*Possidius.*

It was the first time he had used the name in three days.

*Stephen.*

*The fever has gone.*

*The fever has not gone. The fever is resting. The fever will come back.*

*I know.*

He closed his eyes. He opened them again.

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*I want to say a thing that has been on me to say.*

*Say it.*

He thought. The thinking was the slow thinking of a man who had been in a fever for three days and whose mind was working at the rate a fever permitted. After some time he said:

*The night of the courier. In 410. When I came to Augustine's cell with the news from the gate. I knew, on the morning after, what he had asked you to do. I did not know the substance of it. I knew he had asked. I knew you had not refused. I have, since that morning, been in the work without being in the work. I have carried by carrying you. I am going to die in this bed. I want you to know that the carrying-by-carrying-you was a carrying I am glad to have done.*

Possidius held his hand.

*Stephen.*

*Yes.*

*The carrying you did was a carrying. The fact that you did not know the substance does not, on the work's accounting, make the carrying smaller. You have carried. The carrying has been the carrying.*

*Yes.*

*I would not have been able to do the work without you. I would not have been able to do the years. The work was the work and I did the work. You were the years.*

Stephen closed his eyes. He opened them.

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*Aurelius knew.*

*Aurelius knew.*

*Good.*

He closed his eyes. The fever began to come back. Possidius could feel it in the hand under his hand. He did not let go. The fever rose through the afternoon. Stephen did not speak again. He died at the second hour after vespers on the fourth day, with Possidius holding the hand and the lay brother in the corridor outside the door reading the small office for the dying because there was no one else to read it.

Possidius sat with the body for some time. He did not weep. He had not, in thirty years, wept in front of Stephen, and he did not weep now in the room where the body was, because the body was still in some sense Stephen and Stephen had not been a man one wept in front of. He went out into the corridor. He told the lay brother that Stephen had died. He told the lay brother to find the senior bishop's deacon and to make the arrangements for the burial. He went back into the cell. He sat with the body. He did not weep there either.

He wept in his own cell, that night, after the body had been taken to the cathedral for the laying-out. He wept for an hour. He wept the way a man weeps when the thing he has been carrying for thirty years has been removed from his side and the side is now exposed in a way it has not been exposed since he was twenty-eight. He wept until the candle on his desk had burned down to the saucer. Then he stopped. He did not, in the years that followed, weep again in the way he wept that night.

The burial was the next morning. Stephen was buried in the western cemetery at Carthage in a grave that bore his name and the dates and no other inscription. Capreolus said the words. Possidius stood at the grave with Capreolus and the lay brother and three of the deacons. They filled the grave. They tamped. The tamping took until the seventh hour of the morning, as the tamping of graves had taken since the night of the courier in the burying-ground beyond the western wall of Hippo on a different morning twenty-two years before.

Possidius went back to the senior bishop's residence. He went up to his cell. He went through the door into Stephen's cell. He sat on Stephen's cot. He looked at the cot for some time. Then he stood up. He went back through the door to his own cell. He closed the door behind him. He did not, in the years that followed, open it again.

---

*Carthage, the eleventh day after the burial.*

The chest had been at the foot of his cot for twenty-six months. He had not opened it in that time. He had felt, more than once, the impulse to open it, and had decided, more than once, not to. The impulse had not been a strong impulse. The impulse had been a small periodic question — *is the disc still warm; was the warmth on the night of his death the carrying or the bronze* — that he had been content to leave unanswered.

Stephen had been buried for eleven days. The cell next door had been emptied of Stephen's small effects by the lay brothers; the cot was made up in the way an unused cot was made up, with a clean blanket folded at the foot. The door between the cells was closed. Possidius had gone in once, on the third day after the burial, to retrieve a copy of Cassian's *Conferences* that Stephen had been using and that Possidius now wanted on his desk. He had not sat on the cot that time. He had taken the codex and gone out. He had not been in Stephen's cell since.

He sat at his desk in his own cell at the second hour of the night on the eleventh day after the burial. He had finished the day's work on the *Vita*. He had eaten the small evening meal Capreolus's house provided for the residents who took their meals in private. He had read the office. He sat at the desk. The lamp was low.

He went to the chest at the foot of his cot. He knelt. The kneeling was harder than it had been a year before; his right knee, which had been bad for some years and which Augustine had spoken to him about late in their last summer with the kind of black amusement two old men with bad knees shared, was now bad in a different way. He got down on the knee. He opened the chest.

The bronze box was on top of the contents, where he had set it the morning he had left Hippo. He took it out. He took it to the desk. He sat. He set the box on the desk in front of him. He looked at it for a count of perhaps thirty.

He opened it.

The hinges gave their small specific sound. The disc was inside. He took it out. He held it in both hands.

The disc was cold.

It was the cold of bronze that had been in a closed box for twenty-six months in an unheated cell. It was the cold he had been expecting. It was not, he understood after holding the disc for a count of perhaps a hundred, a cold that meant anything in particular. It was the cold of bronze. He had carried the box for twenty-six months and the bronze had been the bronze, and the bronze in the box had been at the temperature of a closed box in a cool room, and that was all the bronze was.

He sat with the disc in his hand.

He thought of the night of Augustine's death. The disc had been warm then. He had thought, on that night, that the warmth had been the disc acknowledging the carrying. He had thought it because Augustine had told him, across nineteen years, that the disc warmed at moments. He had thought it because his hand had been on the disc within an hour of his hand having been on Augustine's cooling hand. He had thought it because his mind, on the night of a great death, had been the mind of a man who would attribute warmth to a thing because he needed the warmth to mean something.

He was not sure now what the warmth had meant.

He thought of Stephen. He thought of the thirty years. He thought of the not-telling. He thought of the morning of the cart and the conversation about the box that Stephen had begun and that he had ended with *I will not tell you what is in it.* He thought of Stephen in the fever in the cell next door, lucid for the last time, saying *the carrying-by-carrying-you was a carrying I am glad to have done.* He thought that he had been told, on that morning, that he could have told Stephen the substance and that Stephen would have carried it and that the carrying would not have been smaller for being shared. He thought that he had not told Stephen because the work had not, on his own private accounting, required the telling. He thought that the work had not, in fact, required the not-telling either. He thought that he had decided not to tell Stephen for reasons he had taken to be the work's reasons and that had been in the cell at Carthage with the cold bronze in his hand, his own reasons. He thought that Stephen had died not knowing what was in the box because Possidius had decided Stephen would die not knowing.

He sat with this.

He did not weep. He had wept once, on the night of Stephen's burial, and the weeping had taken what weeping he had. He sat at the desk with the disc in his hands and let the thing he had decided settle into the place where it would, for the rest of his life, be a thing he had decided.

He set the disc back in the box. The hinges sounded again. He closed the lid. He took the box back to the chest at the foot of the cot. He set it where it had been. He closed the chest.

He went back to the desk. He took up the bone stylus — Augustine's bone stylus, Monnica's bone stylus before that. He pulled the parchment of the *Vita* toward him. He was on the chapter about Augustine's years at Cassiciacum. He continued the chapter.

He worked until the lamp went out.

---

*Carthage, July 437.*

The *Vita Augustini* was finished in the autumn of 436. Capreolus's deacons made three fair copies in the winter. The fair copies went out in the spring of 437, by ship, to Rome and Constantinople and Lérins. The original remained at Carthage in the senior bishop's library, where it would, on the present accounting of Carthage's prospects, not remain for long.

The news about Catholic clergy and the Arian rite came in the early summer of 437, when the Vandal kingdom's officers in the inland dioceses began to require, of the African bishops, an expression of doctrinal flexibility. Capreolus refused. Several bishops in the smaller towns refused. The expulsions began.

Possidius received the formal notice from Capreolus's deacon in the second week of July. He read the notice. He said: *Yes. I will go.* He said it with the small absence of surprise of a man who had been preparing for this for a year. The deacon went out to make the arrangements.

He had three days before the ship.

He spent the three days packing what could be packed. The *Vita Augustini* was already in three copies in three cities. He took the original in his luggage. He took the chest with the bronze box and the bone stylus and the brown wool cloak — which he had been wearing since his ordination, twice re-darned at the shoulder — and the codex of Cassian's *Conferences* that Stephen had been using. He took Jerome's annotated Septuagint, which had been on the desk for seven years. He took a small leather satchel of personal letters, including Augustine's last note to him, which he had kept folded inside the back cover of the Septuagint since the morning of the death. He took the forty-three sheets of Stephen's remembered detail, which he had been keeping in the chest beside the box.

He went to the western cemetery on the morning of the third day. He stood at Stephen's grave for a quarter of an hour. He did not speak. He went back to the residence. He made his goodbyes to Capreolus and to the lay brothers and to the deacons. He took the cart to the harbour at the third hour of the afternoon. He boarded the ship at the fourth.

The ship left Carthage at sunset.

He stood at the stern for the count it took for the harbour gate to recede past the western breakwater. He watched the city go small in the distance, and he watched, just south of the city wall, the small line of cypress that marked the western cemetery, and he watched the cypress until he could no longer make out the cypress as a separate thing from the city wall behind it.

Then he went below to the cabin.

The chest was in the cabin. He sat with it. He did not open it.

---

*Lucania, in the small monastery in the hills, the years from 437 to 460.*

He landed at Naples in the autumn of 437. He went, by arrangement with the bishop of Naples, to a small monastery in the hills of Lucania, three days' journey south, that had been founded forty years earlier by a freedman of the senatorial class and that had a community of eleven brothers and a small library and a cell that had been kept ready for a refugee bishop from the African church. The cell had a desk and a cot and a window facing east. Possidius set the chest at the foot of the cot. He hung the brown wool cloak on the peg by the door.

He was sixty-nine.

He lived in the cell for twenty-three years.

The years did the work the years did. He continued, in the small way an aged refugee bishop continued, to correspond with the African church in such measure as the African church continued to be possible to correspond with. He read. He read the Septuagint and the *Vita* and the works of the desert fathers. He prayed. He kept the daily round. He sat in the small garden of the monastery in the afternoons in the years his knees would still take him to it, and in the years his knees would not, he sat at the window of the cell and watched the eastern hills.

He did not, in the twenty-three years, open the chest.

The brown wool cloak was re-darned a third time in 451 and a fourth time in 458 by an old lay brother who had been doing the mending for the community for forty-five years and who, when Possidius died, took the cloak from the peg and folded it and set it in the chest with the rest of the bishop's effects without remarking that the cloak had been a strange thing for a bishop to have worn for so long.

In the second week of June 452 a refugee priest came to the monastery from a small parish in the lower Po valley that had been overrun, in the previous month, by the army of Attila the Hun. The priest had walked south for thirty-one days. He had come down through Italy ahead of the Hun army's slow methodical sweep across the northern plain. He arrived at the monastery on a Tuesday afternoon at the third hour, with a cloth bag of his parish's sacred vessels strapped under his outer tunic, and he was given the guest cell next to Possidius's by the abbot, who had been receiving refugee priests from northern Italy at the rate of one a fortnight since the spring.

Possidius had the priest at the small refectory for the noon meal on the Wednesday. He listened to the priest's account of the road. The priest was forty-six. He had been a parish priest for fourteen years in the small valley north of the river. He had buried, in the past month, three of his deacons and seven of his lay readers and one elderly woman who had been the mother of the parish for thirty years. He had come south because his bishop, who had also been killed, had told him before the killing that the parish's vessels were to be carried south by whoever could carry them. The priest had carried them. The priest, in the small refectory at the noon meal of the Wednesday, set the cloth bag on the bench beside him and did not, in the conversation, refer to it.

Possidius listened. He listened in the slow careful way a man of eighty-four listened when a man of forty-six was telling him about a journey of thirty-one days on foot from a parish that had ceased to exist. He did not interrupt. He asked the small specific questions a senior bishop asked at intervals to confirm a fact or to fill a gap in the account. The questions did not require long answers. The priest gave the answers.

After some time the priest said:

*Father. You have heard the news of the bishop of Rome.*

*I have not. Tell me.*

*The bishop of Rome went out to meet Attila at the Mincio in the third week of May. He took with him only the senior priests of his cathedral and a small lay party from the senate. He met Attila at the river. He spoke with him for the better part of an afternoon. The terms of the conversation are not known outside the small party. The result of the conversation is that the Hun army has, at the second week of June, turned back. They are now retreating across the northern plain. They will, on the present mapping of the season, be over the Alps before September.*

Possidius set down the cup he had been holding.

*The bishop of Rome turned back the Hun army.*

*The bishop of Rome turned back the Hun army.*

*He went out alone.*

*He went out with the senior priests and the small party. He had no soldiers. The imperial army has been broken in the north for two months and there were no soldiers to send.*

*Yes.*

*Father.*

*Yes.*

*I do not, on my own theology, know how to read this.*

Possidius held the priest's look for a count.

*It will be read,* he said, *in the years to come, in some number of ways that I will not be alive to hear. It will be read by the men of the imperial chancery as a thing the bishop of Rome did because the imperial army could not. It will be read by the bishop of Rome's own people as a thing the bishop of Rome did because the office of the bishop of Rome required it. It will be read, in some other century, in a way I cannot guess. The reading is not, today, our reading to make. The fact is the fact. The bishop of Rome went out to the river. The Hun turned back.*

*Father.*

*Yes.*

*I have been thinking, on the road south, about the work on the city of God that the bishop of Hippo wrote in the year of his last decade.*

*Yes.*

*I have been thinking that the work named, in the eighteenth book if I remember the numbering, the prediction that the institution of the Church would, in the centuries after the empire failed, do what the empire could no longer do. The bishop of Hippo named the prediction. The bishop of Hippo did not, on the dating of the work, live to see whether the prediction held. The prediction held this spring at the Mincio. The bishop of Rome turned back the Hun army. The Church is doing what the empire cannot.*

Possidius did not answer immediately.

He sat with what the priest had said. The small refectory was the small refectory; the noon hour was the noon hour; the lay brother who tended the refectory was at the kitchen door with a clay pitcher and was waiting for the signal to refill the cups. Possidius lifted his hand a small degree to the lay brother. The lay brother went into the kitchen.

*Father,* Possidius said. *The bishop of Hippo was my teacher. He taught me, across the long years of my work with him, a thing that I have, in the long years of my work since his death, found that the man who taught it had not quite finished believing himself when he taught it. He taught me that the work the church did was the work the church did, and that the work would, in the centuries to come, hold or not hold on the merits of the work and not on the imperial power that was, at any particular moment, available to support the work. He taught me also that the institutions which the Church would, in the centuries to come, make of itself would, in those centuries, not be reliably the institutions the work required them to be. He taught me both things. He did not finish reconciling them.*

*Father.*

*Yes.*

*The Mincio was a reconciling.*

*The Mincio was a reconciling for one afternoon. The reconciling was the reconciling of one bishop and one barbarian on one river in the third week of May 452. The reconciling does not complete the question. The question continues. The question will continue to continue. The bishop of Hippo wrote the work knowing the question would continue. The work was written for the long continuing.*

The priest sat with this.

*Father,* he said. *I am glad to be at this monastery.*

*The monastery is glad to have you. You will stay for as long as the staying is the work. When the staying is no longer the work, you will go further. The chest of the parish vessels will go with you. You may, with the abbot's leave, keep them in the cell beside your cot. The chest at the foot of my own cot is also a chest from a parish that has ceased to exist. We will keep our chests in our cells together for whatever weeks the chests require.*

*Father.*

*Eat.*

The priest ate. The lay brother came out from the kitchen with the pitcher and refilled the cups. Possidius and the priest finished the meal in the slow conversation of two men who had been on different roads to the same noon refectory in the small monastery in the hills of Lucania in the second week of June 452. After the meal the priest went to the chapel for the office. Possidius went to his cell.

He sat on the cot.

He did not take down the chest at the foot of the cot. He had not taken down the chest in fifteen years. He had not, in those fifteen years, opened it. He sat with the chest at his feet and thought of Augustine. He thought of the long drafts of the eighteenth book of the City of God and of Augustine at the desk at Hippo in the years he had been writing it. He thought of Augustine's instruction to him in the writing room at Hippo in June 430, when Augustine had set the box on the desk between them and had said *take it.* He thought of the morning of the second hour after first prayers thirty-five days before that, when Possidius had ridden up from Calama for the second time in the spring and had walked into the writing room and had been told, in the small specific way Augustine had told him, that the time for the box to leave the upper shelf was now within the calendar year that was coming.

Augustine had not, on Possidius's reading of him at the desk in those weeks, fully expected the prediction in the eighteenth book to hold in the way the priest at the noon refectory was now reporting it had held. Augustine had written the prediction in faith. The faith had been the faith of a man who carried the work without expecting the work to vindicate itself in his lifetime. Augustine had not, in fact, lived to see it. Augustine had died in the bedroom at the bishop's residence at Hippo in August 430 with the Vandal siege engines on the western wall and the prediction unconfirmed.

The prediction was confirmed twenty-two years later at the Mincio.

Possidius sat with this.

He did not weep. He had not, in the years since Stephen, wept for any of the dead in the way he had wept for Stephen on the night of the burial. He sat. He held the cot's edge with one hand. The other hand rested on his knee. His knees were the knees of a man of eighty-four and were not, going to permit him many further years of the daily round; he had been measuring them at the level of attention an old man gave to his knees, and the measuring told him that the daily round had perhaps two or three years left in it before the cell would be the only round he could keep.

He went to the chapel for compline.

The chest at the foot of the cot stayed where it had been since 437.

Possidius died of an obstruction of the lung in the second week of August 460. He was ninety-two. He had been preparing for it for three years. In the spring of 457 he had asked the abbot of the small monastery, with whom he had a quiet friendship of fifteen years' standing, whether the abbot would consent to receive the chest at his death and to keep it until a particular man arrived. The abbot, who was a man of the kind of monastery that received refugee bishops and did not ask questions, had said yes. Possidius had said:

*The man will arrive. I do not know when. The man will be young. He will not, when he asks for the chest, know exactly what he is asking for. You will know him by the way he asks. When he asks, give him the chest. Tell him to carry it. Tell him not to open it until he has crossed water. Tell him that the man it is for will know it. Tell him that the man it is for will not be him.*

The abbot had said yes.

Possidius died in the small cell with the abbot at the bedside and the lay brother who had darned the cloak in the corner with a clay bowl of water and a small folded linen for the brow.

The chest was carried, after the burial, into the abbot's own cell, where the abbot kept it on the floor of his closet for thirty-four years.

In the spring of 494 a young man arrived at the monastery from a town in the hill country south of Beneventum. The young man was twenty-two. He had been raised by a priest who had been raised by a priest who had been a junior of Capreolus at Carthage in the years when Capreolus had been senior bishop. The young man asked at the gate to be received as a novice. The abbot, who was now eighty-three and had been waiting thirty-four years for this question to be asked in some form by some young man, looked at the young man at the gate and saw, in the young man's face, the thing the abbot had been told he would see and would know.

The abbot took the young man into his own cell. He took the chest from the closet. He set the chest on the table. He said:

*Carry it. The man it is for will know it. He will not be you.*

The young man took the chest. He looked at it for a count of perhaps three.

He said:

*Yes.*

He did not, in the years that followed, open it.
