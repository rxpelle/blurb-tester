# Chapter 11 — The Final Testament (v2, thriller)

*Hippo Regius, the bishop's residence, the twenty-fifth of August 430 CE.*

The bone stylus felt like iron in his fingers. Each letter carved into the wax required conscious effort now, as if the hand were translating thought through a layer of water. Augustine paused between words, letting his chest find its shallow rhythm. The fluid in the lungs had been thickening since the second week of August. Each breath had become work. Each breath would, in some not-very-distant week, become more work than was worth the doing.

He bent over the wax tablet on the wheeled desk Eraclius had brought up from the writing room and set beside the bed. The desk was a small one, the kind a clerk used for travelling. The wax tablet was the wax tablet he had been using since 396. The stylus was the bone one. Some things had not changed in thirty-four years.

He was on the last paragraph of the fourteenth book of the *Retractationes* — the long backward look at his own work, thirty-seven books corrected and qualified over four years, the project of a man who had decided that his errors deserved the same care as his arguments. The fourteenth book was the book on the sermons of his middle years and on the letters of his late years; he had been on the book since New Year and had not, until this morning, expected to reach its closing paragraph. The siege had given him the deadline he had not, in the slow review of his own oeuvre, given himself.

The siege. He could hear it from the bed. The Vandal siege engines were at the western walls of the city — three days now of the slow regular thumping of stone against stone, the way a man knocks at a door he is going to come through whether the door opens or not. The walls had not yet given way. The walls would give way in some month not yet his. He would not be at the desk on the morning the walls gave way. He would, on the present accounting of his lungs, not be alive to hear the sound.

He set down the stylus. He coughed. The coughing took longer than the coughing this morning had taken. The linen square took its share. Possidius, who had been sitting in the chair by the window, came to the bed and took the linen and replaced it with a clean one from the small stack Eraclius had been keeping ready since the spring. Possidius set the soiled linen in a clay jar at the foot of the bed and sat back down without speaking.

Augustine took up the stylus.

He wrote the closing paragraph of the fourteenth book. The paragraph was four lines. The paragraph was the kind of paragraph an old man wrote when an old man had been in the slow patient work of revising his own life's writing for four years and had finally reached the place where the work could end. The paragraph said, in essence: I have written what I have written; I have qualified what I have qualified; I have retracted what I have retracted; the rest is not for me to say. He read it through once. He did not change a word. He set the stylus down.

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*The fourteenth book of the *Retractationes* is finished. Eraclius will have it copied.*

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*Will you write more.*

He thought.

*No. Not on the wax. Three letters by dictation if you will sit and take them. After that, no.*

Possidius drew the chair to the desk. He set out a fresh piece of clean parchment and the iron pen. He did not speak. He had been waiting, Augustine recognised with a small inward smile, for the bishop to say *no more* with the same patience the bishop himself had used, in his fifties, when he had been waiting for one or another stubborn deacon to finish a piece of work the deacon was reluctant to put down.

*The first. To Melania the Younger at Jerusalem.*

*To Melania the Younger,* Possidius wrote at the head of the parchment.

*Daughter. I am writing on the morning I have decided to be the last morning I write. The pneumonia is now the pneumonia it has been moving toward for a year and the breaths I have left are not many. I will not, on present likelihood, see the autumn. The arrangements we made hold. Possidius has the keeping. The keeping is not broken. I have nothing to add to what we agreed. I write only to say what I did not say at the gate, and have not said in any of the nineteen years of letters since: that the gate at Hippo on a wet evening in November 411 was, in the long count of my life, one of the days that did not pass the way other days passed; that knowing you did the work alongside me did not make the work easier to release. With my blessing. Aurelius.*

Possidius wrote it at the slow pace Augustine's voice required. When it was done he held the parchment to the light to make sure the ink was set, then folded it along the standard fold and sealed it with Augustine's signet, which Augustine had given him to use in the spring. He set the letter in the tray for the morning's couriers.

*The second. To the senior bishop at Carthage.*

This letter was shorter. It was an instruction about the bishop's chair at Hippo after Augustine's death — Eraclius to be considered for the succession in the formal way, the diocese to be left in his hands during the interim, the works of charity and the daily round to continue under whatever conditions the Vandals permitted. The letter was the kind of letter a senior bishop writes when he knows a senior bishop is going to read it: no rhetoric, no elaboration, the practical things named in their order. Possidius wrote it. Augustine sealed it himself this time, holding the wax over the lamp with a hand that, for the act of pressing the seal, was steadier than it had been all morning.

*The third.*

*Yes.*

He thought. He had been thinking about the third letter since the previous night. He had been turning, in the slow attention his fever permitted, the question of whether the third letter was a letter that should be written.

He decided.

*Take this. To my dear Eraclius, priest at Hippo. Eraclius. You have been at this house for nineteen years. You have been doing the work of the bishop in everything but title for the last two. I am going to die in this bed in some week not far from now and after I am dead the diocese will be yours to manage under whatever conditions the Vandal commander permits. I am writing to tell you what I have not, in nineteen years, said to you in plain words. You are a better administrator than I have ever been. You have kept this house and this diocese running on a discipline I would not have had the patience for. The brothers love you. The lay brothers respect you. The matrons of the city trust you with their daughters' confessions in a way that they have never, on the evidence of what the matrons themselves have told me, trusted me. The diocese is in good hands. You are the good hands. Do not, in the years to come, allow the absence of one old bishop's voice to make you doubt yourself. The voice was the voice of a man who depended on you for nineteen years. Aurelius.*

Possidius wrote it. He held the parchment to the light. He folded it. He sealed it. He set it in the tray with the others.

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*The wax is gone from me. Take the desk away.*

Possidius rose. He took the desk from beside the bed and carried it to the corner of the room and set it against the wall. He came back. He stood by the bed.

*Aurelius. Will I send Eraclius up.*

*No. Let him have the morning. He will hear about the letter when the letter is on the corner of his own desk, which is the right place for him to hear it. I do not want him in the room while he reads it. He will not want me in the room either.*

*Yes.*

*Sit down. Do not say more for now. I will sleep an hour.*

Possidius sat back in the chair by the window. Augustine closed his eyes. He listened, with his eyes closed, to the slow regular thumping of the siege engines on the western wall and to the quieter sound of his own breath in his own chest. He thought, very briefly, that the two sounds were not very different from each other in their rhythm and in their inevitability and in the slow methodical way they were, each in its own register, doing the work of bringing a thing down.

He slept.

---

*Hippo Regius, the bishop's residence, the hour before dawn of the twenty-seventh of August.*

The household had been keeping watch in shifts since the previous Monday. Possidius slept from midnight to the hour before dawn. The household deacon, a Libyan named Januarius, slept from dusk to midnight. Helena, who had come up from the lower monastery in the spring when the cough had begun to take hold, slept whenever sleep took her, which was seldom.

She was sitting by the bed when Augustine woke, because the cycle had turned to her watch.

The room was cold. It had been cold for two nights — the late African summer had broken without warning in the way it sometimes did, and a wet wind had come in off the sea. The household had banked the small brazier in the corner, but Augustine's hands were cold anyway. His hands had been cold since Tuesday.

*Helena.*

*Bishop.*

*I would like some water.*

She brought water in a clay cup and held it to his lips. He drank less than she had brought. She set the cup on the small table at the bedside and waited, which was the thing Helena had learned to do better than any of the others. To wait without fidgeting. To wait without feeling she needed to fill the silence with care. The waiting was the care.

She was thirty-one. She had come up from the lower monastery as a junior member of the household; she had been with them four years. Augustine had begun by giving her the routine work of the bishop's household — the small letters, the receipts, the management of the lay sisters who came in from the city for the relief work. She had managed all of it without complaint and with a quiet attention that he had begun, in the second year, to take note of. He had begun to teach her the deeper part of the work in the third year. By this spring she had been at the wax under-drafts of the eleventh book of the *Retractationes* with him at the desk for two afternoons a week. She would, if the Vandals let her live, be one of the two or three who carried the work in the Numidian region for the next forty years. Possidius knew this. Helena did not, on Augustine's reading of her, fully know it yet. She would know it when the time came.

*Helena.*

*Yes, Bishop.*

*I want to ask you a question I do not want you to answer quickly.*

*Yes.*

*When you are asked, later, what I was like in this week, what will you say.*

She was quiet for a long count. Outside, somewhere past the cathedral's walls, a Vandal horn sounded thinly and another horn answered it from further along the perimeter. The siege was preparing for its morning.

*That you were afraid of only one thing,* she said at last.

*What thing.*

*That the work would not have been enough.*

He opened his eyes. She was not looking at him. She was looking at the lamp on the table, where the wick had burned down to the oil and was starting to smoke a little. She reached over and trimmed it with a small iron pin and the smoke cleared and she set the pin down and folded her hands.

*That is correct.*

She did not reply. She looked again at the lamp.

He let his head settle back on the pillow. He could feel his pulse in his temples, in the soft flesh under his jaw, in the thin places where the skin had started to go papery over the bone. He was seventy-five. He had begun to notice, in the last year, that he was the age his mother had been when she had died at Ostia, plus nineteen years; that he had been an old man for longer than his mother had been an old woman; that the long extra years had been, on every accounting he could give of them, the work years.

He looked at Helena.

*Helena.*

*Yes.*

*You will be at the lower monastery when the Vandals come into the city.*

*Yes, Bishop. I have agreed it with Sister Sophia.*

*The lower monastery will not be safe.*

*The lower monastery will be less unsafe than the bishop's residence. The Vandals do not, on the reports we have, take an interest in lay sisters. They will take the bishop's residence and the basilica and they will leave the lower monastery for last. By the time they reach it I will be at Tagaste with Sophia.*

*You have arranged it.*

*I have arranged it.*

*Good.*

*Bishop.*

*Yes.*

*I would not, if you have the strength, want this conversation to be the last conversation we have.*

He thought. He was not, on the morning, sure he had the strength for another. He thought of saying so. He thought of letting the moment be the moment. He thought of Helena, thirty-one, who had been keeping watch by his bed for an hour in the cold, who would, in some week not many years from now, be the one a younger sister came to with a question about a passage of his work. He thought of what she would, in that future moment, have to draw on.

*We will have another conversation. Not today. Tomorrow, if I am awake, in some quarter-hour when the room is quiet. I owe you one piece of the work I have not given you. I will give it tomorrow.*

*Yes, Bishop.*

*Now trim the lamp again. It has begun to smoke.*

She trimmed it. The lamp burned even. She sat back in the chair. She did not, after that, speak for a long time, and he did not ask her to. He listened to her breath in the chair and to his own breath in the bed. The two breaths kept different rhythms. Hers was the slow even breath of a woman in her early thirties at rest. His was the shallow rapid breath of an old man whose lungs had been filling for a year. The rhythm of his breath was, on the morning, slower than it had been at evening prayers, which was not a good sign.

The hour before dawn went past. The lamp burned down. Helena trimmed it.

When Possidius came to relieve her at the changing of the watch, Augustine was neither asleep nor quite awake. Helena left the room without speaking. She would come back in the afternoon for a different watch. The afternoon watch would be the one in which the conversation he had promised her would have to happen. He thought he could last to the afternoon. He thought he could, with attention, last most of one further day. He was less sure of the day after.

---

*Hippo Regius, the bishop's residence, the afternoon of the twenty-eighth of August.*

Consciousness came and went like tide.

When lucid he found himself propped against pillows in the narrow bed, with Possidius maintaining vigil in the chair beside the window. Jerome's annotated Septuagint lay open on the corner of the desk where the morning light could still reach it. He had asked, at the beginning of the week, that the Septuagint be brought to the bedroom. It was on the desk now in the position he had asked for it to be put.

When less lucid he was somewhere else. He was at Cassiciacum in 386 with Alypius and with the boy Adeodatus and with his mother, in the small house on the lake, with a lamp on a small table, reading aloud from the *Soliloquies* he had been writing that autumn. He was at Ostia in 387 with his mother in the rented room, with the small lamp, holding the cup of watered wine to her lips in the last hour. He was at the rail of the basilica at Hippo in October 411 looking at Pelagius across a distance of three feet, knowing, at the end of three hours of conversation, what he was going to do. He was on the bench in Monnica's garden in 426 with the parchment of the closing paragraph of the twenty-second book on his knee. The places came and went. He did not, in the moments of lower lucidity, mind the places coming and going. They were continuing to be the places.

He came back from one of the goings-away. Possidius was looking at him from the chair. The afternoon light was on the desk and on Jerome's Septuagint and on the wall behind the bed.

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*Include the mistakes.*

Possidius leaned forward.

*Bishop.*

*In the biography. Include the mistakes.* Each word required intention now, breath parcelled out carefully. *I was wrong about Pelagius's books in the way I read them in the year of the synod — Pelagius said a man could choose good without God's prior help, and I said he was wrong, and the way I said it has done damage I did not intend. I was wrong about the Donatist confiscations, which I countersigned for two and a half years and have continued to be wrong about across the rest of my life. I was wrong, in my sixties, about whether grace eliminates choice or perfects it; I have argued both positions in writing and the arguing has not, on my present reading, been settled. Include all of it. Truth needs the wrong to remain true.*

*Aurelius.*

*Possidius.*

*I will include it.*

*Good.*

He rested. The breath went in and out. After some time he came back.

*The pear tree.*

Possidius leaned forward again.

*The pear tree. When you write the life. Include the pear tree. I know the pear-tree story is embarrassing. I have been embarrassed by it for fifty years. Do not remove its embarrassment. The embarrassment is the point.*

*I will include it.*

*Thank you.*

His breathing grew shallower. Possidius leaned closer.

*The third thing,* Augustine said.

Possidius waited.

*I do not remember.*

*Bishop.*

*I had three things. I remember only two. The pear tree. The mistakes. The third thing is gone. I am sorry. It was probably important.*

*It will come back to you, Bishop. Or it will not. Either way is acceptable.*

*Yes. Either way is acceptable.*

He let his head settle. He thought, very briefly, that he had remembered the third thing at the moment of saying he had not remembered it, and had then lost it again. It was permitted, in a man of his age in his last hour, to lose a piece of a sentence.

The afternoon light shifted. He thought of his mother. Forty-three years ago at Ostia. The rented room. The small lamp. The clay cup of watered wine he had held to her lips in the last hour. She had been fifty-six. He had been thirty-three. She had pressed the bronze disc into his palm — the disc that had now been in Possidius's traveling chest at the foot of the bed for two months, since he had handed it over in the writing room in June — and she had said *now the work begins,* and then she had died.

He had sat with her body for an hour before calling the servants. He had not wanted her first hour dead to be managed by anyone who did not know what she had been.

He understood, lying in the bed in the afternoon light forty-three years on, that he had not understood the hour at Ostia in the way he had thought he was understanding it. He had thought, at thirty-three, that it was an ending. It had been the other thing. It had been a middle. It had been a handoff on a road that neither of them had built and neither of them would finish. In some not-very-distant quarter of an hour or hour or three hours, Possidius would be the next middle on the same road.

The word that came into his mind was *continuous.*

Not a thing that ended. Not a thing that began. A continuous thing.

*Possidius.*

*Bishop.*

*The fair copies.*

*All five at their destinations as of the spring. Carthage, Constantinople, Lérins, Rome, Jerusalem. Sophia at Tagaste has the working copy and the wax under-drafts. The Confessions has been at twenty-three monastic libraries since 416. The corpus is distributed.*

*Good.*

His breath caught. He rode it out. The light had shifted again. The sun was lower. The basilica's bells would have rung for vespers by now if anyone had been left in the cathedral to ring them. No one was. The priest whose job it had been had fled the cathedral two days ago and no one had assigned the task to another, and it was late enough in the siege now that no one was going to.

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*The conversation I owed Helena.*

*She is in the corridor.*

*Bring her.*

Possidius rose and went to the door. He came back with Helena. She came to the bed and stood at the side opposite Possidius, with her hands folded in front of her in the manner she had learned in the lower monastery as a junior.

*Helena.*

*Bishop.*

*I owed you a piece. I will give it to you now and then I will rest. The piece is this. The work you will do in the next forty years, after I am dead and after the diocese has fallen and after the lower monastery has gone wherever it goes, is the work that is the work. It is not a smaller piece of the work that I and Possidius and Melania did. It is the same work. It is not a continuation of an earlier work. It is the work itself. The work has no centre and it has no founders. The men and women who have happened to be in particular places in particular years have done what those places and years required, and the doing has been the doing, and the doing has not been larger or smaller for being done by them or by the people who came before them. When you are doing the work in 470, in some monastery in Numidia under whatever king is then sitting at Carthage, you will be doing the work the way it has always been done. There is no special weight in being one of the named ones. There is no lesser weight in being one of the unnamed ones. The work is the work. Do you understand.*

*I understand, Bishop.*

*Tell me what I just said.*

She thought.

*There is no centre. There is no founder. The work is what whoever is in the place at the time does. Names do not matter. I will be doing the work the way it has always been done, when I am doing it.*

*Good. That is the piece.*

*Yes, Bishop.*

*Now go. I have a small further thing for Possidius and then I will rest.*

She bowed. She did not weep. She went out. The door closed behind her quietly.

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*Thank you for learning quickly. Thank you for staying when staying became dangerous.*

*Thank you for trusting me with this.*

*I trust the work. You happen to be carrying it at the moment.*

The light from the window was beginning to slant toward evening. Augustine could feel his breathing growing shallower. Each cycle was requiring more conscious effort. The pneumonia was winning the patient battle it had been winning for months.

*The two cities may not end in judgment.* He had argued, in the twenty-two books, that history moved toward resolution — the City of God prevailing over the earthly city, the conflict ending in divine verdict. He was not, on the afternoon, certain that was true. *They may continue as a permanent dialectic.*

*Bishop, that is heresy.*

*That is realism.*

For a time there was no speech. Possidius held the hand. The breath came and went at a rhythm that had nothing to do with any will Augustine was still exercising. His body had taken over the work of dying, which was properly its work anyway, and Augustine's part was only to not interfere.

*Possidius.*

*Bishop.*

*I have remembered the third thing.*

*Yes.*

*The third thing was: do not let the biography make me into a man who knew what he was doing. I did not know what I was doing. I made the decisions a man of my temperament made under the conditions that obtained. Some of the decisions were correct. Some of them were not. The biography should make that clear. The man it should describe is the man who did the work without knowing whether the work would hold. That is the man. Do not give me, in the biography, a wisdom I did not have.*

*I will not.*

*Good.*

He closed his eyes. He could feel the sun on the wall. He could feel Possidius's hand on his hand. He could feel the bronze in the chest at the foot of the bed in the way he had felt the bronze across the cloister and across the parlour on the night Melania had come to the gate nineteen years before. He could feel, very faintly, his mother's hand on his head from a moment in his childhood that he had not, in fifty years, called back. He could feel his own breath going slower. He could feel his own pulse going slower. The two slownesses were going at the same rate.

He thought: *the work continues.*

He did not say it. He had said enough. The hand holding Possidius's grew still. Outside, the Vandal siege engines tested Hippo's walls, but the sound seemed to come from a great distance. Augustine of Hippo died as the sun touched the western windowsill and the light on the wall went from the colour of African earth to the colour of the dust that earth becomes when it has dried in the shade for a long time.

---

*Hippo Regius, the bishop's residence, the night of the twenty-eighth of August into the first of September.*

Possidius held the cooling hand until the household began to gather. Helena arrived first; her face was drawn but she did not weep in the room, because she had been told not to weep in the room and because she had been given a piece of work to do and because the doing was the doing. Januarius came in. The two lay sisters from the lower monastery came up. By the second hour after sunset the body had been washed and laid out in the episcopal vestments on the narrow bed, with the candles at the head and the foot, and the brothers from the cathedral had begun to come up in twos and threes to keep the vigil through the night.

By midnight Possidius was alone in the study with the body.

The city was restless beyond the walls. Vandal campfires dotted the darkness; the siege engines had stopped at sunset and would resume at first light; the men on the walls of Hippo were doing whatever the men on the walls of a besieged city did at night. The bishop's residence was quiet. The brothers from the cathedral had gone back to their quarters at the change of the watch.

He went to the foot of the bed.

The traveling chest he had been keeping in the corner of his cell since June had been moved up to the study at the beginning of the week, when it had become clear that Augustine would not, after his death, want to be more than one wall away from it. The chest was at the foot of the bed. Possidius opened it.

The bronze box was on the top of the contents, where he had set it when Augustine had given it to him in the writing room ten weeks before. He took it out. He set it on the desk where Jerome's Septuagint was open to the page of the long marginal note in the larger less-steady hand.

He did not, for some minutes, open the box.

He had been carrying the box in his cell for ten weeks. He had not, in those weeks, opened it. He had been waiting for a moment that would be the moment to open it, and the moment had not come, and the moment had not come, and the moment had not come. The moment, he understood now, had been waiting for tonight. The moment had been waiting for the body in the bed and for the candles at the head and the foot and for the desk with Jerome's Septuagint open on it and for the city outside the walls quiet at the hour after midnight under the campfires of the men who would, in some week not many weeks from now, be in this room.

He opened the box.

The hinges gave their small specific sound — the sound he had heard for the first time on the day Augustine had taken the box down from the upper shelf in June and had heard a second time on the evening Augustine had asked for the chest to be moved to the study, and was now hearing for the third time. He had been told, in 411, that the sound carried two nights — that the next keeper would hear it in sleep before the box was in his hands. He understood, holding the box now, that the sound carried more nights than that. The sound was the sound of every time the box had been opened in the forty-three years it had been in Augustine's keeping, and was the sound of every time it would be opened in the years it would be in his keeping, and was the sound of every time it had been opened in whatever long centuries of carrying had brought it from the priest Simplicianus in Milan to the bedroom in Hippo. The sound was all the openings. The sound was the carrying.

He took out the disc.

The disc was warm.

He held it in both hands. It was warm in the way the box on a high shelf in a cool room had no business being warm, and it was warm in a way that was new — not the way Augustine had described the warmth on the three or four occasions Augustine had described it, but a different warmth, a steadier one, a warmth that did not seem to be coming or going but to be settled, that moved into the palms and stayed there. He sat with it on the desk in front of him for some time. He did not look for a sign in it. He did not, on Augustine's instruction, expect a sign. The sign, if there was to be one, would be a sign for someone in some other century. The warmth tonight was, on his understanding, only the disc acknowledging that the carrying had passed to the next pair of hands. The carrying was the sign.

He set the disc back in the box. The hinges sounded again. He closed the lid. He returned the box to the traveling chest. He closed the chest.

He took out a fresh sheet of parchment from the desk drawer. He took up the bone stylus — Augustine's stylus, the one Monnica had bought him in Carthage when he was a student, which was now, by Augustine's instruction in the second letter of the morning of the twenty-fifth, his stylus. He set the stylus to the parchment.

He wrote, at the head of the page, in the small clean hand he had been writing in for thirty-five years:

*Vita Augustini Hipponensis Episcopi.*

He sat with the title for the count of three. Then he wrote:

*Aurelius Augustinus, Bishop of Hippo Regius, died on the twenty-eighth day of August, in the year of our Lord 430, in the seventy-sixth year of his age, and the fortieth year of his episcopate. The city of Hippo was at that hour under siege by the Vandal army of Gaiseric, which had crossed from Hispania the year before and had been at the walls of Hippo since the early summer. The bishop departed this life in the bedroom of the episcopal residence, with this writer at his side and with the deacons and lay sisters of the household in vigil at the door. He had completed, three days before his death, the closing book of a long work of revision of his own writings; he had dictated three letters on the morning of the same day; he had not, on the days that followed, written any further word.*

He read the paragraph through. He did not change anything in it. He set the stylus down.

He worked through the remaining hours of the night, taking the biography forward in the slow careful sentences that the work he had spent the last twenty years learning would require. He wrote about Augustine's birth at Tagaste in 354. He wrote about Monnica. He wrote about the schools at Madauros and Carthage. He wrote about Rome and Milan and the garden. He included the pear tree. He wrote it the way Augustine had asked it to be written, with the embarrassment intact and not softened.

He wrote until the first light came through the window and the candles at the head and foot of the bed had begun, in the new light, to look like what they were — small flames in the daylight that no longer needed them.

By dawn the body was being prepared for transport to the basilica. The funeral would be in the second hour after the dawn. The city was waking to the renewed thumping of the Vandal siege engines on the western wall. Helena was in the kitchen with the two lay sisters from the lower monastery, organising the bread and water for the people who would come up to the residence after the funeral. Eraclius was in the cathedral with the brothers, preparing the basilica for what would be the last episcopal funeral the basilica of Hippo would see under a Catholic bishop for some long centuries.

Possidius set the stylus down. The first three pages of the *Vita* were on the desk in front of him. He covered them with a small piece of linen. He stood. His back was bad from the long hours at the desk; he stretched it once, the way Augustine had stretched his back on rising for thirty years, and he caught himself in the gesture and stopped, and then he went on with the gesture, because it was the gesture and it was permitted.

He went to the traveling chest. He took out the bronze box. He wrapped it in the silk cloth Augustine had been keeping for it in the cell drawer for nineteen years. He put the wrapped box into the chest under his personal effects, where any inspection would see only a bishop's portable archive and nothing of any other kind.

He closed the chest.

He sat with it for the count of three. Then he stood, took the chest by the handle, and walked out of the study to begin the morning, which would, when the funeral was finished and the basilica was sealed for the brothers' protection and the last farewells were said, end with him on the road to Carthage with the chest in the cart, on a journey whose first stage would take him east through the lines and whose last stage would take him into the rest of his life.

The body of Augustine of Hippo lay in state in the basilica from the second hour to the eleventh hour of that day.

The bronze went south in the cart with Possidius the next morning.

The five fair copies of *De Civitate Dei*, which had been at their destinations in Carthage and Constantinople and Lérins and Rome and Jerusalem since the year of completion, were already, on the morning Possidius left Hippo, beginning the long copying that would carry them through the next thousand years.

The work continued.
