# Chapter — The Basilica (v2, thriller)

*Hippo Regius, the basilica of the African church, the morning of the third Sunday of Lent 391 CE.*

He had come to Hippo for a man.

The man was a freedman of Tagaste who had been making, the better part of a year, the small slow approaches of a man thinking about leaving the world for the small monastic house Augustine had founded on the family property at Tagaste in the year after his return from Italy. The freedman had decided. He had asked Augustine to come to him at Hippo before he made the move; he had business at Hippo of a personal kind he did not want to take to Tagaste with him. Augustine had agreed. He had ridden up the previous week with a small mule and a single lay brother and had been at Hippo nine days. The business had concluded on the Friday. The freedman would return to Tagaste with him at the start of the next week.

This morning was Sunday. The third Sunday of Lent.

He had come to the basilica for the mass at the second hour with the freedman and the lay brother. He had taken his place along the eastern side aisle, where men of his disposition who were not clergy of the diocese took their places when they were visitors at the cathedral. The basilica was the kind of basilica a small coastal diocese built for itself in the last hundred years — three aisles, the centre wide, the side aisles narrow, the high windows up the south wall throwing a slanted morning light onto the central pavement that broke at the fourth column from the entrance into bars of lit dust. The smell was the smell of beeswax and the new-lit incense at the altar and the wool of two hundred tunics warmed by two hundred bodies.

He had marked, in the small habitual marking any monastic visitor performed, that the bishop's chair was occupied by an old man in white whose face he did not, from the eastern side aisle at this hour, recognise.

The bishop of Hippo was Valerius. Greek by birth, nine years in the chair, his Latin the Latin of a man who had learned it in his thirties. The freedman had said of him, on the road from Tagaste, that he was a man of small public ambition and large private prayer who would not, in the next decade, draw attention from either the Donatists in the next valley or the imperial administration at Carthage. The freedman had said it kindly. The freedman had been a member of this congregation for the previous year.

The mass began.

The Lenten readings were the readings proper to the third Sunday. The deacons brought the elements forward at the offertory. Valerius pronounced the prayers in the slow careful Latin of a Greek, and the African congregation said the responses in the African Latin that was their own.

At the place in the mass at which the bishop preached, Valerius rose from the chair and came to the rail. He stood at the rail with his hands on it in the way an old man stands when his back is bad. He looked at the congregation — slowly, from one side aisle to the other, no part of the look resting on Augustine or anyone else in particular. He said, in the slow Latin:

*Brethren of the Hippo church. The diocese has need of a priest. I have asked the Lord, in the prayers of the previous month, for the diocese to be given a priest. The diocese is small. It has not had a man of the standard the work requires for some years. I have asked. I have not, in the prayers, received the answer I have been asking for. I will continue to ask.*

He paused. He held the rail.

*I am old. I am sixty-six. I have been at Hippo for nine years. The diocese requires a priest who will, in the years to come, take the work the diocese will require taking. The man does not, on present knowledge of my own, sit in this congregation. The man may yet be sent. The Lord sends the men he sends.*

He pronounced the closing of the homily. He turned to go back to the chair.

A man in the western side aisle stood up.

Augustine, on the eastern side aisle, did not at first know who the man was. The man was in the dress of a layman. He stood for a count, looking across the nave, finding what he was looking for. Then Augustine recognised him: a deacon of the basilica named Tigisius, whom he had met in the courtyard on the third day of his visit and had taken bread with on the Wednesday. Tigisius was looking at him.

Tigisius raised his arm.

He pointed.

He pointed across the nave at Augustine.

*Brethren of the Hippo church,* Tigisius said, in the loud voice of a deacon at the rail. *The man on the eastern side aisle in the dark tunic, with the lay brother of his household at his side, is Aurelius Augustinus of Tagaste. He has been three months in Africa from his return from Milan. He has founded the small monastic house at Tagaste. He has been a presbyter of nothing and a deacon of nothing and is, on the report of the men who have written to me at Hippo since his return, the man the Lord has sent us in this present month. I name him to the bishop and to the brethren. I name him for the priesthood at Hippo. He is here in this nave at this hour. The Lord has put him at the rail.*

Augustine did not move.

His feet had stopped knowing what they were. The freedman beside him had gone very still — Augustine felt the stillness through the half-inch of air between their shoulders before he felt anything in himself. Then he heard it: the sound a hundred and forty people make when they all turn at once. Tunics rustling, sandals shifting on the pavement, breath. The whole nave on its feet. The whole eastern side aisle on its feet around him. He had stood at some point. He did not remember standing.

The congregation was looking at him.

Tigisius was crossing the nave. He came down the western aisle, around the back of the central rows, up the eastern aisle. He took Augustine by the upper arm. The grip was firm and steady and did not pull. Augustine would, in the years to come, remember the deacon's restraint with a particular respect. The hand on his arm was the hand of a man who had been told, by some discernment Augustine could not in the moment account for, that the man in the side aisle would come if held and would not require pulling.

The men of the eastern side aisle were touching him now. Hands on his shoulders, his back, the small of his back, his other arm. Not pushing, not yet. Holding. The men of the western side aisle were coming across. The whole congregation was moving in the slow methodical way a congregation moves when the congregation has collectively decided that a particular thing is about to be done and that the doing is the doing.

They moved him forward. Through the side-aisle press, into the central aisle, up the central aisle toward the rail. The light from the high windows broke across his face once, twice, three times as he passed each column. The smell of warm wool was thick around him now and under it the colder smell of incense from the altar. His own breath was somewhere in the upper part of his chest where it was no use to him.

He did not resist.

There was nothing in his body in the moment that could have resisted. He could note, in the small still corner of his attention, that he had thought all week about what he would say to the freedman about the small final decisions of the freedman's life, and had not, in any of the thinking, thought one moment about the possibility that he would be the man at this rail at this hour.

He was at the rail.

Valerius was looking at him from the bishop's chair.

Tigisius was beside him with the hand still on his arm. The hundred and forty Christians were behind him in the nave, on their feet, watching. Someone two rows back was breathing through his mouth. A child somewhere said something quietly and was hushed.

Valerius said, in the slow Latin:

*Aurelius Augustinus.*

*Father.*

*The brethren have brought you to the rail.*

*They have.*

*The brethren have named you for the priesthood of the diocese of Hippo.*

*They have.*

*Do you accept the call.*

He held the bishop's look for a count.

His mother came up in him.

She came up the way a person comes up in another person — not as an image, not as a memory, not as a thought, but as a presence in his ribs and behind his face. He was at Ostia in the rented room on the morning of the seventeenth of November 387 with the disc warm in his right hand and her hand in his left and her voice in the room saying *the carrying is the work.* He was here at the rail in the basilica at Hippo with the disc at Tagaste at the bottom of the writing chest and the bishop of a small diocese in front of him and a hundred and forty Christians at his back, and his mother was in his ribs as if she had been in his ribs all morning.

He understood, at the rail, with his mother in his ribs, that she had known this hour was coming. Not this hour exactly. Some hour. She had been watching him for thirty-three years for an hour she had known would have this shape. He had, in three years and four months, thought of the carrying as a small private thing in a box. He understood now that it had never been only that.

He looked at Valerius. He saw, for the first time properly, the bishop's face — the long Greek nose, the loose skin under the eyes, the patience of a man who had learned this morning that the Lord had answered the prayer in the way the Lord answered prayers, which was the way that left the man asking the prayer with more work to do, not less.

He said:

*Father. I accept.*

The basilica did not, in the count after he said it, make a sound. A hundred and forty people stood without sound. Valerius held his look for the count. Tigisius, beside him, took his hand off his arm.

Valerius said:

*Then come up.*

He went up. Valerius rose from the chair. The two deacons came forward from the choir with the small linen and the bowl of holy oil and the censer. Valerius laid his hands on Augustine's head. The hands were dry and lighter than Augustine had expected and rested on his hair without weight. The formula came in the slow Greek-accented Latin. The congregation said the responses. The censer was brought; the oil was brought; the oil was cool on his forehead and smelled of olive and of something resinous underneath. By the count of the third candle in the procession, Augustine was a priest of the diocese of Hippo.

He wept.

He wept the way a man weeps who has not been planning to weep. The first sound came out of him before he knew it was going to. His shoulders went; his face did not compose itself; he did not bring his hand up to it. The brethren in the first row did not look away. The brethren in the first row would, in the years to come, take the weeping for the proper humility of a man whom the Lord had called against his expectation. It was not that. It was the weeping of a man who had, in the last quarter of an hour, understood that the life he had been planning to live was no longer the life he was going to live.

He wept until the rite was finished.

Valerius gave the dismissal. The brethren went out into the bright morning. Tigisius and the deacons brought him to the small chamber behind the altar where the vestments were kept. They took the new vestments off him. They folded them. They did not, on the protocols of the African rite, congratulate him; they acknowledged him; they went out.

He sat on the bench in the small chamber alone.

His hands were shaking. He had not noticed them shaking until he sat. He set them on his knees. They went on shaking. He let them.

He thought of his mother at Ostia and of the disc in the box in the writing chest at Tagaste. He would need the box at Hippo from this week onward. The carrying had moved into the office. The office would carry it. He would write to the senior brother at Tagaste this afternoon. The lay brother would ride back tomorrow with the letter and would, by the end of next week, return with the box in his saddle-bag.

He thought of the freedman in the side aisle. The freedman had come to Hippo to ask Augustine about the small final decisions of his own life, and Augustine had walked into a basilica at the second hour and had not walked out the man who could give them. The freedman would go back to Tagaste alone. The brothers at Tagaste would manage. He told himself the brothers at Tagaste would manage; he told himself this because he needed to tell himself this; he was not certain.

He thought of Una.

Una was at Carthage. Una had been at Carthage for five years. Una would, when she heard the news that he had been ordained at Hippo, have whatever response she had. He would not hear what it was. He would not write to ask. He had not, in his understanding of his own conduct with her since he had sent her back from Milan, given himself the right to.

He thought of the small bronze box at Tagaste.

He stood. He went out of the chamber.

The freedman was still at the side aisle. The lay brother was beside him. The freedman's face — Augustine saw, going to him — had been doing what it was doing for the half-hour of the rite and was still doing it: held. The freedman was a man of fifty-eight who had been a slave in his first thirty years and had been managing his own face in difficult rooms for the rest of them. He had managed it this morning.

Augustine went to him.

*The plan has changed,* he said. *I will not be returning to Tagaste with you. I will be at Hippo from this week. The lay brother will ride with you tomorrow. He will take a sealed letter to the senior brother. The letter will contain the instructions about the house. Tell the senior brother I am sorry. I had not, in any week, expected this.*

The freedman put his hand on Augustine's shoulder. The hand was the hand of a friend.

*Aurelius. The brothers will manage.*

*The brothers will manage. Yes.*

*And you.*

*I will manage. The carrying decided what I am to do.*

The freedman did not, on the words, ask what carrying. The freedman knew Augustine well enough not to ask the questions whose asking implied an answer they did not have.

The freedman embraced him. The lay brother bowed.

They went out of the basilica.

Augustine stood in the empty nave. The candles at the altar were burning down. Tigisius was at the bishop's chair putting away the vestments Augustine had not yet been ordained to wear and would, in the months to come, learn to wear. Valerius had gone to the small parlour at the bishop's residence next door for the noon meal.

He stood in the third row of the central aisle. He did not move for a long count.

His mother was still in his ribs. She had not, in the three years and four months since Ostia, been in his ribs in the way she was in his ribs now. He understood that she would, from this morning forward, be in his ribs in this way for the rest of his life.

He went to the small parlour at the bishop's residence to take the noon meal with Valerius.

The bronze box came to Hippo with the lay brother on the saddle-bag the following Saturday.

It went on the upper shelf of the small writing chamber that had been given to Augustine for his use as the new priest. The chamber would, in 395 and after, become the bishop's writing room.

The disc, when Augustine checked it on the evening it arrived, was cold.

He set it back. He closed the box. He returned the box to the upper shelf. He went to compline.
