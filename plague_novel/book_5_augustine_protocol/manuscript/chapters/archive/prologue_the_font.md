# Prologue — The Font

*Hippo Regius, Easter 397 CE.*

The font was new. He had consecrated it three days before, in the afternoon, with the holy oil still warm and the chamber still smelling of cut stone and the lamp-oil his deacons had spilled while setting the lamps in their niches. Old Valerius had wanted the new baptistery for years and had not lived to bless it; Augustine had buried him in October and had wanted to delay the dedication out of respect, but the season ran on its own clock. Easter would not wait for grief. The font was eight feet across, octagonal — Ambrose's preference, copied from Milan — with a step down on the eastern side and a deeper basin in the centre where an adult could be fully submerged. He had stood in it on Wednesday and the water had come to his thighs.

Now, at the ninth hour of Holy Saturday, he stood again at the eastern step in his under-tunic and looked at the water. The water was cold; the cistern below the basilica had not warmed since winter and would not warm before Pentecost. The water was the colour the water in this font would always be — the colour of cut stone with light in it. He stood at the step and did the work of his mind.

There was no decision to be made. The decision had been made three weeks earlier in his own cell, on the night he received word from the deacon Tigisius that Crispus had preached again at a private gathering in the harbour district and that the gathering had numbered above forty. There had been a decision after the first warning, given through Possidius in January — Crispus had answered with a kiss on Possidius's cheek and a small folded note that read only *I will preach what is true*. There had been a second decision after the second warning, given through Megalius's man Severus in February — Crispus had answered with silence and a continued schedule of sermons. The third warning Augustine had delivered himself, in the courtyard of the basilica, alone, on the day before Lent began. He had not raised his voice. He had said only: *Brother, you must understand that I cannot let what you say continue to be said.* Crispus had answered: *Brother, I cannot let what is true become unsaid because a bishop wishes it unsaid.* They had embraced. The wool of Crispus's wrap had been the rough kind, washed in cold water, the same wool the Donatist clergy in Africa wore everywhere. Augustine had walked back into the basilica.

The decision, then, was not to be made. It had been made in stages, each smaller than the last, until the last was not a decision at all but the carrying-out of a thing long since done.

He waded out of the font and dressed.

Crispus had been a fellow student under Romanianus's patronage thirty years earlier in Madauros. They had read Cicero in the same room and had argued about the *Hortensius* in the same dormitory and had, on one summer evening Augustine still remembered for the smell of the figs the boys had been allowed to eat that night, sworn to each other a kind of brotherhood that the brothers themselves could not now have defined. Crispus had gone north, to Cirta, and had taken orders in the Donatist communion because his mother's family was Donatist and because the Donatists in Cirta had been the visible church of his childhood. He had not believed himself in schism. He had believed himself faithful to the church his mother had been baptized in. When the Donatist primate had sent him south to Hippo three years ago to support the Donatist congregation here, he had embraced Augustine in the street outside the granaries and had said, *Aurelius, this need not divide us*, using the name Augustine had not been called by since Madauros. Augustine had answered, *It need not, but it does.* They had not embraced again.

What Crispus had been preaching, in increasingly large gatherings since the autumn, was a careful and deeply considered case that Augustine's ordination as bishop was canonically void. It went like this. Megalius of Calama, who had laid hands on him in 395, had been ordained in his turn through what the Donatists called the Caecilianist line — the line of those bishops in Africa whom the Donatists had always regarded as betrayers, the men who had handed over the sacred books during Diocletian's persecution. Therefore Megalius's orders were void. Therefore Augustine's orders were void. Therefore every baptism Augustine had performed in two years of episcopate was an empty rite; every infant he had named was unbaptized; every consecration of bread and wine he had performed was bread and wine; every absolution he had pronounced over a dying man was a sound the dying man had heard and gone to judgment without. Crispus had laid this out without anger and without rhetorical flourish, in late Latin so plain that an unlettered docker in the harbour could follow it, and the dockers had been following it. Augustine had read transcriptions of three of his sermons. They were calm. They were patient. They were correct in their internal logic. They were, as a piece of pastoral theology, the most dangerous thing that had ever been said about him.

He could not answer them in argument. He had tried, in two letters and a sermon. The ground was bad ground; the Donatist primates had laid it a hundred years earlier and the African church had never definitively cleared it. He could not move the man off it because the man had been born on it. He could not unsay what the man was saying because the man could read and was reading. He could not exile him under the present imperial law: that would require a denunciation, and the denunciation would itself raise the question of whether his orders were valid enough to make one. He could, perhaps, discredit him in time. Five years. Ten. The sermons in the meantime would multiply.

He could remove the man.

The arrangement was small. Stephen would meet Crispus at the south door of the basilica at the third hour of night, on the pretext of conveying a private message from the bishop: that Augustine had reconsidered, that he wished to speak as men, that the baptistery was open. Crispus would come; he was that kind of man, the kind who would come to a midnight meeting in a basilica because he believed in the thing he believed in and would not turn down a chance to argue it with a brother whose mind he still hoped to alter. Stephen would walk him through the cloister to the new baptistery and would say nothing further until they were inside the door. Augustine would be in the chamber. He would not speak. Stephen would close the door and would do what he had been told to do.

Stephen had wept, in Augustine's cell, when he had been told. He had wept silently with his face in his hands; Augustine had heard the breath catching but no sound of any cry. He was twenty-eight years old, the son of a freedwoman, ordained subdeacon eight months ago and not yet trained in canon law. Augustine had said to him: *I am asking you for your hands. I am not asking you for your soul. Your soul is in mine on this. If there is sin here, it is mine, not yours.* Stephen had said: *Father, I do not want to.* Augustine had said: *I know. That is why I am asking you and not asking another.* The young man had wept for some minutes and had then nodded, once, and had asked when. Augustine had told him.

It happened at the third hour as planned.

Crispus came. He was wearing the light woollen wrap that Donatist clergy wore in Africa to distinguish themselves from Catholic clergy in their heavier vestments, and he was carrying nothing. Stephen walked him through the cloister and into the new baptistery. The lamps in the niches had been left lit; Augustine had lit them himself an hour earlier. Augustine stood on the eastern step of the font in his bishop's tunic. He did not move when Crispus entered. Crispus saw him and stopped.

*Aurelius,* Crispus said.

Augustine did not answer.

Stephen closed the door and crossed the chamber and put his hands on Crispus's shoulders from behind. Crispus did not resist. He looked at Augustine for a long moment, in the lamplight of the new baptistery, and he understood what was about to happen and he did not run. Augustine watched the understanding arrive in his eyes. Crispus said, in the same calm tone in which he had preached his sermons: *Brother, this will not unmake what I have said.*

Augustine said: *No. It will only stop what you would have said next.*

Stephen walked Crispus down into the font. The water was cold; Crispus drew breath sharply at the first step. Stephen pushed his head down at the centre of the basin where the water was deepest, kneeling on the rim to gain weight, both hands on the back of Crispus's neck, and held him there.

The body resisted for some time. Augustine had not known how long it would take. He had imagined, when he had thought it through, that drowning would be quick — a moment of struggle and then the going-still. It was not quick. It took, he thought afterwards, perhaps two full minutes.

He stood on the eastern step and did not move. He had told himself before he came into the chamber that he would not move and that the not-moving was the bishop's part. The bishop's part was to be present and to bear witness and not to look away. He did not look away. His feet were on the wet marble of the step and the cold of the marble had come up through his sandals into his ankles in the time he had been standing. His own breath was high in his chest where it was no use to him. His hands hung at his sides. He had to keep telling them to hang at his sides. The water in the font moved a little around the body. The lamps did not flicker. The smell of the holy oil was still in the chamber from the consecration; he could smell that, and the lamp-oil, and the cold water, and after a while he could smell the urine that Crispus's body released in the second minute of the dying.

The water did not splash much; Stephen's weight kept Crispus low and the resistance was muffled by submersion. At one point one of Crispus's hands came up and gripped Stephen's wrist with the strength of a man dying, and Stephen made a small sound in his throat and closed his eyes and did not let go. Augustine watched the hand. He watched it release. He watched the body stop resisting. He watched Stephen continue to hold the head down for what he judged to be a further full minute after the resisting stopped, because he had told Stephen to be sure, and Stephen, having committed, was being sure. Augustine's throat had closed at some point in the two minutes. He noticed it now, swallowing against it, and the swallow took longer than it should have.

When Stephen stood up, his hands were shaking and his shoulders had not yet remembered how to settle. He stood on the rim of the font with the water running off his forearms and looked at Augustine and did not speak. Augustine said: *Leave him in the water. Go and bring Quodvultdeus and the linen.* Stephen went.

He waited alone with the body in the water. The chamber was very quiet. He could hear his own breath. He could hear the small sound the lamps made in their niches — a sound he had never noticed in any baptistery before, the small sound of oil burning in still air.

The face was below the surface. The water was clouded a little but did not bear blood; drowning bears no blood. Crispus had voided his bowels at the end, and Augustine could smell it faintly under the lamp-oil and the cut-stone and the holy oil still ghosting in the chamber from the consecration. The first thought arrived in him cleanly: *I have made a font that holds a man.*

The second came after, slower. The font would be drained and refilled. It would be reblessed. It would baptize tomorrow, as it had been made to do, as he had made it to do.

The thing was done. It could not be undone. He had judged it the smallest thing he could have done; any larger thing would have unmade more.

He noticed, without surprise, that he was not weeping. The weeping would come at some point — tonight, perhaps, or in some year. He was not weeping now because he was working.

Crispus came up in him then and he set the thinking-of-Crispus down where he could see it but not handle it. Crispus would have understood the calculation. He had been a careful man. He had been a working man. He would have understood that the church requires its men to do the work the church requires. The thought brought no comfort. There was no Crispus left to understand it.

The chamber held Augustine and a body. The chamber held the new font, holding the body. Augustine stood on the eastern step.

Quodvultdeus came with the linen. He did not ask. Quodvultdeus was thirty-six and had been with Augustine since the monastery at Tagaste; he asked questions when they were asked of him to ask, and he did not ask them at other times. The two deacons lifted Crispus from the font and wrapped him in linen on the marble floor. They carried him out the small east door into the burying-ground, and Stephen followed with a lamp, and the three of them dug the grave together by lamplight in the corner of the burying-ground that had been used in the previous century for plague dead and was no longer consecrated. They put the body in the linen into the hole. Quodvultdeus said the words for a man whose name they would not record. They filled the grave. Augustine helped with the digging; he did not direct. The work took until the seventh hour of night.

Afterwards, the three of them returned to the baptistery. They drained the font through the lead pipe to the cistern below. They scoured the basin with hyssop and salt. They drew fresh water from the well in the cloister, three trips with the great copper urn, and refilled the font. Augustine consecrated it again, in a private rite he conducted for the second time in four days, with Quodvultdeus and Stephen as the only witnesses. He used the same words he had used three days before. He did not feel that the words were less true for being said over a font that had held a body. He thought: *the water does not know what it has held; the water knows what it is to be blessed*. The thought was not theology. He did not write it down.

By the second hour after midnight the chamber smelled of cut stone and holy oil and salt and lamp-oil only. Augustine sent the two deacons to wash and to sleep; he stayed alone in the basilica until the bell rang for the vigil. He did not pray. He sat on a stool at the foot of the bishop's chair in the dark and rested. He thought of Crispus once, during the resting, at the moment when he had remembered the dormitory at Madauros and the smell of figs, and the memory passed through him without lodging. He thought of his mother and was surprised by the thought. He thought of the homily he would preach at the vigil and went over the periods. He fell into a half-sleep an hour before the bell.

The vigil came. The basilica filled. The candidates for baptism were brought to the new font and the lamps were lit and Augustine stood at the eastern step where six hours earlier he had stood and watched a man die and he asked the candidates, in the formula proper to the African rite: *Abrenuntias Satanae?* — *Do you renounce Satan?* And they answered, each in turn, *Abrenuntio.* *I renounce.* And he poured the water, three times, in the name of the Father and the Son and the Holy Spirit, on the head of each, and he made the cross on each forehead with the holy oil warm in the bowl Quodvultdeus held for him, and the new Christians went up out of the water in white linen, and the basilica sang the *Alleluia* of the vigil, and the candles burned, and Augustine officiated with the steadiness for which his clergy had begun to be known. He made no error in any rite. His voice did not shake. He looked at no one for sympathy because he required none. He worked. The vigil ran from the eleventh hour of Saturday night into the second hour of Sunday morning, and the basilica did the work the basilica was for, and the bishop did the work the bishop was for, and at the end of it, at the dismissal, the people of Hippo Regius went home to their houses to break the Lenten fast on lamb and bread and oil, and Augustine went into the small chamber behind the altar and removed his vestments and stood at the basin to wash his hands, and his hands were steady.

In the morning, the third hour of Sunday, the second baptism of the new font took place. It was an infant. The infant was a few weeks old, brought to the font by his mother because the father was at sea in a coaster on its way to Ostia and would not be back before the rains. The mother was perhaps twenty-two; a small woman, dark, in the dress of a lower-tradesman's wife, with the bearing of a woman who has prayed nightly for two years for a child and has at the end of those two years been given one. She had given the bishop her son's name in writing the previous week: Marcellus. *After the martyr,* she had written underneath, *not the centurion.* Augustine had smiled at the note when his secretary had read it to him. He smiled at the woman now, in the basilica, in the third hour of Sunday morning, with the new font reblessed and the lamps still burning low from the vigil. She handed him the infant. The infant was warm. He was clenched as a small fist. Augustine held him in the bend of his left arm with his right hand cupped under the small head. The hand under the small head was the hand he had not let move six hours earlier, and the hand felt the warmth of the small head in a way it had not let itself feel anything during the work of the night. He asked the question of the godparents. He poured the water three times. He made the cross with the holy oil on the small forehead. The infant did not cry. The basilica was very quiet. Augustine handed the infant back to his mother and said the words of dismissal and blessing.

The mother kissed his hands. She kissed both of them, the one that had poured the water and the one that had held her son's head. She said: *Father, you have given him the beginning.* He said: *He has been given the beginning. I have only said what was given me to say.* She kissed his hands again. He saw her face very clearly: a small mole below the right eye, a chip in the front tooth she did not yet know she had, the eyelashes wet because she had been weeping quietly during the rite. He thought, with the clarity that had been with him since the night, that he would remember her face. He thought he would remember it because it was useful to remember the faces of those who trusted the office he held. He thought: *the office continues. The office requires its trust. The office did what it had to do six hours ago in this same chamber to keep continuing to deserve the trust this woman is now giving it.* He thought: *the boy will be useful.* The thought arrived without his choosing it, and he did not push it away.

Castissima — the mother's name was Castissima, he remembered now, his secretary had read it to him with the boy's — kissed his hands a third time and went out of the basilica with the infant Marcellus into the bright morning of Easter Sunday in the second year of his episcopate, and Augustine stood at the eastern step of the new font in the empty basilica, with the lamps almost burned out, and waited for the next thing the office required of him.
