# Chapter 10 — The Vandals Come (v2, thriller)

*Hippo Regius, the second week of June 425 CE.*

The reports were in a stack on the corner of the desk. Eraclius had been bringing them up from the basilica gate two and three at a time since the spring, and they had been accumulating in the corner because Augustine had not, in two months, been at the desk for the long stretches the reports required. His lungs had begun, in the previous winter, to give a sound when he breathed that they had not given before — a small wet sound at the bottom of each inhalation, like a piece of damp linen being pulled — and the sound had not gone away in the spring, and the physician at Carthage who had come down for an opinion had said that the sound would not go away and that there were things that could be done to slow it and nothing that could be done to stop it. Augustine had thanked the physician and had paid for the journey. The things the physician had recommended he had done. The sound had not, on his attention, slowed.

He sat at the desk now in the middle of the morning. The window was half-shuttered against the brightness of June and was open enough to let in the harbour smell. The stack of reports was at his elbow. He had taken the top three off the stack and had spread them on the desk in front of him, and he had been reading them in the slow careful manner of a man who would have, ten years ago, read them in a quarter of the time.

Tingis had fallen in March. Hippo was the last port of consequence between Tingis and Carthage; everything between them was what stood between the Vandal army and the grain supply of the western empire. The dispatch from the bishop of Caesarea Mauretaniae confirmed it. The Vandals under Gaiseric — the new king, who had taken the throne when his half-brother Gunderic died in the autumn — had crossed from the Hispanian coast in a fleet that the bishop estimated at eight hundred vessels of various sizes, and had landed at the western end of Mauretania Tingitana, and had begun the work of moving east along the African coast in the slow methodical manner of a people that had brought its women and its children and its herds and was not, on any reading of its movements, planning to go back.

The dispatch from Caesarea was three months old. The dispatch beside it, from a Greek wine merchant of Carthage who corresponded with Eraclius on private matters, was three weeks old. The wine merchant wrote that the Vandals had taken Volubilis without a siege and were now at the western edge of Mauretania Caesariensis. The cities along the coast were surrendering on terms before the Vandal arrival, because the Vandal arrival was understood, by every prefect of every coastal town, to be a thing whose alternative was a thing not to be borne.

The third dispatch was from the senior bishop at Carthage. The senior bishop wrote with the particular dryness of a man who had been head of the African church for fourteen years and who had, in those fourteen years, become a man who reported facts and did not, except in the privacy of letters to particular friends, weigh them. He wrote: *Bishop, the council I had hoped to convene at Carthage in October will not now be possible. The bishops of the western provinces are not in a position to travel. The bishop of Iol Caesarea has written that he expects, on present rates of advance, to be unable to leave his see by August. The bishop of Hippo Regius is hereby informed.*

Augustine set the third dispatch down on the desk.

A knock at the door.

*Aurelius.*

*Possidius.*

Possidius came in. He had been at Hippo since the spring on the matter of the council and the matter of the council's not being held and the matter of the dispatches that had been arriving at the rate of two and three a week since March. He sat in the chair across the desk. The chair had been his chair for the morning briefings for some years now.

*Aurelius. The dispatches.*

*I have read the three on the desk. I have not read the others.*

*The others say the same thing.*

*I assumed they would.*

Possidius sat with his hands flat on his knees. He had taken to sitting that way in the last year. Augustine had been watching it and had not spoken of it.

*Aurelius. How long.*

*The wine merchant at Carthage thinks two years to Numidia. The bishop at Caesarea thinks longer; the bishop at Caesarea has, by his location, an interest in believing it will be longer. The senior bishop at Carthage will not, in writing, speculate. I think the wine merchant is right and the bishop at Caesarea is two years out of date. I think we will see Vandal scouts at Hippo in three years and the army the year after.*

*Three years.*

*Three. Possibly four. Not five.*

Possidius nodded. He sat with the four. The harbour smell came up through the half-shuttered window and went past. From somewhere in the cathedral end of the cloister a young brother was reading aloud, badly, in a Latin that had not yet been corrected by the master of the novices.

*The five fair copies have been at Carthage and Constantinople and Lérins and Rome and Jerusalem since the year of completion.*

*They have.*

*Sophia at Tagaste has the working copy of all twenty-two books and the wax under-drafts of the seventeenth and after.*

*She does.*

*The Confessions has been in twenty-three monastic libraries for ten years. The anti-Pelagian works are in the Carthage scriptorium in seven copies and in fewer at the other locations. The corpus is, on the whole, distributed.*

*The corpus is distributed.*

*The diocese at Hippo will fall.*

*The diocese at Hippo will fall. The basilica will be re-consecrated by an Arian bishop. The Vandals pray to a Christ they believe was made by God rather than being God — a distinction the councils settled seventy years ago and which the Vandals have not settled to our satisfaction. The Catholic clergy who are still in Hippo when the city falls will be killed or they will be exiled or they will be forced into the Arian rite, depending on the temperament of the Vandal officer in command of the city in that month. There may be killings. There may not be. The Vandals at Hippo will be the Vandals after thirty years on the move and after a difficult winter at sea, and the Vandals after thirty years on the move are not, on the reports we have, gentle Vandals.*

Possidius did not speak for a count of perhaps ten.

*Aurelius. You will not leave.*

*I will not leave. The bishop of Hippo dies in Hippo. That is what the office is. I have not held the office for thirty-five years to leave it because the city is going to be taken. Eraclius will take the diocese after me. Eraclius is forty-one and is well and has been doing the work of the bishop in everything but title for the last two years. He will manage.*

*Eraclius does not know the work.*

*Eraclius does not know the work. Eraclius will manage the diocese. The work goes with you.*

He had been intending to say it; he had been intending to say it for some weeks; he had not, in those weeks, said it. He said it now. The room did not change. The harbour smell came up through the window. The novice down the cloister continued reading badly.

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*I had been waiting for you to say it.*

*I know you had.*

*The box.*

*The box stays where it is until the day the Vandal scouts are visible from the basilica tower. On that day or the day after, you will come up the cloister, and you will come into the writing room, and you will go to the upper shelf and you will take the bronze box. You will leave the same morning. You will not say goodbye to me at the gate. You will say whatever goodbye is to be said now or in the days between now and that day. You will go to Carthage by the coast road if it is open and by ship if the coast road is closed. You will be at the senior bishop's house at Carthage within the week of leaving. You will not stop in any house in Hippo to collect anything. You will travel light and you will travel as a bishop on episcopal business, which you will be.*

*And the diocese of Calama.*

*The diocese of Calama is in the same situation as the diocese of Hippo. Calama will fall in the same season. The bishop's chair at Calama will be filled by an Arian within a year of the Vandal arrival. Your auxiliary at Calama will manage what can be managed and will, with God's permission, survive. He is a competent man. You have trained him. The diocese is not the work.*

*The diocese is not the work.*

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*You have been the bishop of Calama for twenty-five years. You have been my deacon and my priest and my brother for thirty-five. The diocese has been the diocese. The work has been the work. They have run alongside each other and you have served both. There is going to come a day in the next four years when you will have to choose between them, and you will choose the work, and the choosing will be the hardest thing you do in your life. I am telling you now, in this room, on this morning of June 425, that the choice has been agreed between us, and that I have, in the choosing, taken the weight of the choice from you. You did not choose. I chose. You are obeying your bishop. The leaving of the diocese is on me and not on you.*

Possidius sat with this. He sat with it for a long time. His hands stayed flat on his knees. After a while he raised one hand and passed it across his face once and put the hand back down.

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*Thank you.*

*You will need to remember it. You will need to remember it on the morning. You will need to remember it for the years afterwards, when the men who were left at Calama have been killed and you are at Carthage with a box in your luggage and you have not been able to write to them and you are reading what news there is. You will need to remember that I took the choice. The remembering is part of what I am giving you with the box.*

*I will remember.*

*Good.*

They sat for a small time longer. The novice down the cloister had stopped reading and was being corrected by a quieter voice that was Stephen's. Stephen was fifty-six now. Stephen had been the master of the novices for nine years.

Possidius rose.

*Aurelius. I will be at compline.*

*Good.*

He went out. Augustine sat at the desk with the three dispatches in front of him. After some time he took up the bone stylus and the wax tablet from the corner and wrote on the wax, in the small clean hand: *Three years. Possibly four. The box to leave the morning of the scouts.* He scraped it clean. He wrote it again, in shorthand only he and Possidius now read. He set the wax tablet on the corner.

He took up the parchment of the *Retractationes*. The work had been going for two years and was on the seventh of an intended fourteen books. The work was the slow review of his own oeuvre — the books and treatises and letters of fifty years of writing, taken in order, with the marginal corrections and clarifications that an old man owed to his younger self. The work was tedious. It was necessary. It was the kind of work that an old man did when he had finished the work he had been put on the earth to do and was waiting, with a particular dignified patience, for the rest to be over.

He wrote half a paragraph on a passage from the *De Doctrina Christiana* of 396, in which he had said something about the relation of pagan learning to Christian doctrine that he was now, twenty-nine years on, willing to qualify but not, in fundamentals, retract.

He stopped at the end of the half-paragraph and read it through. He coughed for a count of perhaps fifteen. The cough was the cough that had begun in the previous winter and was now, in June, his usual cough. He coughed into the linen square Eraclius had taken to keeping on the desk for him. The linen took its share. He set the linen back on the desk.

He went on with the work.

*Hippo Regius, the third week of October 428 CE.*

Alypius came up by foot from the harbour with his deacon, on the morning of the feast of St Cyprian, having sent advance notice from Carthage but not from the harbour at Hippo. He arrived at the basilica gate at the third hour. The gate-keeper, who was the same gate-keeper who had been at the gate since the year of the courier eighteen years before and whose throat had closed that day and who had not, in the years since, opened it again on a matter outside the daily round, opened the door of the writing room without knocking.

*Father. Bishop Alypius.*

Augustine looked up from the desk.

*Bring him.*

The gate-keeper went out. Augustine stood. He stood in two stages, the way he stood now, the right knee taking the first part of the standing and the left knee taking the second part, with the desk under his hand for the balance. He was at the door when Alypius came in.

They embraced.

The embrace was the embrace of two men who had been friends for fifty-eight years, since they had read Cicero together in the same room at Tagaste at fourteen and fifteen, and who were now in the second-last year either of them would be alive in. The embrace was hard. They held it for the count of perhaps five. They stepped back.

Alypius was seventy-four. He was a year older than Augustine and looked it. The bronze-tipped staff he had been carrying for some years was now bearing the larger part of his weight when he walked, and he walked slowly, and he was thinner than he had been in the spring. The deacon who had come up with him stood at the door. Augustine looked at the deacon.

*Father. He will be at the lay parlour. We will be private.*

The deacon nodded and went out. Alypius came to the chair across the desk and sat. The sitting was, like Augustine's standing, in two stages.

*Aurelius.*

*Alypius.*

*The harbour is not what it was in March.*

*No. The fishing fleets that were going west have been pulled in. The Carthaginian merchants have stopped insuring cargo bound for any port west of Hippo. The grain shipments from Numidia to Italy have been re-routed through the eastern ports. The harbour is half what it was in May. By February it will be a quarter of that. By the year after, it will be the harbour for whatever fleet the Vandals decide to keep here.*

*So I had heard. I wished to see it.*

*Yes.*

Augustine sent Eraclius for wine. The wine that came was the Gallic wine that had come up from Carthage in the last shipment before the trade had gone uncertain in the spring; there were six amphorae left in the cellar, and Augustine had been keeping them for visits of a particular weight. He had judged today to be one. They drank.

*Aurelius. I have come to make a particular kind of farewell.*

He had known. He had known when the deacon of Tagaste had written, a fortnight before, that the bishop of Tagaste would be passing through Hippo on his way back from Carthage. There had been no business at Carthage requiring Alypius to make the journey. The journey was the journey it now was.

*I am glad you came.*

*I will not be at Hippo again.*

*No.*

*I have decided to be at Tagaste when the Vandals come to Tagaste. The diocese of Tagaste is the diocese I was given. The diocese is small. The clergy at Tagaste are, with three exceptions, men I have known since they were boys. The exceptions are men I have known since they were younger boys. The diocese will not survive the Vandal taking. I would not survive evacuation. I am seventy-four and my legs are what they are and the journey to Carthage was the last long journey I will make. I have decided to be at Tagaste when the time comes. The time will come, on present rates, in the spring of the year after this one. I will be at Tagaste in the spring.*

*Yes.*

*I am asking your blessing on the decision and not your permission for it. The decision is made.*

*You have my blessing on the decision. You did not need it. You have it.*

Alypius nodded. He took another drink of the Gallic wine. The wine was good.

*Aurelius. There is a thing.*

*Say it.*

*The fair copy of the *Confessions* that has been in the small house of the lay readers at Tagaste for twenty years. It is the second copy you sent me. I have not used the second copy. I have been using the first. The second has been in the small house with a velvet wrap on it. I have given instructions to the deacon at Tagaste that on the morning the Vandals are at the gate of the city, the fair copy is to be taken by the lay-reader Faustus, who is twenty-three and is the best rider among the lay readers, and is to be carried to Madauros and from Madauros to the desert monastery at Bagai. The deacon has agreed. Faustus has agreed. Faustus knows what the copy is. The copy will, on the deacon's plan, be at Bagai by the third day after the city falls. The desert monastery at Bagai is, on the present reports, beyond the line the Vandals are likely to push to.*

*Good.*

*That was the thing I came to tell you. The Tagaste fair copy will not be in Tagaste when Tagaste falls.*

*Good.*

*I would not have you hear it second-hand.*

*I would not have heard it second-hand. I would have heard it. But I would rather hear it from you.*

They sat for a small time.

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*Do you remember the *Hortensius*?*

*I remember the *Hortensius* — Cicero's argument that only the philosophical life was worth living. We had it from Romanianus's library — he was paying for our education that year — in the spring of the year I was nineteen. You read it first. You read it in three nights and you came to me at the river one afternoon and you told me the small wooden bench by the third fig tree was the bench on which a particular kind of life was about to be decided.*

*I remember. I remember the bench.*

*I read it after you. I read it slower. I read it in five nights. The decision was not, on my reading, decided that summer. It was decided at Milan twelve years later. But the bench at Tagaste was where I first looked at the question that the bench at Milan answered.*

*The bench at Milan answered.*

*It answered.*

Alypius smiled. The smile was a smile his face had made on and off across fifty-eight years of friendship. It had not changed greatly in the last decade.

*Aurelius. I will be at vespers tonight.*

*I would be glad of you at vespers.*

*I will be at the small parlour after for a meal with Eraclius and Possidius. I will be at the harbour the morning after. I do not need you to come to the harbour.*

*I will not come to the harbour.*

*Good.*

He took the staff and came upright with it. He looked at Augustine across the desk.

*Aurelius.*

*Alypius.*

*I will pray for you. I will pray for the work. I will pray for Possidius. I will pray for the boy at the harbour who took the bronze cross from the boy at Ostia eighteen years ago, who is now twenty-eight and is, on the deacon at Carthage's report, alive in a small house in the Aventine quarter with three children and a wife and a small business in cured fish. The work has been the work. The praying for it is the praying I have been doing.*

Augustine had not known the boy was alive. He had not known the boy was twenty-eight. He had not, in eighteen years, asked.

*Thank you.*

They did not embrace again. They shook hands across the desk in the formal manner of two bishops concluding episcopal business. Alypius went out. Augustine sat at the desk with the cup of Gallic wine in front of him. He did not drink the rest of the wine. He sat. After a long time he stood and went to the window and pushed the shutter further open and watched, through it, the courtyard until Alypius's bronze-tipped staff had clicked across the stones and through the cloister door and the sound was gone.

*Hippo Regius, the last week of May 429 CE.*

The Greek captain came to the gate at the second hour, having put in at Hippo on a route he would not normally have taken because the route he would normally have taken was no longer safe and because the news he was carrying could not, in his judgement, wait for the next ship. His name was Nikephoros. He had been carrying mail and small cargo between the African ports for thirty years. Eraclius brought him to the writing room without delay.

*Bishop.*

*Captain.*

*Bishop, the Vandals have crossed the strait. They began the crossing at the new moon and they finished the crossing in the second week of May. I saw the last of the ships from the deck of my own ship at the Pillars in the third week. The fleet is, on my count, in the order of two hundred vessels and is, by every man I spoke to at Tarifa, carrying the entire people. They are at Tingis now. They will be moving along the coast within a fortnight.*

*Gaiseric.*

*Gaiseric himself was on the lead ship of the second wave. I saw the banner. The banner is the banner the dispatches have described. Purple, with the gold cross of his Arian rite. There is no mistaking it.*

Augustine sat with the captain's words. He did not speak for a count. The captain stood with his cap in his hand.

*Captain. You have done me a service.*

*Bishop. I would not have a bishop hear it from a man less reliable than myself.*

*Eraclius will provide for you. Eat. Rest. There is a bed in the guest quarters if you can stay the night.*

*Bishop, with thanks, I will sail with the morning tide. I have business at Carthage.*

*Then go in peace.*

The captain bowed. He went out. Augustine sat at the desk. He took up the wax tablet at his elbow. He scraped clean what had been on it, which was a draft of a letter to a young priest at Cirta on a question of liturgical practice. He wrote, in the small clean hand:

*Possidius. The crossing has begun. The fleet is at Tingis. On all previous estimates, the army will be at Hippo within fourteen months. Not less than ten. Not more than eighteen. The day for the box is now within the calendar year that is coming. Come down from Calama by the next post. We have things to set in their final order.*

He set the stylus down. He coughed. The cough this time took longer than the coughs in June 425 had taken. The linen took what it took. He set the linen aside.

He took up the parchment of the *Retractationes*. He was on the eleventh book of the work. The eleventh book was the book on the *Enchiridion*, which he had finished in 421 for his friend Laurentius and which had been, in his own estimation, one of the more compressed and useful things he had written in his late years. The book on the *Enchiridion* was going slowly because the *Enchiridion* itself had been a tightly-built work and there was, on Augustine's review, less to retract than there had been with most of his earlier books.

He worked through the morning on the slow paragraphs of the eleventh book. He took the noon meal at his desk. He worked through the afternoon. He coughed at intervals. The coughing did not go away.

In the late afternoon, when the light had begun to go and the lamp had been lit, he set the parchment down and went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box. He opened it. The hinges gave their small specific sound. The disc was inside. The disc was cold.

He held the box for a count.

He had not warmed in his hands in some years. He had stopped expecting it to. He had stopped, in the last two or three years, attaching any particular significance to the cold. The disc was bronze. Bronze in a closed box on a high shelf in a cool room was cold. That was what bronze was. He had carried the disc for forty-two years and it had been warm in his hand four times. He did not expect a fifth.

He closed the box. He put the box back on the shelf. He went to vespers.

*Hippo Regius, the third week of April 430 CE.*

The messenger reached the writing room at the second hour after morning prayers. Augustine was at the desk on the twelfth book of the *Retractationes*; he had been on the twelfth book since the New Year and was now, in April, on its second-last chapter. The messenger was a layman from Tagaste who had ridden through the night with the message and who was, Eraclius reported when he brought him to the door, holding himself together with what reserves a man of forty-eight has after a hard ride and a bad piece of news.

*Bishop.*

*Tell me.*

*Bishop Alypius is dead. The Vandals were at Tagaste on the morning of the day before yesterday. They were not the army. They were the advance party — perhaps two hundred horse, perhaps three. Bishop Alypius was at the basilica when they came through the city gate. He met them at the basilica steps. He spoke to their officer. He did not stand aside. The officer's man cut him down on the steps. The basilica was burned to the foundation. Father, I am sorry to be the one bringing it.*

*The clergy at Tagaste.*

*Three killed at the basilica with the bishop. Two have made the journey to Madauros with the lay-reader Faustus. The deacon is with the lay community in the western quarter and is intending to remain. The lay community will, by his account, manage. The Vandals did not stay. The advance party rode east the same afternoon. The main army is two weeks behind.*

*Faustus took the *Confessions*.*

*Faustus took the *Confessions*. He was at Madauros yesterday and is going on to Bagai. The deacon at Tagaste sent the report by me because he could not send it by Faustus. Father, Faustus has the *Confessions*. The work is intact.*

*Thank you. Eraclius will provide for you. Eat. Rest. There is a bed.*

The man bowed. He went out.

Augustine sat at the desk. He did not move for a long count. The basilica bell at the cathedral end of the cloister rang for the half-hour. He heard it. He did not move. The bell rang for the next half-hour. He heard that one too. He did not move. After perhaps an hour Eraclius came in without knocking, which was no longer outside Eraclius's practice — Eraclius had been making his own choices about what to knock for since the spring — and stood at the desk.

*Aurelius.*

*Yes.*

*Will I bring food.*

*No.*

*Will I send for Possidius.*

*Send a letter. Three lines. *Bishop Alypius has been killed at Tagaste on the eighteenth. The basilica was burned. The fair copy is safe with Faustus. Come down.* That is the letter. Send by the fastest courier.*

*Aurelius.*

*Eraclius.*

*I will bring food.*

*Bring food. I will not eat it. Bring it.*

Eraclius went out. Augustine sat at the desk. He picked up the bone stylus and set it down without writing anything. He picked up the linen square and coughed into it for some time. The linen took its share.

Alypius had been killed standing on the steps of his own basilica because he had, two years before, in the writing room at Hippo, told Augustine that he was going to be at Tagaste when the time came.

Augustine thought of the bench by the third fig tree. Alypius, nineteen years old, in the spring afternoon, saying that the bench was where a particular kind of life was about to be decided. Alypius had been right. The bench had been right. The steps had been right too, in their way. The choosing had been the choosing of a man who had always chosen correctly.

The choosing had been on Alypius and not on Augustine. The choosing had been the choosing of a man of his age and his disposition.

It did not, on the morning, help.

Augustine sat at the desk with the linen in his hand. After some long time he reached out and pulled the parchment of the *Retractationes* toward him. The twelfth book was on the desk. He read through the paragraph he had been writing when the messenger had come. He did not change a word. He took up the bone stylus. He continued the paragraph.

He worked through the day.

When Eraclius brought the bread at the noon hour Augustine ate three small bites and drank a cup of water. When Eraclius came back at the evening hour Augustine ate nothing and drank a cup of water. Eraclius did not ask. Augustine worked. The light went. The lamp was lit. Augustine went on writing. By the second hour after vespers he had finished the second-last chapter of the twelfth book of the *Retractationes*.

He set the stylus down. He coughed for some time. The linen took it.

He did not go to compline. He stayed at the desk until the second hour of the night. Then he got up, blew out the lamp, and went to his cell. He lay on the straw mattress. He did not pray. He did not weep. He thought of Alypius reading the *Hortensius* in three nights at Tagaste in the spring of 369 by a lamp that was not this lamp in a room that was not this room in a body that was, in some sense, the same body that Alypius had carried to the steps of the basilica at Tagaste and had died on. He thought of the *Hortensius*. He thought of the bench by the third fig tree.

He slept.

*Hippo Regius, the third week of June 430 CE.*

Possidius came up from the harbour at the second hour, dust on him, the small mule he had ridden up from Carthage tied at the gate. He had been at Carthage for a fortnight on the matter of the senior bishop's conference on the receipt of refugees from the western dioceses. He had come back the morning after the dispatch from the bishop of Calama had reached him at Carthage, which dispatch had reported that the Vandal army's advance scouts had been seen on the high ground east of Madauros on the morning of the eighteenth.

He came directly to the writing room.

*Aurelius.*

*Possidius.*

The dust had not yet been brushed off the cloak. Possidius did not sit. He stood at the desk.

*The scouts.*

*The scouts are east of Madauros. The army is two weeks behind them. The scouts will be at Hippo by the end of the week.*

*Yes.*

Augustine stood. The standing was in two stages and the second stage took longer than it had taken in October 428. He put his hand on the desk for the balance. He went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box. He brought the box back to the desk.

He set the box on the desk between them.

Possidius looked at the box. He had seen the box on the upper shelf for nineteen years and had not, in those nineteen years, touched it. He looked at it now in the way a man looks at a thing he has been waiting to take for nineteen years and has, in those years, been hoping he would not have to take.

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*Take it.*

*Aurelius.*

*Take it.*

He picked up the box. The box was small. The box was the size of a man's two cupped hands. The hinges did not give their sound, because Possidius did not open the lid. He did not need to. He held the box. He looked at Augustine.

*Aurelius. I will not be at the gate when you die.*

*I know.*

*I have, in the choosing of the day, accepted that.*

*The choosing was on me. It is on me still.*

*The diocese.*

*Eraclius will manage the diocese. Eraclius is the man for the diocese under the Vandals. He has the temperament. He will be killed or he will be exiled or he will be left to manage the diocese under whatever conditions the Vandal commander permits, and on each of those three possibilities he will conduct himself as the bishop of Hippo should conduct himself. He is the man.*

*Yes.*

*Possidius.*

*Yes.*

*Carthage first. Constantinople if Carthage is not safe by the autumn. After Constantinople, the choice is yours. I have given you the keeping of it. The keeping is yours now. The man you pass it to in your turn is your choice. I will not name him. I do not know him. You will know him when you have him in front of you.*

*Yes.*

*Go now. Do not say goodbye at the gate. Do not come back to this room. The next time you come into this room there will be another bishop at this desk and you will not need to come.*

Possidius held the box against his chest. He stood at the desk for a long count. Augustine did not look away from him. After a long count Possidius nodded once. He turned. He went out.

The door closed behind him. Augustine heard his sandals along the cloister. He heard the gate-keeper at the basilica gate. He heard Possidius's voice, quiet, exchanging the goodbye with the gate-keeper that the gate-keeper had been at the gate to receive. He heard the small mule on the stones outside the gate, and the mule going away down the road toward the harbour.

He sat at the desk.

The upper shelf was, for the first time in nineteen years, without the bronze box on it. The shelf had a small clean rectangle on the dust where the box had stood. Eraclius would dust the shelf in the morning and the rectangle would be gone and the shelf would be, after the morning, the shelf of a writing room in which the box had never been.

He took up the parchment of the *Retractationes*. He had three chapters of the thirteenth book to write and the whole of the fourteenth book to come. He did not, on the present accounting of his lungs, expect to finish the fourteenth.

He took up the bone stylus.

He went on with the work.

Outside the window, in the warm June afternoon, the city was preparing for siege in the slow methodical manner of a city that had been preparing for it for a year. The shutters of the harbour shops were being nailed closed. The grain in the basilica's granary had been moved to the inner cellar three weeks before. The cisterns were being topped up from the wells in the cloister. The Vandal scouts would be visible from the basilica tower within a week. The army would be at the walls within the month.

Augustine did not look up at any of it. He bent over the parchment. The cough took him at intervals. The linen took its share. He continued the chapter.
