# Chapter 2 — Una, and the Living Wound (v2, thriller)

*Hippo Regius, the last week of August 410 CE — four days after the courier.*

Possidius came back from Calama in the middle of the week, two days early, having heard the news of Rome on the road. He arrived in the late morning, dust on him, the small mule he rode tied at the gate. He embraced Augustine in the cloister. The embrace was hard. He had ridden the last twenty miles thinking through the things he might find — Augustine collapsed, Augustine in confusion, Augustine ill, Augustine dead — and the things he had thought through had not included Augustine standing in his own cloister with the prayers sung and the daily round in motion and the refugee work organized. He embraced him a second time, harder, without speaking, then stepped back. His hands were not steady. He kept them at his sides and waited for them to be.

Augustine took the second embrace standing. He did not return it as fully as it was given. He had been carrying things for four days that could not yet be put down, and the body of Possidius against his body felt to him like a body that did not yet know what it was being asked to bear. He let his friend step back. He kept his jaw loose and his eyes where they had been.

*Aurelius. You look badly.*

*The week has been bad.*

*The week has been bad for everyone.*

*Yes.*

They walked together to the writing room because Possidius wished to see the work and because Augustine wished him to. They sat at the table — the same table — and Augustine showed him the first paragraph. Possidius read it twice and set it down.

*This will be very long.*

*Yes.*

*This will be the principal work.*

*Yes.*

*You have been carrying it for years.*

*I have.*

Possidius nodded. He looked around the room by habit, the way a long friend looks at the workplace of another long friend after an absence. He looked at the upper shelf. The bronze box was where it had not been before. He held the look for a beat longer than was easy and moved his eye on. He looked at the brazier. There was a residue in it that had not been there when he last sat at the table. He moved his eye on. He looked at the corner of the floor near the leg of the table. The salt and vinegar had dried into a whitish bloom which had been wiped twice but not entirely. His eye stayed on the bloom for a long count.

He looked back at Augustine.

He had been in Calama on the Sunday and the Monday. He had been on the road on the Tuesday. He had heard about the courier at the gate of his own basilica from a man who had ridden up from Hippo with a bundle of letters and a piece of news that the man had been told to give only to the bishop of Calama and to no one else. Possidius had thanked the man. He had ridden the rest of the way at the pace of a man who did not wish to attract attention by riding faster. He had not asked Stephen, when he arrived, what had been done in the writing room on the night of the courier; he had asked only whether the carter had been paid and sent away clean, and Stephen had said he had been.

He did not look at the upper shelf again. He did not look at the brazier again. He did not look at the floor again. He looked at Augustine. He held the look for the count of three. Augustine did not look away and did not offer a sentence.

*I will be with those preparing for baptism this afternoon. I will see you at vespers. You should eat at the noon table today.*

*I will eat.*

*Promise me.*

*I promise.*

Possidius went out. Augustine sat alone for some minutes. He could feel the friend's three pauses still in the room, like things that had been put down on the table beside him and not picked back up. There were two of them in the work now and one had been told nothing. The not-telling would be a thing between them now and would be a thing between them for as long as the work required, and he felt the not-telling settle into the place between his shoulder blades where things of that kind settled.

Covering the parchment, he went to the noon table and ate.

In the afternoon, while Possidius was with the baptism candidates, he returned to the writing room, took down the bronze box and the parchment together, and began the second paragraph. The shelf had room.

---

*October 410. Two months later.*

The work was going. He had thirty-one paragraphs of the first book and the broad shape of the next nine. He had also, on a separate roll which was not on any shelf and which he kept inside the lining of his outer tunic when he was not at the desk, the matching of the forty-one markers to the books he intended to write. The matching was the harder work. The first book did the chapter on Brennus and the Gauls; the chapter would carry, beneath the proper teaching about the city of stones falling and the city of God standing, the first of the seven Akkad markers, in the form of an apparently incidental aside about the appointment of magistrates without examination. A reader four hundred years from this writing, holding the matching key, would read the apparently incidental aside as the first marker of the first city. A reader without the matching key would read it as the apparently incidental aside.

He had placed nine of the forty-one. The method was working. The method depended on the matching key surviving as well as the wrapper, and the matching key was a single small roll of parchment in the lining of his tunic. He had not yet decided how to make the matching key survive. He had decided he would decide that later.

Stephen knocked at the door at the half-hour after vespers, three short.

*Father.*

*Come.*

Stephen came in and closed the door behind him.

*The lock at this door has been tried again. Last night, while you were at vespers. The third time this week.*

Augustine set the stylus down.

*Scratches?*

*Two new ones. Small. The same place.*

*Have you seen anyone?*

*No. The cloister is dark at vespers. The brothers are at the cathedral. The novices are at the kitchen for the lay supper. The gate is closed.*

*Eraclius?*

*Eraclius would knock.*

*Yes.*

Augustine sat with it for a count of perhaps ten.

*Stephen. Tonight after compline I will be here. I will leave the lamp lit and the door unlatched. I will sit in the corner where the brazier is. If anyone comes, I will see who. You will be in the cloister at the end nearest the kitchen, not visible from the door. If a man comes out of this door who is not me, you will not stop him. You will see his face. You will tell me his face in the morning.*

*Father.*

*Go.*

Stephen went.

Augustine sat for a further count. He took down the bronze box from the upper shelf. He opened it. The disc was inside. The leather packet with the papyrus was inside. Both were in the position he had left them. He closed the box. He placed it on the desk.

He went to the lower shelf and took down a thick codex of Ambrose's *De Officiis*, which he had bought in his first year as bishop and which he no longer consulted because he had read it through six times and could quote any chapter from memory. He opened the codex at the binding. The thread along the inner spine had loosened over the years, in the way the threads of well-handled codices loosen. He took up his pen-knife and parted the binding further at the centre. The cavity inside the spine was thin, the width of two fingers, the depth of a span. He laid the leather packet flat in the cavity. He laid the disc on top of it, polished face down. He drew the cover back over and tucked the loose threads into the original holes. The codex looked as it had looked. He set it back on the lower shelf between two other works of the desert fathers in the same hand and binding.

He left the bronze box empty on the desk where the next person who came in could see it.

He blew out the lamp and went to compline.

He returned to the writing room at the half-hour after compline. He left the lamp lit on the desk and the door unlatched. He sat in the corner by the brazier with his back against the wall, in the dark behind the lamp, and did not move.

The wall was cold. October had brought the cold up into the stone and the cold came through his outer tunic and through the wool of his under-tunic and into his shoulder blades and stayed there. The lamp on the desk threw a cone of yellow light over the bronze box and the empty desk-top and the lower edge of the upper shelf. Outside the cone the room was a dark with shapes in it. From his corner he could see the door, the desk, the cone of light, the upper shelf. He could not see his own hands. The brazier was at his right elbow, unlit; the charcoal in it had a smell of last winter still on it.

He counted sounds. The cathedral bell at the half-hour. The watch in the cloister at the third quarter. A dog at the harbour. The inn at the end of the road. A door somewhere shutting at the cathedral end of the cloister. His own breath, which he heard because there was nothing else in his corner to hear, and which slowed without his asking after the first half-hour and stayed slow.

His right knee began to ache by the first hour. By the second hour the ache had moved up into his hip and the cold had reached through to the small of his back. He did not move. He was not certain he could now have moved without the moving being heard.

He sat for two hours.

No one came.

At the third hour of night he stood — the standing was a series of small operations against a body that had set against him in the corner — blew out the lamp, latched the door from inside, and went down the cloister to his cell. Stephen was where he had been told to be. Augustine touched his shoulder once in passing. Stephen nodded and went the other way.

In the morning the lock at the writing-room door had two new scratches.

---

*November 410. The third week of the month.*

He had not known that Adeodatus was buried in the western cemetery outside Carthage.

The letter that told him this came down by the regular post on a Wednesday morning, in a bundle with two from the senior bishop at Carthage and a small sheaf of grain receipts that the basilica was expected to forward. Eraclius brought the bundle to the writing room at the second hour and set it on the table without remark. Augustine was at the desk, on the second draft of the chapter on Brennus and the Gauls, and was not pleased with the second draft. He pushed the parchment aside and took up the bundle.

He read the senior bishop's two letters first, because the seal asked for that — a query about the council date, a request to host a delegation in February. He set them on the right side of the desk for replies and the grain receipts on the left for Eraclius's clerk. The third letter was addressed in a hand he did not know, careful and clerkly: *to Aurelius Augustinus, Bishop of Hippo Regius, with the courtesy of the church.* The seal was a plain wax disc, no signet, pressed by the side of a thumb. He cut it with his thumbnail.

Four pages. A clerk's hand throughout — the writer had dictated, not written. A freedman — once enslaved, now released — or a man of the working middling sort who had paid a few coppers for a clerk's hour.

*To the most reverend Bishop of Hippo Regius, from Anthony, freedman of the late household of Maximus and overseer of the lower properties on the Way of the Cothon, greeting in our Lord.*

*Bishop, I write to inform you of a death. My sister, who in the records of our late master's household appeared by the name Una, died on the twenty-sixth day of October in the small house she had kept for nineteen years on the lower street behind the harbour, of a fever which had been with her since the spring and which the physicians of the lower town judged in October to be incurable. She had received the consolations of the Church from the priest of the basilica of the Two Martyrs, who attended her in her last week. She was buried on the day after her death in the western cemetery outside the city, beside the small marker she had set up there twenty-one years ago for the boy Adeodatus, who as you will remember was also buried in that ground.*

Augustine set the letter down on the desk. He closed his eyes for a count of perhaps thirty. His hands had taken the second sheet up before his mind had told them to and were holding it without reading it. He set them flat on the desk on either side of the letter and waited for them to be willing to move.

He had not known.

Adeodatus had died at Tagaste in the late summer of 389. The boy had been seventeen years and four months. The fever had taken him in five days. Augustine had buried him in the small Catholic cemetery at Tagaste, beside the family plot, and had composed the inscription himself. The composing of the inscription had taken him an evening; he could remember the lamp by which he had written it. He had not known that, in the same autumn, somewhere in Carthage, she had also been at a graveside. She had paid a stoneworker for a marker. She had visited it. She had been buried beside it in the November before this morning, and the marker was the only stone in any cemetery in the world that named the boy in her hand. In twenty-one years no person who could have told him had told him, because he had not given any person reason to believe he would have wished to be told.

He opened his eyes and read on.

*She left no testament because she had little to leave. The small house was held in lease from the master's estate. The household goods are few. The women who lodged in the house at the time of her death — six of them, of various conditions — have been given two weeks to find other lodging.*

*Of my sister herself. I will say to you what I will say to no other man, because she asked me, in the second-last week of her life, to send to you on her death the letter you wrote her in the spring of 383, which she had kept folded inside the chest at the foot of her bed since she received it, and which I now enclose with this letter — she did not ask me to write to you of anything else, but the priest of the Two Martyrs has counselled me to write what is in this letter, judging that some of it would matter to you for reasons of conscience that he did not invite me to inquire into.*

*She kept the small house for nineteen years. She did not marry. She ran the house as a place of lodging for women in difficulty, who could pay what they could pay and who in the months when they could not pay were allowed to stay and to make up the rent later, and many of them made the rent in the manner that women in the harbour quarter make the rent when the fish-trade is poor and the textile work has dried up. My sister did not enquire into the manner of the rent. She fed the women who came in. She nursed those who fell ill. She buried two with her own hands in the western cemetery in the years when the plague was bad and the city would not pay for the burials of harbour women.*

*She was, as you will remember, of a quiet and stubborn character, much given to silence. She did not at any time in nineteen years send to you for help, although she had your address. She did not send. She did not write. She did not, in my hearing, ever speak your name. She referred to you, when she had occasion to refer to you, as the bishop in Numidia, as if you were a public man whose office was the only thing of you that survived for her.*

*The chest at the foot of her bed I have not yet opened. The contents, my sister told me before the fever took her speech, are: the letter you wrote her in 383, which I now enclose; a wooden cup that you once sent her by a courier with a small sum of money in the year that Adeodatus was born; a hair-comb of bone of no special value; certain papers of accounts from the lodging-house. There is also a small bronze hand-mirror, which she said you had given her in the year of your meeting in Carthage, and which she said might be returned to you if you wished.*

*Bishop, write to me by return what your wish is. The chest weighs perhaps three pounds. The estate will pay the carriage.*

*I am your servant in our Lord,*
*Anthony.*

He read the letter through a second time, then a third. On the third reading he found that he was reading not the letter but the spaces between the lines, and he set it down. He had assumed — without permitting himself to examine the assumption — that she had returned to her father's house in Thagaste, that some kinsman had married her, that she had borne other children. He had constructed a life for her in the back of his mind in the years he could not avoid thinking of her, and the constructed life was not this life. The life she had had was a life she had built herself, in a small house, for nineteen years, on the lower street behind the harbour, and at the end of it she had been buried beside the boy. She had not asked him for any part of it. She had not, in nineteen years, sent.

The second sheet was the enclosure: his own letter to her of 383, in his own hand, twenty-seven years gone.

He recognised the hand at the first letter. He had been twenty-eight when he wrote it. It had been written from Rome in the spring after he had crossed from Carthage, before he met Ambrose, before he knew what would happen. Eight lines. Not a love letter. A domestic letter of small things. The wine at Rome was bad. He had taken a room near the Caelian Hill. His teaching was going badly. He had had a tooth pulled by a man in the Subura who had hurt him. Adeodatus was to be told that the small wooden horse with the broken leg was at the bottom of his father's writing chest if he had been looking for it. The letter ended: *Mind yourself in the heat. The heat in Carthage in May has always been too much for you. I will write again at the next post.* He had not signed his first name. He had signed: *Aurelius, who loves you.*

He had not, as far as he could remember, written to her again from Rome. He had not written from Milan. He had had her sent back to Carthage from Milan in the spring of the year that followed this letter, and he had not written then. He had not written after.

The page was light in his hands. The ink had stayed black; the parchment was a little yellow at the edges and a little soft from twenty-seven years of being folded. He could feel the four creases the page had been folded in. She had folded it once and had not refolded it differently in twenty-seven years, which meant she had read it not by unfolding it again and again — which would have given it new creases — but by leaving it folded and going to it for the closing line, *Aurelius, who loves you,* and refolding it along the same four lines.

He did not weep. He did not pray. He kept his hands flat on the desk, one on each side of the page, and looked at the closing line. The parchment was the temperature of the room, not the temperature of a hand, and he understood that she had not been holding it when she died. After some time the bell for the third hour began, or he heard it begin, and he let it ring.

After some further minutes he reached out and folded the two letters together along the brother's fold. He stood up, went to the lower shelf, lifted Ambrose's *De Officiis* from between the desert fathers, parted the binding, and laid the folded letters flat in the cavity beside the disc and the leather packet. He drew the cover back over and tucked the threads into the original holes. He set the codex back on the lower shelf between the desert fathers.

The two of them rested there together — the letter and the cargo — and he turned away from the shelf without looking again at where he had placed them.

---

He took up wax and stylus.

He had been preparing, that month, a clean exemplar of the *Confessions* for Paulinus of Nola, who had asked some months earlier for a fair copy. The fair copy was being made by a junior scribe in the scriptorium, working from Augustine's working copy. Augustine had told the boy to bring him the working copy whenever a passage was about to be transcribed that Augustine wished to amend. The boy had brought it that morning, before the meeting, with a small wax tablet on which he had written the next passage to be transcribed and a query about a punctuation mark.

The next passage was Book Six, chapter fifteen.

Augustine took up the working copy, opened it, found the chapter, and read it through.

The chapter, as he had written it ten years ago, ran in its essential sentences as follows:

*Meanwhile my sins were multiplied; and she with whom I had been wont to share my bed, having been torn from my side because she was an obstacle to the marriage I was preparing to make, my heart, which clave to her, was broken and wounded and shed blood. And she returned to Africa, vowing to Thee never to know any other man, leaving with me the natural son I had had by her. But I, unhappy man, no imitator of a woman, unable to bear the delay of two years until she whom I sought should be of age to marry, took to me another woman, not a wife. Nor was that wound of mine which had been made by the cutting away of the former one healed; but after fervour and the most acute pain, it festered, and the pain became colder but more desperate.*

He had written it ten years ago. He read it now. The wound that festered was not metaphor. It was the wound, and was still festering, and would still be festering on the day he died.

The further thing was naming her.

She was *quaedam* in the working copy — *a certain woman.* She was *quaedam* in the published copies that had been going out across the Mediterranean for ten years, and *quaedam* in the copy the boy in the scriptorium was making for Paulinus. She had been *quaedam* on the day he had written the passage at this same desk in this same writing room, with the lamp on the same shelf, having decided in the moment of writing — he remembered the moment — to leave the name out. He had told himself at the time that to name her would be to give the reader access to a person who did not belong to the reader. He had told Possidius the same thing, the one time Possidius had asked. He had told the bishop of Stridon, who had not asked.

Sitting at the desk now, he was not certain.

He took up the wax tablet, scraped it clean of the boy's punctuation query, and set the stylus to the wax. The stylus was the bone one, warm where his fingers had been on it; the wax was cold. He set the bone to the wax.

He wrote: *Et illa in Africam redierat, vovens tibi alium se virum nescituram, relicto apud me naturali ex illa filio meo* — *And she returned to Africa, vowing to Thee never to know any other man, leaving with me the natural son I had had by her.*

Then he wrote her name.

The hand wrote it without difficulty. He had not written it in twenty-five years and had not, as far as he was aware, even spoken it aloud, and yet the hand had retained the shape of it the way a hand retains the shape of a child's name long after the child is grown. He looked at it. The letters sat in the wax. He saw how he might let the name stand, write the rest of the passage around it — *And [her name] returned to Africa* — and send the amended passage to the boy in the morning, and have the boy transcribe it into the fair copy for Paulinus, and have the fair copy go out to Nola, and have the name go out from there into the copies that Paulinus's community would copy, and into the *Confessions* as it would be read in the next century and the century after, and into the historical record of the Latin Church for as long as the Latin Church should make copies. The move was one stroke of his pen.

A minute passed. Maybe two. The lamp on the desk guttered once. The room held nothing but his hand and the name he had written in the wax.

Then he drew the flat blade of the stylus through the wax across her name, hard enough that the wax buckled, three short strokes, the way one strikes through a clerk's error. The wax made a small sound under the blade. The name was no longer legible.

In the cleared space above the strike-through he wrote: *quaedam.*

He read what he had written. *Et quaedam in Africam redierat.* *And a certain woman returned to Africa.*

After some time he set the stylus down. The wax would be re-melted by the boy in the scriptorium tomorrow morning. The fair copy would say *quaedam.* The published *Confessions* would continue to say *quaedam.* The historical record of the Latin Church would, in the matter of this woman, continue to be silent, and the silence would continue to be the silence he had made.

*Forgive me.*

He could not finish the sentence. He did not know to whom the sentence was addressed. He did not write it down.

He capped the wax tablet by reflex and set it on the corner of the desk where the boy would see it. The stylus went back in its place.

---

The clerk of the procurator's office arrived in the late afternoon, having ridden up from the harbour and presented himself first at the gate and then at the writing room with the gate-keeper's introduction. He was a small man of perhaps thirty-five in a clerk's tunic with the procurator's seal pinned at the shoulder. His name was Julian. He had been to the writing room twice before in the previous year, with similar business; he and Augustine had a working acquaintance.

*Bishop.*

*Julian. Sit. Wine?*

*Thank you, no, Bishop, I am to be back at the harbour within the hour. I have one writ for your countersignature.*

He took a folded parchment from his satchel and laid it on the desk, set in the middle, and sat back. The seal was the procurator's seal in red wax, and below it the smaller seal of the Numidian treasury official. The fold was the standard imperial fold. Augustine reached out and unfolded the writ.

It was a writ of confiscation under the Edict of Unity of February 405 — for the seizure of the grain stores held in the storehouse attached to the Donatist place of worship at the village of Tibilis Minor in the territory of Calama. The grain in question was the seed-grain set aside for spring sowing and the winter-grain held against the lean months of February and March. The village was a Donatist congregation of fifteen households, as enumerated in the procurator's last visitation. The writ required the countersignature of the senior bishop or of the bishop assigned to the case.

Augustine had been the bishop assigned to Donatist cases in the territory of Calama since 408, when Possidius had asked, on his own initiative, that the work of countersignature be transferred to Hippo because the work was painful to him and would be done less painfully by Augustine. Augustine had agreed. He had been countersigning writs of this kind, on average, two a month for two years and four months. He had countersigned, by his own count, fifty-six.

He read the writ through. He knew the village.

He had been to Tibilis Minor in the year of his consecration of the new font, in the spring after Easter, on a routine visit to the Catholic minority — six families of fishermen and one family of stoneworkers, who met in a converted stable on the western edge of the settlement. He remembered the elder of the Catholic congregation, a man named John, freedman of the local stoneworking estate, who had wept when Augustine gave the funds for the roof and had insisted Augustine stay to supper at his own house, where Augustine had eaten fish stew and a kind of barley bread that had had a small quantity of oil pressed through it before baking and had been very good. He remembered also the Donatist elder of the village, with whom he had had a brief and not unfriendly conversation in the small market square — a man whose name he did not now recall, who had been polite, who had offered him water from the village well, and who had said, at the end of the conversation, *Bishop, our grandfathers parted from your grandfathers. Let us not part further than they did.* Augustine had said: *That is in our hands.* The Donatist elder had nodded and walked away.

He read the writ a second time. The writ was correctly drawn. The grain quantities were specified — seventeen of seed-grain, forty-three of winter-grain. The procurator's enumeration of the fifteen households was attached as a schedule. The schedule listed only the names of the heads of household and the count of dependents per household, not the names of the dependents. The dependents totalled fifty-eight.

Fifteen households of fifty-eight dependents at Tibilis Minor would not, without seed-grain and without winter-grain, get themselves through to the next harvest by their own labour and any charity their Donatist co-religionists at Calama could afford them. He had been doing the arithmetic of these confiscations long enough to have a working figure. Of fifty-eight dependents, the loss between November and April would be between six and eight.

He did not put the figure into words. He took up the iron pen and dipped it. The ink was the office ink, which was darker than his own and which he kept because the office ink was harder to forge.

He countersigned the writ on the line indicated, in the standard formula:

*Aurelius Augustinus, Episcopus Hipponensis Regius, secundum misericordiam confirmavit. According to mercy, confirmed.*

He blotted the signature with sand. He tapped the sand off. He folded the writ along the standard fold. The folding had its own muscle memory now; the hand did it without him. He passed the writ across the desk.

Julian took the writ, glanced once at the signature, nodded, and put it in his satchel.

*Bishop. The procurator thanks you.*

*The procurator is welcome.*

*The visitation of the village is to be effected by the cohort under Quintus on December the third. The grain will move to the procurator's stores at Calama. The procurator will, as is customary, allocate a portion to the basilica's relief work at Calama as a gift in kind, which will appear in your monthly accounts under the usual head.*

*Thank you. Eraclius will reconcile.*

*Bishop, by your leave.*

*Go in peace.*

Julian rose, bowed slightly, and went out. The door closed. Augustine heard his sandals along the cloister and then on the stones of the courtyard and then, after a pause for the gate-keeper, the sound of his horse on the stones outside the gate, and then the sound of the horse going away down the road to the harbour.

He sat at the desk.

The wax tablet of the *quaedam* sat on the corner where the boy from the scriptorium would see it in the morning. The writ was in Julian's satchel and on its way to the harbour. The two objects were not on the desk together.

He went to vespers.

---

The vespers cycle that day contained the canticle of the Virgin. *Magnificat anima mea Dominum.* He sang it in his proper place, in the second voice as the cantor took the first. The verse came round: *He hath put down the mighty from their thrones, and hath exalted the lowly. He hath filled the hungry with good things, and the rich He hath sent empty away.* The Latin came out of his mouth in the shape it had come out of his mouth at every vespers in his life. His eyes went to the small flame of the sanctuary lamp on the high altar and stayed there. The flame did not move. There was no draught in the chapel and the flame stood still. He watched the flame and the verse went on through him and out of him into the choir. He could feel it in the back of his throat where it always sat at this verse, the particular opening of the vowel on *esurientes* — the hungry — and the word stayed on him a moment longer than it usually did before it resolved and the choir took it on. The flame still did not move. He pronounced the dismissal, took off his vestments in the small chamber behind the altar, washed his hands in the basin, and went to his cell.

He did not dream.

In the morning the boy from the scriptorium came to the writing room at the second hour to take the corrections from the wax tablet. The boy read the correction. He copied it carefully into the working notes for the fair copy: *quaedam*. The boy was a careful boy and did not inquire into the three deep stylus-strokes drawn through whatever had been written before. He took the tablet to the warm-corner of the scriptorium where the wax was re-melted on a small brazier for re-use. The wax was re-melted that afternoon. The tablet was used the next morning for a list of converts under instruction. Of what had been written on the wax in the writing room the day before, nothing remained.

The fair copy of the *Confessions* went out to Paulinus of Nola in the week before Christmas. The fair copy said *quaedam.*

In the second week of February, in the year following the writ, a small report from the cohort at Calama reached the procurator's office and was forwarded, in summary, to the basilica at Hippo as part of the customary monthly accounts. The report noted that the grain confiscation at Tibilis Minor had been effected without disturbance on the appointed day; that the village had not resisted; that the Donatist elder had wept but had not impeded; and that there had been, in the period from the date of confiscation to the date of the report, six deaths in the village among dependents under fourteen and over sixty, of causes consistent with the season. The report did not give the names of the six. Augustine, reviewing the monthly accounts at the end of February in the writing room, read the figure six and the figure for the Calama relief allocation and the figure for the basilica's grain stores, signed the bottom of the page in the formula he used for the monthly accounts, and turned the page.

Of the six, four were children. The youngest was a girl of eleven months whose mother, a Donatist woman of twenty-six named Anna, had not yet given her a name. The girl died in the second week of January of the dehydration that followed a dysentery the mother could not treat for want of broth. She was buried, on the day after she died, in the small ground beside the converted stable where the Donatist congregation of Tibilis Minor met for worship. There was no marker. The grave did not appear in any record. The mother did not survive the spring. Her name was not in any document signed by Aurelius Augustinus.

In the writing room at Hippo, on the lower shelf, between two works of the desert fathers, Ambrose's *De Officiis* sat where it had sat since November. Inside the spine were the disc, the leather packet with the Alexandria papyrus, and the folded letter from Anthony enclosing the letter Augustine had written to Una in the spring of 383. The *Confessions*, copied and sent, said *quaedam*. The codex looked as it had looked. No one took it down.

The lock at the writing-room door was tried twice more in December, and not again.
