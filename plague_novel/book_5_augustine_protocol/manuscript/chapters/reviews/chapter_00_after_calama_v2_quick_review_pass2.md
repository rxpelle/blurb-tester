### CRITICAL (2)

**1. [Developmental Editor] thriller plot / narrative drive** (Throughout; crystallized in the autumn coda)
- **Issue:** The chapter's inciting thriller event — a bishop beaten badly enough to require military escort, with the attackers unidentified and 'the men who commissioned the priests' still at large — is treated as administrative background noise. There is no investigation, no revelation, no escalation within the chapter's four weeks. The only plot movement on this thread is a parenthetical: 'In the autumn the proceedings concluded. Two priests were convicted. A third was exiled to Caesarea.' The crime that opens the chapter is resolved off-page, in summary, with a shrug. For an intellectual historical thriller, the inciting threat should drive at least one scene of discovery or danger inside this chapter.
- **Suggestion:** During the recovery weeks, give Augustine and Possidius at least one substantive conversation about the attack — what Possidius saw, who he suspects, what he fears. This scene can happen early in the second week when Possidius is well enough to talk but not well enough to be guarded. It should introduce a specific unresolved threat (a name, a document, a known agitator) that the reader will carry forward. The autumn coda's 'men who commissioned the priests, who had not been found' then becomes a live threat rather than a closed case.
- **Before:** `In the autumn the proceedings concluded. Two priests were convicted. A third was exiled to Caesarea. In his October letter Possidius wrote only of the roof which the rains had made acute and of the council preparation. He did not write of the men who had commissioned the priests, who had not been found.`
- **After:** `During recovery week two, insert a scene: Possidius, sitting up at the window, tells Augustine what he actually saw in the first moments — a specific detail (a ring, a family insignia, something overheard) that points beyond the priests to someone with access and standing. Augustine recognizes the name or the emblem. He does not say so. He writes the letter to the imperial vicar that afternoon with the additional detail included. Then the autumn coda: 'The men who had commissioned the priests had not been found. He had not, in the letter to Possidius, mentioned that he knew whose ring it was.'`

**2. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation — Thriller Stakes** (Throughout; most acute in the closing paragraphs)
- **Issue:** The chapter never states that Donatists attacked Possidius. The cause of the attack is only implied through a passing reference to 'legal proceedings against the Donatist priests at the next valley' and a closing mention of 'men who had commissioned the priests, who had not been found.' A general reader finishes four weeks of recovery without knowing who tried to kill Possidius or why. The thriller's core threat — ongoing, unnamed, and unresolved — is invisible unless the reader already knows 5th-century African church history.
- **Suggestion:** One sentence early in the letter-writing passage (the natural place for context) should name the attackers and their motive. It doesn't need to be a lecture — it can be filtered through Augustine's operational thinking as he drafts the letters. Then the closing 'men who had commissioned the priests, who had not been found' lands as a live threat rather than an opaque administrative note.

- **After:** `In the autumn the proceedings concluded. Two Donatist priests — men of the faction that had declared the attack on Possidius a legitimate act of resistance against a compromised church — were convicted. A third was exiled to Caesarea. He did not write of the men who had commissioned them, who had not been found. The commission was the part that mattered. Priests did not act on their own.`

### MAJOR (7)

**1. [Developmental Editor] plot logic / mystery object mechanics** (Bronze disc section, paragraphs beginning 'After some time he went to the upper shelf')
- **Issue:** The disc is introduced with insufficient operational logic for a reader to invest in it as a thriller object. We know: Monnica gave it to him, it was warm that night, it has been cold for sixteen years, it is meant for a man Augustine must judge worthy. We do not know: what the disc does, what warm means beyond Augustine's mother's presence, what the recipient is supposed to do with it, or what stakes are attached to passing it correctly. 'The man it is for will know it' is evocative but provides nothing for a reader to hold. Three check-ins (first night, weekly summary, November coda) with no new information on any of them erodes rather than builds mystery.
- **Suggestion:** On the first introduction, give Augustine one specific thing he knows about the disc's purpose — not everything, but one concrete fact that creates a thriller question. Does it open something? Does it authenticate something? Does its warmth indicate proximity to a specific lineage or text? The weekly check-in ('The disc, when he checked it that night after compline, was cold') and the November check-in need to differ from each other — on the second, Augustine should notice or think something new, even a small wrong inference, so the reader senses the mystery moving.
- **Before:** `'Carry it,' Monnica had said. 'The man it is for will know it. He will not be you. If he does not come in your lifetime, you will pass it to a man you have judged worthy of carrying it after you.'`
- **After:** `'Carry it,' Monnica had said. 'The man it is for will know it by what it does in his hand. He will not be you. There is something in him it will recognise — something he will have found, or will be about to find — and the bronze will tell you before he does.' She had not said what that thing was. He had been watching for it for sixteen years.`

Everything else (the fever that didn't come, the physician's visits, the letters Possidius dictates) is summary. As written, the middle is a beautifully rendered holding pattern.
- **Suggestion:** Replace approximately one page of the recovery summary with a single scene from the third week — Possidius and Augustine at the desk together, a document between them, Possidius saying something about the attack or its commissioners that Augustine receives without showing what he knows. This scene anchors the compression around it and gives the reader something to hold across the time jump to the departure.
- **Before:** `By the third week he was at the writing room with Augustine at the noon hour... They read the dispatches together. They drafted replies.`
- **After:** `On the second day of the third week Possidius asked for the file of dispatches from the valley diocese. Augustine gave it to him. Possidius read the third letter twice and set it face down on the desk. 'The man who wrote this was at the basilica the morning of the attack,' Possidius said. 'He was at the door when I came out.' He did not say the man's name. Augustine read the return address on the outside of the letter. He took the letter from the desk and put it in the locked drawer under the tray. He gave Possidius the next dispatch.`

**3. [Developmental Editor] structure / thematic coherence** (The disc thread and the Stephen/Possidius thread, entire chapter)
- **Issue:** The chapter carries two major threads — the disc and the man it awaits; Augustine's recognition of Stephen's love for Possidius — and they never speak to each other. The chapter house epiphany ('He had been watching the men of his household for those sixteen years without seeing what was in front of him') gestures at a connection but doesn't make it. If Augustine has failed to see what was in front of him in the household, and the disc is for a man he must judge worthy who will come from a direction he hasn't been watching, the two threads are pointing at the same insight. That insight needs one moment of explicit convergence — not stated flatly, but earned by juxtaposition.
- **Suggestion:** In the chapter house scene, let Augustine's recognition include a beat where he considers, and dismisses, the thought that the disc could be for Stephen or Possidius. He has been watching the household for the man, and has not seen these two men clearly. The dismissal itself should be slightly uncertain — not definitive — which opens the question for the reader without answering it.
- **Before:** `He would, in the years to come, watch better. The thought arrived in him as a small specific intention and stayed.`
- **After:** `He would, in the years to come, watch better. The thought arrived with something else behind it — a question he did not form fully because the forming of it would require him to name what he had been watching at the bedside, and he was not, today, prepared to name it. The disc was for a man he had not yet recognised. He had been standing in rooms with his men for eight years and had not, until this morning, seen them. The thought stayed in the chapter house with him. He did not take it to the writing room.`

**5. [Immersive Experience Editor] Visceral Moment / Emotional Resonance** (Paragraph beginning 'On the sixth day, at the noon visit, Possidius was asleep.')
- **Issue:** The recognition of what Stephen carries is the chapter's central emotional revelation — Augustine sees, in his most trusted deacon of nine years, an unexpressed love for his nearly-dead best friend, and understands that he has been blind to it for weeks. The coal-under-ash metaphor is precise and good. But the prose moves from recognition to recovery in two sentences. 'He sat with it on the stool. He did not let it travel into his face.' We don't inhabit the cost of that holding. The restraint is appropriate to period and character, but we need to feel it costing him something before he stands.
- **Suggestion:** Give Augustine's body one more specific physical registration before he stands — not emotion named, but sensation noticed. The restraint remains; the body pays the bill for it.
- **Before:** `He sat with it on the stool. He did not let it travel into his face. He did not look at Stephen and he did not look at Possidius.`
- **After:** `He sat with it on the stool. The room had not changed. The light through the high window was the light it had been when he came in. He did not let the thing travel into his face; he held it in the chest where it had arrived and breathed through the holding until the breathing was ordinary again. He did not look at Stephen and he did not look at Possidius.`

**6. [Accessibility Editor] Concept Accessibility — The Central MacGuffin** (The disc passages: 'After some time he went to the upper shelf...' through 'He went to compline'; also the November closing)
- **Issue:** The bronze disc is introduced without explaining its mechanism. The reader is told the disc is cold, that it was warm once (when Monnica held it at Ostia), and that 'the man it is for will know it.' But the operative logic — that warmth signals the right person, cold signals the wrong one — is never stated. Augustine checks the disc repeatedly and the word 'cold' accumulates weight through repetition, but a general reader has no reason to understand that cold means anything beyond room temperature. The thriller's central mystery object fails to function as mystery because the reader doesn't know what question it's supposed to be answering.
- **Suggestion:** One sentence — Augustine's own internal articulation of the mechanism, even in the half-committed way he holds most things — would anchor all subsequent disc references. It could come the first time he opens the box, as a memory of what his mother told him the warmth meant.
- **Before:** `'Carry it,' Monnica had said. 'The man it is for will know it. He will not be you. If he does not come in your lifetime, you will pass it to a man you have judged worthy of carrying it after you.'`
- **After:** `'Carry it,' Monnica had said. 'The man it is for will know it. He will not be you. The bronze will tell you when he is near — it will warm in his presence the way it warmed in mine. If he does not come in your lifetime, you will pass it to a man you have judged worthy of carrying it after you.' He had understood, at Ostia. He had not understood what sixteen years of cold would feel like.`

**7. [Accessibility Editor] Exposition Balance — Jargon Dump, Misplaced Stakes** (Paragraph beginning 'The four letters took the afternoon' / the letter-drafting passage)
- **Issue:** The Donatist schism — the theological and political conflict that motivates the entire attack — is explained in a single dense subordinate clause buried inside an administrative inventory of letters: 'the African schism that held the catholic bishops to be traitors who had handed over the Scriptures under Roman persecution and were, on Donatist reading, no longer the church.' This is accurate but it arrives as a parenthetical, not as stakes. The reader is given the what (schism, betrayal, traitors) without the so-what (and that is why Possidius nearly died). It reads like a footnote rather than a threat.
- **Suggestion:** Pull the Donatist explanation out of the letter-inventory and give it a sentence of its own, connected explicitly to the attack. The explanation itself is good — it just needs to be placed where it earns its keep: not in a list of correspondence, but when the threat becomes active in the narrative.
- **Before:** `One to the imperial vicar at Sirmium, drafted now and locked in the desk until the senior bishop's response came back — the vicar would, in the spring, hear Augustine's case for state action against the Donatists, the African schism that held the catholic bishops to be traitors who had handed over the Scriptures under Roman persecution and were, on Donatist reading, no longer the church.`
- **After:** `One to the imperial vicar at Sirmium, drafted now and locked in the desk until the senior bishop's response came back. The Donatists held that the catholic bishops had betrayed the faith by handing over the Scriptures to Roman authorities during the persecutions — traitors, in their reading, who had forfeited the right to call themselves the church. The attack on Possidius had been their argument made physical. In the spring the vicar would hear Augustine's case for state action. The letter had to be right.`

**3. [Immersive Experience Editor] Sensory Grounding / Interior Life** (Paragraphs beginning 'The afternoon went the way an afternoon went...' through 'The four letters took the afternoon.')
- **Issue:** Augustine writes four politically significant letters with his closest friend lying wounded two cells away. The section is informationally strong — we understand the letters and their stakes. But there is no sensory texture, no moment of hesitation, no physical signal that the man writing these letters is doing so against the background of a friend's injury. The emotional suppression is intentional and appropriate to character; but suppression should cost the body something visible.
- **Suggestion:** One physical detail — pen held over parchment, ink drying at the nib, a pause before a name — would anchor the section without sentimentalizing it.
- **Before:** `The four letters took the afternoon.`

**4. [Immersive Experience Editor] Sensory Fatigue / Repetition** (Five instances from 'The disc was cold' through the November closing)
- **Issue:** 'The disc was cold' is a deliberately sustained refrain and it works powerfully through the first three iterations — the contrast with Monnica's warmth is the thriller's engine. But by the fourth and fifth instances the phrase has been naturalized. The reader anticipates it. 'Cold' has become information rather than sensation. The November closing — which should be the chapter's most resigned and interior instance — lands no differently than the earlier ones.
- **Suggestion:** Vary the final instance in description or in Augustine's physical reception of it, to distinguish resignation from the earlier vigils.
- **Before:** `The disc was cold, as he had known it would be.`
- **After:** `He held the disc. It was cold in the way it had been cold every time since Ostia — the fact as bare as the metal, nothing to read into it that he had not already read.`

**5. [Immersive Experience Editor] Scene Grounding / Emotional Transition** (Sentence 'He went back through the gate. He went to the writing room.')
- **Issue:** This is the chapter's most emotionally concentrated transition: Possidius has just left; Stephen was not at the gate; Augustine walks back alone through the arch. It is rendered in two procedural sentences with no physical grounding. The reader doesn't feel what it is to make that walk — from the public farewell back into the interior of the household, now altered.
- **Suggestion:** One physical detail in the transition — the gate's shadow, the sound of wheels going past the first house, the courtyard opening out — gives the walk its weight without commentary.
- **Before:** `He went back through the gate. He went to the writing room.`
- **After:** `He went back through the gate. The shadow of the arch crossed him. The wheels were already past the first house on the road; he did not turn to confirm this. He went to the writing room.`

**6. [Immersive Experience Editor] Showing vs Telling / Interior Life** (Paragraph beginning 'He ate at the writing-room desk.')
- **Issue:** 'He ate the bread without paying attention to it' is a telling statement about dissociation. This is the chapter's most unglamorous moment — a bishop eating bread and cheese alone at a desk, his wounded friend asleep two walls away — and it has the potential to be quietly devastating. It is given two sentences and then dispensed with.
- **Suggestion:** One more physical detail about the bread itself, or about the act of chewing without taste, would make this the small human counterweight to the formal machinery of the day. It doesn't need more space; it needs one more degree of specificity.
- **Before:** `He ate the bread without paying attention to it. He drank the wine for the warmth and not for the taste.`
- **After:** `He ate the bread. He did not taste it. The chewing was the chewing of a man whose mouth was doing what it had been told while the rest of him was two walls away on a cot. He drank the wine for the warmth and not for the taste.`

**7. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation — Augustine's Identity** (Opening paragraphs)
- **Issue:** The chapter opens with 'In seven years as bishop of Hippo' and treats Augustine's identity as established. For a series reader, this is fine. For a general reader encountering the chapter without prior context (reviews, excerpts, bookshop sampling), Augustine is simply 'a bishop' for the first several paragraphs. His status as one of the most significant intellectual figures of late antiquity — the fact that his thought will shape Western civilization for a thousand years — is invisible, which undercuts the stakes of his internal life and the disc.
- **Suggestion:** A single orienting clause in the first paragraph, or a brief author's note at the chapter head, would suffice. It doesn't need to be heavy. Even 'Augustine of Hippo, whose letters already circulated from Carthage to Milan' gives the reader a sense of the man's reach without stopping the scene.
- **Before:** `In seven years as bishop of Hippo he had received a great many carts on that road.`
- **After:** `In seven years as bishop of Hippo — and in the twenty before that as a teacher of rhetoric whose students were now priests and governors across the African dioceses — he had received a great many carts on that road.`

### SUGGESTION (3)

**1. [Developmental Editor] pacing / opening** (Paragraphs 2–4 (the extended flashback to how Augustine came to be at the gate))
- **Issue:** The opening scene — Augustine at the gate — is strong, but the second and third paragraphs immediately interrupt it with a detailed flashback (Eraclius, the three knocks, Stephen at the side table). The interruption is unnecessary: we don't need to know the mechanics of how Augustine received the news before we've seen Possidius arrive. The arrival is the scene; the receiving-of-news is preamble to it.
- **Suggestion:** Cut the flashback to one sentence ('He had been at the gate since the second hour, when the junior officer's news had come up from the harbour') and either fold the Eraclius/three-knocks material into a later scene, or remove it. The chapter earns more momentum by staying at the gate.
- **Before:** `The gate-keeper had been at the door of the bishop's residence at the noon hour after the junior officer had come up from the gate; Eraclius — who was nineteen now and had been at the basilica for nine months as a junior in the household — had received the news in the corridor and had brought it directly to the writing room, knocking three times in the way he had been told to knock when news could not wait.`
- **After:** `He had been at the gate since the second hour, when Eraclius had brought the news up from the harbour in the clean voice he had trained the boy to use for things that could not wait. That had been three hours ago.`

**2. [Immersive Experience Editor] Scene Grounding / Sensory Presence** (Long paragraph beginning 'The cart came up the harbour road at the second hour of the afternoon')
- **Issue:** This paragraph is the chapter's most information-dense passage and does essential structural work. But it is almost entirely procedural: the logistics of the escort, the pallet, the freedman, the officer. Augustine is watching all of this arrive but we don't experience the watching through his body. The excellent sensory detail — 'road dust, animal sweat, the iron-flat smell of used bandage linen' — comes only after the cart arrives. The act of standing and watching for two hours in August in Hippo is not in his body yet.
- **Suggestion:** Add a single line of Augustine's physical experience of the watching before the cart is at the gate — the sun on him, the specific silence in which he and Stephen have been standing, the quality of his attention — before the procedural inventory takes over.
- **Before:** `The cart came up the harbour road at the second hour of the afternoon, in the slow methodical way a cart with a wounded man on the floor of it came up a road that had not been mended in three seasons.`
- **After:** `The cart came up the harbour road at the second hour of the afternoon. Augustine felt the sun on the back of his neck and did not move. The cart came the slow methodical way a cart with a wounded man on the floor came up a road that had not been mended in three seasons.`

**3. [Accessibility Editor] Character-Driven Explanation — Stephen's Subtext** (Sixth-day recognition passage: 'On the sixth day, at the noon visit...')
- **Issue:** The physical cues for what Augustine recognizes in Stephen (shoulders down, hands quiet, mouth softened, eyes resting) are beautifully observed but very compressed. The deliberate non-naming is artistically correct — the moral weight of the chapter depends on Augustine choosing not to name it. However, for readers who process subtext less fluently, three discreet physical signals in rapid succession may not accumulate into recognition. The chapter earns the silence by requiring the reader to see what Augustine sees; a small fraction of readers will not make the leap and will read the passage as simply 'Stephen looked concerned.'
- **Suggestion:** Consider adding one more beat to the observation sequence — something Augustine has seen before in a different context, which he can anchor the recognition to internally without naming it. The 'he had seen it before, in his own life, in the years before his conversion' is the right gesture; it could be one concrete image more specific than that generality.
- **Before:** `He had seen it before, in his own life, in the years before his conversion. He had called it by its name then. He did not call it by its name now.`
- **After:** `He had seen it before, in his own life, in the years before his conversion — in the way he had looked at Adeodatus's mother in the first months at Carthage, before he had known what the looking meant. He had called it by its name then. He did not call it by its name now.`
