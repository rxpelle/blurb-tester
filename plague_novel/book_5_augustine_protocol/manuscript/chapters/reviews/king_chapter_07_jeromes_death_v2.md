## Voice & Narrative Persona (Grade: C+)

This chapter is executed by a skilled writer who has deliberately erased themselves from the prose — and that's the core problem. King's principle isn't that the narrator should be invisible; it's that the CRAFT should be invisible while the storyteller remains present. Here, we have the opposite: the craft is visible everywhere (the balanced clauses, the repeated structural tics, the mannered rhythms), while the person telling the story is nowhere. There is no face behind this prose. No editorializing, no intrusion, no opinion, no humor, no warmth, no danger. The narrator functions as a glass wall between the reader and the characters — technically clear, but cold. The chapter has real emotional intelligence — Jerome's death is moving, Augustine's grief lands — but it arrives at you from a great distance, like a letter written in the third person about someone else's pain. The prose is literary in the worst sense King means: it's performing its own literariness. 'He sat with this.' 'He sat with them.' 'The room was the room.' These aren't invisible — they're stylistic tics that announce themselves on every page. The worst offender is the long institution paragraph, which reads like a parody of recursive modernist prose and says in 120 words what could be said in 20. Against all this, there are moments where a real voice breaks through — 'Death was approaching with the patience of a good scholar, taking notes as it came' is the best sentence in the chapter precisely because it has personality, because a storyteller reached in and editorialized. The sentence has a smirk in it, a dark intelligence. There need to be fifty more sentences like it. Right now there's one.

### Good Examples
> 'Death was approaching with the patience of a good scholar, taking notes as it came.' — The one sentence in the chapter where the narrator has an opinion. It's wry, specific, earns its metaphor. This is what the whole chapter should sound like.

> 'The silence was not a hole but a presence — the presence of everything that had been said sitting now on the desk in his own writing room with no further sentence behind it.' — Specific, earned, genuinely moving. The narrator is thinking here, not just observing.

> 'He felt the want of him.' — Three words doing the work of three paragraphs. The voice is direct, almost blunt. This is the chapter at its best.

> 'The ink stains under his fingernails were the ink stains of forty years of copying. They would not, when his body was washed for burial, come out.' — Physical, specific, no fuss. The detail earns its emotional weight.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been when he had taken up the work. He had assumed it would remain the institution it had been into his fifties, into his seventies... and the institution he had served was no longer the institution he had served.' — This is the chapter's lowest point. Recursive, self-regarding, performs profundity without achieving it. King would gut it to two sentences.

> 'He sat with this.' / 'He sat with them.' / 'He sat with the letter on the desk in front of him.' — Used three separate times as a structural beat. The first time it's a technique; by the third it's a tic the narrator is hiding behind instead of telling us what the sitting feels like.

> 'The room was the room.' — Sounds like it means something. Means nothing. This is the literary affectation King warns about: the sentence that performs depth by withholding content.

> 'He felt the weight of pattern: the sense of himself as one life inside the longer shape of a movement that had been unfolding for three thousand years...' — Abstract where it should be physical. King would ask: what does that feel like in the body, right now, in this room? This is the narrator explaining the theme instead of dramatizing the dying man.

### Suggestions
- Give the narrator an opinion. Once per page, let the storyteller step in and say something directly — a dark aside, a wry observation, a blunt editorial judgment. 'Death was approaching with the patience of a good scholar' works because someone said it. Do that more.
- Kill every instance of 'He sat with [noun]' as a structural beat. It's become your chapter's verbal tic. Replace it with something physical and specific each time — what does his body do? What does the room sound like? What does he smell?
- Gut the institution paragraph completely. The idea is sound; the execution is recursive vanity. Rewrite it as two sentences, blunt and declarative. Let the weight come from simplicity, not elaboration.
- Find the dark humor in Jerome. He's a dying old man who spent 70 years being wrong about almost everything. That's tragicomic. Let him think something acidic, something self-mocking. A flicker of mordant wit would make him feel real in a way that pure solemnity can't.
- 'The room was the room' needs to go. If you want a close, write something specific: what is in the room, what is it doing, why does it matter. The Septuagint on the desk, the broken wax seal, the unfinished letter — you already have it. Trust the specific things.

## Deep POV & Interior Monologue (Grade: C+)

This chapter is written by someone who knows how to write — the sentences are clean, the historical texture is real, the emotional architecture holds. But nearly every time the narrative approaches a character's skull, it bounces off. We are told what Jerome recognized, what he felt, what he assumed — we are rarely inside the moment when any of it happens. The psychic distance stays at Level 3-4 (narrator reporting character's inner life) for most of the chapter when it should be crashing down to Level 1 (we ARE the character). The long assumed-paragraph — 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties...' — is the clearest symptom: this is essayistic summary of a mind, not a mind thinking. Augustine's grief inventory — 'He had lost Monnica. He had lost Adeodatus.' — is a list, not an experience. King's rule here is simple: don't tell me he felt the weight of the room. Put me in the room so I feel it with him. The chapter comes closest to deep POV in the ink stains passage near Jerome's death and in Augustine's 'want of him' speech — both of which demonstrate that the writer CAN do this, which makes the choice to stay distant everywhere else feel like a missed opportunity rather than an inability.

### Good Examples
> 'His body was cataloguing its failures with the methodical precision he had once applied to manuscript variants.' — Jerome's scholarly mind filtering his own dying through professional habit. This IS deep POV: the character's unique mental voice shaping perception.

> 'Death was approaching with the patience of a good scholar, taking notes as it came.' — Metaphor generated from inside Jerome's frame of reference, not imposed from outside. The reader is behind his eyes here.

> 'He felt, instead, the small particular silence that arrived when a man one had been speaking to for forty years stopped speaking, and the shape of the silence was the shape of forty years of conversation, and the silence was not a hole but a presence.' — This is the chapter's best moment of interiority. Abstract grief rendered as a specific sensory/spatial thing. No filtering word needed here — we feel it.

> 'I would rather have him in Bethlehem, wrong, writing me letters I would have to answer with effort, than have his confession on my desk. The confession is the more useful gift. The wanting is the wanting.' — Augustine's voice thinking aloud, the syntax enacting grief's contradiction. This is the chapter landing.

> 'The room was very quiet.' — King's exact move. Don't explain. Let the object hold the feeling.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He felt something shift in his understanding the way a damaged passage of text suddenly resolves into meaning.' — 'He felt' is a door slamming between reader and character. Cut to: 'Something shifted — a damaged passage of text suddenly resolving into meaning.' Now we're there.

> 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been... He had assumed it would remain... He had assumed...' — This is a essay about Jerome, not Jerome thinking. Seven decades of intellectual reckoning rendered as a thesis paragraph. Where is the specific memory that cracks him open? What single image arrives? King would find the one concrete thing and make it bleed.

> 'reminded him too acutely of what he had once been' — Reported reaction. 'Too acutely' is the writer telling us the intensity rather than making us feel it. What does he see exactly when he hears those sandals? What does it cost him?

> 'He did not feel forgiveness. He did not feel the peace that the deathbed accounts of the Christian dead always claimed.' — Negating what isn't there. This is what King warns against. What DOES he feel? Not the absence of peace — the actual texture of what sits in its place.

> 'He had recognised, as he read, what the bishop of Hippo had been building. He had recognised it in installments. The recognition was complete now.' — Three sentences of summary where there should be one sentence of arriving. What was the passage where it clicked? Make us read that moment as Jerome reads it.

### Suggestions
- Cut every instance of 'he felt,' 'he noticed,' 'he recognized,' 'reminded him,' and 'he saw' and ask: what replaces it? If the answer is a sensory detail or a thought rendered directly, write that instead. These filtering words are the distance you're maintaining.
- The assumed-paragraph needs to be broken into a present-tense moment of recognition. Give Jerome a single specific memory — a face, a document, a conversation — that cracks the assumption. Summary is the enemy here. One scene beats seven lines of abstraction.
- When Jerome reads the Septuagint margins, let us read with him. Quote a specific marginal note — his own words, encountering him like a stranger — and let the gap between who wrote it and who is reading it do the work. This is King's move: the concrete object carrying the weight.
- The scrolls from Gaul, Hispania, and Carthage are summarized by the narrator, not processed by Jerome. One sentence of political summary per scroll, then cut to Jerome's mind working on what it means — his specific, idiosyncratic reaction in his specific voice, not a general response.
- The Augustine section loses POV during the losses list ('He had lost Monnica. He had lost Adeodatus.'). Choose one loss — one that rhymes with Jerome — and go deep into it for a single paragraph. Specificity > comprehensiveness. King never lists grief; he picks the wound and presses it.
- Jerome's final consciousness needs more texture before it goes out. 'Then the thought went and there was no further thought' is clean but earns nothing because we haven't been inside his consciousness closely enough in the preceding pages. The death lands flat because we haven't been living in him. Fix the distance throughout and the ending will hit.

## Backstory & Characterization (Grade: B-)

This chapter contains some of the finest character-through-detail work in the manuscript — and some of the most damaging exposition dumps. The split is almost geographic: the Augustine section (second half) earns nearly everything it claims, while the Jerome section (first half) frequently tells us who Jerome is rather than letting him show us. King builds character the way a sculptor works — he removes material until the figure is visible. This chapter sometimes does that. It also sometimes writes an essay about the figure instead. The letter Jerome writes to Augustine is a genuine triumph: a man revealing himself through action (writing by hand, not dictating; refusing to name co-conspirators; asking not for forgiveness but for utility) rather than through the narrator explaining him to us. But the paragraph about institutional change — 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been when he had taken up the work' — is a circular, self-analyzing spiral that King would cut in its entirety. Jerome does not need to explain his own epiphany to us in the abstract. The Septuagint margin notes already show it, in Jerome's own hand, across decades. That is the chapter at its best: backstory delivered as artifact, character revealed through the gap between the fifty-year-old's note and the seventy-year-old's correction in darker ink. When the chapter trusts that technique, it is excellent. When it abandons it for interior monologue and historical summary, it loses the reader.

### Good Examples
> 'The ink stains under his fingernails were the ink stains of forty years of copying. They would not, when his body was washed for burial, come out. He had once, in his fifties, tried to scrub them out for a formal occasion at Constantinople; the stains had not gone.' — Fifty years of a life in three sentences. The detail earns its specificity: Constantinople places him in the corridors of power; the failure to scrub tells us the work owned him, not the other way around. King-grade character revelation through physical fact.

> 'Jerome: "I will not see your face again." Marcus: "Father. The brothers will be at vespers in an hour."' — Marcus cannot say goodbye to a man who is his father and his master and his world, so he says the only true and deflecting thing available to him. This is characterization through avoidance. We learn Marcus entirely in four words. We learn nineteen years of relationship in the gap between what is said and what cannot be said.

> 'the boy in the night of the courier whose name he had not, in ten years, spoken aloud' — This is how King does backstory. Not an explanation. An oblique reference that implies a catastrophe, a grief, a specific human connection lost in a specific way. The reader does not need to know who the boy was. The weight of the unnamed name is the characterization.

> 'He had lost his right knee, in some functional sense, three years ago.' — Perfect. The qualification 'in some functional sense' tells us everything about how Augustine processes loss — with precision, with a kind of dark humor, cataloguing himself the way a scholar catalogues a damaged text. One sentence of character work.

> 'Hebrew "remembers" carries the connotation of active memorial, not passive nostalgia... [then, below it, in darker ink, written some years later:] Or perhaps the remembering is the rebuilding. Perhaps the act of transmission preserves more than the institution ever could.' — This is the chapter's masterstroke. Fifty years of backstory delivered as palimpsest: the young scholar's argument, the old man's correction, the evolution of a mind visible in the pressure of the stylus and the color of the ink. Augustine touching the indentation with his finger is the reader touching it too.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been when he had taken up the work. He had assumed it would remain the institution it had been into his fifties, into his seventies, into the centuries to come...' — This paragraph runs for six sentences and circles the same thought without landing. Jerome is thinking about himself in the abstract. King would replace this entire paragraph with a single concrete action: Jerome finding a specific note he wrote in his thirties, reading it, setting it down. The realization arrives through the thing, not through the character explaining the realization to himself.

> 'He had argued for their setting-aside not from malice but from fear.' — The narrator telling us Jerome's internal motivation is the cardinal sin here. We do not need this sentence. The letter Jerome writes to Augustine — 'I thought you were naive when you began to say these things... I have, over the seven years between then and now, gone from thinking you naive to thinking you mistaken to thinking you incomplete to thinking you correct' — already shows us motive through progression. Trust the letter. Cut the explanation.

> 'Jerome's pride was one of the few things that had not diminished with age.' — This is a thesis statement about a character, not characterization. It sits between two strong action-beats (Marcus entering, Marcus leaving without speaking) and it explains what those beats already show. King would end the observation at 'had learned not to offer assistance unless directly asked.' The sentence about pride is the author not trusting his own scene.

> 'The Visigothic king Wallia had completed the consolidation of his hold on south-western Gaul; Theodoric was now contending for primacy among the Visigothic nobility and the federate treaties with the Romans were under renegotiation. Two monastic communities in the lower Rhône valley had been abandoned in the spring...' — Three scrolls of historical summary dressed as scene. We are being lectured. King puts historical context in the body of a living character reacting to it — what does Jerome's face do when he reads about the Vandal galleys? Which detail makes him stop? Which one does he put down without finishing? The scrolls tell us facts. A character in physical contact with those facts would tell us everything.

### Suggestions
- Cut the entire 'institutional change' paragraph ('He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties...') and replace it with Jerome turning a specific page of the Septuagint and finding a single marginal note he wrote at thirty — a note that is precisely, embarrassingly wrong about something that has now come to pass. Let the object do the work.
- The three scrolls section needs a body in it. Jerome must react physically to at least one piece of information — put the scroll down, sit still, pick it back up, not finish it. Right now he reads three documents and we get three history lectures. Give him one specific detail that hits him in the stomach and cut the rest to a sentence.
- Delete 'Jerome's pride was one of the few things that had not diminished with age.' The scene earns that conclusion without stating it. Trust the reader to understand why Marcus has learned not to offer help.
- The opening paragraph's clause 'reminded him too acutely of what he had once been' needs to be cashed out or cut. What was he once? Show it in a specific recalled image — a memory of himself at thirty at a lectern, a specific room, a specific argument he won — or don't mention it. 'Too acutely of what he had once been' is gesture without image.
- In the Augustine section, the line 'He was an old man, and old men loss' appears to be a typo ('loss' for 'lose') but also reads as the one moment where the narrator steps outside Augustine's perspective to characterize him from outside. Decide: is this the narrator's voice or Augustine's thought? Either is valid but the current sentence sits awkwardly between them.
- The last image — the lamp burning alone in the empty room — is earned and excellent. Consider whether the chapter needs every paragraph between Jerome's death and Augustine receiving the package. The transition ('Hippo Regius, the third week of November 420') is clean. The courier detail grounds us. But 'the package had told him; the way a man knows, before he reads a particular letter, that the letter is a particular kind of letter' is the narrator explaining intuition rather than showing it. Cut the explanation. Let Augustine hold the package for a beat before cutting the seal.

## Setting & Sensory Detail (Grade: B+)

This chapter contains some genuinely excellent sensory work — moments that would make King nod — but it retreats into abstraction at exactly the wrong moments. The telling-detail instinct is alive and working: metallic guilt, dried-blood light, ink that will not wash out for burial. These earn their keep. But there is almost no smell anywhere in a chapter set entirely in places that would reek with recognizable life — a fifth-century scriptorium, a dying man's cell, an African bishop's writing room. More damaging: the author tends to go abstract precisely when emotion peaks. Jerome's death scene, the moment of reckoning at the Septuagint, Augustine's grief — all retreat to philosophical abstraction at the instant King would plant you harder in the physical world. The rule King lived by: the more weight a scene carries emotionally, the more you need the sensory anchor. The body going numb is not an excuse to abandon the body. The Augustine section is notably more grounded than Jerome's, which is backwards — the dying man's sensory world narrows and intensifies, it does not expand into 'the weight of pattern.' The chapter's best sensory work is scattered and brilliant; the gaps are systematic.

### Good Examples
> 'The taste in his mouth, when he thought of it now, was metallic.' — Taste is the rarest sense in fiction and the one that hits hardest. This single line converts fifty years of institutional complicity into a physical fact. It does exactly what King means by the telling detail: it cannot be summarized, only experienced.

> 'The cold of the wall came through the wool of his outer robe and into his shoulder blades.' — Three layers of specificity — cold, material, anatomy — in one sentence. The body's decay is never named; it's rendered. This is how you age a character without using the word 'old.'

> 'The light at this hour, through the small high window, was the colour of dried blood on the stone of the western wall. He had watched this light from this cell for fifty-six Septembers.' — The color choice does triple duty: it's accurate to late-afternoon desert light, it presages the death to come, and the second sentence converts the visual into fifty-six years of time. This is setting as biography.

> 'The ink stains under his fingernails were the ink stains of forty years of copying. They would not, when his body was washed for burial, come out.' — This is the chapter's best moment. One physical detail that contains a life, a death, a legacy, and a quiet, devastating irony. King would have written this sentence.

> 'The lamp burned low and burned even and did not flicker.' — The chapter's final image, and the author earns it. The restraint is right. An unflickering lamp in an empty room, after everything, is more affecting than any summary of what the room meant.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He felt the weight of pattern: the sense of himself as one life inside the longer shape of a movement that had been unfolding for three thousand years and would continue unfolding long after his name was forgotten. The thought brought neither comfort nor terror. It was simply what was, like the stone walls of the cell or the steady rhythm of his failing heart.' — Jerome is dying and the prose goes philosophical. A dying man does not feel 'the weight of pattern.' He feels the wool against his back, the specific Latin phrase bleeding through the stone that he himself translated, the smell of the lamp oil, the cold moving up from his feet. The author names the abstraction instead of building the sensation that would produce it in the reader. King would have kept us in the body.

> 'He lifted the cloth and opened the manuscript to a random page. The margins were dense with his own handwriting.' — The Septuagint is a manuscript Jerome has spent fifty years arguing against. It should have physical presence: the smell of old vellum and iron gall ink, the weight of the codex in arthritic hands, the sound of the pages turning in a shuttered room. Instead it's a container for the ideas. King would have made you touch it before he told you what it meant.

> 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been when he had taken up the work. He had assumed it would remain the institution it had been into his fifties...' — This paragraph is pure abstraction at the moment of Jerome's central reckoning — the moment the chapter exists to deliver. There is no sensory anchor anywhere in it. Jerome should be gripping the edge of the lectern, or the dust from the previous note should still be on his palm, or something. The repetition-as-form is doing literary work, but it is doing it in a vacuum. The body has been abandoned.

> 'The room was the room.' — This is a dodge dressed up as restraint. Augustine's writing room in Hippo has been established almost entirely through what is happening in it rather than what it is. We know there is a desk and a brazier that is unlit. We know nothing about the smell (sea air? lamp oil? the particular dustiness of African dry season?), the temperature on Augustine's hands, the sound the stylus makes on the parchment. 'The room was the room' is the author declining to do the work King would have done in one specific, well-chosen detail.

### Suggestions
- Give the scriptorium a smell. A fifth-century monastic scriptorium smells of iron gall ink, tallow smoke, old vellum, and the particular dampness of a room carved into limestone hillside. Jerome has spent fifty years in this smell. The first time he enters the scriptorium in this chapter, one good smell-sentence would ground everything that follows.
- During the dying scene, trade one of the abstract formulations for a specific sensory anchor. What Latin phrase is bleeding through the stone? Jerome translated those words. Let him hear his own translation coming back to him distorted by two feet of rock. That is the scene.
- The Septuagint needs to exist as an object before it exists as an argument. Weight, smell, texture of the binding, the sound the pages make in a shuttered room. When Jerome lifts the cloth, let us touch the thing alongside him. The physical fact of the object — the fifty-year-old manuscript, the ink he can smell aging — will make the marginal arguments land harder, not softer.
- The Augustine section is more sensory than the Jerome section — notably, Augustine feeling 'the small indentation of the stylus through the parchment' is the most tactile moment in the chapter, and it belongs to the man who is reading, not the man who wrote. Redistribute some of that physical specificity back to Jerome's sections, where the stakes are higher.
- Hippo has a harbor. Hippo smells like the sea, like North Africa in November, like a city that is not Bethlehem. One sentence of exterior geography for Augustine's location — not description for its own sake, but the specific sensory fact that establishes this is Africa, not a monastery in Judaea — would sharpen the contrast between the two settings and make the arrival of the package feel like the crossing of real distance.

## Tension & Dread Building (Grade: B)

This chapter builds tension the way a slow tide comes in — quietly, methodically, inexorably — which is exactly right for its subject matter. But King's genius isn't just inevitability; it's the needle of unease that gets under your skin in the mundane details. The chapter earns its dread through body horror (the cataloguing of Jerome's failing flesh), atmospheric compression (the September heat pressing against shutters), and two genuinely masterful quiet-before-the-storm moments: the exchange with Marcus and the final image of the lamp burning in the empty room. Where it stumbles is in the long middle passages where the prose becomes ruminative rather than loaded — tension bleeds out when a character sits with something too long without the world pressing in on him. The most dangerous structural problem is that the chapter has about four natural climaxes (the letter-writing, the deathbed, the package arriving in Hippo, Augustine writing back to a dead man) and handles each with roughly equal weight, which diffuses the escalation King would demand. Each beat should feel like a tightening of the rope. Instead it sometimes feels like loosening and re-tightening. The chapter's dread is real and sophisticated — it's the dread of irreversibility, of dying wrong, of the world outlasting you in a shape you didn't intend — but it needs sharper points of contact with the reader's nervous system.

### Good Examples
> 'Death was approaching with the patience of a good scholar, taking notes as it came.' — Death personified without melodrama. King would sign his name to this. It works because it's specific to Jerome, because it uses his own language against him, and because patience is more terrifying than violence.

> 'The light at this hour, through the small high window, was the colour of dried blood on the stone of the western wall.' — The ordinary made unsettling. Fifty-six Septembers of this light, and only now does it look like blood. The reader feels the finality before Jerome names it.

> 'I will not see your face again.' / 'Father. The brothers will be at vespers in an hour.' — This exchange is devastating precisely because Marcus doesn't break. He gives Jerome something to do with the time that's left. King knows that the plainly stated truth, met with practical kindness, hits harder than weeping.

> 'He had known it before he opened the letter. The package had told him; the way a man knows, before he reads a particular letter, that the letter is a particular kind of letter.' — Dramatic irony working perfectly. The reader is ahead of Augustine and watches him arrive at what we already know. The fifty-three-day transit of that package is horror in the form of geography and time.

> 'The lamp burned low and burned even and did not flicker.' — The room continuing its indifference after the living have left is the chapter's best image. King ends scenes this way — the world carrying on, uncaring, which is more frightening than anything that announces itself as frightening.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been when he had taken up the work. He had assumed it would remain the institution it had been into his fifties, into his seventies, into the centuries to come... and he had, in his sixties, watched institutions begin to change, and he had, in his seventies, watched them go on changing...' — This paragraph runs the tension directly into the ground. The repetition is meant to feel cumulative, but it reads as stalled. Every 'He had assumed' is a beat of pressure released rather than held. King would cut this to three sentences and let the white space do the work the repetition is trying to do.

> 'He sat with this. The chair was too hard. His back was cold against the wood. He turned a few more pages of the Septuagint and stopped. The dust of the lectern was on his hand.' — 'He sat with this' is a four-word tension-killer. It tells the reader the character is processing instead of making the reader process alongside him. The physical details (cold back, dust on hand) are good but they arrive after the sitting, not during it. The dread should live in the dust on the hand, not be announced by the sitting.

> 'The courier had crossed from Caesarea Maritima to Carthage and from Carthage by coastal ship to the harbour at Hippo, and the package had been on his person for fifty-three days.' — Fifty-three days. That is a terrifying number — Jerome's last words riding on a stranger's body for fifty-three days across a collapsing world. But the sentence delivers it as logistics. King would make you feel those fifty-three days as weight. Something like: 'Fifty-three days in an oiled leather pouch, crossing two seas while Jerome was buried.'

> 'Sleep came in fragments over the following days.' — The dying itself is handled in a paragraph. The chapter builds elaborately to Jerome finishing the letter, then compresses his final days to almost nothing. King doesn't let you skip the dying. The long approach is the chapter's strength; the shortcut through the end is its structural weakness. Those final nights — the hammering heart, the rejected poppy extract, the Latin translations sounding like echoes of a conversation he can no longer remember — deserve the same granular attention as the letter-writing.

### Suggestions
- Cut the long 'He had assumed' paragraph by two-thirds. Keep one sentence: 'He had assumed the institution would remain the institution, and he had been wrong in the way a man is wrong who has not yet seen an institution change — until his sixties, when he watched it begin to, and his seventies, when he watched it go on.' The compression holds the thought and keeps the rope tight.
- Give Jerome one more moment of specific physical dread before the deathbed — something intrusive and concrete that the dying body does without permission. King's characters are betrayed by their bodies in specific ways: a noise the throat makes, the hands doing something the mind didn't order. Jerome's body should embarrass him once before it kills him.
- The fifty-three days the package travels needs a sentence that makes the reader feel the transit as exposure — Jerome's confession riding through Visigothic Gaul, Vandal-patrolled sea lanes, a dying empire. One sentence of that and the package arrives in Hippo charged with everything it passed through.
- Replace 'He sat with this,' 'He sat with them,' and 'He sat with the letter' with the specific physical thing each man does with his hands while he processes. King never lets his characters sit with things. They hold objects too tight, they pick at edges, they lay hands flat on surfaces. You do this in the Augustine section ('put both hands flat on the desk') and it's the right instinct — apply it to Jerome's scenes too.
- The deathbed needs one more paragraph. Not sentimentality — the cold practical strangeness of dying. The moment when Jerome realizes his breath is counting itself. The specific texture of that narrow bed. King would give the reader something to hold in the dark of those last hours. The ink-stains-on-the-hands passage is the right material; it needs to breathe longer.
- The three scrolls from Gaul, Hispania, and Carthage are macro-dread delivered as pure information. Embed one detail in each that lands like a cold hand: not 'two monastic communities had been abandoned' but something specific and irretrievable — a title, a library now ash or flood, a named brother who walked away. The empire falling should have a proper noun for Jerome to hold.

## Dialogue & Voice (Grade: B-)

The dialogue in this chapter is doing two different things, and the gap between them is where the chapter lives and dies. At its best — the Jerome/Marcus farewell, the 'Jerome is dead' repetition — the dialogue achieves what King means by subtext: meaning conveyed through what a character does NOT say, through deflection, through the pause before an inclined head. At its worst, the dialogue is an essay with italics. The longer speeches — Augustine explaining the Septuagint's significance to Possidius, Augustine's self-analysis about vindication — read like a writer explaining his themes to the reader using a character's mouth. Possidius was in that room for twenty years. Augustine does not need to tell him what Jerome has 'given us.' That's 'as you know, Bob' dressed in ecclesiastical robes. The fundamental problem: every speech in this chapter is complete, polished, and self-contained. Real people — even learned fifth-century bishops in grief — trail off, restart, contradict themselves, fall silent mid-thought. The prose has a stunning stillness, but stillness is not the same as natural rhythm. The two characters are also vocally indistinguishable. Jerome and Augustine and Possidius and Marcus all speak in the same austere register, and while historical fidelity demands some formality, it does not demand that every man in the monastery sound like the same man writing letters in a quiet room. The letter — technically not dialogue — is actually the most emotionally alive writing in the chapter, which tells you something. The writer is stronger at formal written speech than at people talking.

### Good Examples
> '*Marcus.* / *Father.* / *I will not see your face again.* / [Marcus inclines his head] / *Father. The brothers will be at vespers in an hour.* / *Yes. Go to vespers. Pray for me. I will pray for you in such time as I have.*' — This is the chapter's best dialogue. Marcus does not deny, does not comfort, does not weep. He redirects to vespers. That redirect IS the grief. Pure subtext. King would approve.

> '*Jerome is dead.* / *Jerome is dead.*' — Augustine repeating Possidius's words back, not as question but as confirmation to himself. This is how grief actually sounds — the mind making it real by saying it again. Economical and true.

> '*It is permitted to feel both.*' — Possidius's single line after Augustine's long speech about wanting Jerome back despite being right. Three words. Doesn't preach, doesn't explain, doesn't expand. Just holds the space. This is a character voice that works.

### Needs Improvement
> '*He has given us the working notes for the work. He has given us his own arguments against our position, written in his own hand across fifty years, with his confession of their wrongness in the margins of his confession.*' — Augustine explaining to Possidius what they are both looking at, which Possidius can read himself. This is the author explaining the chapter's theme to the reader. Possidius already knows all of this. Cut it or find the real thing underneath it.

> '*Three copies of this letter. By three different routes. The Septuagint with the principal copy. Pack it in oiled leather. If the ship goes down between here and Africa, the leather will float for some hours. If it washes ashore somewhere, it may be found by someone who will know what it is.*' — This sounds like a man composing instructions, not a dying man speaking. It's perfectly formed, perfectly logical, perfectly ordered. A man two weeks from death with trembling hands dictates in fragments, not paragraphs. The information is right; the speech rhythm is wrong.

> '*I should feel vindicated. Jerome spent seventy years building the Christian world the wrong way... I do not feel vindicated. I feel the want of him.*' — The sentiment is right and true but this is an essay in dialogue clothing. Augustine is articulate, yes, but he's articulating too cleanly, too completely, like a man who has already written the draft. Give him one false start. One sentence he doesn't finish. Let him find the truth while speaking, not deliver it premade.

### Suggestions
- Break up the longer Augustine speeches. He's talking through grief in real time — let him start a sentence and stop. 'He spent seventy years building — ' and then a pause, and then the turn. Process should be visible in the rhythm of the speech, not just its content.
- Differentiate Marcus's voice from Possidius's. Marcus is young, nineteen years in service, learned to be silent. Possidius is a peer, someone Augustine walks to vespers with. These two should not sound like the same function. Give Marcus one word wrong, one syllable that's his alone — not anachronistic, just human.
- The Augustine-Possidius exchange about the codex's significance is 'as you know Bob' — Possidius is standing over Augustine's shoulder reading the same page. Either cut Augustine's explanation or replace it with something only Augustine would say that Possidius couldn't know: an interior flash, a memory, an association that doesn't need to be explained to Possidius because it's only for Augustine. Let the reader eavesdrop on a private thought, not a summary.
- Jerome's instruction to Marcus about the three copies needs fragmentation. He's dying. His breath is shallow. The thought doesn't arrive all at once. 'Three copies. Three separate routes.' Then a pause, then: 'Pack the Septuagint in oiled leather.' Let the weight of the moment break the speech into pieces — the pieces will carry more weight than the complete paragraph.
- Consider giving Possidius one moment of subtext that exceeds his usual function as responder. He has been with Augustine for twenty years. There is something he wants to say about Jerome that he doesn't say — perhaps something that makes him complicit, or something tender, or something that would comfort if said but that he withholds because Augustine is still working through it. Right now Possidius exists only to respond. Let him want something.

## Pacing & Structure (Grade: B+)

This chapter has a clean three-act spine — Jerome composing his confession, Jerome dying, Augustine receiving the news — and the scene cuts are handled with confidence. The Hippo section in particular moves with real rhythmic intelligence: the short exchanges between Augustine and Possidius hit like hammer blows, and the final image of the empty room with the burning lamp is textbook King, letting the physical world carry the emotional weight the prose earns. But the chapter has two structural problems that a King-level editor would flag immediately. First, there's a long reflective paragraph midway through Jerome's section — the 'He had assumed' passage — that runs one looping sentence through six recursive clauses without variation, stopping the reader cold at the exact moment the prose should be tightening. The technique is clearly intentional, meant to mirror Jerome's circular thinking, but it reads as showing off rather than serving the scene. Second, the letter at five-hundred-plus words is a pacing trap. It's important — the emotional center of the chapter — but it goes too long without interruption, and the momentum it took careful work to build bleeds out across it. King keeps letters short or breaks them with physical reaction. The three-scroll sequence (Gaul, Hispania, Carthage) also runs repetitively, accumulating information without building dread. The chapter knows where it's going; the problem is the speed at which it lets itself get there.

### Good Examples
> 'Death was approaching with the patience of a good scholar, taking notes as it came.' — Compression and earned metaphor. One sentence does the work of a paragraph. This is the prose being invisible while the meaning hits hard.

> '*I will not see your face again.*' — Short sentence at peak emotional moment. King's rule: when something matters most, use the fewest words. This line lands because everything around it is longer.

> 'He felt, instead, the small particular silence that arrived when a man one had been speaking to for forty years stopped speaking, and the shape of the silence was the shape of forty years of conversation, and the silence was not a hole but a presence' — The long sentence earns its length here because the rhythm enacts the meaning. It accumulates the way grief accumulates.

> 'The room was very quiet.' — Perfect final line. King ends scenes this way constantly. The camera pulls back to the physical world, the emotional weight sits in the white space after, and the reader supplies the rest.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been when he had taken up the work. He had assumed it would remain the institution it had been into his fifties, into his seventies, into the centuries to come. He had assumed that what he was building would be preserved by men of his disposition for as long as Latin was read. The assumption had been the assumption of a man who had not yet seen an institution change, and he had, in his sixties, watched institutions begin to change...' — This paragraph loops through the same phrase six times without gaining momentum. It wants to feel like a dying man's circular cognition; it reads like the author is demonstrating a technique. King would break this into short declaratives — 'He had assumed. He had assumed wrong. He had assumed it for fifty years.' — and let the fragmentation do the work.

> The letter ('Augustine, / I have read the seventeen books...') running to five-hundred-plus words without physical interruption — No reaction shot from Jerome, no trembling hand setting down the stylus mid-sentence, no pause. The letter is the emotional center of the chapter and it should be cut by a third or broken twice with Jerome's body failing him as he writes. Readers skip long embedded letters in fiction. King knows this. The letter earns its existence but not its length.

> The three-scroll sequence from Marseilles, Tarraco, and Carthage — Three reports in identical structure (geography, political situation, church status) arriving in succession flattens the pace to a newsreel. Each scroll should feel different in rhythm and urgency, or the third should land as a gut-punch contrast to the first two. Right now they read like research notes dropped into the scene.

### Suggestions
- Break the 'He had assumed' paragraph into short declarative fragments. Let the sentence length collapse as Jerome's certainty collapses. 'He had assumed the institution would hold. He had been wrong. He had been wrong for fifty years, and the wrongness was complete now.' Then silence.
- Cut the letter by 30 to 40 percent. The first and third paragraphs are essential; the middle paragraphs about 'men of his disposition' are explanation the reader doesn't need. Jerome would not over-explain to Augustine. The terseness is the trust.
- Differentiate the three scroll reports rhythmically. Make the first long, the second half as long, the third a single sentence — the African bishop's greeting as a gut-punch contrast to the political collapse in the first two scrolls. Let the reader feel the compression before Jerome processes it.
- Add one sharp physical anchor inside the letter-writing scene — a specific moment where Jerome's hand fails so badly he has to start a sentence over, or where he crosses out a word and the crossing-out costs him something. Ground the spiritual confession in the body.
- The opening paragraph establishes Jerome's age and condition with good efficiency, but the historical context (Rome fallen, Goths in Gaul) arrives before we're emotionally invested in Jerome. Move the geopolitical summary to the scroll-reading scene where it belongs and let the first page be Jerome alone with his hands.

## Themes & Subtext (Grade: A-)

This chapter is doing something genuinely difficult — arguing a theological and institutional thesis through character and object rather than through exposition — and it mostly succeeds. The central theme, that sincere men building the wrong institution can do more damage than cynical ones, never gets stated as a thesis. It emerges from Jerome's hands, from the ink stains that won't wash out, from a dead man's margin notes becoming evidence against his own life's work. That's organic. The Septuagint codex is the chapter's best symbolic move: an instrument of fifty years of intellectual combat repurposed as the most honest confession Jerome could make, and the author trusts the reader to feel the weight of that without explaining it. Augustine's grief — 'I do not feel vindicated. I feel the want of him' — handles the subtext of the entire chapter's argument about means and ends in a single spoken line. The closing image of the empty room, the lamp burning even, the unfinished letter on the desk, is close to perfect: the story meaning more than its plot without announcing that it does. Where the chapter loses ground is in one extended paragraph of authorial explanation — the 'assumption' passage — where the prose shifts from showing Jerome's reckoning to arguing it, and in Augustine's catalogue of losses, where names the reader doesn't know are asked to carry grief they can't quite hold. These are places where the author stopped trusting the scene and started explaining the theme. King's rule applies: if you've written the scene right, you don't need to annotate it.

### Good Examples
> 'Or perhaps the remembering is the rebuilding. Perhaps the act of transmission preserves more than the institution ever could.' — Jerome's own margin note, in his larger and less steady old-man's hand, written over his earlier certainty. The theme of the entire book arrives through a physical object, in the distinction between two scripts aging on a single page. The author doesn't comment. Perfect.

> 'The ink stains under his fingernails were the ink stains of forty years of copying. They would not, when his body was washed for burial, come out.' — Work inscribed on the body. The symbolic freight is earned because the ink stains are first a real, concrete, physical fact. King's principle: the symbol has to be a thing before it can mean something.

> 'I do not feel vindicated. I feel the want of him. He was wrong about almost everything that mattered, and he was wrong for the reasons of a man who wanted what we wanted and saw the means to it differently and chose the means he chose with his whole heart.' — The emotional core of the chapter's argument about institutions and intention, delivered as grief rather than as thesis. This is how subtext works.

> 'If the ship goes down between here and Africa, the leather will float for some hours. If it washes ashore somewhere, it may be found by someone who will know what it is.' — Jerome's instruction about the oiled leather. Meaning surviving catastrophe. The empire is falling, but the thought might still wash ashore. Never over-explained.

> The closing image: 'The bone stylus lay on the parchment of the unfinished letter. The lamp burned low and burned even and did not flicker. The room was very quiet.' — The story ends with an empty room and a lamp that doesn't flicker. No comment. The work continues without the workers. This earns its weight.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been when he had taken up the work. He had assumed it would remain the institution it had been into his fifties, into his seventies, into the centuries to come... the institution he had served was no longer the institution he had served.' — The chapter has already dramatized this realization. This paragraph argues it instead. The word 'assumed' appears five times in twelve lines. King: if you've built the scene right, you don't need to explain it. Cut at least two-thirds of this.

> 'He had lost Pelagius, who was not dead but was in a desert in which he would die without further attention from the world. He had lost Atticus and the silence at Sketis and the man at Lérins who had, in the end, narrowed what he taught.' — The reader doesn't know Atticus. Doesn't know the man at Lérins. The catalogue of grief is trying to accumulate emotional weight from names that carry no weight yet. It works for Monnica and Adeodatus because those are names we bring feeling to. The unfamiliar names ask the reader to mourn strangers on command. That's the author doing the work the reader should do.

> 'The thought brought neither comfort nor terror. It was simply what was, like the stone walls of the cell or the steady rhythm of his failing heart.' — 'Like the stone walls of the cell' is a stock stoic-death simile. The observation deserves a less expected anchor.

### Suggestions
- Cut the 'assumption' paragraph from 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties...' down to two sentences maximum. The scene has already dramatized Jerome's reckoning. Trust it. The repetition of 'assumed' is the author doing the reader's work.
- Trim Augustine's loss-catalogue to the losses the reader can feel: Monnica, Adeodatus, 'the boy in the night of the courier.' Drop or replace the unfamiliar names — Atticus, the man at Lérins — or give them one concrete detail that makes the loss land. 'He had lost the deacon of the synagogue at Hippo who had smiled into his hand at the rail of the basilica nine years before' is the right kind of specific. More of that.
- The 'dried blood on the stone of the western wall' simile for the evening light is strong. Use something equally specific — not 'stone walls of the cell' — when anchoring Jerome's deathbed equanimity. The equanimity deserves an image as precise as the hands or the ink stains.
- Consider whether Jerome's letter needs the explicit line: 'The letter I sent you in the year after the sack was a tactical letter... It contained sentences I did not, when I wrote them, believe.' The revelation is powerful. But the explanation of its purpose ('I wrote it to keep you cooperative') is slightly over-explained. The reader will understand the weight of confession without the motive being spelled out.
- The Psalm 137 margin-note moment — Jerome's two annotations in two different hands — is the thematic heart of the chapter and possibly the series. Make sure it gets white space before and after. It's currently slightly buried in the sequence of Augustine's response. Let it breathe.

## Cultural Specificity & Time Period (Grade: B+)

This chapter does something genuinely difficult: it achieves King-level specificity in a setting 1,600 years removed from any living reader's experience. The anchor points are real and earned — specific named figures, specific texts, specific geography, specific courier routes measured in days. The material culture of 420 CE monastic life is rendered through touch and sound and smell: ink stains that won't scrub out for a formal occasion in Constantinople, wax that has to be warmed against a lamp because old hands can't soften it with body heat, oiled leather to keep a codex afloat if the ship goes down. These are King details — practical, sensory, irreplaceable. You could not move this chapter to another century without rewriting it. The political texture is also strong: the Visigoths consolidating Gaul, the Vandal galleys ranging the western Mediterranean, federate treaties under renegotiation, two monastic libraries abandoned and their contents moved to Lérins. That's not ancient Rome as set dressing — that's a specific political moment. However, there are two places where the 21st century bleeds through the parchment and the immersion breaks. 'Insurance premiums' is modern financial terminology with no ancient equivalent and it yanks the reader hard. 'The long weight of its own contradictions' is Hegelian phrasing — it belongs in a cultural studies seminar, not in Jerome's dying thoughts. These aren't small slips. King would call them prose crimes. The psychological interiority is also occasionally too analytically clean for a man dying in 420 CE — the emotional register sometimes reads as contemporary therapy-speak rather than late-antique Christian thought. That said, the chapter earns its grade on the strength of its physical specificity and the authentic texture of the monastic-scholarly world.

### Good Examples
> 'The ink stains under his fingernails were the ink stains of forty years of copying. They would not, when his body was washed for burial, come out. He had once, in his fifties, tried to scrub them out for a formal occasion at Constantinople; the stains had not gone.' — This is exactly right. Specific, physical, irreplaceable, and it does double duty as character. King would underline this.

> 'the wax he had to warm against the lamp because his hands were too cool now to soften wax by holding it' — One sentence that puts you in an old man's body in 420 CE better than a page of description could.

> 'The courier had crossed from Caesarea Maritima to Carthage and from Carthage by coastal ship to the harbour at Hippo, and the package had been on his person for fifty-three days.' — Fifty-three days. Not 'several weeks.' The precision earns the reader's trust.

> 'Hebrew "remembers" carries the connotation of active memorial, not passive nostalgia' — The marginal notes in Jerome's Septuagint are a masterstroke of period grounding. This is what a fifth-century textual argument actually sounded like.

> 'two monastic communities in the lower Rhône valley had been abandoned in the spring. The libraries of those communities had been carried, by such brothers as had remained, into Lérins' — This is the political collapse rendered at human scale, through the thing scholars actually feared losing.

### Needs Improvement
> 'the merchants of Carthage were doubling their insurance premiums on cargo bound for Italy and Gaul' — 'Insurance premiums' is a modern financial concept with specific modern terminology. Carthaginian merchants in 420 CE would have spoken of maritime risk in terms of partnerships, oaths, and God's favor — not premiums. This is a significant anachronism that cracks the period immersion.

> 'the disease that killed all empires: the long weight of its own contradictions' — This is Hegel filtered through twentieth-century Marxist historiography. Jerome in 420 CE would have understood imperial collapse in terms of sin, divine judgment, and the movement of Providence — not dialectical contradiction. It's the author's 21st-century voice breaking through the character's.

### Suggestions
- Kill 'insurance premiums' and replace it with something like: 'the merchants of Carthage were carrying armed escorts on cargo ships bound for Italy and doubling the shares they owed their partners on arrival' — the same economic reality, rendered in period-appropriate terms.
- Replace 'the long weight of its own contradictions' with Jerome's actual interpretive framework. He would have thought in terms of Augustine's concept of the two cities, or in terms of Orosius's providentialist history — both of which were the actual intellectual currency of 420 CE. 'The disease that killed all empires, which was the weight of what the world promised and could not deliver' would be truer to his world.
- The psychological interiority in the final pages — 'neither vindicated nor bereaved nor surprised' — is clean and modern. Augustine in his grief would have reached for scripture involuntarily, the way a man reaches for a known handhold. A line or two of Psalm 137 surfacing in his mind, or a fragment of Paul, would both deepen the characterization and tighten the cultural grounding.
- Consider adding one material detail to the Hippo scene that distinguishes it from Bethlehem — different climate, different building materials, different ambient sounds. The 'warm November fog' is a good start but Hippo's North African sensory texture could be pushed further to reinforce the geographic specificity the chapter earns elsewhere.

## Prose Polish (King Polish) (Grade: B+)

This is sophisticated, restrained historical fiction with a strong instinct for clean prose — and that instinct makes the moments where the writing calls attention to itself hit harder. King's 10% rule applies surgically here, not brutally. The dialogue is excellent throughout: untagged, unadorned, carrying full weight without a single adverb to prop it up. The physical details of Jerome's decay are specific and earned. The ending image is genuinely invisible prose doing maximum work. But there are three recurring problems. First, a verbal tic: 'He sat with this / He sat with them / He sat with the letter' appears four times and starts reading like the author reaching for gravitas instead of finding it. Second, the chapter's worst single moment — the institution-repetition paragraph — is a rhetorical set piece that stops being Jerome thinking and starts being the author demonstrating. You feel the writer at the keyboard. Third, a handful of similes and constructions that are too beautiful too deliberately, the literary equivalent of a singer overselling a note. The bones here are genuinely strong. The trimming is the work.

### Good Examples
> 'Death was approaching with the patience of a good scholar, taking notes as it came.' — The metaphor earns itself because it uses Jerome's own identity as its engine. This is how King builds invisible metaphor: not from the outside in, but from the character outward.

> 'His body was cataloguing its failures with the methodical precision he had once applied to manuscript variants.' — Same principle. Prose you don't notice because the character and the image are inseparable.

> 'The lamp burned low and burned even and did not flicker.' — The chapter's last sentence. Prose disappears completely. The reader feels the stillness without being told to feel it. This is the target.

> 'Or perhaps the remembering is the rebuilding. Perhaps the act of transmission preserves more than the institution ever could.' — Jerome's marginal note in the Septuagint. Two sentences doing thematic, character, and plot work simultaneously without announcing it. King would approve.

> The Marcus dialogue throughout — no tags, no adverbs, full weight. 'I will not see your face again. / Father. The brothers will be at vespers in an hour.' Nineteen years of relationship in eight words. That is the craft.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been when he had taken up the work. He had assumed it would remain the institution it had been into his fifties... and the institution he had served was no longer the institution he had served.' — This is the chapter's worst passage. 'Institution' appears six times. The parallelism is constructed, rhetorical, literary. You stop reading Jerome's mind and start reading the author's architecture. King's rule: the 2nd draft is the 1st draft minus 10%. This paragraph needs to lose 40%. Cut to: He had assumed the Church he served would remain the Church it had been. That assumption had held until his sixties. It did not hold after.

> 'The silence returned like water flowing back into a depression.' — Too self-consciously beautiful. You notice it as a simile. You step out of the scene. King: 'The most important things to remember about back story are that (a) everyone has a history and (b) most of it isn't very interesting.' Same for similes — most of the ornate ones aren't earning their place.

> 'and the bell sounded the way the bell had sounded for nine years, and the bell would sound that way after Augustine and would sound that way after the basilica had gone, in the hands of whatever men in whatever century took up the work of replacing the bell when the bell wore out.' — The bell anaphora is the same problem as the institution paragraph: deliberate construction calling attention to the writer's hand. Cut it to two beats, not four.

> 'This is the most useful thing he could have done. / Yes. / He knew it was the most useful thing he could have done. / He knew.' — Two people saying the same thing twice in succession. The scene has earned restraint. This reaches past restraint into the self-conscious. Cut one exchange. The silence will carry it.

> 'The taste in his mouth, when he thought of it now, was metallic.' — 'Metallic' for guilt or regret is a literary fiction near-cliché. Jerome has spent fifty years with ink and parchment and stylus and wax. His regret should taste like something more specific to who he is.

### Suggestions
- Apply the 10% rule to the institution-repetition paragraph specifically. Jerome's mind wouldn't rehearse the abstraction this formally — it would land on the specific detail that cracks the assumption open. Find that detail and cut the rest.
- Vary 'He sat with.' Four uses turns a deliberate stylistic choice into a tic. Keep one — probably the Augustine scene where it lands hardest — and replace the others with what Jerome's hands or breath or body actually does in that silence.
- The loss inventory in Augustine's grief section is strong, but 'He had lost his right knee, in some functional sense, three years ago' is undercut by 'in some functional sense.' Either commit to the dark turn fully — 'He had lost his right knee, three years ago' — or cut it. The hedge kills the joke and softens the image.
- Cut one of the Possidius echo exchanges. 'He knew it was the most useful thing he could have done. / He knew.' is one beat too many. The reader already knows. Trust the reader.
- Replace 'metallic' with something period-specific and character-specific. Jerome's mouth. His trade. What does fifty years of wrong smell like to a man who has spent those years with ink?
