### CRITICAL (2)

**1. [Developmental Editor] anachronism — factual error** (Paragraph 7 (lower-shelf check before meeting Pelagius) and return scene after Cirta funeral)
- **Issue:** Augustine conceals the object inside 'Cassian's Conferences,' and checks it again on return from Cirta. John Cassian did not write the Collationes (Conferences) until approximately 420–428 CE — roughly a decade after this chapter's October 411 CE setting. The book cannot physically exist in Augustine's writing room. This will detonate against any reader with patristic knowledge and requires correction before publication.
- **Suggestion:** Replace with a codex that plausibly existed at Hippo by 411 CE. Ambrose's De Officiis works well and carries thematic freight — the author of Augustine's own conversion, a master of institutional power, whose title (On Duties) speaks to everything Augustine is about to do. Jerome's correspondence or Rufinus's Latin translation of Eusebius's Historia Ecclesiastica are also period-accurate alternatives. The choice itself can mean something.
- **Before:** `He went to the lower shelf, lifted Cassian's Conferences from between the desert fathers, parted the binding by an eighth of an inch, and looked.`
- **After:** `He went to the lower shelf, lifted Ambrose's De Officiis from between the letters of the desert fathers, parted the binding by an eighth of an inch, and looked.`

**2. [Developmental Editor] anachronism — vocabulary / unexplained object** (Paragraph 7)
- **Issue:** 'The disc was where he had laid it.' 'Disc' reads as a modern word and yanks the reader from 411 CE. Beyond vocabulary, this object is checked twice — before the meeting and again after an eight-day absence — marking it as series-significant, yet it receives no identification. A reader of this chapter alone cannot determine whether it is a wax seal, a bronze medallion, a coin, or something else. On the return check, Augustine verifies only that 'the binding had not been parted' — he does not confirm the disc itself is present. If it matters, that absence is a gap.
- **Suggestion:** Replace 'disc' with the period-appropriate noun for the object — wax tessera, bronze medallion, seal-disc of lead or bronze. Even if established in earlier chapters, one contextualizing noun grounds it here. On the return check, confirm the object itself: 'The bronze tessera was where he had laid it. The binding had not been touched.'
- **Before:** `The disc was where he had laid it. The leather packet was where he had laid it. The folded letter from Anthony was where he had laid it.`
- **After:** `The bronze tessera was where he had laid it. The leather packet was where he had laid it. The folded letter from Anthony was where he had laid it.`

### MAJOR (8)

**1. [Developmental Editor] narrative clarity — thematic crux** (Interior monologue paragraph beginning 'He thought one thing only...')
- **Issue:** The moral pivot of the entire chapter rests on: 'The work cannot be eaten. The work is the wrapper. The wrapper has to hold. I will have to destroy him.' This is the sentence Augustine's character arc has been building toward. 'The work cannot be eaten' is vivid. 'The wrapper has to hold' is vivid. 'The work is the wrapper' is opaque — a wrapper for what? The metaphor collapses at exactly the moment it needs to bear the chapter's full weight.
- **Suggestion:** Either clarify what is being wrapped — his doctrine, the African church, forty years of argument — or remove the middle sentence entirely. Three clean beats land harder than three beats with one muddled one. The chill is in the decision, not the metaphor.
- **Before:** `The work cannot be eaten. The work is the wrapper. The wrapper has to hold. I will have to destroy him.`
- **After:** `The work cannot be eaten. The man walks free and within ten years there is a Pelagian Latin church and the doctrine — the forty years of argument, the letters, the sees, all of it — splits at the seam. The wrapper has to hold. I will have to destroy him.`

**2. [Developmental Editor] pacing — thriller subplot underdeveloped** (Section beginning 'A man came up the western road yesterday...')
- **Issue:** An unknown man approached the basilica gate, did not enter, and departed south by hired mule. This is introduced mid-ride to Cirta and resolved in six lines before the party mounts again. The reader has no means to assess faction, threat level, or urgency. As a thriller element it carries significant structural weight — someone is already watching Augustine within days of the Pelagius meeting — but the chapter treats it as procedural housekeeping. It arrives and disappears too quickly to accumulate.
- **Suggestion:** Either expand to a real scene — give Possidius a beat of genuine unease, give Augustine one line of interior processing that tells the reader how he reads the threat — or hold this thread for Chapter 4 where it can open a full scene. If it stays here, Augustine's instruction ('He is not to act. He is to find me') needs one phrase that signals why: whose face Stephen might recognize, what network the watcher might belong to. The reader needs a hook, not a procedure.
- **Before:** `A man came up the western road yesterday at the hour Pelagius would have left the Salvia at Carthage. He took rooms at the inn at the second mile-stone. He paid for three days. He has not stayed three days. He went south at the second hour of this morning by a hired mule.`
- **After:** `Consider adding, after Augustine's instruction to watch the harbour: a single interior beat in which Augustine names — even privately, even ambiguously — which side he suspects sent the man. The fact of surveillance is less unsettling than the question of whose surveillance it is.`

**3. [Immersive Experience Editor] Showing vs Telling / Sensory Grounding** (Paragraph 5 ("When Eraclius had gone..."))
- **Issue:** The chapter's first emotional pivot — Augustine absorbing the news that Pelagius is in the cloister — is rendered in a single telling sentence: 'The room had not changed and yet was not the room it had been a quarter-hour ago.' The reader is told that Augustine's perception has shifted without being shown what he perceives differently. The writing room where he has spent two years preparing to refute this man has no physical presence — no smell, no quality of light, no texture — so the subjective disruption has nothing concrete to register against.
- **Suggestion:** Ground the pivot in one or two specific sensory details that dramatize the shift from inside Augustine's perception. The most effective technique here is to let his eye land on something he has looked at every morning that suddenly looks different — the iron-lock chest on the upper shelf is already established and available. Let the room stay physically unchanged and let that sameness become the disruption.
- **Before:** `The room had not changed and yet was not the room it had been a quarter-hour ago.`
- **After:** `The room had not changed. The ink smell was the same. The light was the same on the wall. He looked at the small chest on the upper shelf with the iron lock, which he had looked at every morning for two years, and it was a different object from what it had been this morning.`

**4. [Immersive Experience Editor] Scene Grounding / Interior Life** (Paragraph beginning "The older man was tall and lean...")
- **Issue:** The physical description of Pelagius is thorough — height, hair, coloring, tunic, bare feet — but rendered as a visual inventory, not as Augustine's experience of seeing this man for the first time. We learn the facts of Pelagius's appearance but never feel what it costs Augustine to look at him: where the eye goes first, what the body registers involuntarily. The one moment that does break through — 'Augustine looked at the hands and then looked away from them and then, against his choosing, looked back' — is the best line in the passage but arrives late, after the catalog is complete. For Augustine to enter his own courtyard and see standing in it the man he has spent two years arming himself against, there needs to be at least one beat where his body registers the reality of the man before the analysis takes over.
- **Suggestion:** Insert a single reflexive physical response early in the description — not analysis, a reflex — that shows Augustine's body receiving what his mind isn't ready to process. The bare feet detail is strong; let Augustine's eye go there involuntarily before it climbs to the face.
- **Before:** `He was perhaps fifty-two. He wore a plain tunic of undyed wool, belted at the waist with a piece of dark leather. His feet were bare. The soles on the courtyard stones were as calloused as the feet of a working ploughman.`
- **After:** `He was perhaps fifty-two. Augustine had not, until this moment, thought of him as a specific height. He wore a plain tunic of undyed wool, belted at the waist with a piece of dark leather. His feet were bare — Augustine's eye went there before he chose to look — the soles on the courtyard stones as calloused as the feet of a working ploughman.`

**5. [Immersive Experience Editor] Interior Life / Visceral Moments** (Paragraphs beginning "Then, Pelagius said..." through the start of Augustine's extended response)
- **Issue:** During what is staged as the most intellectually and spiritually disorienting exchange of Augustine's life, the text gives us the content of Pelagius's argument with almost no physical correlate of what it feels like to be inside Augustine's body receiving it. The fig-tree detail ('his eye had gone to it without his knowing') is excellent but arrives only after the speech ends. During Pelagius's actual argument — the widow, the child, the pagan, the one will — Augustine is physically absent from the scene. 'The sentence sat in him in a way the sentence had not sat in him when other men had said it' is a strong formulation but functions retrospectively. The reader understands Augustine is being reached; they are not inside the reaching.
- **Suggestion:** Add one brief physical beat during Pelagius's speech — not introspective analysis, something the body does without permission — that shows the argument landing before the mind has processed it. The existing fig-tree moment after the speech is excellent; an earlier involuntary physical response during the speech would give it greater force by contrast.
- **Before:** `A widow at her loom who does not curse her debtors is not, I think, doing nothing. These are small actions. They are not the great conversion of the heart that comes by the great grace.`
- **After:** `A widow at her loom who does not curse her debtors is not, I think, doing nothing. Augustine became aware, without having decided to, that he had pressed both feet flat to the courtyard stones. He did not lift them. These are small actions. They are not the great conversion of the heart that comes by the great grace.`

**6. [Immersive Experience Editor] Emotional Resonance / Sensory Detail** (Paragraph beginning "He wept, briefly, when the bier went into the crypt...")
- **Issue:** The Lawrence funeral is the chapter's only moment of unambiguous grief — the one place where Augustine is permitted to feel something without the bishop's office interceding. The prose gives it three sentences, and the second sentence immediately performs a meta-commentary on the weeping: 'He did not weep as a public gesture.' This categorizes the grief as authentic rather than showing its authenticity through a physical detail. The reader is told the weeping is genuine but denied the experience of it. This is the chapter's most significant missed emotional landing, and it is especially costly because the surrounding scene — the packed cathedral, the cold crypt, the old wax smell — already has the sensory material to do the work.
- **Suggestion:** Cut the interpretive second sentence and replace it with a single sensory detail that triggers the weeping without explaining it. The crypt's physical specificity is already established; use it.
- **Before:** `He wept, briefly, when the bier went into the crypt. He did not weep as a public gesture. Possidius, beside him, put a hand on his arm. Augustine accepted the hand.`
- **After:** `When the bier went into the crypt the cold came up and with it the smell of the old candle-wax, and he wept, briefly, without having decided to. Possidius put a hand on his arm. Augustine accepted the hand.`

**7. [Accessibility Editor] Jargon Management** (Paragraph beginning '*The grace was prevenient,* he said.')
- **Issue:** 'Prevenient grace' is the theological hinge of the entire courtyard debate — the term that names what both men are actually arguing about — but the word 'prevenient' is never glossed. A tenth-grade reader will likely read past it as ecclesiastical noise and miss that it carries a precise, load-bearing meaning: grace that arrives before any motion of the will, making willing itself possible. The word recurs three times without definition, which means the conceptual core of the dispute stays locked behind a single undefined adjective.
- **Suggestion:** Insert one inline gloss at first use via em-dash. The cold-voice style carries it without sounding like a footnote.
- **Before:** `*The grace was prevenient,* he said. *Without it, the will would not have moved. With it, the will did move.*`
- **After:** `*The grace was prevenient — it came first, before any motion of the will was possible. Without it, the will would not have moved. With it, the will did move.*`

**8. [Accessibility Editor] Exposition Balance / Jargon Dump** (Paragraph beginning 'The project is the following. There is a man called Pelagius the Briton...' — the dictation of works to Peter)
- **Issue:** The bibliographic catalogue inside Augustine's dictation stalls the scene at its most dramatically charged moment. Parenthetical provenance details — 'in thirteen books,' 'one is from Rome and two are from Carthage and which are, between them, complete' — add no characterization and pay off in nothing. A reader who has been fully absorbed in the courtyard scene is likely to lose the thread here before reaching the morally electric exchange about selective transcription, which is the best scene in the chapter.
- **Suggestion:** Cut bibliographic provenance entirely. Keep only what the chapter has already told us matters: the Demetrias letter, because you've flagged it as 'the letter that has done the most damage.' Let that detail do the weight-bearing work. Trim everything else to title and count.
- **Before:** `a tract called *De Natura* which I have in three copies in this room, of which one is from Rome and two are from Carthage and which are, between them, complete`
- **After:** `a tract called *De Natura*, of which I have three copies here, complete between them`

### MINOR (4)

**1. [Developmental Editor] pacing — death-plot threshold unmarked** (Section beginning 'In the morning Augustine sealed the seven letters...')
- **Issue:** The chapter reveals retroactively, after Mark has sailed, that two of the seven letters arrange for Pelagius's eventual confinement in the Egyptian desert 'in which a man of fifty-two who had walked barefoot from the Hippo harbour would not survive the second month.' The retrospective reveal is effective — the innocence of Mark's departure makes the disclosure more chilling. But there is no moment in which Augustine crosses from intellectual opponent to arranging a man's death. The intellectual-fraud dossier is one moral category; the Justin letters are another. The reader sees the execution but not the decision.
- **Suggestion:** Add a single interior paragraph — no more than three or four sentences — between the sealing and the calling of Mark, in which Augustine sits with the two letters that differ from the other five. He need not agonize; a cold registration is sufficient and more in character. The act of sealing the death-letters with the same seal as the doctrinal letters is the gesture the scene is built for and it needs one moment of stillness before it.
- **Before:** `In the morning Augustine sealed the seven letters with his own seal in the writing room, alone, and laid them on the desk in the order of delivery...`
- **After:** `Insert before this line: a short paragraph in which Augustine holds the two letters for Sixtus and Justin separately. He does not hesitate. But the chapter should register, even briefly, that he knows these two are not like the other five — and proceeds anyway.`

**2. [Immersive Experience Editor] Showing vs Telling** (Paragraph beginning "The reference was apt...")
- **Issue:** 'He flinched inside, in the part of himself that was not a bishop.' The thematic architecture here — bishop-self vs. man-self — is doing real work and the surrounding sentences are strong: 'The reference was apt' is excellent showing, 'He did not let it show' is clean showing-by-negation. But 'flinched inside' is a direct tell of interior state that weakens both flanking sentences. The phrase names the emotion rather than delivering it.
- **Suggestion:** Replace 'flinched inside' with a single physical detail. The bishop/man-self split should stay — it earns its place — but let the physical sensation carry the flinch rather than naming it.
- **Before:** `He flinched inside, in the part of himself that was not a bishop.`
- **After:** `In the part of himself that was not a bishop, something tightened once and did not unclench.`

**3. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation / Undefined Term** (Paragraph ending '...the synodical proceedings against Pelagius's teaching that we will, in time, bring forward in Africa.')
- **Issue:** 'Synodical proceedings' lands as the destination of everything Augustine is building — the dossier, the letters to Rome, the letter-delivery chain — but the term is not explained. A general reader understands something formal and threatening is coming, but not that this is a church trial with the power to declare his teaching heresy, strip him of standing, and exile him. Without that understanding, the bureaucratic ruthlessness reads as vague menace rather than specifically chilling institutional violence.
- **Suggestion:** One appended clause is sufficient.
- **Before:** `It will be the basis for the synodical proceedings against Pelagius's teaching that we will, in time, bring forward in Africa.`
- **After:** `It will be the basis for the synodical proceedings against Pelagius's teaching — the formal condemnation, brought before the assembled bishops — that we will, in time, bring forward in Africa.`

**4. [Accessibility Editor] Character Complexity / Reader Orientation** (Final section — Augustine's sleeplessness and return to the writing room after Lawrence's funeral)
- **Issue:** The chapter reveals Augustine's true operating motive precisely: 'The work cannot be eaten. The work is the wrapper. The wrapper has to hold.' A general reader may exit categorizing him as a pure institutional villain, missing what makes him tragic: that his theological position was genuinely held before institutional self-interest rendered it indistinguishable from itself. The chapter gestures at this — he 'meant the homily,' he 'flinched inside' at the Ostia reference — but the sleeplessness at the end reads as administrative satisfaction rather than a man who knows Pelagius's reading of the *cor inquietum* line was correct and has chosen to act anyway. One additional beat — not resolution, just the weight of the choice — would deepen the moral complexity for readers who may not catch the tragedy on first pass.
- **Suggestion:** In the final paragraph, after 'He slept well,' consider one sentence of cold interiority that registers what that ease costs him — not remorse, but acknowledgment. The chapter has earned it: the man who meant the homily, who drank the water from the dead man's cup, who could not stop looking at Pelagius's hands, should carry some small sign of what he has settled into. 'He slept well' is good. 'He slept well, which was the thing he had most feared he would do' is better.

### SUGGESTION (3)

**1. [Developmental Editor] structure — debate asymmetry** (Section beginning 'He gave the answer he had been giving for ten years...')
- **Issue:** Pelagius argues in direct speech across three substantial paragraphs; Augustine's counter-argument is entirely summarized. The asymmetry is clearly intentional — positioning the reader to feel Pelagius's force and to register Augustine's own sense that his argument 'did not perhaps finish what he had been claiming for it.' But because we never hear Augustine at his intellectual best, the tragedy is slightly diminished: the reader sees the strategy of his decision but not its full cost.
- **Suggestion:** Give Augustine one paragraph of direct argument before the summary — his strongest single move, perhaps the mass damnata or Romans 7 in his own voice — then transition: 'He continued in this manner for the better part of half an hour.' This allows Pelagius's grammatical stroke on inquietum to land against Augustine at full power, and makes 'I will have to destroy him' feel like the act of a man who has genuinely tested the argument, not simply declined to make it.
- **Before:** `He gave the answer he had been giving for ten years. He gave it in the language of the technical position.`
- **After:** `He said: the will of fallen man is not wounded but bound — bound as a man is bound who has lost the use of his legs, who may still feel the will to walk but cannot translate the will into the walking. [Continue in Augustine's voice one more beat before:] He continued in this manner for the better part of half an hour, at his full strength.`

**2. [Immersive Experience Editor] Visceral Moments** (Paragraph beginning "He thought one thing only..." through "Then he said, aloud")
- **Issue:** The decision to destroy Pelagius is framed beautifully — 'the verdict assembling itself in his chest a beat before the words assembled themselves in his head' — but the space between that interior verdict and the spoken dismissal is empty. Augustine decides to destroy someone who has just handed him back his own best sentence with a reading it plainly bears, and then immediately speaks the polite farewell. One physical detail in that gap — where his eyes go, what his hands do, what he looks at in the half-second before he opens his mouth — would make this the chapter's most viscerally powerful moment rather than merely its most architecturally powerful one.
- **Suggestion:** Add a single physical beat between the assembled verdict and the spoken words. Not a new thought — a body moment that marks the cost of the crossing.
- **Before:** `I will have to destroy him.

Then he said, aloud:

Pelagius. The light is going.`
- **After:** `I will have to destroy him.

He looked at Pelagius's face and held it a count.

Then he said, aloud:

Pelagius. The light is going.`

**3. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation / Plot Object** (Paragraph beginning 'He went to the lower shelf, lifted Cassian's *Conferences*...')
- **Issue:** 'The disc was where he had laid it. The leather packet was where he had laid it.' These are checked with the care of a man verifying a weapon, and the scene registers accordingly — but only if the reader already knows what 'the disc' is. If this is Chapter 3 and the disc has been introduced in Chapters 1–2, general readers will track. If it hasn't, the moment lands as atmosphere without object, and the thriller charge of Augustine guarding this thing is lost.
- **Suggestion:** If the disc hasn't been named in prior chapters, add a one-word identifier on first mention — 'the copper disc' or 'the disc of Anthony's cipher,' whatever it is — so the reader's attention has something concrete to grip. If it has been established, no change needed.
