### CRITICAL (3)

**1. [Developmental Editor] Structure / Thriller Engine** (Entire chapter)
- **Issue:** The chapter's catalytic event — the Donatist attack on Possidius — happens entirely off the page and is delivered only as a second-hand report through Eraclius. This is the inciting act of violence that drives all subsequent action. In an intellectual historical thriller, withholding it as backstory robs the chapter of its primary source of dramatic stakes. The reader receives a wounded man but never feels the wound being inflicted. The chapter is therefore built on a hole at its center.
- **Suggestion:** Either open in medias res with a brief prologue scene of the attack on Possidius at Calama before cutting to Hippo, or use Possidius's fragmented recollection during the recovery period as a delayed reveal — delivered in his own halting voice, not Eraclius's clinical report. The violence needs a body. The reader needs to feel what Augustine is receiving at the gate, not just be informed of it.
- **Before:** `the bishop of Calama had been injured on the road three weeks before`
- **After:** `Consider: Possidius, on the third day, describes one detail — the sound of the door splintering, the man who came first, the thing he heard them say before the blow — and stops. Augustine does not ask for the rest.`

**2. [Immersive Experience Editor] visceral moment / showing vs. telling** (Paragraph beginning 'Augustine went to the cart.')
- **Issue:** The first sight of the wounded Possidius is the chapter's primary emotional climax — Augustine seeing his close friend for the first time after days of imagining what the wounds might be. It receives a single phrase of interiority ('Something turned in his chest before he reached the side of it') and then immediately becomes wound inventory. The reader never inhabits the body of a man approaching something he has been dreading. 'Something turned' names the sensation without landing the reader inside it; the clinical pivot to the swelling at the collar line and the distance in the face evacuates the moment before it can work.
- **Suggestion:** Give the approach two beats instead of one. Let Augustine's body resist or prepare before the observational mode reasserts. The wound inventory can follow — it should follow, it is exactly right for Augustine — but the reader needs to arrive there with him, through the body, rather than being handed the report from outside.
- **Before:** `Augustine went to the cart.

Something turned in his chest before he reached the side of it. Possidius was on the pallet under the canvas. He was awake.`
- **After:** `Augustine went to the cart.

Something turned in his chest before he reached the side of it — a catch below the sternum, not pain, not exactly. He put his hand on the wood of the cart rim and stood a moment before he looked. Then he looked. Possidius was on the pallet under the canvas. He was awake.`

**3. [Accessibility Editor] Jargon Dump / Exposition Balance** (Paragraph beginning 'The afternoon went the way an afternoon went when a bishop had to write four letters...')
- **Issue:** The four-letters paragraph is the only place the political thriller stakes are explained, and it compresses five distinct concepts into a single dense block: Donatists, African schism, imperial vicar, Sirmium, and the council of Carthage. The parenthetical explanation of the Donatist schism ('the African schism that held the catholic bishops to be traitors who had handed over the Scriptures under Roman persecution and were, on Donatist reading, no longer the church') arrives inside a bureaucratic list, where a reader is tracking logistics, not absorbing doctrine. A 10th grade reader will likely skip past it. This is the only moment the chapter explains WHY the threat to Possidius exists, and that explanation is buried.
- **Suggestion:** Break the Donatist context out of the list and give it a moment of its own, grounded in what it means for a bishop's physical safety — not in theological terms. The reader doesn't need to understand the schism; they need to understand that it gets bishops attacked. Move the explanation earlier (when the cart first arrives with its military escort) so the danger registers before the recovery begins.
- **Before:** `One to the imperial vicar at Sirmium, drafted now and locked in the desk until the senior bishop's response came back — the vicar would, in the spring, hear Augustine's case for state action against the Donatists, the African schism that held the catholic bishops to be traitors who had handed over the Scriptures under Roman persecution and were, on Donatist reading, no longer the church.`
- **After:** `Consider a single earlier sentence — perhaps when the escort's drawn sword is described — that does this work: 'The Donatists held that Augustine and men like Possidius were traitors to God, unworthy to call themselves the Church. In Calama, that conviction had found a man with a stone.' Then the four-letters paragraph can remain a list without carrying the full explanatory burden.`

### MAJOR (8)

**1. [Developmental Editor] Plot Logic / Antagonist** (Throughout; specifically the autumn paragraph ('In the autumn the proceedings concluded...'))
- **Issue:** The Donatists function throughout the chapter as a theological and legal category rather than as human antagonists. Two priests are convicted and one exiled, but none are individuated — we have no face, no name, no moment of menace. For a thriller, this is structurally damaging: the reader cannot fear an abstraction. The parenthetical 'the men who had commissioned the priests, who had not been found' is the most thriller-charged sentence in the chapter, but it's buried in a single subordinate clause at the end.
- **Suggestion:** Give at least one antagonist a name and a human action — even at the remove of a letter or dispatch. The line about the men who commissioned the attack should be expanded and foregrounded. It's the chapter's actual thriller hook, and it's currently the quietest sentence in the book.
- **Before:** `He did not write of the men who had commissioned the priests, who had not been found.`
- **After:** `He did not write the name Flavius Marcellinus had spoken in the third session of the proceedings — the name the convicted priests had refused to confirm. Augustine read that gap in Possidius's letter the way he read a gap in any text: as the place where the meaning lived.`

**2. [Developmental Editor] Pacing / Voice — Expository Intrusion** (Paragraph beginning 'One to the imperial vicar at Sirmium...')
- **Issue:** The four-letter paragraph in 'The afternoon went...' section delivers a textbook gloss on the Donatist schism ('the African schism that held the catholic bishops to be traitors who had handed over the Scriptures...') that reads as author's note rather than inhabited prose. It breaks the chapter's cold, observational register — the only moment where the narrative voice speaks to the reader over Augustine's shoulder rather than through his perception.
- **Suggestion:** Strip the parenthetical gloss. Trust the reader to encounter the Donatist conflict through dramatic action, not definition. If the reader needs orientation, let Augustine's own frustration or conviction carry it: show him writing the letter with a particular controlled fury, not summarizing the doctrinal dispute for us.
- **Before:** `One to the imperial vicar at Sirmium, drafted now and locked in the desk until the senior bishop's response came back — the vicar would, in the spring, hear Augustine's case for state action against the Donatists, the African schism that held the catholic bishops to be traitors who had handed over the Scriptures under Roman persecution and were, on Donatist reading, no longer the church.`
- **After:** `One to the imperial vicar at Sirmium, drafted now and locked in the desk: the case for state action. Augustine had been building it for three years. Possidius on the pallet in the guest cell was the argument made in bone and linen.`

**3. [Developmental Editor] Pacing / Structure — Info-Dump** (Paragraph beginning 'Carry it, Monnica had said...')
- **Issue:** The bronze disc's entire operating logic is delivered in a single passage: what it does, how it works, who it is for, the time elapsed, and the condition it requires. This is the thriller's central MacGuffin and it arrives as retrospective explanation rather than accumulated mystery. The reader gets all the rules at once, which removes the capacity for discovery. Additionally, the paragraph that precedes it — inventorying every time Augustine has consulted the disc — over-explains what the object means emotionally before the reader has had time to feel it.
- **Suggestion:** Let the disc speak through behavior across the chapter before delivering Monnica's instruction. Augustine can hold the cold bronze without us yet knowing what it is for; the reader can feel its coldness before knowing what warmth would mean. Monnica's words could come in a smaller quantity, or be distributed: one fragment now, another recalled later in the chapter when the recognition about Stephen arrives.
- **Before:** `Carry it, Monnica had said. The man it is for will know it. He will not be you. The bronze will tell you when he is near — it will warm in his presence the way it warmed in mine. If he does not come in your lifetime, you will pass it to a man you have judged worthy of carrying it after you.`
- **After:** `She had said: the bronze will tell you. She had not said when. He had been waiting for the telling for sixteen years.`

'They embraced.' is two words for the chapter's most freighted physical moment. The second embrace does more work with 'in the way of two bishops at a public gate,' but Augustine's body is still absent from both. The chapter has established the right shoulder as the wound's physical signifier; that detail is available and unused here.

- **Before:** `They embraced.

*Aurelius.*
*Possidius.*`
- **After:** `They embraced. Possidius's right arm came around him with less than it had carried before. Augustine felt it. He did not remark on it.

*Aurelius.*
*Possidius.*`

**5. [Immersive Experience Editor] interior life / emotional resonance** (Section beginning 'The afternoon went the way an afternoon went when a bishop had to write four letters of consequence in three hours.')

The entire section reads as plot summary.

- **After:** `Augustine took each report without lifting his eyes from the parchment and gave the small acknowledgements a bishop gave at such reports, and Eraclius went out, and Augustine wrote on. **6. [Immersive Experience Editor] scene grounding / visceral moment** (Section beginning 'He went to the chapter house and stood in the chapter house alone for the count of perhaps three hundred.')

'In his ribs and behind his face' is the chapter's best interior writing; it deserves a room the reader can stand in.

- **Before:** `He went to the chapter house and stood in the chapter house alone for the count of perhaps three hundred. The chapter house was empty at this hour. The small high window gave on the courtyard, and the light through it laid a single bar across the marble of the floor and onto the foot of the lectern.`

The small high window gave on the courtyard, and the light through it laid a single bar across the marble of the floor and onto the foot of the lectern. The marble was warm under his feet.`

**7. [Accessibility Editor] Concept Accessibility / Reader Orientation** (Section beginning 'After some time he went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box.')
- **Issue:** The disc — the chapter's most important thriller element — is introduced with no context. The reader spends three long paragraphs tracking when Augustine last opened the box (the dying boy, Adeodatus, his mother at Ostia) before understanding what the object is or why it matters. The mechanics (it warms in the presence of the right person) don't arrive until Monnica's remembered speech, which is buried deep in the section. A general reader following thriller pace will read this as a sentimental keepsake from his dead mother, not a supernatural object with plot significance. The revelation lands flat because the stakes were never established.
- **Suggestion:** On first introduction, give the reader one orienting sentence about what the disc is — not its full history, but enough to signal 'this is extraordinary, pay attention.' The Monnica speech can remain where it is, but the disc needs a hook at the moment it appears. Even one line of interior thought at the moment of taking it down — something that signals Augustine himself does not fully understand it — would tell the reader this is not ordinary.
- **Before:** `After some time he went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box. He had not taken the box down in some years.`
- **After:** `After some time he went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box. It was the thing his mother had put in his hands at Ostia the night before she died — the thing she had told him would be warm when the right man was near, and cold until then. He had not taken it down in some years.`

**8. [Accessibility Editor] Concept Accessibility / Character-Driven Explanation** (Sixth-day recognition scene: 'On the sixth day, at the noon visit...' through 'He went to the writing room.')
- **Issue:** The emotional center of the chapter — Augustine recognizing what Stephen feels for Possidius — is handled with such restraint that a general reader will likely miss what has been recognized. 'He had called it by its name then. He did not call it by its name now.' A 10th grade reader has no guarantee they know Augustine had romantic relationships before his conversion. Without that context, 'the thing he had seen in his own life, in the years before his conversion' could mean grief, obsession, admiration, or fear. The recognition scene is the chapter's moral and emotional pivot; if it doesn't register, the departure scene and the final Stephen exchange lose all their weight.
- **Suggestion:** The scene does not need to name what Augustine sees — that restraint is exactly right for the register. But it needs one concrete, physical detail that tells the reader what category of feeling this is, not just that it is a feeling he once had. A bodily reference is enough. The description of Stephen's looking (shoulders down, hands quiet, mouth softened) is good physical observation; extend it by one beat that makes Augustine's memory specific without naming it.
- **Before:** `He had seen it before, in his own life, in the years before his conversion. He had called it by its name then. He did not call it by its name now.`
- **After:** `He had seen it before, in his own life, in the years before his conversion — in the mirror, once, looking at a man who was sleeping in his bed and did not know he was being looked at. He had called it by its name then. He did not call it by its name now.`

**2. [Developmental Editor] Character Arc — Over-Explanation** (Paragraphs beginning 'His mother came up in him...' through '...He did not take it to the writing room.')
- **Issue:** The chapter house reflection is the chapter's thematic culmination, and it largely works — but the penultimate paragraph over-names what the reader has already understood. 'He had been watching the men of his household for those sixteen years without seeing what was in front of him in any of them' explains the insight the scene has already demonstrated through action (the bedside watching, the disc coldness, the departure exchange). Stating the theme directly after having dramatized it perfectly is the chapter's one moment of insufficient trust in its reader.
- **Suggestion:** Cut or compress the explicit statement. End on the question about the disc — the unanswered question about whether the man it is for might already be in the household — and let the reader carry the larger implication themselves.

**3. [Developmental Editor] Structure — Coda Disconnect** (Final section ('In the autumn the proceedings concluded...'))
- **Issue:** The autumn/November coda is tonally correct and thematically useful, but structurally it feels grafted onto a chapter that has already reached its natural close at the departure gate. It introduces new information (the convictions, the unidentified men who commissioned the attack) without developing it, creating a thread the reader expects to be picked up and which the chapter then leaves alone. The November disc-check, while resonant, duplicates the cold-bronze note already sounded after the sixth-day recognition.
- **Suggestion:** If the coda stays, give it a specific forward hook — one concrete detail about the men not found that creates dread rather than just absence. If the disc-check at the end of the chapter house scene already closes the chapter's emotional arc adequately, consider cutting the coda entirely and opening the next chapter in the autumn with the proceedings.
- **Before:** `In November Augustine took the disc down on an evening when no other particular event of the day required him to.`
- **After:** `Consider ending the chapter at the cathedral door, or opening the coda with the single piece of intelligence that should frighten us — the name, or the silence where a name should be.`

**4. [Immersive Experience Editor] showing vs. telling** (Evening eating scene: 'Augustine ate the bread without paying attention to it.')
- **Issue:** 'Without paying attention to it' tells the emotional state (dissociation, suppressed worry) analytically. The reader is informed that Augustine is not tasting; the more immersive version lets the reader experience the not-tasting as a specific sensory event.
- **Suggestion:** Replace the analytical observation with a specific sensory displacement — what does bread taste like to a man whose attention is in a cell two corridors away?
- **Before:** `Augustine ate the bread without paying attention to it. He drank the wine for the warmth and not for the taste.`
- **After:** `Augustine ate the bread. He was aware of having eaten it afterward, but not during. The wine he tasted — thin, slightly warm — and he drank it for that and not for any other reason.`

**5. [Immersive Experience Editor] showing vs. telling / interior life** (Third-week writing-room scene: '*This is the household I have made,* he thought, and was surprised by the thinking of it.')
- **Issue:** The epiphanic moment is articulated cleanly as a thought and then immediately unpacked analytically ('He had not made it on his own intentions. The household had made itself around him.'). The surprise is told. The reader understands the insight intellectually but does not experience its physical arrival. The pen, the light, the three people in the room are present; the body is not.
- **Suggestion:** Give the surprise a brief physical beat before the analysis — something in the hand holding the pen, a stillness, a pause — so the moment registers as a body event first and an interpretation second.
- **Before:** `*This is the household I have made,* he thought, and was surprised by the thinking of it. He had not made it on his own intentions.`
- **After:** `*This is the household I have made.* He held the thought, the pen still on the parchment where he had stopped it. He had not made it on his own intentions.`

**6. [Accessibility Editor] Pacing / Reader Orientation** (Second paragraph: 'The gate-keeper had been at the door of the bishop's residence at the noon hour...')
- **Issue:** This paragraph tracks the chain of information from gatekeeper to Eraclius to Augustine in five sequential clauses before arriving at the simple fact: Augustine heard the news and stood up. The procedural detail (how Eraclius was told to knock, the specific timing, Stephen's copying work) buries what should be an urgent beat. For a general reader already orienting to the historical setting, this much mechanism before the emotional response slows the scene's tension. The reader hasn't yet been told who Possidius is or why Augustine would wait two hours at a gate for him.
- **Suggestion:** Either condense this paragraph to two sentences and move quickly to the gate, or invert the order: give us Augustine already at the gate, then briefly explain how he got there. The procedural chain works better after the reader understands the relationship between the two men.
- **Before:** `The gate-keeper had been at the door of the bishop's residence at the noon hour after the junior officer had come up from the gate; Eraclius — who was nineteen now and had been at the basilica for nine months as a junior in the household — had received the news in the corridor and had brought it directly to the writing room, knocking three times in the way he had been told to knock when news could not wait.`
- **After:** `Word had come at the noon hour by the usual relay — gate to corridor to writing room, three knocks, the kind that meant it could not wait. Augustine had set down the stylus. He had stood.`

**7. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation / Character Clarity** (Bronze box section: 'He had not opened the box since the night Adeodatus had died at Tagaste...')
- **Issue:** Adeodatus is named before Augustine's relationship to him is established. The next sentence refers to 'his mother' and 'his son' in sequence, making the connection available — but 'Adeodatus' comes first, and a reader unfamiliar with Augustine will spend a moment wondering who this is before the gloss arrives. - **Before:** `He had not opened the box since the night Adeodatus had died at Tagaste in the late summer of 389, which was now fourteen years past.`
- **After:** `He had not opened the box since the night his son Adeodatus had died at Tagaste in the late summer of 389, which was now fourteen years past.`

### SUGGESTION (4)

**1. [Developmental Editor] Chapter Hook — Opening** (Paragraphs 1–4)
- **Issue:** The chapter opens on stasis: two men waiting at a gate in the heat. The emotional hook — 'In seven years as bishop of Hippo he had received a great many carts on that road. He had not received one carrying Possidius' — is buried in paragraph two. For a thriller chapter, this inversion costs momentum. The second paragraph is the actual opening.
- **Suggestion:** Consider inverting: begin with the emotional distinction ('He had not received one carrying Possidius') and then descend into the physical waiting. The atmospheric first paragraph works well as the second paragraph once the reader's emotional stake is established.
- **Before:** `Augustine was at the gate with Stephen. The gate arch held the morning's heat the way Hippo stone held it in August... In seven years as bishop of Hippo he had received a great many carts on that road. He had not received one carrying Possidius.`
- **After:** `In seven years as bishop of Hippo he had received a great many carts on the harbour road. He had not received one carrying Possidius. He stood at the gate with Stephen in the heat the stone gave back through the soles of the feet, and he watched the road.`

**2. [Developmental Editor] Character — Stephen's Interiority** (All recovery-week passages)
- **Issue:** Stephen is the chapter's most dramatically charged character — the man whose feeling is recognized, whose decision not to come to the gate is the most significant act of self-management in the chapter — yet he is observed entirely from outside. The prose register is Augustine's cold perception throughout, which is correct for POV discipline. However, one micro-moment of Stephen's subjectivity breaking through Augustine's observation would deepen his human reality without violating POV. Currently he is the object of the chapter's most important recognition but remains a figure in someone else's seeing.
- **Suggestion:** A single moment where Augustine notices something he cannot fully read — a sound, a word, a stillness that exceeds his interpretive frame — would establish that Stephen has an inner life that escapes observation. This would also reinforce the chapter's theme: that watching is not the same as seeing.
- **Before:** `He was managing in the way Stephen had managed across the years he had been in the household, which was the way of a man who had decided some long time ago that the management was the work.`
- **After:** `He was managing in the way Stephen had managed across the years he had been in the household. Once — at the third day, the morning hour — Augustine had heard a sound from the corridor outside the guest cell that he could not, in the next moment, account for. He had not gone in.`

**3. [Immersive Experience Editor] structural / emotional resonance** (Final line of chapter: 'He went to compline.' Mirrors earlier section closing 'He went to compline — the last office of the day, the prayer that closed the dark.')
- **Issue:** The chapter ends on the same beat as the earlier cold-disc section. The repetition may be intentional — the disc is always cold, the closing is always the same prayer — but the man going to compline in November has lived through the chapter-house epiphany, Possidius's departure, Stephen at the desk, and sixteen years of not-seeing. He is not the same as the man who went in August. The identical closing risks reading as structural habit rather than deliberate echo, and it slightly deflates the coda's preceding sentence, which is already a better ending.
- **Suggestion:** Either close on 'The bronze was bronze.' which is stronger and final, or differentiate the November compline movement with a single phrase that acknowledges the weight of what has passed since August.
- **Before:** `He went to compline.`
- **After:** `The bronze was bronze. [End.]

— or —

He went to compline. The prayer was the same prayer it had always been. He said it the same way he had said it for twelve years at Hippo, and it meant what it had always meant, and he let it.`

**4. [Accessibility Editor] Character-Driven Explanation / The 'Smart Friend' Test** (Departure scene: 'Possidius looked at Augustine. The look held...' through 'Neither of them said it.')
- **Issue:** The silent exchange between Possidius and Augustine at the gate — where both acknowledge that Stephen did not come to see Possidius off, and both understand why — is the chapter's most sophisticated moment. It works beautifully for careful readers. But it relies on the reader tracking: (1) that Stephen deliberately absented himself, (2) that Augustine registered this absence, (3) that Possidius registered it and is now communicating that he registered it, and (4) that neither man will say this aloud. That is four inferences in quick succession at the very end of the chapter. A general reader tracking story events (not subtext) may simply read this as two old friends saying goodbye.
- **Suggestion:** One small physical detail from Augustine's interiority — just after the look breaks — would anchor the subtext without naming it. Something that tells the reader Augustine knows Possidius knows, without spelling out what either of them knows. The chapter has already established the 'small heat' motif; using it here would close the loop.
- **Before:** `The look broke. The small heat that had arrived on the sixth day was in his chest again, and he let it be there, and then they embraced again, briefly, in the way of two bishops at a public gate.`
- **After:** `The look broke. The small heat was in his chest — and he saw in Possidius's face, for one count, that Possidius had seen it there. Then they embraced, briefly, in the way of two bishops at a public gate.`
