## Voice & Narrative Persona (Grade: C+)

This chapter has a voice — but it's the voice of a style, not a storyteller. The prose is extraordinarily controlled, liturgical almost, with a hypnotic repetition pattern that's clearly deliberate: 'He set it back. He closed the box. He returned the box to the shelf.' Three times. Like a prayer. That kind of control impresses, and consistency counts for something. But King's test is harsher: is there a person in the room telling me this story? Someone with opinions, heat, humor, grief that leaks through the prose? The answer here is no. The narrator has zero personality. He reports. He observes. He transcribes. He never editorializes, never breaks register to tell us what something MEANS or why it matters or what WE should feel. Everything — Marcellinus being beheaded, a dying man walking across dust to his own chapel dedication, Augustine holding a mysterious bronze disc — is rendered in the same cool, formal, auteritarian tone. The emotional register never modulates. That's not invisible prose; it's armored prose. It protects itself from feeling. The closest thing to a narrative voice with actual personality is the Marcellinus coda — 'The man it was made to was dead before the second of the twenty-two books had been finished. The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years.' THAT is a narrator with a perspective. THAT is someone who has opinions about what they're telling you. It arrives too rarely. The stripped-dialogue convention — italics without quotation marks, no speaker attributions except sparse labels — announces 'This Is Literature' rather than 'Come, I have a story.' Mantel did this in Wolf Hall and made it work because her close-third voice BLED personality even in restraint. This prose restrains itself into near-silence. King would say: you built a beautiful locked room and forgot to let the reader in.

### Good Examples
> 'He was fifty-seven and his right knee was, in this weather, the right knee of a man of seventy' — this is the narrator with an opinion, a slight dark edge, a personality. This is voice.

> 'The man it was made to was dead before the second of the twenty-two books had been finished. The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years.' — the one moment where the narrator steps back, surveys, and speaks with genuine weight. This is what the whole chapter should sound like.

> 'He worked until the lamp went out.' — flawless closing line. Invisible prose doing maximum work. King would underline this.

### Needs Improvement
> 'Mark came back on the Wednesday — the middle of the week, in the new convention Augustine had taken to using for the day-names since the year of the courier — on a coastal ship out of Ostia that had touched at Carthage on the Sunday and had come down the harbour road in two days.' — King calls this throat-clearing. The parenthetical explaining the Wednesday convention is pedantic annotation, not storytelling. It signals the narrator is more interested in historical accuracy than in pulling the reader through the door.

> 'He set it back. He closed the box. He returned the box to the shelf.' — used three times with near-identical phrasing. As a single moment it works. As a mechanical refrain used identically three times, it becomes ritual posturing rather than earned resonance. Repetition should accumulate meaning; this accumulates only style.

> The entire stripped-dialogue convention — italicized speech without quotation marks, without attribution beyond the rare 'Augustine said' — creates archaeological distance where intimacy is needed. We are reading a transcript of the past, not being trusted into a conversation. King would say: let me hear these people. The affectation is costing you readers.

### Suggestions
- Give the narrator one or two moments of direct address or editorializing per section — not many, just enough to put a person in the room. Something like: 'That is the thing about grief among men who have work — the work continues regardless, and the continuing is not callousness. It is simply what a life of work demands.' The reader needs to feel guided, not just observed.
- Vary the emotional register. The same cool tone covers Marcellinus's arrest, Pinianus dying, a courier delivering receipts, a monastery dedication. Differentiate these emotionally in the prose itself — not in what characters say, but in the texture and pace of the sentences. Let the chapter feel the weight of what's heavy.
- Reconsider the opening sentence — restructure it to lead with action or image, not with a calendar annotation. 'Mark came back on the Wednesday, thinner than he had been in October, and smiling.' Start there. Move the date convention, or cut it.
- Use the bronze disc more dynamically — three identical visits to the shelf produce diminishing returns. Each visit should advance our understanding of what the disc means to Augustine. The third visit, after Marcellinus's death, is the most powerful; make the first two shorter and less ritually identical so the third lands with full weight.

## Deep POV & Interior Monologue (Grade: C+)

The prose is working at a deliberately archival remove — almost Gregorian chant in its cadence, its repetition, its refusal of heat. That is a genuine aesthetic choice and it sometimes earns its effects. But for the specific criterion being evaluated, it fails consistently and at the worst possible moments. The psychic distance hovers at level four or five throughout: we are observers of Augustine, not residents. The most emotionally charged event in the chapter — the execution of his dearest friend — is met with the flattest, most exterior prose in the book. 'He read the dispatch through. He set it on the desk beside Marcellinus's last letter of the previous month. He took up the iron pen. He went back to the fourth book.' That is four action beats in a row at the moment when every single one of King's principles demands we go inward. The restraint isn't earned here; it's evasion dressed as discipline. The one extended interior passage — the 'two parts of himself' construction — actually works, and it reveals what the whole chapter could have been: a mind that contains its own civil war. But it is the exception. The chapter tells us Augustine thought about Marcellinus and then gives us a biography. It tells us he sat with a letter and does not tell us what the sitting felt like. 'He did not move for a long count' is an external camera reporting on a man who has just been told his friend is in a death cell. King would have climbed inside the skull and stayed there. The disc motif is genuinely powerful as an action-rendered-interior — cold metal in a warm hand doing the work that no sentence explaining Augustine's grief could do — but the rest of the chapter needs to trust the same instrument.

### Good Examples
> 'He listened with the part of himself that was a bishop receiving a successful courier's report and he listened with the part of himself that was a man hearing the closing seal of an operation he had set in motion in October the consequences of which the courier did not know and would not, on Augustine's intention, ever know.' — The chapter's best interior moment. Dual consciousness rendered as simultaneous experience, not reported summary. We are inside the split.

> 'He thought he was going home from his last conversation with Pinianus. He carried the conversation with him on the road back.' — Clean, unfiltered, fully interior. No 'he felt' or 'he noticed.' The thought is the thought. The second sentence carries physical and emotional weight in nine words.

> 'The disc was cold.' — Four words doing everything a paragraph of grief-processing prose would do worse. This is King's method: the gesture, the object, the temperature of a thing in a hand. Let the cold stand for what cannot be said.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He thought about Marcellinus. Marcellinus was forty-five. He had been the imperial commissioner who had chaired the conference at Carthage in 411...' — Thought reported, then immediately abandoned in favor of biography. This is psychic distance at its widest. We needed the actual mind — the flicker of his face, the particular memory that surfaces first, the involuntary arithmetic of how many letters, how many margins — not a dossier.

> 'Augustine read the letter through. He sat with it.' — Maximum deflection at maximum emotional charge. 'He sat with it' is a four-word locked door. What is happening behind it? King would have kicked it open. We need the body's response — the thing the chest does, what the hands do with a letter you don't want to put down — not a stage direction.

> 'He did not move for a long count.' — External observation masquerading as interior life. A camera can see a man not moving. We need what is moving inside him. This construction — 'for a long count' — appears four or five times in the chapter and each time it performs the same evasion: signaling interior intensity while refusing to enter it.

> 'He read the dispatch through. He set it on the desk beside Marcellinus's last letter of the previous month. He took up the iron pen. He went back to the fourth book.' — This is the execution news. His friend is dead, already dead when the senior bishop wrote, and the response is four blocked action beats at the same tempo as the scene where Eraclius brings hot water. King's dictum: the reader must feel what the character feels. Here the character apparently feels nothing, or the narrator cannot bear to look.

### Suggestions
- At the arrest dispatch — the letter is four lines, Augustine knows what they mean, and we get 'He did not move for a long count.' Cut that and enter the skull: what is the first specific thought? Not 'he thought about Marcellinus' but the actual thought — the qualification on the third book, the way Marcellinus wrote the word 'perhaps' in the margin, the sound of his voice at Carthage in 411. The mind in crisis goes to the specific and the small. Put us there.
- The execution news is the chapter's climax and it is written at the chapter's lowest psychic temperature. 'He read the dispatch through. He set it on the desk...' — give us one sentence of genuine interiority before he picks up the pen. Not grief performed, not grief explained. One thing that his mind does in the thirty seconds between setting the dispatch down and taking up the pen. One involuntary thing.
- The 'two parts of himself' construction in the Mark scene is your best instrument. It renders dual consciousness without filtering words. Use it structurally throughout: Augustine is always a bishop and a man and a keeper of things the bishop does not know. That split is the character. Right now it appears once. It should organize the whole.
- Eliminate 'he noticed,' 'he thought about,' 'he decided,' and every construction of the form 'he [verb of cognition] [content of cognition].' These are psychic distance markers. 'He decided not to take the box down' becomes: 'He left the box on the shelf.' Let the decision show itself as action. 'He thought about Marcellinus' becomes the thought itself, in the character's idiom, following the character's logic.
- The disc is doing interior work that the prose around it refuses to do. Consider what Augustine thinks about while holding the disc — not 'he thought of Marcellinus, Pinianus, Mark' as a list, but one actual image from one of those people. Lists are distant. Images are close. Give us the specific memory that rises when cold bronze sits in a warm palm at the end of a man's worst October.

## Backstory & Characterization (Grade: B+)

This chapter operates at a remarkably high level of King-craft: characters are almost entirely revealed through what they do, how they carry themselves, what they choose. Mark's transformation from nervous to confident deacon is rendered entirely through a single behavioral detail — the vanished forward step in the bow — rather than through interiority or authorial summary. Melania is defined in one sentence by what she's doing at the altar: examining masonry, not praying. Pinianus walking to that chapel without showing his face is the whole man in one action. Augustine's four-letter sequence after Marcellinus's arrest — formal to formal to formal to personal — tells you exactly who he is and in what order he holds things. Then the drop: 'He went back to the fourth book.' These are King-level moments. The chapter stumbles in one significant place: the Marcellinus backstory paragraph is a resume dump, the one crack in an otherwise well-sealed architecture. The author tells us who Marcellinus was rather than letting Augustine remember him through a specific moment, a sentence he said, a gesture. That paragraph reads like a historian's footnote inserted into a novelist's scene. The Pinianus death-deflation paragraph ('Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer') is also a misstep — it pulls the reader out of the emotional present to reassure us that the scene we just lived through was premature, which kills the resonance of what was just earned through pure action.

### Good Examples
> "He did not, Augustine noted, bow with the small forward step that nervousness had given him in October. He bowed cleanly and stood." — Three months of character development delivered in one behavioral observation. No psychology. No summary. Just the changed body.

> "The oral acknowledgments I have repeated to myself daily on the return journey to be sure I have them in their precise wording." — Mark's entire character in one sentence of dialogue: conscientious, literal, proud in the right way. We know this person.

> "She was at the altar in the position of a woman who had been examining the work of the masons and the carpenters with the careful eye of a woman who had spent her family's wealth on every stone and who was making sure the stones were what she had paid for." — Melania established completely. Not praying. Auditing. The whole woman is here.

> "He walked across the dust to the chapel under the noon sun on his own legs... and he did not, in the walking across the dust, let any of the three of them see his face." — Pinianus's dignity, love, and terminal pride in a single physical action. This is the whole scene.

> "He drafted the letter to Marcellinus... He wrote about the small things of the household at Hippo that Marcellinus had asked after in his last letter. He wrote about his own knee." — Character revealed through correspondence: Augustine's loyalty to intimacy even in crisis. The knee. King would love the knee.

> "He did not change it." — The dedication. Five words of backstory and character and grief. Everything that needed to be said.

### Needs Improvement
> "Marcellinus was forty-five. He had been the imperial commissioner who had chaired the conference at Carthage in 411 that had ended the Donatist crisis on the terms favourable to the Catholic side. He had been a Christian of the careful steady kind that Augustine had recognised... as the kind of Christian who would... become a friend rather than a contact." — This is the chapter's one real failure. It's a resume. It's the author telling us who Marcellinus is because the author hasn't trusted a specific memory or object or sentence to carry the weight. King would have Augustine reach for Marcellinus's last letter, re-read one line, and let that line do all of this work in three words.

> "Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer. He lived through the autumn and the winter and the next spring and the next summer and the autumn after, and he died eventually at Jerusalem in the year Augustine would not be alive to know about." — This paragraph detonates the scene that preceded it. The consecration walk, the face not shown, the 'I will not see you again' — all of it was building toward a farewell of maximum gravity. The author flinches. The authorial intrusion that corrects Augustine's assumption doesn't add irony; it drains the scene of its emotional earned weight.

### Suggestions
- Cut the Marcellinus resume paragraph entirely. Trust the relationship to emerge from the dispatch's final line ('Marcellinus has asked, as his one personal request... that the bishop of Hippo be informed') and from the letter on Augustine's desk and from the dedication being non-negotiable. Let the reader assemble who Marcellinus is from those fragments. That assembly is the reader's pleasure.
- Cut or radically compress the Pinianus death-deflation paragraph. If you need it at all, put it at the end of the Tagaste section as a single sentence — 'He was wrong about the timing' — and nothing more. Right now it's five sentences of explanation where one clause would do, and it trades the scene's earned power for an accuracy no one needed.
- The 'carrier' reveal — 'He had been the carrier for twenty-six years' — arrives cold. The disc, the cold, the ritual taking-down are doing excellent mysterious work throughout. But the word 'carrier' as a named role is the one moment it tips from mystery to unexplained label. Consider letting the ritual speak entirely, or let Augustine think the sentence without the named role: 'He had been holding it for twenty-six years.' The function without the title.
- The Pinianus shortening detail — 'He was perhaps an inch shorter than he had been' — is excellent King instinct, but it wants one more sensory anchor. What does the shortening look like specifically? The shoulders? The neck? King would pick the one visible thing and name it.

## Setting & Sensory Detail (Grade: C+)

The chapter is set in Roman North Africa across three years and four distinct locations — a writing room, a coastal road, a half-built monastery, and the imagined interior of a Carthage prison cell — yet the physical world presses against the reader only intermittently. The prose is highly intelligent and the period atmosphere is maintained through period-accurate diction and logistics, but King's standard requires that you SWEAT the setting onto the page. There is almost no smell anywhere in this chapter. A harbor town, a building site in African summer heat, a refectory where a dying man sits — none of these places have an olfactory life. The tactile register is thin: we get 'the disc was cold' (effective, earned through repetition) and 'the brazier was not throwing what it threw in November' (excellent, specific), but long stretches pass where the physical world exists only as stage direction. Mark arrives from three months on Mediterranean coastal ships — he should carry the sea on him, salt-stiffened clothes, a particular thinness that smells like bad water and old bread. Pinianus is visibly dying but the body of a dying man in summer heat has a specific presence that the chapter does not render. The Tagaste building site is 'dust' and nothing else. The writing room, which is the primary setting and returns three times across three years, is never given a real physical life — we don't feel its dimensions, smell the parchment and iron gall ink, hear the scratch of the stylus against the specific grain of African wax. The chapter's best sensory work is brief but shows what the whole could be: 'the light coming off the harbour at the slant the harbour gave it in autumn, the small particular wind off the sea that started at this hour and ran until evening' is good, precise, earned. The telling-detail instinct is also present in the best moments (the foreman who was the carter's son who carried Monnica's body; the bow without the nervous forward step). But King's test is simple: can you put your hand out and touch this room? Mostly here, you cannot.

### Good Examples
> 'the light coming off the harbour at the slant the harbour gave it in autumn, the small particular wind off the sea that started at this hour and ran until evening' — seasonal, time-of-day specific, sets mood without announcing it; the only passage in the chapter where you feel North Africa as a physical place

> 'the brazier was not throwing what it threw in November' — temperature rendered through negative comparison rather than stated outright; tells us it's cold and that cold has history in this room

> 'The hinges sounded. The disc was cold.' — minimal but earns its weight through repetition; the disc's temperature becomes the chapter's quiet emotional register

> 'The standing was a slow operation that began with a hand on the arm of the chair and ended with a small adjustment of the second hand against the wall behind him for balance. He was perhaps an inch shorter than he had been' — Pinianus's decline rendered as specific physical choreography; setting detail doing double duty as character revelation

> The foreman as 'the son of the carter who had owned the cart that had carried Monnica's body from Ostia to the harbour' — a location detail that opens a shaft straight into the depth of Augustine's past; a single sentence doing the work of a paragraph

### Needs Improvement
> 'He arrived at the basilica gate at the second hour of the afternoon, on foot, with the leather satchel under his outer tunic' — Mark returns from three months on coastal ships and the writing room receives him without a single sensory note; no smell of the sea on him, no salt-bleached cloth, no physical evidence of three months of hard travel beyond 'he was thinner'; King would give us the smell of the man before he opened his mouth

> 'The monastery was on a piece of land three miles east of the town, in the small valley the family had owned for two generations' — the Tagaste sequence is set at a construction site in African summer heat and the chapter gives us almost nothing: 'dust' appears twice, no smell of cut stone or sawdust or sweat or animal, no sound of the workmen's hammers before they stop; the heat is stated ('slow heat of an African summer') but never felt against the skin

> 'Augustine was at the desk on the third paragraph of the second book of the work' — the writing room is the chapter's primary and recurring setting, appears in all three time periods, and is never physically established; we do not know if it is small or large, how the light enters, what the desk smells like, what the walls look like, whether the floor is tile or stone or packed earth; it exists as a conceptual location, not a room

> 'He drafted the reply to the senior bishop in the slow careful sentences a senior bishop drafted to another senior bishop' — the long dispatch-writing sequence after the arrest news is the chapter's emotional center and passes entirely without sensory grounding; no weight of the iron pen, no smell of the ink, no physical sensation of a man holding himself together through the act of writing

### Suggestions
- The writing room needs one establishing pass — not a paragraph, a sentence or two — that gives it physical reality: the temperature of the stone floor, the smell of the tallow candle or the iron gall ink, the specific quality of light through whatever opening exists. King's rooms are tactile before they are scenic.
- Mark's return from the sea is a missed sensory opportunity. Before his first word, let the reader smell three months of coastal travel on him. That physical detail also does narrative work: it makes the journey real, makes the period real, and makes Augustine's observation of the boy more acute.
- The African heat in the Tagaste section should press against the reader's body. King would give us the heat radiating off cut stone, the specific quality of sweat drying in dry summer air, the smell of a building site — lime, sawdust, animal. 'Slow heat' is a phrase; the reader needs a sensation.
- Pinianus is dying and the chapter renders this beautifully in behavioral terms, but a dying man in summer heat has a specific physical presence — skin color, the particular sound of effortful breathing, the smell of illness. One carefully chosen detail here would deepen everything the chapter does well with his decline.
- The bronze box and the disc are the chapter's most effective sensory objects, but they can carry more. The disc is 'cold' — but cold like metal? Like stone? Like something that should be body-temperature and isn't? One additional specific quality (weight, surface texture, color in the writing-room light) would make it more than a symbol and give it the King-standard physical reality that makes a recurring object haunting rather than merely significant.
- Sound is nearly absent from this chapter. The building site should be loud before the workers stop. The refectory where Pinianus sits has no ambient sound. The harbor Augustine looks at from the window has no gulls, no rope-creak, no water. In King, ambient sound is always present — it tells us where we are and what the day feels like even when nothing is happening.

## Tension & Dread Building (Grade: A-)

This chapter is operating at a high level of slow-burn dread, using the tools of historical fiction to hide the horror in plain sight. The tension architecture has three load-bearing columns: the bronze box with its cold disc (a classic King-style mysterious object, never explained, always present at moral tipping points); the dramatic irony of Marcellinus's execution (the letters go out the morning after he is already dead — the reader eventually understands this before Augustine does, and it is devastating); and the wax tablet entry, which reveals that Augustine orchestrated something terrible that the innocent courier Mark will 'never know.' The prose style — bureaucratic, precise, emotionally controlled — functions as a mask over the horror, and that contrast between surface calm and underlying darkness is the engine of the dread. Where the chapter loses tension is the Tagaste section, which is beautifully written but runs long without a single dread marker tying it back to the shadow of what Augustine did in October. And at the precise moment of maximum emotional impact — when Augustine learns Marcellinus is already dead — the chapter zooms out into a century-spanning summary that dissipates the grief before the reader can sit in it. King would never pull the camera back at the moment of impact. He would press it closer.

### Good Examples
> 'The man is in the cell at Sketis where he will finish.' — Scratched onto a wax tablet and immediately erased. This is the chapter's coldest line. 'Where he will finish' is doing brutal work. Someone has been sent to their end, Augustine arranged it, and Mark carried the letters that did it without knowing. Three words carry the whole weight of the moral horror.

> 'The disc was cold.' — Three words, used three times, always at the same beat: the box is opened, the disc is held, and it is cold. King's great lesson is that you name the monster once and let repetition do the rest. You never explain the disc. The reader's imagination fills every silence around it, and the coldness accumulates meaning with each recurrence.

> 'He had been the carrier for twenty-six years. He would be the carrier for whatever years remained.' — This landing beat in the October scene is perfect. It tells you everything about what the disc is without telling you anything. The dread is existential and it arrives quietly, at the end, when the lamp is about to go out.

> 'The four letters went out with the morning post. Marcellinus and his brother Apringius were executed at Carthage on the thirteenth of September — the day before the senior bishop had written...' — The placement of this revelation is exactly right. The sentence construction is ruthless: we watch Augustine draft the four letters, we feel the care he put into each one, especially the letter to Marcellinus about his knee and the continuation of the work — and then we are told Marcellinus had been dead for over two weeks before any of it was written. The dramatic irony lands like a verdict.

> 'He listened with the part of himself that was a bishop receiving a successful courier's report and he listened with the part of himself that was a man hearing the closing seal of an operation he had set in motion in October the consequences of which the courier did not know and would not, on Augustine's intention, ever know.' — This is the chapter's most efficient dread engine. Two listening selves, one innocent man reciting receipts between them, and the reader suddenly understanding that this warm successful return masks something Augustine will never explain to anyone.

### Needs Improvement
> 'Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer. He lived through the autumn and the winter...' — This is the chapter undercutting its own dread. The entire Tagaste section builds toward a farewell scene of quiet devastation — Pinianus walking across the dust under the noon sun on legs that are failing, not letting any of them see his face. That is a King moment. Then the narrator steps in and announces that he didn't die yet, and the air goes out of the room. The effect is almost comic. Let the dread stand unresolved. The reader does not need to be reassured.

> 'He did not, in the fourteen years of further work on the City of God between the autumn of 413 and the autumn of 426, change it. The dedication remained the dedication. The work was finished and the work was copied and the fair copies went out across the empire and into the centuries and the dedication was the dedication on every copy.' — This is the prose standing up to give a speech. Augustine has just learned his friend was executed before he could send the letter he spent the previous scene drafting. That is a moment that should land in the body. Instead the narrative zooms out a thousand years. The grief is replaced with a historical summary. Come back to Augustine's hands on the desk, the smell of the ink, the weight of the iron pen — then give us the centuries if you must.

> 'The journey was a journey, father.' — Mark's dialogue in the early section is almost entirely functional and correct in register, but this one line is a small false note. It reads like a modern deadpan — slightly too contemporary, slightly too self-aware. Mark is twenty-four and has been carrying letters he cannot open for three months; his voice should be earnest to the point of slight awkwardness, not wryly deflective.

### Suggestions
- The bronze disc needs one more beat of physical texture — not explanation, but sensation. Not 'cold like a coin' but something stranger, something that tells the body it should not be this cold. King's principle: the inexplicable thing earns its power from specificity of detail, not mystery alone. One more sentence about what cold means when Augustine holds it would make the repetition of 'The disc was cold' hit three times as hard.
- The Tagaste section needs a single line — one image or one beat — that ties it back to the shadow of what Augustine did in October. Right now it reads as a self-contained elegy, beautiful but disconnected from the chapter's dread architecture. Augustine is spending an afternoon with a dying man whose death he has accepted — and somewhere in a cell at Sketis, because of letters carried by a twenty-four-year-old who read Cicero while he waited for his ship, someone else is finishing. Let that shadow pass once across the Tagaste afternoon.
- When Augustine receives the news of the execution — 'He took up the iron pen. He went back to the fourth book.' — this is the right move and it is devastating. But it arrives too quickly. Add one physical beat before he picks up the pen. Not grief-explaining. Not summarizing what he feels. Just a body in a cold room: the weight of the chair, the particular quality of October morning light, the dispatch still on the desk beside Marcellinus's last letter with its careful qualification about Roman history. King always lets the reader sit in the moment before the character closes the door on it.
- The chapter ends with 'He worked until the lamp went out.' This is excellent — do not change it. But consider cutting the summary paragraph before it ('The dedication remained the dedication...into the centuries...') and letting the final section run straight from Augustine holding the disc and thinking of the dead to that last line. The lamp going out should be the last thing, not a postscript to a historical survey.
- The dramatic irony of Mark not knowing what he carried needs a small payoff that the reader can feel, not just understand intellectually. When Mark asks, at the door, if there is 'a further word on the substance of what the letters contained,' and Augustine explains that the not-being-told is what permits a deacon to do this work 'without complication' — that is good. Consider adding, after Mark goes out, one sentence in which Augustine looks at the wax tablet he is about to write the Sketis note on, and considers Mark's face at the door. Let us feel the cost of the kindness of not telling him.

## Dialogue & Voice (Grade: B-)

This is a writer who understands subtext—what's NOT said is often doing the heavy lifting, and there are moments of genuine emotional devastation delivered through understatement. The Pinianus deathbed exchange and Mark's quiet ask about the letters both work because the reader feels the weight beneath the surface. But King's central test for dialogue is simple: could you record this and it would sound like a person talking? Almost nothing here passes that test. Every character—the young deacon, the dying nobleman, the bishop's foreman who knew him as a boy, the aristocratic abbess—speaks in the same formal, periodic, 'on the present accounting' cadence. The author has confused period register with character voice. A rough foreman in 412 CE didn't speak like a bishop drafting a dispatch, even if you're rendering both in modern English. The deeper problem is that several exchanges are exposition in dialogue clothing: Mark explains to Augustine why he gave oral rather than written acknowledgments—Augustine already knows this; it's for us. Augustine explains his own power structure out loud to Mark. King's 'as you know Bob' alarm would be screaming. What saves the grade is the subtext work: Melania's one-line crack ('He had hoped you would'), Pinianus's 'Write to her also. She will need the letters more than she will say.' These are the moments where the dialogue earns its place on the page—where the silence inside the sentence is louder than the words.

### Good Examples
> 'Write to her also. She will need the letters more than she will say.' — This is masterclass subtext. A dying man who has accepted his death and whose last concern is not himself but how his wife will perform grief. The 'more than she will say' is the whole character of Melania in seven words.

> 'The journey was a journey, father.' — The one moment in the chapter where dialogue sounds like a real person deflecting a real question. It's wry, it's self-possessed, it reveals Mark's character in a single sentence. More of this.

> 'I will not, on the present trajectory, see you again.' — The clinical precision of 'present trajectory' is a dying man's dignity. He won't say 'I'm dying.' He gives it the grammar of a business projection. That control is the whole character.

> 'Thank you for coming. He had hoped you would.' — Two lines. Melania has spent the chapter being a CEO inspecting her construction site. This is the hairline crack in the armor. It lands because it's unexpected and because 'he had hoped' is past tense—Pinianus has stopped hoping for much.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The substance of the letters is the kind of substance that a senior bishop carries and a junior deacon does not. The carrying of the letters by a deacon who does not know the substance is the proper division.' — Augustine is explaining his own reasoning about information hierarchy to the person he just exercised it over. This is the bishop telling himself (and us) why he did what he did. Real people don't explain their own power structures; they just use them.

> 'The oral acknowledgments I have repeated to myself daily on the return journey to be sure I have them in their precise wording. I will give them to you now if you wish.' — Mark is explaining to Augustine why he's about to deliver oral acknowledgments. Augustine knows. This is stage directions dressed as speech. Cut 'The oral acknowledgments I have repeated to myself daily on the return journey to be sure I have them in their precise wording.' Just: 'Three of the recipients gave me oral acknowledgments. I have them.' Then give them.

> 'Bishop. The chapel is what we agreed. / It is. / The choir wing is behind. It will be finished in October. The lay sisters' wing is finished...' — Melania sounds identical to Augustine. She's a Roman aristocrat who has given away a fortune and buried her secular life. She should have a different music than a North African bishop. The author has given everyone the same syntactic DNA.

> 'Pinianus. The husband is in the small refectory. He is not, on the deputy abbess's word, well. He has been there since the first hour. The lady has had a chair brought to him. He is sitting.' — The foreman who knew Augustine as a boy, who is a working man on a dusty construction site, delivers his information in perfectly shaped declarative sentences with the pacing of a court brief. He sounds like everyone else in the chapter.

### Suggestions
- Give each character one verbal tic, syntactic habit, or rhythmic signature that belongs only to them. Melania is efficient—maybe she talks faster, drops subjects. Mark is precise—maybe he over-qualifies. Pinianus might have the pauses of a man who must plan each sentence around his breath. The foreman might speak in shorter bursts with the idioms of a man who measures things.
- Audit every exchange for 'as you know Bob.' When a character explains something the other character already knows, cut it. Mark doesn't need to tell Augustine why he gave oral acknowledgments. Augustine doesn't need to tell Mark the theory of compartmentalized information. Trust the subtext you're already good at.
- Let one character interrupt or abandon a sentence. Pinianus in particular—a dying man with failing stamina—might start a sentence and not finish it. That incompleteness would be more devastating than any completed thought about trajectories.
- The Mark scene's subtext is strong but the payoff line—'There will not be a further word'—is being undercut by Augustine's over-explanation before it. Cut from 'The substance of the letters...' down to just: 'You were not told because not knowing is what lets a deacon do this work. You have carried in good faith. There will not be a further word.' The silence in that last sentence does more work than two paragraphs of Augustine explaining his own epistemology.
- Consider giving Pinianus's 'I will not, on the present trajectory, see you again' a beat of reaction from Augustine before his reply. Right now Augustine says 'I will write' immediately. A half-beat—a pause rendered as a short sentence of action, him looking at Pinianus's hands or the floor—would let the line breathe and make his response feel chosen rather than automatic.

## Pacing & Structure (Grade: B-)

This chapter is architecturally ambitious — three time-stamped scenes across eighteen months, each orbiting the same controlled, emotionally suppressed Augustine — and when the pacing works, it works beautifully. The Tagaste section is the strongest piece of sustained pacing in the chapter: slow, deliberate, death-haunted, with the physical drama of Pinianus's walk to the chapel doing exactly what King means when he talks about 'the telling detail.' The September section's metronomic drafting sequence — four letters, same rhythm, same suppression — is genuinely sophisticated structural work. But the chapter's overall tempo is uniformly measured, and King's most essential pacing lesson is contrast: fast when action demands, slow when tension needs to build. This chapter never accelerates. A courier delivers news, a man is dying, a friend is beheaded — and all three are rendered in the same careful, controlled prose register. The opening section runs long relative to its importance, the chapter ends with an authorial intrusion that physically removes the reader from the scene at the worst possible moment, and some of the parenthetical constructions grind the rhythm to a halt before it even starts. The bones are excellent. The tempo needs surgery.

### Good Examples
> "He walked across the dust to the chapel under the noon sun on his own legs. He went the slow careful pace of a sixty-year-old whose body had been failing for some time, and he did not, in the walking across the dust, let any of the three of them see his face." — Perfect slow pacing around death and dignity. The short final clause lands like a held breath. King would keep every word.

> "He took up the iron pen. He drafted the reply... He sealed the letter and set it in the tray. He took up a second sheet... He sealed it and set it in the tray. He took up a third sheet... He set it in the tray. He took up a fourth sheet." — Metronomic repetition that mirrors Augustine's emotional suppression under grief. The machinery of duty masking the wound. Structurally invisible and emotionally devastating.

> "He worked until the lamp went out." — White space plus five words after the long accumulation of the chapter's final movement. This is exactly what King means by paragraph breaks for emphasis. Everything before it earns this.

### Needs Improvement
> "Mark came back on the Wednesday — the middle of the week, in the new convention Augustine had taken to using for the day-names since the year of the courier — on a coastal ship out of Ostia that had touched at Carthage on the Sunday and had come down the harbour road in two days." — This is the chapter's first sentence. It already has a parenthetical brake in it. King opens the door and pulls you through; this opens the door and explains what a door is. The scene doesn't need this annotation to function.

> "Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer. He lived through the autumn and the winter and the next spring and the next summer and the autumn after, and he died eventually at Jerusalem in the year Augustine would not be alive to know about. But Augustine, on the inland road back from Tagaste in the slow heat of June, did not know that." — This pulls the reader entirely out of the scene at the moment of maximum emotional weight. The reversal defuses exactly the tension the walk-to-the-chapel earned. King's rule: stay in the character's body. Let the reader sit in Augustine's wrong knowledge. The correction can come in a later scene as a brief aside, not here as a narratorial intervention.

> "The dedication of the City of God was *to my dearest son Marcellinus*. He did not change it. He did not, in the fourteen years of further work on the City of God between the autumn of 413 and the autumn of 426, change it... The man it was made to was dead before the second of the twenty-two books had been finished. The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years." — This paragraph is beautiful writing that destroys the scene's pacing. It's the author stepping in front of the camera after the most devastating moment in the chapter to explain the historical significance. Stay at the desk. Stay with the iron pen. The reader can feel the thousand years without being told about them.

### Suggestions
- Cut the opening parenthetical entirely: 'Mark came back on Wednesday, on a coastal ship out of Ostia that had touched at Carthage and come down the harbour road in two days.' The reader doesn't need the calendar gloss. Trust them.
- Trim Mark's oral-acknowledgments scene by half. We don't need the three recitations. We need Augustine's dual-listening — the bishop and the man — and we have it. The scene earns its keep when it gets to 'The two parts heard the sentences differently.' Get there faster.
- Cut or relocate the Pinianus-reversal paragraph. Let Augustine carry his wrong grief on the road home. A brief factual mention at the chapter's end — or the next chapter's opening — restores the irony without dismantling the Tagaste section's earned weight.
- Give Augustine a physical reaction to the execution news. 'He sat with it' is the right instinct but we need one more beat — a breath held, hands flat on the desk, something in the body. King always anchors the reader in the character's physical reality at moments of shock.
- Cut the historical-perspective paragraph after the executions and end that section with: 'He took up the iron pen. He went back to the fourth book.' The dedication's permanence is implicit in that gesture. The reader feels the thousand years. You don't need to tell them.

## Themes & Subtext (Grade: B+)

The chapter carries real thematic weight, and most of it arrives the right way — through situation, not announcement. The core themes are genuinely interlocked: institutional power hollows out individual conscience; work outlasts the dead; the carrier pays a price the carried thing never does. What makes this chapter sing is the bronze disc and the cold. Augustine checks it four times across the chapter. It's never explained. It stays cold. That's King-level symbolic restraint — the symbol does its work precisely because it refuses to explain itself. Similarly, the 'two-part listening' when Mark debriefs is organic theme emergence at its best: the cost of operating in moral shadow rendered through the act of hearing, not through interior monologue about guilt. The Marcellinus section is devastatingly good — the letters written to save a man already dead, the dedication that outlasts him by a thousand years, Augustine going back to the fourth book the same afternoon he learns his friend will likely die. That's where theme and story fuse completely. The chapter stumbles at the seams where the author steps in to manage the reader's response. The Pinianus coda ('Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer') corrects Augustine's mistaken belief from outside the story and then tells us Augustine 'carried the conversation with him on the road back' — both gestures that editorialize where the text should simply hold. And 'He had been the carrier for twenty-six years. He would be the carrier for whatever years remained' is a thesis statement in a chapter that earns the right not to need one. The subtext is already there. The author breaks it open unnecessarily.

### Good Examples
> 'Augustine listened. He listened with the part of himself that was a bishop receiving a successful courier's report and he listened with the part of himself that was a man hearing the closing seal of an operation he had set in motion in October the consequences of which the courier did not know and would not, on Augustine's intention, ever know.' — Theme of institutional power splitting the self, shown through the act of listening, never named.

> 'Mark, on the other side of the desk, heard nothing of the difference; Mark heard the sentences he was reciting and the bishop receiving them.' — This single sentence carries the whole weight of how secrecy isolates. The courier delivers what he cannot read. The bishop receives what the courier cannot know. Profound subtext with zero authorial underlining.

> 'The man it was made to was dead before the second of the twenty-two books had been finished. The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years.' — The chapter's central theme — work as the only form of faithfulness that endures — landing with surgical precision and zero sentimentality.

> 'He went the slow careful pace of a sixty-year-old whose body had been failing for some time, and he did not, in the walking across the dust, let any of the three of them see his face.' — Dignity against mortality, shown entirely through physical action. King would approve: not a word about courage, and yet.

> 'I read the four books of Cicero's De Officiis I had taken with me twice through during the wait... I have, in the doing of it, found it has changed the way I think about the duties of a clerical office.' — Mark read a book on duty while carrying letters about duty whose contents he's forbidden to know. The irony never announces itself. This is the best kind of subtext — the kind the character can't see.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He had been the carrier for twenty-six years. He would be the carrier for whatever years remained.' — This is the author stepping out from behind the curtain to write the chapter's theme sentence. Everything in the prose to this point has been disciplined, oblique, precise. This is a thesis. The cold disc and the receipts locked in the chest with the iron lock already said this without saying it.

> 'Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer. He lived through the autumn and the winter and the next spring and the next summer and the autumn after, and he died eventually at Jerusalem in the year Augustine would not be alive to know about. But Augustine, on the inland road back from Tagaste in the slow heat of June, did not know that. He thought he was going home from his last conversation with Pinianus. He carried the conversation with him on the road back.' — This coda is a structural misstep that undermines the theme it's reaching for. The power of Augustine's wrong belief is that he carries it. By correcting it from outside the narrative and then telling us he 'carried the conversation with him on the road back,' the author interprets the scene instead of trusting the reader to feel it. King's rule: if you have to tell us what the character is carrying emotionally, you've already lost the scene.

> 'The consecration was the consecration.' — Too gestural. It calls attention to itself as a craft choice — the elegant dismissal, the knowing elision — and the seams show. Compare to the foreman's speech, which earns its compression through specificity. This earns nothing through cleverness.

### Suggestions
- Cut 'He had been the carrier for twenty-six years. He would be the carrier for whatever years remained.' entirely. The disc is cold. The receipts go in the chest. Augustine goes back to the seventh chapter. The reader already knows what he carries. Trust them.
- Collapse the Pinianus coda. End the Tagaste section at 'He worked until the lamp went out' — or wherever the section naturally closes after Tagaste. If you need the reader to know Pinianus outlived Augustine's expectation, find one detail in the scene itself that plants that seed without correcting Augustine's wrong belief from outside the narrative. His wrong belief is the point. Protect it.
- The bronze disc is the chapter's best symbol precisely because it's never explained. Consider making it slightly stranger — not just cold, but cold in a specific, wrong way, the way King would give you one more degree of wrongness that makes a thing unforgettable. Right now it's restrained; it could be one beat more present.
- The 'two-part listening' passage is the chapter's best writing. Examine every scene where Augustine processes information — dispatches, reports, letters — and ask whether that split consciousness could be visible in more of them. The theme of institutional and personal selves in tension could run deeper through the fabric of the whole chapter if that lens is consistently applied.
- The Mark thread — the Cicero, the oral acknowledgments recited daily, the clean bow he's no longer nervous about — is rich with subtext about the formation of a young conscience inside an institution that uses him. Consider whether the chapter ends in the right place or whether a brief fourth beat with Mark (not a resolution, just a glimpse) would give the 'carrier' theme a human register beyond Augustine's interiority.

## Cultural Specificity & Time Period (Grade: A-)

This chapter is remarkably well-grounded for historical fiction. The author has done serious archival work and it shows — real figures (Marcellinus, Melania, Pinianus, Possidius), real events (the 411 Donatist conference, Marcellinus's execution in September 413, the City of God's dedication), and real texts (De Officiis, City of God) are woven together with the precision of someone who has spent time with late antique sources. King's standard is specificity that feels earned rather than researched, and much of this chapter hits that mark: the postal travel times are plausible, the social hierarchies are authentic, the material culture (bone stylus vs. iron pen, wax tablet vs. parchment, bronze box with sounding hinges, the chest with the iron lock) is period-appropriate and never explained. Where the chapter falls short of King's standard is at the sensory layer — we know what happened in this world but we don't quite smell it. The brazier that is 'not throwing what it threw in November,' the thin January light at Hippo, the harbor wind 'that started at this hour and ran until evening' — these are gestures at sensory texture that don't quite land. King would have given us the ring of an adze from the twenty workmen, the smell of fresh limestone, a detail of what Mark ate at the harbour. The mysterious disc is the chapter's most significant cultural failure: it reads as a thriller device imported from a different novel, and its cultural meaning — is it Manichaean? Neoplatonic? Something from Augustine's Manichean past? — is never anchored. A cold object in a bronze box means nothing in 412 CE unless we know what Augustine believes it is. King's supernatural objects are always culturally specific (the Overlook in its 1970s commercial-hotel horror, It in its 1950s small-town dread). This disc floats free of its era entirely.

### Good Examples
> 'the middle of the week, in the new convention Augustine had taken to using for the day-names since the year of the courier' — a single subordinate clause that places us in a precise cultural transition moment: the seven-day week naming convention is new enough to require attribution. Extraordinary anchoring.

> 'the son of the carter who had owned the cart that had carried Monnica's body from Ostia to the harbour for the African ship she had not in the end taken' — this is the best piece of cultural texture in the chapter. Monica died at Ostia in 387 CE. The foreman Augustine has known since boyhood. The lineage of a small North African town where everyone's family history is part of the social fabric. King would love this.

> 'He had been the imperial commissioner who had chaired the conference at Carthage in 411 that had ended the Donatist crisis on the terms favourable to the Catholic side' — not backgrounded as exposition, delivered as memory. The political-religious texture of the late empire rendered in one sentence.

> 'He read the four books of Cicero's De Officiis I had taken with me twice through during the wait, which I had not done before, and which I have, in the doing of it, found has changed the way I think about the duties of a clerical office' — the right book for a young educated deacon in 412, deployed with the right effect on the right character. Period-specific reading life.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The disc was cold.' (repeated three times as motif) — This is the chapter's worst cultural failure. The disc has no provenance, no material description, no meaning within the world of 412 CE North Africa. Augustine was a former Manichaean, a convert, a bishop, a philosopher — what is this object to him cosmologically? Cold in what sense — cold like metal in a stone room in January, or cold like absence of something it once held? King's Overlook Hotel is terrifying because we know exactly what it is and what it costs. This disc is terrifying to no one because we have no idea what it is.

> 'Twenty workmen were on the site when Augustine and Possidius came up the cart-track from the road. The workmen stopped what they were doing when they saw the bishop's small party.' — King would never let twenty men stop working without giving us what they were working on and what stopping sounded like. The sudden quiet when the bishop arrives. The specific sound of a trowel set down. The dust-smell. This is a missed sensory opportunity at a scene transition.

> 'He drafted the letter to the proconsul at Carthage... He drafted the letter to the consistory at Ravenna... He drafted, in his own hand without dictation, the letter to Marcellinus' — the threefold letter-writing sequence is structurally strong but the repeated 'He drafted / He sealed / He set it in the tray' strips the physical act of writing in 413 CE of all texture. What does parchment feel like under an iron pen versus a bone stylus? What does the wax smell like when it takes a seal? These are the specific sensory details of epistolary culture in late antiquity that would ground the sequence.

### Suggestions
- Give the disc a cultural identity. In one sentence — even a displaced, ambiguous sentence — tell us what Augustine believes the disc is or was. Is it from his years in Carthage before his conversion? His Manichaean period? Something from a meeting with a desert father? The supernatural object must be culturally embedded to generate dread. Right now it generates only puzzlement.
- Add one or two Punic or North African vernacular details to the Tagaste section — the workmen would not all be speaking educated Latin. The foreman Augustine has known since boyhood would have a name and a mode of address that reflects the bilingual reality of 5th-century North Africa. One word of Punic, one gesture that belongs specifically to this coastal African culture, would anchor the monastery scene more firmly than any amount of correct historical reference.
- Develop the sensory texture of writing. Augustine is at his desk for most of this chapter. What does the fourth book of the City of God smell like — the fresh parchment, the iron gall ink, the oil in the lamp? King writes about writers at work with physical specificity (the desk, the weight of the pen, the sound of the room). The writing room at Hippo needs one more sensory register beyond light and warmth.
- The nine-day wait at Ostia is a buried opportunity. Mark read De Officiis twice. What was Ostia like in January 412? What did a junior deacon from North Africa do in a port city for nine days? One sentence of specific Ostian detail — the harbor taverns, the smell of fish sauce from the warehouses, the cold of a Tiber-mouth winter — would make Mark's return feel earned rather than reported.
- Trust the real stakes more. The Marcellinus execution is historically devastating — a real man, a real friendship, a real book. The chapter handles it with appropriate restraint, but the line 'The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years' is doing heavy lifting that the surrounding prose hasn't quite set up. Bring us one more concrete sensory detail of Marcellinus as a physical person before he is dead — what did he look like? What did Augustine notice about him in the spring of 411 at their first meeting? One image would make the dedication's survival feel tragic rather than elegiac.

## Prose Polish (King Polish) (Grade: B-)

This chapter is working toward a specific, disciplined voice — the measured formality of a fifth-century bishop's world — and at its best moments it achieves something real. The staccato disc scenes, the Pinianus death sequence, the final line. These are invisible prose doing visible work. But the chapter has a serious and pervasive disease: subordinate clause stacking. Sentences routinely sprout three, four, five dependent clauses, each one a small hedge against being misunderstood, each one costing the reader momentum. The 2nd draft = 1st draft minus 10% principle hasn't been applied. It needs to be. The stylistic tic 'on the present accounting' appears five times — a deliberately archaic construction that works once, calls attention to itself by the third use, and by the fifth is making the reader aware they're reading. The long em-dash asides (the foreman's parenthetical runs 38 words) stop scenes dead at moments that should be moving. The good news: the bones are right. The instincts about understatement are right. The ending earns everything. But the author is doing the work the reader should be doing, explaining and qualifying and hedging instead of trusting the sentence to land.

### Good Examples
> 'The disc was cold.' — Three words. Does everything. Cold means nothing has changed, that whatever the disc is meant to signal hasn't signaled. King would underline this and say: the whole chapter aims at this sentence.

> 'He did not stand again that afternoon.' — Devastating. No commentary, no 'which signaled the gravity of his condition.' The prose gets out of the way and lets the fact hit.

> 'He was fifty-seven and his right knee was, in this weather, the right knee of a man of seventy; he rode the small mule at the slow pace the small mule preferred and did not, in the five days, complain about the pace once.' — Character, age, dignity, stubbornness, all in one sentence. The repetition of 'slow pace the small mule preferred' is controlled and rhythmically correct.

> 'The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years.' — The best sentence in the chapter. Earned, plain, enormous. King would say this is what you've been building toward.

> 'He worked until the lamp went out.' — Perfect final line. Invisible prose. You don't see the writing; you see the man at the desk in the dark.

### Needs Improvement
> 'Mark came back on the Wednesday — the middle of the week, in the new convention Augustine had taken to using for the day-names since the year of the courier —' — The aside explains what Wednesday means. The reader does not need this. Kill it. 'Mark came back on the Wednesday' is enough.

> 'The foreman — a man Augustine had known at Tagaste since both of them were boys, who had been the son of the carter who had owned the cart that had carried Monnica's body from Ostia to the harbour for the African ship she had not in the end taken — came forward and bowed.' — Thirty-eight words of parenthetical when the foreman walks forward and bows. Monnica's death is a beautiful detail buried alive. Give it its own sentence. Let it breathe.

> 'He had been a Christian of the careful steady kind that Augustine had recognised, the first time he had met him in the spring of 411, as the kind of Christian who would, in the long arithmetic of Augustine's correspondence with imperial officials over twenty years, become a friend rather than a contact.' — This is the Marcellinus introduction, the emotional center of the dispatch section, and it's strangled by its own syntax. 'The recognition had been correct. Marcellinus had been a friend.' These two sentences that follow are the good sentences. Lead with those.

> 'a small additional cloth bag at his shoulder that he had not had when he had left' — 'additional' tells us it's new; 'that he had not had when he had left' is redundant. 'a cloth bag he'd left without' does the same work in half the words.

> 'I have, in the doing of it, found has changed the way I think about the duties of a clerical office.' — The syntax has broken down here. 'found has changed' is missing a word and the whole construction is knotted. Even cleaned up, 'in the doing of it' is the author reaching for period voice and instead snagging the reader.

### Suggestions
- Apply the 10% cut mechanically before the next pass. Print it, take a red pen, and cut every sentence by at least one clause. The chapter is ~5,500 words. It should be ~4,900. You will lose nothing and gain everything.
- Kill 'on the present accounting' after its first use. Once, it's a period-appropriate formula. Five times, it's a tic. Readers notice tics before they can name them — they just feel the writing slowing down.
- The em-dash asides are the chapter's biggest structural problem at the sentence level. Every one that runs more than fifteen words should be pulled out and either given its own sentence or cut. The foreman parenthetical, the Mark bowing description, the Marcellinus identification — all need surgery.
- The Marcellinus dispatch section needs to be restructured from the inside out. Start with the fact: 'He had been writing the City of God to Marcellinus for two years and one month.' Build from there. The friendship comes before the official biography. Right now the official biography is blocking the friendship.
- Trust the reader at the dialogue's elliptical moments. When Mark asks about the substance of the letters and Augustine gives his long careful non-answer, the paragraph explaining that Mark had been 'hoping, in the small way clergy of his age hoped, to be told what the task had been about' is unnecessary. We can see it. Let the scene perform, don't annotate it.
