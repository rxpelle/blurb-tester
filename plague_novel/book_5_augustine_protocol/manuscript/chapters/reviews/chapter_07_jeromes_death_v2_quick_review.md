### CRITICAL (5)

**1. [Developmental Editor] pacing / prose register** (paragraph 13 (beginning 'He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties'))
- **Issue:** The chapter's single weakest passage, and it occurs at Jerome's central moment of reckoning — the worst place for a prose failure. The recursive syntax ('and he had, in his sixties, watched institutions begin to change, and he had, in his seventies, watched them go on changing') was evidently meant to mime the grinding, delayed quality of an old man's recognition arriving in installments. Instead it blurs rather than accumulates. Each clause promises new insight and restates the previous one. The final sentence ('the institution he had served was no longer the institution he had served') is purely tautological. This paragraph follows the chapter's best metaphor ('like a damaged passage of text suddenly resolves into meaning') and diffuses that image's power rather than landing it.
- **Suggestion:** Trust the preceding metaphor and cut the paragraph to its essential insight. The surrounding physical details — 'The chair was too hard. His back was cold against the wood.' — are already doing the slowing-down work this paragraph attempts. The revision should add content, not restate: what specifically did Jerome build that was captured? Name it.
- **Before:** `He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been when he had taken up the work. He had assumed it would remain the institution it had been into his fifties, into his seventies, into the centuries to come. He had assumed that what he was building would be preserved by men of his disposition for as long as Latin was read. The assumption had been the assumption of a man who had not yet seen an institution change, and he had, in his sixties, watched institutions begin to change, and he had, in his seventies, watched them go on changing, and the institution he had served was no longer the institution he had served.`
- **After:** `He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was building would be preserved by men of his disposition. He had watched it change instead: first slowly, in his sixties, then with a thoroughness, in his seventies, that removed the ground from the assumption. What he had built had been captured by the institution. The institution was not what he had built it for.`

**2. [Immersive Experience Editor] Showing vs. Telling / Emotional Resonance** (Paragraph 13 — the 'he had assumed' block)
- **Issue:** This is the emotional climax of Jerome's entire arc — the moment a man confronts the foundational error of his life — and it is rendered entirely in abstract, repetitive paraphrase. The clause 'the institution he had served was no longer the institution he had served' appears in structural variation six or seven times. The rhythm aspires to convey the circular obsession of late-life regret, but the repetition runs too long and the passage contains not a single physical sensation. Jerome's body, so vividly present in the opening pages, disappears at exactly the moment his interiority demands it most. The reader understands the realization intellectually but cannot feel it.
- **Suggestion:** Cut the repetition by two-thirds. Anchor the reckoning in a concrete physical moment — Jerome's hands on the manuscript, a specific page he stops on, a sound from the courtyard that suddenly means something different. Let one sharp sensory detail carry what seven iterations of the same sentence cannot. The metallic taste earlier in the chapter is exactly the right move; deploy it here instead of there, or add something equivalent.
- **Before:** `He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been when he had taken up the work. He had assumed it would remain the institution it had been into his fifties, into his seventies... and the institution he had served was no longer the institution he had served.`
- **After:** `He had assumed, when he began, that the institution he was serving would outlast him. He had been wrong in the precise way a builder is wrong who lays a foundation and does not test the soil. He set his hand flat on the open page. The parchment was cold. Outside, the brothers were singing the wrong psalm — one of his own translations — and the Latin sounded like a language he had borrowed and not returned.`

**3. [Accessibility Editor] Jargon Management / Reader Orientation** (Paragraph 1)
- **Issue:** The Vulgate is named but never explained. It appears in the chapter's first sentence as the capstone of Jerome's life — yet a 10th grade reader is given no frame for what it is, why it mattered, or what 'the definitive list of texts the Latin Church would not read' has to do with it. The intellectual stakes of the entire chapter rest on understanding this.
- **Suggestion:** Add a brief, embedded gloss the first time Vulgate appears. It should feel like information, not a textbook insert.
- **Before:** `The ink had dried on the last page of the Vulgate three months ago.`
- **After:** `The ink had dried on the last page of the Vulgate three months ago — his life's work: a single Latin Bible meant to replace every competing translation and become the one scripture the Church would read for centuries.`

**4. [Accessibility Editor] Jargon Management** (Throughout (first appearance: paragraph 13, 'his annotated copy of the Septuagint'))
- **Issue:** The Septuagint appears approximately ten times and is the physical object at the center of the chapter's climax. It is never defined. A 10th grade reader will likely associate the word with nothing, undermining the weight of Jerome sending it to Augustine and of Augustine reading its margins. 'The Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures' appears once in passing but is easy to miss and doesn't explain why Jerome had spent decades arguing against it.
- **Suggestion:** On first use, plant a brief anchor. Subsequent references can then carry full weight.
- **Before:** `Beneath a cloth cover, untouched for months, lay his annotated copy of the Septuagint — the Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures that he had spent decades arguing against.`
- **After:** `Beneath a cloth cover, untouched for months, lay his annotated copy of the Septuagint — the Greek Old Testament that the early Church had used for three centuries before Jerome argued it was an inferior substitute for the original Hebrew. He had won that argument. He had won it completely. He was no longer certain he should have.`

**5. [Accessibility Editor] Concept Accessibility / The 'Smart Friend' Test** (Throughout; most concentrated in Jerome's letter (paragraphs 35–43) and Augustine's response (final section))
- **Issue:** The central intellectual argument — what Jerome was 'wrong' about and what Augustine was 'right' about — is never stated plainly. The chapter delivers enormous emotional weight around an absence: a 10th grade reader will feel Jerome's remorse without knowing what he regrets. The distinction between 'institutional preservation of scripture' and 'distributed preservation of meaning' appears once, in a margin note, without unpacking. A general reader finishes knowing Jerome made a catastrophic error. They do not know what it was.
- **Suggestion:** The letter to Augustine is the natural place to name the thing plainly. Jerome is a man writing to be understood, not performing obscurity. One or two direct sentences inside the letter would anchor the intellectual stakes without breaking voice.
- **Before:** `You have been right about prevention. You have been right about the institutions. You have been right that what I have spent my life building cannot, by its construction, do what I have claimed for it that it would do.`
- **After:** `You have been right that the Church's survival does not depend on controlling a single authoritative text locked inside a single institution. You have been right that meaning, distributed across a thousand communities reading in their own tongues, outlasts empire. I built a fortress. You have been building a river. The fortress will fall with Rome. The river will find new ground.`

### MAJOR (8)

**1. [Developmental Editor] anachronism / historical accuracy** (paragraph 2 ('Seventy-three years. Fifty-six of them in this monastery.'))
- **Issue:** Jerome arrived in Bethlehem in 386 CE. At his death in September 420, he had been at the Bethlehem monastery for approximately 34 years, not 56. The figure 56 would require his arrival at age 17 — years before his Roman education and early career — which is historically impossible. A chapter that wears its scholarship carefully elsewhere (accurate detail on Book XV of the City of God, correct death date, authentic Possidius) will lose historically literate readers on the second paragraph.
- **Suggestion:** Change to 'Thirty-four of them in this monastery' — accurate and still impressively long for a single posting. If the larger number is needed for thematic weight, 'Fifty-six of them in service' is defensible by counting from his early ascetic years in Syria (c. 374).
- **Before:** `Seventy-three years. Fifty-six of them in this monastery.`
- **After:** `Seventy-three years. Thirty-four of them in this monastery.`

**2. [Developmental Editor] thriller architecture / scene purpose** (Full chapter)
- **Issue:** The chapter carries a thriller designation and the series appears to sustain an active external plot — institutional conspiracy, suppressed texts, antagonist forces working against Augustine's project. Jerome's letter gestures at 'men with whom I have done it' who 'will continue,' and the Septuagint's voyage across the Mediterranean has obvious thriller exposure. But the chapter takes no action on any of this. Jerome dies and Augustine grieves without either of them taking a defensive action, receiving a threat, or learning anything that changes the external situation. The chapter delivers thematic and emotional closure with genuine skill. It delivers no thriller momentum. Whether this is a problem depends entirely on what Chapter 10 does.
- **Suggestion:** If this is penultimate and the external plot resolves in the next chapter, no change needed. If this is the final chapter, the conspiracy Jerome will not name needs at least one concrete forward beat — a line in the Possidius dialogue about the men Jerome referenced already moving against the Hippo diocese, for instance — to signal that the thriller's spine is not simply left open. The chapter's elegiac register would survive one paragraph of plot-forward material.

**3. [Developmental Editor] plot logic / narrative opacity** (Augustine's loss list: 'He had lost the boy in the night of the courier whose name he had not, in ten years, spoken aloud.')
- **Issue:** This is the most opaque item in Augustine's grief inventory, placed between the clearly identified (Monnica, Adeodatus, Pelagius) and the thematically legible ('the silence at Sketis'). 'The boy in the night of the courier' implies a specific prior event — a death, a betrayal, possibly a murder connected to the thriller plot — and the ten-year silence on the name signals significance beyond ordinary loss. If this references an established character from an earlier chapter or book in the series, the circumlocution earns its weight. If it has no prior anchor, it reads as deliberate mystification in a passage that works only through specificity.
- **Suggestion:** If this boy is established earlier in the novel with plot significance, keep the reference exactly as written — it is affecting. If not, either give him a prior scene somewhere in the book (even a brief one) or cut the item. The loss list is strong enough without an entry that functions as a riddle.

**4. [Immersive Experience Editor] Visceral Moments / Sensory Detail** (Section beginning 'On the thirtieth of September the pain in his chest grew sharp and constant')
- **Issue:** A chapter titled 'Jerome's Death' spends six sentences on the actual dying. Jerome dismisses his attendants, lies down, hears vespers, reflects abstractly on pattern, notices ink stains, and then — 'Then the thought went and there was no further thought.' The ink stain detail is excellent and should survive. Everything else in the death passage is compressed to the point of evasion. We have spent the entire chapter in Jerome's body — the cramped hand, the shuffling feet, the cold wall through wool — and at the moment of physical dissolution we retreat into concept ('the weight of pattern,' 'neither comfort nor terror'). This is the chapter's most important visceral moment and it reads as intellectual summary.
- **Suggestion:** Slow the dying down. Stay in the body. What does Jerome actually feel — not what he thinks about what he feels? The chest pain is mentioned and immediately set aside. The shallow breathing from earlier should return here, worse. The stone ceiling above the bed, the specific sound of vespers through the wall, the failing warmth in his hands. Let the abstractions arrive last, briefly, earned by the physical texture that precedes them. The 'weight of pattern' is a fine thought — but it should surface from sensation, not replace it.
- **Before:** `He did not feel forgiveness. He did not feel the peace that the deathbed accounts of the Christian dead always claimed. He felt the weight of pattern: the sense of himself as one life inside the longer shape of a movement...`
- **After:** `The pain in his chest was not sharp now but wide, a pressure that seemed to come from the stone of the wall itself. He could feel his heartbeat in his teeth. The brothers at evening prayer sounded very far away, and then they sounded close, as if the stone had thinned. He looked at the ceiling. He could not find the crack he had used, for twenty years, to locate north. He did not feel forgiveness. He felt instead the strange weight of continuing — of being still one thread in a pattern longer than his life — and then he did not feel that either.`

**5. [Immersive Experience Editor] Emotional Resonance** (Augustine loss-list paragraph — 'He had, in his sixty-six years, lost a great many people...')
- **Issue:** The list is too long and too opaque. Monnica and Adeodatus will land for readers with Augustine knowledge; the others (Atticus, Pelagius described as 'not dead but in a desert,' 'the man at Lérins,' 'the senior bishop at Carthage,' 'the deacon of the synagogue') blur into one another without context. Most critically, 'the boy in the night of the courier whose name he had not, in ten years, spoken aloud' is introduced as if it carries the weight of a known grief, but the reader has never met this person. The unnamed boy reads as either a meaningful mystery (requiring some grounding here) or a distraction. By the time we reach 'He had lost his right knee, in some functional sense, three years ago,' a line that could be quietly devastating, the list has numbed the reader rather than built. The cumulative effect undercuts the emotional work the next paragraph ('the small particular silence') does beautifully.
- **Suggestion:** Cut the list by half. Keep Monnica, Adeodatus, and one or two others with a single concrete image each. Either give the boy in the night one clarifying phrase ('the boy he had not been in time to reach,' or similar) or cut entirely — unnamed figures need at least one sensory anchor to register as loss. End the list on the knee, which earns its absurdity through its specificity. The tightened list will make the silence passage hit harder.
- **Before:** `He had lost Monnica. He had lost Adeodatus. He had lost the boy in the night of the courier whose name he had not, in ten years, spoken aloud. He had lost Pelagius... He had lost Atticus and the silence at Sketis and the man at Lérins...`
- **After:** `He had lost Monnica, who had known him better than anyone living. He had lost Adeodatus, who had been the best of what he had made. He had lost a boy whose name he did not say aloud, and that loss had gone in a room by itself and stayed there. He had lost, two years ago, the deacon of the synagogue at Hippo who had smiled into his hand at the rail nine years before. He had lost, in a functional sense, his right knee.`

**6. [Accessibility Editor] Exposition Balance / Pacing** (Paragraph 16 ('He had assumed, when he had taken up the work...'))
- **Issue:** This paragraph uses deliberate anaphoric repetition ('He had assumed... He had assumed...') to render Jerome's dawning recognition. The technique is sophisticated but the paragraph makes a single point across seven clauses of increasing abstraction. A general reader will likely lose the thread before the paragraph resolves. The concept underneath — that Jerome built his work on an institution he believed permanent, and it wasn't — is clear in idea but buried in the style.
- **Suggestion:** Retain the rhythm but cut two of the middle clauses and make the final recognition land harder. The idea should feel like a revelation, not a proof-by-accumulation.
- **Before:** `He had assumed, when he had taken up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would remain the institution it had been when he had taken up the work. He had assumed it would remain the institution it had been into his fifties, into his seventies, into the centuries to come... the institution he had served was no longer the institution he had served.`
- **After:** `He had assumed, when he took up the work in his thirties, that the institution he was serving would last. He had assumed it into his fifties, into his seventies, with the certainty of a man who had never watched an institution die. He had now watched one dying for ten years. The institution he had served was not the institution he had served.`

**7. [Accessibility Editor] Concept Accessibility** (Augustine section, paragraph on the City of God scrolls (Jerome's reading of Books 11–12))
- **Issue:** Augustine's 'long work on the city of God' is mentioned as the key that unlocks Jerome's conversion of mind. A 10th grade reader doesn't know what the City of God argues, and the chapter never explains it. Jerome's recognition ('The bishop of Hippo had been right... in the essential thing') means nothing without some sense of what that essential thing is. This is where the intellectual payoff should land for the general reader.
- **Suggestion:** Give one sentence on what Augustine was arguing. It need not be theologically precise — just enough for the reader to grasp the stakes.
- **Before:** `He had recognised, as he read, what the bishop of Hippo had been building. He had recognised it in installments. The recognition was complete now.`
- **After:** `He had recognised, as he read, what the bishop of Hippo had been building: an argument that the Christian world survived not because Rome preserved it, not because one authoritative Church controlled it, but because the idea itself was too distributed, too alive in too many people, for any single catastrophe to kill. The recognition had come in installments. It was complete now.`

**8. [Accessibility Editor] Jargon Management** (Third scroll summary, paragraph 28 ('the work of the Pelagian sequel'))
- **Issue:** 'Pelagian' is dropped without gloss in a dense summary paragraph. Pelagianism is Augustine's defining theological controversy — the debate about whether humans can earn salvation through willpower alone, or depend entirely on grace. A reader who doesn't know this misses that Jerome and Augustine are allied on this front even while disagreeing on scripture. It's a minor orientation gap, but it compounds the chapter's general problem of assumed knowledge.
- **Suggestion:** A single parenthetical or appositive handles this without slowing the scroll summary.
- **Before:** `the work of the Pelagian sequel was being managed jointly by the diocese of Hippo and the diocese of Carthage`
- **After:** `the work against the Pelagian teachers — who held that men could save themselves without God's grace, a position both Jerome and Augustine had condemned — was being managed jointly by the diocese of Hippo and the diocese of Carthage`

### MINOR (6)

**1. [Developmental Editor] pacing / over-explication** (Dialogue beginning 'He has given us the working notes for the work.')
- **Issue:** The discovery of Jerome's two-layer marginal note on Psalm 137 — early precision versus late doubt, pressed hard enough into the parchment to leave a physical indentation — is the chapter's finest moment. The physical detail does everything. The dialogue that immediately follows translates what was vivid into what is explained: 'He has given us the working notes... This is the most useful thing he could have done... He knew it was the most useful thing he could have done... He knew.' The reader already understood all of this from the touch of Augustine's finger on the parchment.
- **Suggestion:** Cut the middle of the exchange and land on the silence. The confirmation requires only two lines.
- **Before:** `*He has given us the working notes for the work. He has given us his own arguments against our position, written in his own hand across fifty years, with his confession of their wrongness in the margins of his confession.*

Possidius looked at the page over his shoulder.

*This is the most useful thing he could have done.*

*Yes.*

*He knew it was the most useful thing he could have done.*

*He knew.*`
- **After:** `Possidius looked at the page over his shoulder.

*He knew it was the most useful thing he could have done.*

*He knew.*`

**2. [Developmental Editor] pacing / redundancy in the letter** (Jerome's letter, third paragraph ('I have, over the seven years between then and now, gone from thinking you naive to thinking you mistaken to thinking you incomplete to thinking you correct'))
- **Issue:** The two sentences immediately before this already establish the arc — naive, then dangerous. The four-stage enumeration restates the same journey in a slightly more labored list. In a letter meant to be Jerome's most stripped communication, the accumulation of stages reads as overwriting where compression would be more in character.
- **Suggestion:** Cut the four-stage list. End the thought at 'I thought you were dangerous when you continued to say them after.' Then move directly to the single image of the world describing itself in his words — that image earns more than the enumeration.
- **Before:** `I thought you were naive when you began to say these things in the year of the sack. I thought you were dangerous when you continued to say them after. I have, over the seven years between then and now, gone from thinking you naive to thinking you mistaken to thinking you incomplete to thinking you correct, and the going has been the going of a man who was watching the world describe itself to him in the words you had been using.`
- **After:** `I thought you were naive when you began to say these things in the year of the sack. I thought you were dangerous when you continued to say them after. The going from there to here has been the going of a man watching the world describe itself to him in words you had already used.`

**3. [Immersive Experience Editor] Showing vs. Telling** (Paragraph 9 — Marcus enters, Jerome dismisses him)
- **Issue:** 'Jerome's pride was one of the few things that had not diminished with age.' This is direct telling of a character trait at a moment when the scene is otherwise rendered through behavior. We have just watched Marcus learn 'not to offer assistance unless directly asked' — which shows the pride. The explanatory sentence is redundant and slightly condescending to the reader.
- **Suggestion:** Delete the sentence. The prior sentence ('The young monk had learned not to offer assistance unless directly asked') already shows the pride. Trust the implication.
- **Before:** `The young monk had learned not to offer assistance unless directly asked. Jerome's pride was one of the few things that had not diminished with age.`
- **After:** `The young monk had learned not to offer assistance unless directly asked.`

**4. [Immersive Experience Editor] Voice Tic / Pacing** (Multiple locations: after the Septuagint inspection, after the scrolls, the final Augustine passage)
- **Issue:** 'He sat with this' and 'He sat with them' appear as structural beats at least three times in close proximity. The construction is doing real work the first time (Jerome absorbing his realization) but by the second and third use it has become a tic — a way of marking pause without earning it. When a gesture is repeated this frequently it stops reading as deliberate and starts reading as a default.
- **Suggestion:** Keep one instance. For the others, find a different way to mark the pause — a specific action, a sound from outside, a physical sensation — or simply begin the next thought. The reader will feel the pause if the prose holds still without being told to sit.
- **Before:** `He sat with this. The chair was too hard... [later] He sat with them. [later] He sat with his hand on the cover of the codex.`
- **After:** `Retain 'He sat with his hand on the cover of the codex' (it earns the phrasing because the hand on the cover is a concrete image). Replace the earlier two with specific actions or observations.`

**5. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation** (Augustine section, grief-inventory paragraph ('He had, in his sixty-six years, lost a great many people...'))
- **Issue:** The grief list mixes accessible losses (Monnica, Adeodatus) with series-internal references that are opaque to any reader without prior chapters: 'the boy in the night of the courier whose name he had not, in ten years, spoken aloud,' 'Atticus and the silence at Sketis,' 'the man at Lérins who had, in the end, narrowed what he taught.' These read as deliberate series callbacks, which is fine, but placed inside a grief inventory alongside real historical figures, they create a texture of significance the general reader cannot parse. The passage risks feeling evasive rather than resonant.
- **Suggestion:** Either give each opaque reference one grounding phrase (even vague: 'the boy he had failed to save'), or trim them from this passage and let Jerome's loss be the scene's only grief. The list's power comes from accumulation; the callbacks dilute that without context.
- **Before:** `He had lost the boy in the night of the courier whose name he had not, in ten years, spoken aloud.`
- **After:** `He had lost the boy from the desert crossing whose name he had taught himself not to speak. [Or: cut and let the named losses carry the inventory]`

**6. [Accessibility Editor] Jargon Management** (Paragraph 10 ('He could hear, very faintly through the stone, the brothers at sext.'))
- **Issue:** 'Sext' — the canonical midday prayer — appears without gloss. In context, 'sext' and 'vespers' and 'morning psalms' are all used, and the cumulative effect of these untranslated liturgical time-markers makes the chapter's clock harder to read. 'Vespers' is common enough to pass; 'sext' is not.
- **Suggestion:** One-word gloss suffices.
- **Before:** `the brothers at sext.`
- **After:** `the brothers at sext, the midday office.`

### SUGGESTION (4)

**1. [Developmental Editor] chapter close / final image** (Final two sentences ('The lamp burned low and burned even and did not flicker. / The room was very quiet.'))
- **Issue:** 'The room was very quiet' is the chapter's flattest phrase — a stage direction appended to an image sequence that already communicated silence with precision. The lamp doing its steady work in an empty room after two old men have left to pray is the correct ending. The summary robs it.
- **Suggestion:** End on the lamp. Cut the final sentence entirely.
- **Before:** `The lamp burned low and burned even and did not flicker.

The room was very quiet.`
- **After:** `The lamp burned low and burned even and did not flicker.`

**2. [Immersive Experience Editor] Sensory Detail** (Hippo Regius section — Augustine's writing room)
- **Issue:** The Hippo scene is the chapter's strongest, but the physical room exists primarily in negative: the brazier is unlit, the fog is warm, the bell rings at a distance. We know what is absent (fire, cold) more than what is present. Augustine touches the codex, cuts the letter, sets things on the desk — these gestures are precise and good — but there is no smell in this room, no quality of light. November fog in North Africa has a specific texture: damp wool, mold on stone, the harbor smell. One or two sensory specifics would complete the grounding without adding length.
- **Suggestion:** Add one olfactory or tactile detail to the Hippo room — not as description but woven into an action. When Augustine cuts the leather wrap, the smell of sea-water and oiled hide. When Possidius moves, the damp of his robe. This rewards the reader's imagination without slowing the scene.
- **Before:** `Augustine cut the leather wrap with his thumbnail. Inside was the principal copy of the letter, sealed separately, and the codex of the Septuagint...`
- **After:** `Augustine cut the leather wrap with his thumbnail. The leather released the smell of sea-water and something organic — fifty-three days of the Mediterranean. Inside was the principal copy of the letter, sealed separately, and the codex...`

**3. [Immersive Experience Editor] Emotional Resonance — Highlight What Works** (The Psalm 137 annotation and the 'shape of silence' passage)
- **Issue:** Not an issue — an observation. The Psalm 137 margin note discovery is the finest moment in the chapter: two-stage annotation in two different scripts and inks, the argument and then the self-correction, Augustine feeling the stylus indentation with his pad, Jerome having pressed hard as men press when writing something they have not said before. This is the chapter at its best — abstract grief made physical and specific. Similarly, 'the shape of the silence was the shape of forty years of conversation, and the silence was not a hole but a presence' is the emotional payoff the whole chapter earns. These two passages are the benchmark. The chapter's weaknesses are exactly where it departs from what these two moments do.
- **Suggestion:** When revising, ask of every major emotional beat: what is the equivalent of the stylus indentation? What is the physical object or sensation that carries the abstract weight? The chapter answers this question brilliantly twice. The aim is to answer it the same way in the institution paragraph and the deathbed passage.

**4. [Accessibility Editor] Exposition Balance** (Scroll summaries from Gaul, Hispania, and Carthage (paragraphs 26–29))
- **Issue:** The three scrolls provide rich period texture and establish the collapsing world Jerome inhabits. But they don't visibly connect to the chapter's intellectual argument until Jerome makes the connection himself, several paragraphs later. A general reader may experience this as historical world-building that delays the scene's purpose. One transitional sentence, as Jerome sets the scrolls down, would make the chapter's logic audible.
- **Suggestion:** After Jerome sets the scrolls beside the Septuagint, give him one silent act of association — not a speech, just a gesture that links the political collapse to the manuscripts in front of him.
- **Before:** `Jerome set the three scrolls down beside the Septuagint. He sat with them.`
- **After:** `Jerome set the three scrolls down beside the Septuagint — the letters of a failing empire beside the book he had spent his life trying to replace. He sat with them.`
