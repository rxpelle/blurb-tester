## Voice & Narrative Persona (Grade: A-)

This narrator has a personality — it's just not the one King usually deploys. Instead of the warm, arm-around-the-shoulder storyteller who editorializes openly, this voice is cold, precise, and moral in the way a surgeon is moral: it cuts exactly where it means to cut and then steps back. The narrator's opinions don't come in asides; they come in juxtaposition and omission, which is actually harder to pull off. 'He slept well' at the end of a scene in which Augustine has just rationalized the destruction of a better man is not neutral reporting — it's a verdict delivered in three words with perfect economy. That's a narrator with an edge, a point of view, a conscience watching a man without one. The voice is consistent all the way through, from the gate-keeper scene to Cirta to the final lamp extinguished, and it never wavers into the kind of literary affectation that kills historical fiction — the narrator never preens, never reaches for the decorative phrase, never shows you the research. Where the voice stumbles is in the long theological exchange, where it retreats almost entirely into court-stenographer mode, and in the opening Pelagius physical description, where the cataloguing is thorough but the narrator's eye goes neutral in a way it doesn't earn. The narrator's most distinctive move — giving us Augustine's self-knowledge ('This is what I do to the young men') without comfort or softening — is the chapter's signature and its best argument for itself. A King narrator grabs you. This one watches you back, and the watching is its own kind of grip.

### Good Examples
> 'He slept well.' — Three words at the chapter's close. Everything that preceded it — the theological debate, the forged dossier, the death warrant delivered in a sealed satchel by a boy with a stutter — makes these three words the most damning sentence in the chapter. The narrator doesn't editorialize. It doesn't have to. This is the narrator's personality in its purest form: the moral verdict disguised as a neutral report.

> 'This is what I do to the young men.' — Augustine's internal voice, delivered in the narrator's clinical third-person register. A King narrator would call this 'the moment you've been waiting for,' and it earns that. The narrator steps out of reportage and gives us a man watching himself do the thing he knows is wrong, using the present tense as an indictment that won't expire.

> 'He meant the homily. The meaning had not at any point been incompatible with the work that was waiting on the upper shelf.' — The narrator's dry irony is at its sharpest here. The sentence says: he was sincere. The sentence also says: sincerity and evil are not incompatible. That's a narrator with a point of view delivered with restraint that makes it more devastating, not less.

> 'The boy was discharging the commission in good faith. The bishop was doing what was required of the bishop.' — The narrator splits the frame in two, and the split IS the editorial comment. Augustine's self-dissociation rendered in parallel syntax. The narrator lets the structure do the moral work.

### Needs Improvement
> 'His hair was grey, cropped at the line of the jaw in the British manner. His face was the colour of a man who lived out of doors. He was perhaps fifty-two. He wore a plain tunic of undyed wool, belted at the waist with a piece of dark leather. His feet were bare...' — Six consecutive short declarative sentences cataloguing Pelagius's appearance. The narrator's eye here goes flat and procedural. Compare this to the chapter's best moments — the narrator's personality disappears and what's left is a costume list. King would say: the narrator needs to want something from this description. Let the eye betray Augustine's feeling, not just his observation.

> The long theological exchange in the courtyard — from 'In your conversion, in the garden at Milan' through Augustine's half-hour reply — reads like a transcript of the debate more than a narrator guiding us through it. The narrator nearly vanishes. We get the argument in full, which is intellectually impressive, but the narrator's signature detachment and dry moral framing drops out for several pages. A King narrator would find one moment in that exchange to slip the knife in — a single interjected observation that keeps us feeling the presence of the storytelling mind.

### Suggestions
- The narrator needs at least two more moments like 'He slept well' distributed through the chapter's middle — moments where the voice surfaces briefly to deliver a judgment in the same compressed, devastating register. The theological debate section is the obvious place. Even a single sentence between Pelagius's speech and Augustine's response that lets the narrator's eye land somewhere telling — not on the argument but on Augustine's hands, or what he does with his breathing — would restore the narrator's presence during the chapter's longest stretch.
- The physical description of Pelagius needs the narrator's personality put back in. Right now it reads like stage directions. Let Augustine's eye do the moral work — what does this man's body tell Augustine about the argument he's about to lose? King's narrators notice things because the noticing reveals the noticer. 'His feet were bare. The soles on the courtyard stones were as calloused as the feet of a working ploughman' is already good — but the narrator could land on one detail and let Augustine's response to it mean something before moving on.
- Consider one moment of direct, second-person reach — not necessarily to the reader but in the form of a rhetorical question embedded in the narrative voice, the way King's narrators sometimes step slightly outside the frame to acknowledge the reader exists. This chapter earns the distance it maintains, but one moment of the narrator appearing to speak to us rather than simply observing would sharpen our sense of who is telling this story and what they think we should understand by it.
- The opening paragraph is strong but the narrator's personality doesn't fully announce itself until the pen set down carefully on its tray. Consider whether the very first sentence can carry more of the narrator's characteristic edge — right now it reads slightly like the opening of good historical fiction rather than the opening of THIS narrator's particular vision of events.

## Deep POV & Interior Monologue (Grade: B+)

This chapter operates in a distinctive literary register — austere, behavioral, chronicle-inflected — that produces real power in its best moments but defaults to a middle psychic distance that keeps us watching Augustine rather than being him. The author has internalized one half of the deep POV equation: behavior as revelation (the pen set down in its exact position, the hand that stays on the desk, the eye pulled without volition to a fig about to fall). These are King-grade observations. But the other half — rendering thought as it happens in the character's own mental voice, unmediated — is deployed only in scattered bursts. Long stretches of dialogue are summarized ('He gave the answer he had been giving for ten years'), and Augustine's intellectual response to Pelagius's argument, which is the dramatic core of the chapter, is filtered through a narrator who tells us about the processing rather than putting us inside the processing. The chapter's single best moment — 'If I let him go to Palestine and let his writings circulate with the obvious truth of his face — within ten years there will be a Pelagian Latin Church that will eat my work. I will have to destroy him' — demonstrates exactly what the rest of the chapter is working around instead of inhabiting. When the author commits to direct interior voice, the prose becomes something genuinely frightening. The withholding of that voice for most of the chapter is a stylistic discipline that has real literary justification, but measured against King's standard — put the reader behind the character's eyes, make the prose invisible — it is also a missed opportunity to make a great chapter devastating.

### Good Examples
> 'The fig tree in the western corner had a fig that was about to fall, and his eye had gone to it without his knowing. He brought his eye back. Your will had been your will since boyhood. The sentence sat in him in a way the sentence had not sat in him when other men had said it.' — Involuntary attention (the fig) is classic King interiority: the mind betrays what the bishop won't. The thought rendered as landing, with its weight measured against previous encounters, is exactly the deep POV technique the chapter needs more of.

> 'He thought one thing only, and he thought it in the steady internal voice of the work, and he felt the verdict assemble itself in his chest a beat before the words assembled themselves in his head: If I let him go to Palestine and let his writings circulate with the obvious truth of his face — within ten years there will be a Pelagian Latin Church that will eat my work. The work cannot be eaten. The work is the wrapper. The wrapper has to hold. I will have to destroy him.' — The chapter's apex. 'The verdict assemble itself in his chest a beat before the words assembled themselves in his head' is precise phenomenology. The thought in Augustine's own stripped voice — 'the wrapper has to hold' — is the man.

> 'The reference was apt. Augustine took it without flinching outwardly. He flinched inside, in the part of himself that was not a bishop.' — The bifurcation of self — the bishop-performance and the man underneath it — is the chapter's central dramatic tension, and here it is rendered in three lean sentences without a filtering verb in sight.

> 'This is what I do to the young men.' — Five words of interior voice that do more damage than the paragraph of procedural explanation around them. This is Augustine knowing himself, and the knowing is cold.

> 'He slept well.' — After 'He did not sleep until very late' in an earlier section, this closing line is a devastating piece of character work delivered as pure behavioral report. No filter. No explanation. The reader does the work. King would approve.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The not-eating was a fact Augustine took in without comment.' — 'Took in' is a filter construction. We're watching Augustine notice rather than inhabiting his noticing. In deep POV: 'The man had not eaten. The figs were untouched. The bread was untouched.' Let the reader feel the significance without the narrator brokering it.

> 'Augustine considered.' — The classic filter verb. It announces thought rather than rendering it. Delete it and give us what he considers, in his voice, immediately.

> 'He gave the answer he had been giving for ten years. He gave it in the language of the technical position. He spoke of the massa damnata, the damned mass of fallen humanity...' — This is the chapter's largest POV failure. Augustine's half-hour argument, which is the intellectual equal of Pelagius's case, is entirely summarized. We don't experience him reaching for his best weapons and feeling their weight. We're told the performance happened. King's rule: if it matters, show it in real time or cut it. The summary here accidentally argues that Pelagius won.

> 'Augustine, who had been preparing a small Latin courtesy in his head since Eraclius had come to the writing room, found that he did not say it.' — The relative clause 'who had been preparing' turns interiority into biography. In deep POV this would be: 'He had a small Latin courtesy ready — he'd been turning it over since Eraclius left. He did not say it.' Present in the moment, not reported from a remove.

> 'He had not, in the years since, met another face that had stood in for that face. Ambrose's face was a public face; Simplicianus's was a teacher's.' — The inventory of faces is doing real work, but the sentence structure keeps us in the narrator's ledger rather than in Augustine's memory. The mother's face should intrude, not be catalogued. Deep POV would put us in the Ostia garden for one involuntary second before Augustine pulls himself back.

### Suggestions
- Find five places where Augustine's argument or response is summarized and write one of them in real time, in his own interior voice. 'He gave the answer he had been giving for ten years' is a placeholder. What is the first actual sentence of that answer? What does it feel like to say it while knowing, for the first time, it may not be enough? The gap between the public argument and the private doubt is where this chapter lives.
- Eliminate all instances of 'Augustine considered,' 'he took in,' 'he noticed,' 'he observed,' and any construction where a filter verb stands between the character and the thing being perceived. Replace each with the perception itself, unmediated.
- The *cor inquietum* recognition — the moment Augustine realizes Pelagius has just handed him his own load-bearing sentence with a reading that dismantles his position — deserves a paragraph of genuine interior experience. Right now it gives us 'His mouth was dry' and 'He did not reach for the cup,' which is good behavioral rendering but not enough. What does it feel like to have your own best sentence used against you by someone who wept over it? Give us fifteen seconds inside that.
- The chapter's opening section (before Augustine sits at the desk) is written in chronicle mode — past perfect, third-person distant, no interiority. This is a deliberate choice and it has period-appropriate gravity. But consider opening even one beat before Eraclius's arrival with Augustine mid-thought in the writing room — a single sentence of his interior life before the world intrudes. It would deepen the contrast between the man-at-work and the bishop-under-siege.
- The Mark section is the chapter's most procedurally distanced passage. Augustine does not lie — the chapter is meticulous about this. But the gap between what he tells Mark and what he withholds is the moral engine of the whole piece. Give us one interior sentence during that conversation: not after it, not in retrospect, but in the moment, while the boy is smiling. What Augustine feels in that specific second — or refuses to feel — is the chapter's real subject.

## Backstory & Characterization (Grade: A-)

This chapter is doing something genuinely difficult — revealing the inner lives of two fifth-century theologians through argument, physical gesture, and administrative action — and it pulls it off at a high level. King's core principle is that character is behavior, and this chapter understands that deeply. Augustine is not described as ruthless, disciplined, politically calculating, and haunted. He is shown to be those things through the precise placement of a pen on its tray, the checking of hidden materials, the cold internal sentence 'I will have to destroy him,' and the final three words 'He slept well.' Pelagius is revealed entirely through his body — the calloused soles, the darn in a different-colored thread, the single small swallow of water — and through the quality of his argument, which is unanswerable. The chapter's greatest achievement is Peter's moral crisis rendered through the movement of a stylus: the pause, the stillness, the fraction of movement, the stillness again. That is a complete psychological event in four sentences. The weaknesses are occasional resort to biographical summary — 'the second son of a small landowner' for Peter, the long relative clause establishing Lawrence's relationship to Augustine — which are insertions of told backstory into a chapter that elsewhere earns everything through show. These are minor intrusions in an otherwise exceptionally controlled performance. The Lawrence funeral section earns its backstory through action (Augustine actually weeping, the weight of the bier described as 'the weight of a tall man gone thin in his last illness') and then delivers the most devastating irony of the chapter: Augustine returning to the writing room and working until the second hour after midnight with clean efficiency, having just wept over his old friend.

### Good Examples
> 'He set it down carefully, on its tray, in the position it lived in. The hand that had set it down stayed on the desk a small moment and then moved to his lap.' — Two physical actions define Augustine's entire psychology: the obsessive precision, the will to contain. No adjective required.

> 'He slept well.' — Three words ending the chapter. The entire preceding hour of Cicero-as-justification, the sealed letters routing a man to the Egyptian desert, and this. King would envy this. It is backstory and characterization and moral verdict in a fragment.

> 'The boy's stylus paused on the wax. He did not lift his head. The stylus stayed where it had stopped. Augustine watched the stylus.' — A complete moral crisis in four sentences. Character under pressure delivered entirely through the movement of an object. The watching is devastating.

> 'The soles on the courtyard stones were as calloused as the feet of a working ploughman.' — Pelagius's entire theology of bodily discipline in one physical detail. This is a man who walks what he preaches. King's specific-over-general principle at full strength.

> 'The smile was not the political smile he had worn for sixteen years in courtyards. He caught himself wearing it, and he did not stop wearing it.' — Augustine's backstory (sixteen years of public performance) delivered in a caught reflex. And then the choice to stay with the real smile. That choice is characterization.

> 'He thought one thing only... If I let him go to Palestine and let his writings circulate with the obvious truth of his face — within ten years there will be a Pelagian Latin Church that will eat my work. The work cannot be eaten. The wrapper has to hold. I will have to destroy him.' — The bureaucratic voice of the death sentence. 'The obvious truth of his face' is the telling phrase — Augustine is not condemning a heretic, he is condemning a man who is right.

> 'He had a quick smile and a slight stutter on initial p's that he had been working since boyhood to control... The p would not come.' — Character introduced economically, then the detail pays off under pressure exactly when it should. The stutter arrives on the word 'promise.' That is earned.

> 'Augustine reached out, picked up the cup, and drank what the older man had not drunk. The cup was light in his hand. The water tasted of nothing in particular — he had expected, against reason, that it would taste of something — and he set the cup down empty.' — An action that delivers grief, loss, hunger for grace, and the gap between expectation and reality in one continuous gesture. No interior monologue needed.

### Needs Improvement
> 'Peter was twenty-two, the second son of a small landowner from the south, a sub-deacon of the basilica...' — 'The second son of a small landowner' is pure resume. It does nothing. We don't need his origin. What follows ('the cleanest hand in the scriptorium') is earned and specific; this prefix is not.

> 'Lawrence, the bishop of Cirta, who had been a friend of Augustine's since the years at Carthage when both were students under the same grammarian and who had stood as one of the consecrating bishops at Augustine's episcopal ordination in 395...' — This is a relative clause doing the work a scene could do. The backstory is real and needed, but it's dropped in as a biographical invoice. By contrast, the homily delivers the same relationship ('the small house at Carthage in which they had read Cicero together at nineteen and twenty') through Augustine's own voice in a public act of grief — which is the version that works.

> 'He was perhaps fifty-two.' — Unnecessary. We know he is old. The calloused feet, the grey hair cropped at the jaw, the discipline of fasting — these have already aged him. The approximate number actually weakens the accumulation of physical specifics around it.

### Suggestions
- Cut 'the second son of a small landowner from the south' from Peter's introduction. What remains — sub-deacon, cleanest hand in the scriptorium — is all you need and it's earned. The origin does no work here.
- The Lawrence biographical clause ('who had been a friend of Augustine's since the years at Carthage when both were students under the same grammarian and who had stood as one of the consecrating bishops...') should be cut from the header sentence. The homily already delivers the Carthage friendship through Augustine's own words in a live scene. Trust it to do that work alone. The reader doesn't need the credentials before the action.
- Consider cutting 'He was perhaps fifty-two' from the Pelagius physical description. The body has already told us what we need. The approximation signals uncertainty about the character and slightly flattens what is otherwise a catalogue of precise and damning specifics.
- The line 'He had been preparing, for two years, the materials by which he intended to refute the man who was now standing in his cloister' is a small tell. The paragraph already shows this — the iron-locked chest, the going to the lower shelf to check — so the telling is redundant. Cut the sentence and let the actions carry it.
- Augustine's interior accounting of the letters to Mark — particularly the paragraph beginning 'The framing omitted that two of the seven letters did not in fact concern doctrine but concerned the placement of a man in a province' — is the one place where the prose explains its own machinery at slightly more length than necessary. The paragraph works. But consider whether the final revelation ('a man of fifty-two who had walked barefoot from the Hippo harbour would not survive the second month of confinement') could arrive even later and more quietly — perhaps as a single closing clause rather than a full periodic sentence — so that the reader's recognition lands a beat after the chapter's forward motion has moved on.

## Setting & Sensory Detail (Grade: A-)

This chapter does something rare and difficult: it builds a sensory world out of almost nothing — a courtyard, a writing room, a cold crypt — and makes each location feel inhabited and specific. The best details are doing two or three jobs at once. The harbor smell ('fish and pitch and salt') arrives, crosses the courtyard, and goes past — that verb 'went past' turns a smell into weather, into time passing, into the indifferent world continuing while two men argue about the soul. The fig tree in the western corner earns its keep twice: first as a setting anchor, then as the object Augustine's eye flies to 'without his knowing' at the precise moment Pelagius's argument begins to land — the natural world indifferent to the crisis, and Augustine's distraction revealing that the argument is hitting him somewhere he doesn't want to admit. The drinking of Pelagius's water at the end is the chapter's best sensory beat: 'The water tasted of nothing in particular — he had expected, against reason, that it would taste of something.' The anticlimax IS the meaning. Taste used as emotional payoff. That's King-level double duty. Where the chapter falters is in the writing room — the place where the actual evil is done. The courtyard, where nothing sinister happens, is rich with light and smell and sound. The writing room, where Augustine dictates a forgery and sentences a man to death, is sensory-bare. The conspiracy is conceived in a space with almost no physical presence. The December road to Cirta has no cold in it. The Salvia sailing is compressed to five words. These are real gaps, not stylistic asceticism — the contrast in sensory density between the 'innocent' space and the 'guilty' space is backwards from where it should be.

### Good Examples
> 'The smell of the harbour, half a mile away, came up the road and into the courtyard on the wind — fish and pitch and salt — and went past.' — Three precise smells, and the verb 'went past' animates the wind as a presence that enters and leaves, making the setting live in real time.

> 'the courtyard held the light without holding the heat' — This is a detail you can only write if you've stood in a Mediterranean courtyard in October. It nails temperature and light simultaneously, and it requires no explanation.

> 'The fig tree in the western corner had a fig that was about to fall, and his eye had gone to it without his knowing.' — Setting doing triple duty: grounds the space, marks the season, and reveals Augustine's mind beginning to crack under Pelagius's argument without Augustine admitting it.

> 'The water tasted of nothing in particular — he had expected, against reason, that it would taste of something — and he set the cup down empty.' — Taste used as emotional reckoning. The sensory disappointment carries the whole weight of what Augustine has decided to do to the man who drank from this cup.

> 'From somewhere inside the cloister a voice was reading aloud — Stephen, probably, at the prayers — and the cadence of the reading came faintly across the courtyard and went away.' — Ambient sound that locates us in a working monastery, marks the passage of time, and then disappears. The 'went away' mirrors the chapter's whole structure of things that arrive and pass.

> 'The soles on the courtyard stones were as calloused as the feet of a working ploughman.' — Touch and comparison that tell us everything about Pelagius's life without a word of explanation. King calls this the 'telling detail.' This is one.

> 'The smell of the church was the smell of incense and beeswax and packed wool, and beneath it, in the cold of the December stone underfoot, the smell of cold stone.' — Layered smell, the 'underfoot' adding body and touch to what could have been merely atmospheric. 'Packed wool' is the right detail — not 'the congregation' but the material fact of their bodies.

### Needs Improvement
> The writing room — where Augustine dictates the forgery, where Peter pauses with his stylus, where the conspiracy is born — has almost no sensory life. We know there is a desk, a shelf, a chest with an iron lock. We do not smell the lamp oil or the old parchment, do not hear the scratch of the stylus on wax, do not feel the cold of a room lit only by a single lamp after midnight. The place where the evil actually happens is the chapter's most sensory-absent space, which is the reverse of where the weight should land.

> 'They rode the first stage in silence.' — A December ride from Hippo to Cirta across North African hill country has no cold in it, no frost on the grass, no sound of hooves, no breath-cloud from the horses. The road to a funeral in winter deserves one physical detail. Its sensory blankness feels like an omission rather than a choice.

> 'on the Friday's tide the Salvia sailed' — The moment when Augustine's death sentence goes into motion — irreversible, sealed, crossing the water — is compressed to five words with no sensory grounding at all. No harbor dawn, no smell of pitch, no creak of rigging, no sound of the ship moving away. A moment this morally weighty should have at least one physical anchor.

### Suggestions
- Give the writing room one or two specific sensory anchors — particularly smell. The room where the conspiracy is born should feel more physically present than the courtyard where the innocent conversation happens. Lamp oil burning low, cold stone, the particular smell of old vellum or fresh ink — something that makes the corruption embodied rather than abstract.
- The December road to Cirta needs one physical detail. It doesn't need much — even a single line about cold air or the sound of the horses — but right now it reads like an editorial cut rather than a scene. King would never let a scene transition that carries this much moral weight pass without grounding it in the body.
- The Salvia's departure could earn one concrete image — the sound of lines being cast, the cold harbor morning, the smell of pitch — to match the weight of what the ship is carrying. Five words for the moment of irreversible action is too little. One more sentence, sensory and specific, would make the reader feel the door closing.
- Augustine's bad knee is a terrific recurring King-style detail — 'His right knee was bad. He did not let it show in the walking.' — but it disappears after the courtyard scene. Bring it back once, briefly, in the writing room or at the crypt. The body's small complaints have a way of making moral grandeur feel human and real, and this one is established and then abandoned.

## Tension & Dread Building (Grade: A-)

This chapter is a masterclass in what King calls the slow leak — dread seeping through the cracks of ordinary procedure before the floor gives way. The tension architecture is nearly flawless in its first half: the gate-keeper who doesn't know names, Eraclius's changed face, the pen set down with such deliberate care, the coded glance at the hidden objects in the codex — all of it does what King's best work does, making the familiar strange before we're told anything is wrong. The dramatic irony is devastating and sustained. Pelagius is, demonstrably, the better man arguing the truer position, and we watch Augustine know this, briefly catch himself smiling genuinely, and then assemble the machinery of destruction. The villain is not the villain; the protagonist is. That inversion, held without sentimentality or authorial editorializing, is the engine of the chapter's dread. 'He slept well' at the very end is King-level compression: two words that indict a man, a church, and fifteen centuries. The weaknesses are real but narrow: the surveillance subplot (the watcher at the milestone post-house) is introduced and immediately mothballed, releasing tension instead of coiling it; and the single most important revelation in the chapter — that two of the seven letters constitute a death sentence — is buried in the same procedural syntax as the grain-office courier address. It should land like a fist and instead lands like a footnote. The Peter section is the chapter's emotional heart and is close to perfect; the Mark section misfires on its most important sentence.

### Good Examples
> 'The gate-keeper, who was new and did not know names of men outside the African church, had repeated the name to Eraclius, who had crossed himself once in private and had then walked to the writing room with a face on him that Augustine had not seen before.' — Dread delivered through institutional detail. The private crossing, the new gate-keeper's ignorance, the unprecedented face: three pieces of information that tell us everything before anything has been named.

> 'He set it down carefully, on its tray, in the position it lived in. The hand that had set it down stayed on the desk a small moment and then moved to his lap. He kept his face the face it had been when Eraclius came in.' — King's 'ordinary made unsettling' principle at full force. The pen lives in a position. The face is a managed object. A man's entire interior is disclosed by what his hands do before he controls them.

> 'If I let him go to Palestine and let his writings circulate with the obvious truth of his face — within ten years there will be a Pelagian Latin Church that will eat my work. The work cannot be eaten. The work is the wrapper. The wrapper has to hold. I will have to destroy him.' — The pivot. The quiet moment before the storm is the storm. What's chilling is the clean logic: no passion, no hate, just architecture. The phrase 'the obvious truth of his face' is what makes it devastating — Augustine admits Pelagius is right, and that's exactly why he must die.

> 'By the second week he will be marking passages without the small pause. By the third month he will be a young man of a particular kind, and the kind will not unlearn what it has learned tonight in the next forty years of his life. This is what I do to the young men.' — This is the chapter's coldest moment. Not the death sentence. This. The forty-year corruption of a twenty-two-year-old is narrated with the detachment of a man noting weather patterns, and the phrase 'the kind will not unlearn' does everything.

> 'He reached out, picked up the cup, and drank what the older man had not drunk. The cup was light in his hand. The water tasted of nothing in particular — he had expected, against reason, that it would taste of something — and he set the cup down empty.' — The disappointed expectation is perfect Kingian horror: the moment where the character reaches for transcendence and gets nothing. Augustine drinks Pelagius's leavings hoping for a sign, receives only water, and continues. The emptiness of the cup is the chapter's spiritual verdict before the chapter delivers its moral one.

> 'He slept well.' — Two words doing the work of a paragraph. After 'He did not sleep until very late' following Peter's corruption, the final sentence of the chapter is a closed door. Augustine has crossed over. The contrast is precise and damning.

### Needs Improvement
> 'A man came up the western road yesterday at the hour Pelagius would have left the Salvia at Carthage. He took rooms at the inn at the second mile-stone. He paid for three days. He has not stayed three days. He went south at the second hour of this morning by a hired mule.' — This surveillance subplot is introduced with real menace — who is watching? a Pelagian spy? a prefectural agent? — and then dissolved into procedure: 'go to the harbour, walk it for two weeks, find me if you see the face.' The coiled tension releases without paying off. King would either make this watcher palpable — a description, a behavior, a reason for fear — or cut it entirely. As written it punctures the dread of the Peter scene that precedes it without replacing it.

> 'The framing omitted that two of the seven letters did not in fact concern doctrine but concerned the placement of a man in a province under a particular kind of supervision; that the deacon Justin in the prefecture had been cultivated for two years in expectation of being asked to facilitate such placements; that the request now being made through Justin would, in the year of its eventual execution, route a man whose conviction had not yet been secured to a region of the Egyptian western desert in which a man of fifty-two who had walked barefoot from the Hippo harbour would not survive the second month of confinement.' — This is the death sentence. Pelagius is going to die in the Egyptian desert, barefoot, fifty-two years old. The reader is told this in one subordinate clause buried inside a sentence about bureaucratic routing. 'Would not survive the second month of confinement' is doing the entire emotional weight, and the sentence structure buries it under 'placement in a province under a particular kind of supervision.' King's principle: earn the quiet exit with a moment of stillness before it. Let the reader see the barefoot feet on the harbour stones, and then land the sentence.

> 'The fig in the western corner did not fall.' — Used well once, this becomes a tic by its third appearance. The first use during the theological debate is good — it grounds us, it names Augustine's distraction. But returning to it as a marker of tension ('The fig tree in the western corner moved a little in the small wind' during the post-conversation scene) has diminishing returns. By the third repetition it's a nervous habit rather than a deliberate device. One fig tree moment, placed precisely at the pivotal argument about the will, would land harder than three.

### Suggestions
- The watcher at the milestone needs to be either paid forward with menace or cut. If kept: give Stephen one physical detail about the man's face that makes him frightening — something off about him, something that doesn't belong — so that when Augustine says 'he is not to act, he is to find me,' we feel the implication. If cut: the chapter loses nothing and the Peter scene's momentum is uninterrupted.
- The death-sentence revelation needs its own sentence, alone, with white space around it. Pull 'a man of fifty-two who had walked barefoot from the Hippo harbour would not survive the second month of confinement' out of its subordinate clause and let it stand. Then cut to: 'The boy was discharging the commission in good faith.' The contrast does the work without editorial intervention.
- During the theological debate, give Augustine one moment of genuine fear — not intellectual discomfort, but visceral fear — that Pelagius is right and that Augustine knows he is right. Right now the internal experience reads as cool and managerial. King's technique: put a physical sensation in the body. Augustine's hands on the table, the cold water he doesn't drink, the dry mouth — these are there, but fleeting. A single moment where the reader feels Augustine's theology cracking from the inside, before he seals it back up, would make the 'I will have to destroy him' pivot land with greater force.
- The funeral homily section is beautiful and earns its length — but consider cutting the second half of it (from 'He meant the homily' through 'He accepted the hand') and instead moving directly from the homily's delivery into the return to the writing room and the single sentence: 'He meant the homily. He delivered it. He went home and cut the forty-one transcriptions in four hours. He slept well.' The irony is more devastating when the gap is narrowed, not widened.
- The codex of Cassian's Conferences — used beautifully as a hiding place in the opening — is checked once on departure and once on return, but what's inside (the disc, the leather packet, the folded letter from Anthony) is never paid off within the chapter. Either seed one more glimpse that makes the contents feel like a live threat, or let the one opening check stand as sufficient. Right now the return check feels like a loose thread waiting to be pulled — which may be intentional for a later chapter, but in this chapter it creates micro-tension that dissipates without release.

## Dialogue & Voice (Grade: A-)

This is exceptional dialogue craft operating at a level most literary fiction never reaches. The chapter's central achievement is its mastery of subtext: the theological argument between Augustine and Pelagius is conducted simultaneously on three tracks — what is said, what is meant, and what has already been decided before either man opens his mouth. The Peter scene is a genuine masterclass. The boy's half-finished question — 'Father, is that —' followed by silence — and Augustine's refusal to ask it for him is exactly how King means dialogue should work: not what characters say but what they cannot or will not say, and what that silence costs both of them. The Possidius exchange at the post-house ('Cirta first?' / 'No. Rome first.') shows two men communicating in the compressed shorthand of a decade of shared dangerous work — no explaining, no stage-setting, just the information and the understanding of it. The fatal civility of 'Will we meet again.' / 'I think not.' / 'I think not also.' lands like a body blow precisely because neither man raises his voice. Two weaknesses: the Mark scene goes on too long in procedural instruction that could be cut by a third without losing anything, and the Cicero passage Augustine delivers to Peter reads slightly more like a prepared rationalization than spoken thought — it's a little too finished, too rhetorical, which is partly the point but which nudges it from dialogue into monologue. The chapter's great discovery is that in the right hands, a conversation about predestination and grace can have more menace than a man with a knife. That discovery is almost fully realized.

### Good Examples
> 'Father, is that — ' / He stopped. He did not finish the question. He waited. / Augustine waited too. The boy needed to ask the question or to decide not to ask it. Augustine would not ask it for him. — The whole moral architecture of the chapter lives in that unfinished sentence and the silence after it. Perfect.

> 'Will we meet again.' / 'I think not.' / 'I think not also.' He did not say it sadly. He said it in the tone of a man recording a probable fact. — Devastating. Pelagius knows. Augustine knows. No one says so. King calls this the iceberg — nine-tenths under the water.

> 'Cirta first?' Augustine said, without altering his: 'No. Rome first. Cirta when the Pelagian materials are clean.' Possidius nodded once and went on toward the cathedral. — Two words and the whole campaign is in motion. This is what long friendship under pressure sounds like.

> 'The pears,' he said. 'I think we have all stolen pears, Bishop, when we were boys, but it is a great achievement to have written about them so that one cannot afterwards eat a pear without thinking of the will.' — Pelagius's voice here: warm, precise, slightly ironic, not quite a compliment. Distinct from anyone else in the room.

> 'Father — I have begun. I have set out the materials for your review on the small table. The transcriptions I have made will not satisfy you in their first form; I have erred on the side of fidelity to the qualifications. I will tighten on your direction.' — Peter on the wax tablet. Even his written note sounds like him: deferential, a little anxious, already complicit and already knowing it.

### Needs Improvement
> The entire Mark commissioning scene from 'You are to leave for Rome on the next ship out of the harbour, which will be the Salvia on Friday's tide' through 'The order matters. Do you understand the order.' — This is eight paragraphs of procedural instruction that flattens dramatically. Mark barely exists as a person; he's a delivery vehicle. His stutter moment is the one spark of human life and it's surrounded by a ledger of logistics. King would cut this to four exchanges: the commission, the stakes, the stutter, and the exit. Trust the reader to understand that a satchel of seven letters is being hand-delivered.

> 'Peter. There is a passage in Cicero, in the De Inventione, on the duty of the orator in a forensic case. Cicero says that the orator's duty is to set the matter before the judges in the form most useful to the orator's case...' [continues for six sentences] — Augustine is rationalizing to himself as much as answering Peter, which is valid dramatically, but the speech is too complete, too argued-through. Real self-justification has gaps and accelerations. A man trying to make this sound righteous would rush through the weak part ('The orator trusts the opposing orator to do the same') and linger on the strong. As written it has the cadence of a letter, not a voice.

### Suggestions
- Cut the Mark scene by at least forty percent. Keep: the commission, the two hidden letters, the stutter ('I p-promise — Father, I know it'), and the boy smiling as he goes. The list of seven recipients and the detailed delivery order kills momentum. The reader doesn't need the street address of the conspiracy to feel its weight — they need Mark's face.
- Let Peter's silence do more work. After 'I will do it,' give him one more beat — a physical action, not a line of dialogue. He picks up the key. He doesn't look at Augustine. Something that shows his body has understood something his voice was not able to ask. The scene currently ends a line or two too early after the moral pivot.
- In the theological exchange, consider breaking Pelagius's central long speech ('And if a will has at all times the capacity to respond...') at least once with a beat of reaction from Augustine — a small physical tell, a pause, a shift in posture. The speech runs uninterrupted for three long paragraphs and then Augustine's response is only interior. One interjection or visible reaction from Augustine inside the speech would make it feel more like two men in a room and less like a lecture series.
- The *cor inquietum* exchange is the chapter's best theological moment. 'You read the line attentively.' / 'I have read the line many times, Bishop. I have wept over the line. I think it is the line.' — That 'I have wept over the line' is the one moment where Pelagius lets Augustine see him. Make sure nothing in the surrounding paragraphs over-explains it. Right now the narration tells us Augustine's mouth is dry, that he had spoken half an hour without drinking. Trust the reader to feel that dryness without being told it is there.

## Pacing & Structure (Grade: A-)

This is a sophisticated, largely masterful piece of pacing work — the kind of slow-burn control that King uses in his best non-supernatural horror, where the monster is a man making reasonable decisions. The chapter runs six scenes in roughly ascending moral gravity: arrival, debate, decision/deployment (Peter), deployment (Mark), funeral, and return. The structural spine is clean and the sentence-level rhythms are exceptional — short declaratives hammer home shock and decision, longer periods carry the philosophical weight of the debate, and the two-word final line lands like a trapdoor. The chapter's great achievement is the deliberate mismatch between its pace and its content: it moves slowly, thoughtfully, even beautifully, while Augustine assembles the machinery of destruction. That tension is the engine. The weaknesses are real but correctable: the theological debate is about 15% too long, the Mark scene is structurally redundant with the Peter scene and undersells its own horror, and a surveillance subplot arrives post-funeral without the weight it needs. The funeral itself is a bold pacing choice — right when the plot demands acceleration, the writer hits the brakes — but it pays off because it renders Augustine's genuine grief, which makes everything else more monstrous. King would approve the instinct, though he'd tighten the execution.

### Good Examples
> 'Augustine set down the pen. He set it down carefully, on its tray, in the position it lived in. The hand that had set it down stayed on the desk a small moment and then moved to his lap.' — Freeze-frame sentence structure. The hand detaches and becomes its own actor. This is how King handles shock: slow the clock, make every gesture monumental.

> 'The boy's stylus paused on the wax. He did not lift his head. The stylus stayed where it had stopped. Augustine watched the stylus.' — The chapter's best moment of physical pacing. Moral horror rendered as a stopped object. The repetition of 'stylus' instead of 'he' keeps the horror at arm's length, which makes it worse.

> 'If I let him go to Palestine and let his writings circulate with the obvious truth of his face — within ten years there will be a Pelagian Latin Church that will eat my work. The work cannot be eaten. The work is the wrapper. The wrapper has to hold. I will have to destroy him.' — The structural pivot of the entire chapter. Italicized, cold, in one internal breath. The chapter turns here and everything before is prologue. Delivered after pages of genuine admiration, this lands with the weight of a verdict.

> 'He slept well.' — Two words. The chapter's final line. Everything before it is context; this is the verdict. King understands that the scariest sentence in a long chapter is often the shortest.

### Needs Improvement
> 'a commentary on the Pauline epistles in thirteen books; a tract called De Natura which I have in three copies in this room, of which one is from Rome and two are from Carthage and which are, between them, complete; a long letter to a Roman matron called Demetrias...' — This procedural inventory runs over 200 words in speech. It is necessary information, but the pacing here stalls right at the threshold of the chapter's great moral horror. The reader is about to watch a church father fabricate heresy charges; instead they get a bibliography. Compress this to a third of its length.

> 'The state of the Pelagian house at Aquileia. The death of an old monk in Britain whom both had known by reputation. The condition of the roads through Etruria after the autumn rains. The condition of the Carthage post in this present year.' — The small-talk list works conceptually (two men circling each other) but in execution it's a series of summary notations rather than scenes. It deflates the tension between the opening of the conversation and the arrival of the real argument. One or two of these items, dramatized briefly, would do more work than four items listed.

> 'A man came up the western road yesterday at the hour Pelagius would have left the Salvia at Carthage. He took rooms at the inn at the second mile-stone...' — This surveillance subplot arrives sandwiched between the funeral scene and the return to writing. It is significant — someone is tracking Augustine, possibly Pelagian agents — but it lands in passing dialogue between horse changes and is never resolved in this chapter. Either give it a scene or cut it to a sentence. Right now it arrives with insufficient weight and then disappears, leaving the reader's attention split at the wrong moment.

### Suggestions
- The theological debate runs roughly 1,800 words in the courtyard. The load-bearing exchanges are three: Pelagius's main will-argument, Augustine's formal response, and the cor inquietum exchange. Everything between — the small talk, the lists of holy men, the secondary volleys — could be compressed by 300-400 words. The debate should feel long by the reader's body clock, not by actual word count. King's rule: earn every page. The pear passage ('I think we have all stolen pears') earns its page. Much of the intervening middle does not.
- The Peter scene and the Mark scene are structurally parallel: Augustine deploys a young subordinate, we watch the subordinate's moral processing, we see how much he understands. The Peter scene is the chapter's emotional and moral climax. The Mark scene, which follows, cannot match it and risks diluting it. Consider whether the Mark scene could be compressed to its essential horror — the reveal that two letters route Pelagius to desert confinement — and let the Peter scene breathe as the chapter's true ending-before-the-ending.
- The surveillance subplot (the watcher on the western road, Stephen in the bell tower) needs a decision: develop it or cut it. As written, it introduces threat and then abandons it within three paragraphs. If this is a setup for a future chapter, a single line is enough. If it belongs here, give it a scene — show Stephen's face, let Augustine sit with the implication. Right now it arrives between the funeral and the return to work, where it competes with both.
- The structural echo you've built between 'He did not sleep until very late' (after the Peter scene) and 'He slept well' (the final line) is the chapter's hidden architecture — the reader who notices it gets a second dose of horror. Prime this echo. Consider adding a single line at the return to the writing room that recalls the earlier sleeplessness, so the reader's ear is tuned to catch 'He slept well' as a callback rather than a coincidence.
- The physical description of Pelagius on his arrival runs four paragraphs before he speaks a word. The detail is earned — 'The soles on the courtyard stones were as calloused as the feet of a working ploughman' is perfect — but two of the four paragraphs could be condensed. King's principle: description is fine, but every line of description is a line of deferred action. Get Pelagius talking faster. The man is more terrifying in motion than in still life.

## Themes & Subtext (Grade: A-)

This chapter is doing something genuinely rare: it uses the theological argument as the thematic engine, so the debate about the will IS the moral drama, not decoration on top of it. Pelagius argues the will is 'wounded but standing' — capable of small goods, able to respond. Augustine then proves it, in the worst possible way: he chooses, in full knowledge, to destroy a man whose argument he cannot defeat. The deepest irony in the chapter never gets named. It doesn't need to. Augustine's decision in the courtyard — 'I will have to destroy him' — is Exhibit A for Pelagius's case. A will that had no power could not make that choice. A man in a state of pure fallen incapacity could not premeditate with this much craft. The plot enacts the theology. That is structural irony operating at the level of story architecture, and it works because the reader arrives at it themselves. The chapter is also doing strong work on the theme of institutional power versus intellectual truth: Augustine loses the argument, knows he loses it, and then launders the loss through bureaucratic procedure — sealed letters, junior secretaries, a courier who goes out smiling. The corruption ripples forward through good-faith intermediaries who carry it without understanding it. Mark goes out smiling. Peter's stylus pauses, then moves. The machinery is ordinary and cheerful. This is how the chapter holds its horror without announcing it. Where the chapter shows strain is in the Peter scene, where Augustine over-explains his own rationalization ('The orator does not lie. The orator selects') — it's in character and dramatically earned, but it does the reader's interpretive work for them slightly more than King would allow. The surveillance subplot introduced at the end also hasn't yet found its thematic anchor. It reads as plot infrastructure rather than a resonant complication of the themes already in motion.

### Good Examples
> 'I will have to destroy him.' Then he said, aloud: 'Pelagius. The light is going. You will want to be back at the harbour for the evening tide.' — The gap between the internal verdict and the courteous surface is the entire theme of the chapter, compressed into two lines. King's 'invisible prose' principle: the horror doesn't need to be announced. The juxtaposition does everything.

> 'Augustine, who had had the answer ready for this question since Pelagius had stood up from the stool in the courtyard four hours earlier...' — The bureaucratic planning session with Peter is horrifying precisely because of this detail. Augustine rehearsed the Cicero speech before Peter asked the question. He anticipated the conscience. This is subtext: the corruption isn't impulsive; it's architected.

> He reached out, picked up the cup, and drank what the older man had not drunk. The cup was light in his hand. The water tasted of nothing in particular — he had expected, against reason, that it would taste of something — and he set the cup down empty. — This is the chapter's best single image. It operates below the conscious level. Envy, spiritual hunger, the desire to consume what Pelagius has, the anti-communion of drinking a holy man's leavings — none of this is stated. King's rule: the image does the work.

> 'By the third month he will be a young man of a particular kind, and the kind will not unlearn what it has learned tonight in the next forty years of his life. This is what I do to the young men.' — The corruption passing forward through the young is one of the chapter's quietest and most devastating themes. Augustine sees it clearly and continues. The last sentence is slightly on-the-nose but is saved by its placement: it's a thought, not a judgment, and it doesn't stop anything.

> He meant the homily. [...] The meaning had not at any point been incompatible with the work that was waiting on the upper shelf. — This is the chapter's most disturbing psychological claim, and it's delivered with perfect flatness. Authentic feeling and great evil coexist in the same man without contradiction. No author's note required.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The orator does not lie. The orator does not say what is not in the texts. The orator selects from the texts what shows the form of the case the orator is making...' — The Cicero speech is Augustine explaining his own rationalization in explicit, pedagogical terms. It's in character and it's historically plausible, but it works against the chapter's stronger instinct to let the reader arrive at the horror themselves. A shorter version of this — three sentences instead of eight — would trust the reader more and land harder.

> A man came up the western road yesterday at the hour Pelagius would have left the *Salvia* at Carthage. He took rooms at the inn at the second mile-stone... — The surveillance subplot introduced at the post-house is plot infrastructure that hasn't yet earned its thematic place in this chapter. It reads as setup for a future chapter rather than a resonant complication of what we've just watched Augustine do. The chapter would be tighter ending at the return from Cirta, with Augustine sleeping well.

### Suggestions
- Trust the wooden cup more than the Cicero speech. The image of Augustine drinking Pelagius's water does in one sentence what the rhetoric lecture does in eight. Consider cutting the Cicero speech by half and letting the reader complete the thought.
- The fig tree appears at least four times but never falls — this is almost certainly intentional (the moment of moral catastrophe that doesn't announce itself), but the pattern is close enough to repetition that it risks feeling like a planted symbol rather than an observed detail. Let it appear twice, not four times, and it will feel more earned.
- The surveillance subplot (the man on the western road, Stephen in the bell tower) introduces a thriller register that isn't yet connected to the chapter's thematic core. Either anchor it to a specific emotional beat — Augustine's fear that Pelagius's face is already circulating, that holiness is contagious — or hold it for a later chapter where it can land with meaning.
- The Monica moment is the chapter's most emotionally charged piece of subtext — Augustine sees his mother's face when asked if he has known holy men, and keeps going anyway — but it's slightly undercut by Augustine's inward flinch being described rather than performed. 'He flinched inside, in the part of himself that was not a bishop' tells us what the image of the well-rim and Monica's hand should be left to show. Cut the flinch sentence; let the image of the Ostia garden do the work.
- Peter's stylus pausing on the wax is one of the best physical enactments of conscience in the chapter. Consider giving it one more beat — the stylus that pauses, starts, pauses again — before the boy asks his question. The conscience should have a stutter the way Mark has a stutter on initial p's. That parallel would connect the two young men as variations on the same theme of complicity and would feel discovered rather than arranged.
- The thematic irony at the core of the chapter — that Augustine's premeditated choice to destroy Pelagius proves Pelagius's argument about the will — never needs to be stated, and the chapter wisely doesn't state it. But it could be sharpened by one small addition: after Augustine's internal verdict ('I will have to destroy him'), have him think specifically of Pelagius's line — 'the will is wounded but standing' — without comment. Let the reader close the loop themselves. That's King's method: put the two things adjacent and walk away.

## Cultural Specificity & Time Period (Grade: A-)

This chapter operates at a level of historical immersion that is genuinely rare. The author has done the scholarship and then — crucially — done the harder work of making the scholarship invisible. The material culture of 411 CE North Africa is embedded in the prose the way King embeds the details of Castle Rock: not as decoration, but as the air characters breathe. Wax tablets, cedar pen-boxes, iron-locked chests, earthen pitchers, the wooden cup that weighs nothing — the physical world is specific and earned. The ecclesiastical machinery (scriporium, cloister, sub-deacon, lay parlour, synodical proceedings, quartan fever, pectoral cross of bronze) is period-accurate and deployed without apology or footnote. The theological texts cited — the real *Confessions*, the real *De Natura*, the real Demetrias letter, Cassian's real *Conferences*, Cicero's real *De Inventione* — function as cultural infrastructure the way brand names work in King: they locate characters in a specific intellectual and material universe. Pelagius's physical description — bare feet with ploughman's calloused soles, undyed wool, no silver, British-cropped hair at the jawline — is the product of deep research expressed as image. Peter's cedar box, the boy's wax tablet, the rolls from Carthage kept behind the cabinet sleeve: this is a writer who knows what a late-antique scriptorium smelled like. The Roman administrative titles (praetorian prefecture, master of the offices, grain office, vicar at Ostia) land with operational weight. There is, however, one significant absence that haunts the chapter: Alaric's Sack of Rome in 410 CE — the year before this scene — is nowhere. The *De Civitate Dei*, which Augustine mentions drafting ten books of, was written *as a direct response to that catastrophe*. For characters living in 411 CE, the Sack of Rome would have been as inescapable as 9/11 in a 2002 story. The theological stakes — whether human will can generate moral good, whether God's grace is all, whether pagan Rome fell because Christians weakened it — were supercharged by that event. Its absence makes the debate feel slightly academic when it should feel like two men arguing about a fire they can still smell. That one gap keeps this from being perfect.

### Good Examples
> 'The smell of the harbour, half a mile away, came up the road and into the courtyard on the wind — fish and pitch and salt — and went past.' — Zero anachronism, precise sensory anchor for a North African port in late antiquity. The 'and went past' is brilliant: it tells us the wind is moving, the morning is bright, and this smell belongs to the world outside the theological argument that is about to begin.

> 'His feet were bare. The soles on the courtyard stones were as calloused as the feet of a working ploughman.' — This is the whole of 5th-century British asceticism in two sentences. It characterizes Pelagius's theology through his body before he speaks a word. King would love this: doctrine shown as skin.

> 'He had read the *De Doctrina Christiana* and the *De Trinitate* and the early books of the *Enarrationes in Psalmos*. He spoke of these works as one speaks of works one has used.' — Period-specific texts deployed naturally, with that last sentence doing the cultural work: not 'admired' or 'studied' but *used*. Correct for the period's relationship to patristic writing.

> 'The letters are addressed: to the priest Sixtus at the Lateran; to the deacon Justin in the office of the praetorian prefecture; to the secretary in the grain office, a man called Cornelius; to the master of the offices' deputy in the consistory...' — Late Roman administrative machinery rendered as operational thriller detail. The grain office, the praetorian prefecture, the master of the offices — all real, all correct, none explained.

> 'He was smiling as he went.' Three words after the stutter scene. The smile is the culture: the deacon's faith that he is doing holy work, his ignorance of what he is actually carrying. The period-appropriate innocence of a young man trained in obedience lands because the cultural machinery around him is so specific.

### Needs Improvement
> 'a long letter to a Roman matron called *Demetrias*, which is also here, which is the letter that has done the most damage to date in Africa' — The Demetrias letter was written in 413-414 CE, after Demetrias took the veil. The scene is October 411. This may be deliberate compression, but it is an anachronism. In a chapter this scrupulous, it catches the eye.

> 'He had been preparing, for two years, the materials by which he intended to refute the man who was now standing in his cloister.' — This is accurate and fine, but it is also the one place where the *reason* the preparation is so urgent — the Sack of Rome the year before, the crisis of faith across the Empire — is closest to surfacing and doesn't. The sentence explains strategy but not stakes. King's version would have the Sack of Rome in the room the way a bad smell is in a room: something everyone notices and no one names.

> 'Pelagius asked what shape the remaining twelve would take. Augustine said he was working it out.' — The conversation here feels slightly contemporary in register. 'Working it out' is modern phrasing. The surrounding prose is period-tuned; this phrase briefly drops out of that frequency.

> 'The courtyard at Hippo was small, square, with a fig tree in the western corner that was bearing late...' — Visually specific, but the tactile and thermal data of the courtyard is thin. We get light and smell but not the feel of the stone bench, the temperature of the stone underfoot in October shade, the weight of the air. King would put the reader's body into the courtyard, not just their eyes.

### Suggestions
- Put the Sack of Rome in the room. Not as exposition — as pressure. One sentence from Augustine about the letters he has been receiving from Rome since the fall, one thought about pagans who say the Christian God did not protect the city. The theological argument then becomes what it actually was: two men deciding what to believe about human capacity in the ruins of civilization. The debate earns its urgency.
- The stutter on 'p' is a superb period-specific humanizing detail — but the speech-master in boyhood is the only moment the reader gets the social texture of how Roman boys of Mark's class were educated. One more such detail in the chapter — Peter's family in the south, the grammarian at Carthage that Lawrence and Augustine shared — would braid the characters into a recognizable social fabric without slowing anything down.
- The quartan fever for Lawrence is exactly right. Trust more of those medical, biological, material specifics. What does cold stone in a December crypt smell like? What does beeswax from a hundred candles do to air? The cathedral scene at Cirta comes close but steps back from full sensory commitment at precisely the moment full commitment would pay off.
- Verify the Demetrias letter timing. If the scene is strictly October 411 CE and the letter is 413-414, either compress the timeline deliberately and own it, or replace the Demetrias letter with a different Pelagian text from before 411 — his commentary on the Pauline epistles, mentioned correctly earlier in the scene, would do the work.

## Prose Polish (King Polish) (Grade: A-)

This is exceptional historical prose — disciplined, rhythmically controlled, and mostly invisible in the right way. The story takes over. You don't notice sentences; you notice Augustine's jaw tightening and Pelagius's bare calloused feet. The italicized dialogue stripped of tags is a bold choice that pays off completely, and the ending — 'He slept well' — lands like a door closing on a man. King would respect this enormously. But there are identifiable, fixable problems. The most damaging is a single-word tic: 'small' appears so often it becomes a nervous habit rather than a precision tool — 'a small moment,' 'a small degree forward,' 'a small period,' 'a small mouthful,' 'a small inch,' 'a small clean column,' 'the small pause,' 'a small lamp,' 'a small darn.' When a word recurs this frequently, the reader's eye starts snagging on it, and the prose stops being invisible. Beyond that, the administrative sections — letter-recipients, deacon Mark's provisioning instructions — run 15-20% too long; the forensic precision that works so well in the theological debate becomes a drag when applied to logistics. And two or three emotional beats are over-qualified: the author tells us something and then explains what he told us, which King identifies as the writer not trusting the reader. The 2nd draft = 1st draft minus 10% principle points squarely at: the 'small' audit, the Mark section, and a handful of emotion-qualifications that undercut moments that are already working perfectly without them.

### Good Examples
> 'He slept well.' — The entire chapter earns this two-word ending. Augustine has corrupted a young secretary, dispatched an assassin in a sealed satchel, and wept genuinely over his dead friend. 'He slept well' is Hemingway-level compression. King would circle it and write 'YES' in the margin.

> 'Augustine reached out, picked up the cup, and drank what the older man had not drunk. The cup was light in his hand. The water tasted of nothing in particular — he had expected, against reason, that it would taste of something — and he set the cup down empty.' — The parenthetical confession ('against reason') is the best sentence in the chapter. It shows us the whole of Augustine's interior life in one aside without ever saying the word 'grief' or 'reverence.'

> 'The smile was not the political smile he had worn for sixteen years in courtyards. He caught himself wearing it, and he did not stop wearing it.' — Two sentences doing the work of a paragraph. Pure King: behavior over emotion, the character surprising himself.

> 'If I let him go to Palestine and let his writings circulate with the obvious truth of his face — within ten years there will be a Pelagian Latin Church that will eat my work.' — The word 'face' is the key. Not 'arguments,' not 'theology.' Face. This is where the chapter reveals itself.

> 'The soles on the courtyard stones were as calloused as the feet of a working ploughman.' — Specific, visual, carries moral weight without editorializing. The reader does the work of understanding what those feet mean about this man.

### Needs Improvement
> 'a small moment' / 'a small degree forward' / 'a small mouthful' / 'a small inch of water' / 'a small clean column' / 'the small pause' / 'a small period' (used twice) / 'a small lamp' — 'Small' is the prose tic that breaks the invisibility. King: 'You've got a tell. It's a word you reach for when you need a modifier and don't want to commit to a real one. Audit every instance. Cut half. Make the rest earn their spot.'

> 'He flinched inside, in the part of himself that was not a bishop.' — 'He flinched inside' is the sentence. Stop there. 'In the part of himself that was not a bishop' explains what the prose has already established over ten pages. The qualification weakens what it intends to deepen. King's rule: if you have to explain the effect, you don't trust the cause.

> 'The reference was apt. Augustine took it without flinching outwardly.' — 'The reference was apt' is the author stepping in front of the scene. We can see it's apt; that's why it lands. Cut the editorial judgment and start with 'Augustine took it.' Then 'He flinched inside' does double work.

> 'He did not say it sadly. He said it in the tone of a man recording a probable fact.' — The second sentence is perfect. The first is redundant — it just pre-explains the second. 'He said it in the tone of a man recording a probable fact' tells us everything, including that he didn't say it sadly. King's 10% rule starts here.

> 'You will deliver each in person to the named recipient. You will not entrust them to household servants or to under-officials. You will wait, where waiting is convenient, for a written acknowledgment, which you will return to me. Where waiting is not convenient, an oral acknowledgment from the recipient himself will do.' — The Mark section runs twenty-five lines of provisioning and delivery-order logistics. The precision that makes the Pelagius debate devastating becomes white noise when applied to courier instructions. Cut to the essential: the sealed satchel, the route, the danger the boy doesn't know he's in. The reader is ahead of the prose here.

### Suggestions
- Run a global search for 'small' and audit every instance. Target: cut at least half. Where 'small' is doing real work ('a small bronze pectoral cross' earns it — the detail matters morally), keep it. Where it's a hedge ('a small period,' 'a small moment,' 'a small degree'), delete it or replace it with a specific word.
- Cut the Mark delivery-order section by roughly 30%. The reader doesn't need the full roster of seven recipients in order; they need to understand that Augustine is routing a death sentence through a courier who doesn't know it. One or two named recipients establishes the network. The paragraph beginning 'The framing omitted that two of the seven letters...' is the payoff — get there faster.
- Remove all instances of 'outwardly' and 'inwardly' as modifiers on emotional verbs. 'He flinched inside, in the part of himself that was not a bishop' → 'He flinched inside.' 'He took it without flinching outwardly' → 'He took it.' The interior/exterior distinction is already built into the narrative mode; the adverbs are scaffolding that should have been pulled down.
- Trust 'He did not say it sadly' to be carried by what follows, and cut it. Apply this audit to the four or five places where the chapter pre-explains a beat that the next sentence already delivers.
- In the crypt scene: 'It smelled of stone and of the wax of old candles' → 'It smelled of stone and old candles.' Minor, but every syllable. King's 10% often lives in prepositions and articles.
