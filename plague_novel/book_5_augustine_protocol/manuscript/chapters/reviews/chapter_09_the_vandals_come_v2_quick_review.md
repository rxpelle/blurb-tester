### CRITICAL (3)

**1. [Developmental Editor] continuity / factual consistency** (Section 2 (October 428 CE), Alypius's arrival paragraph and his subsequent dialogue)
- **Issue:** The narrator establishes Alypius's age as seventy-four ('Alypius was seventy-four. He was a year older than Augustine and looked it.'), then three paragraphs later Alypius gives his own age as seventy-six ('I am seventy-six and my legs are what they are'). Both cannot be true in the same scene.
- **Suggestion:** Decide on one figure and make both instances match. Historically, Alypius was born circa 354 CE, placing him at approximately 73–74 in October 428; seventy-four is the defensible number. Correct the dialogue to align with the narrator tag.
- **Before:** `Alypius was seventy-four. He was a year older than Augustine and looked it. [...] 'I am seventy-six and my legs are what they are.'`
- **After:** `Alypius was seventy-four. He was a year older than Augustine and looked it. [...] 'I am seventy-four and my legs are what they are.'`

**2. [Developmental Editor] historical anachronism / timeline error** (Section 1 header and paragraph 3)
- **Issue:** The dispatch describes Gaiseric as 'the new king, who had taken the throne when his half-brother Gunderic died in the autumn.' Historically, Gunderic died in 428 CE. A dispatch being read in June 425 cannot reference this event—Gunderic was still alive and reigning. Furthermore, the 'eight hundred vessels carrying the entire people' corresponds to the historically attested Vandal crossing of 429 CE. The June 425 date makes both descriptions impossible and will halt any historically literate reader.
- **Suggestion:** The most defensible correction is that 'June 425 CE' is a draft error for 'June 428 CE'. This resolves the Gunderic succession anachronism, aligns the crossing description with 429, and compresses the Section 1-to-Section-2 gap from 3.5 years to 4 months—far more dramatically coherent. Augustine's 'three years' estimate then misses by one year (scouts arrive June 430); a single-year miscalculation by a sick old man reading secondhand dispatches is entirely believable. The current gap also makes 'Not five' a hostage to fortune, since scouts in fact arrive exactly five years after June 425.
- **Before:** `*Hippo Regius, the second week of June 425 CE.*`
- **After:** `*Hippo Regius, the second week of June 428 CE.*`

**3. [Immersive Experience Editor] showing vs. telling / emotional resonance** (paragraph beginning 'He had known Alypius for fifty-eight years')
- **Issue:** The chapter's central grief moment — the death of Augustine's closest friend — is delivered as a biographical inventory. Eight facts of shared history are enumerated in a single block (three cities, ordination, confession, father's grave, Milan garden letter, baptism, every important hour). This is summary, not experience. The reader is informed of the loss rather than made to feel it. The cold-reportage register earns its power by selection and compression, not by listing. A list of credentials is the opposite of grief.
- **Suggestion:** Cut the enumeration entirely. Select one specific physical memory — sensory, irreversible — and let it carry everything. The bench at the third fig tree has already been introduced in the Alypius scene two sections earlier; that detail belongs here, completing the circuit. Then let the wrongness of the choosing land directly, without explanation.
- **Before:** `He had known Alypius for fifty-eight years. He had been at the same desk with him in three cities. He had been ordained with him. He had heard his confession twenty-nine times. He had said the words over his father's grave when his father had died at Tagaste in the year of the consul Tatianus. Alypius had been the first man Augustine had told, in writing, of the night in the garden at Milan; Alypius had been the second man baptised, after Augustine, at the font Ambrose had filled at Easter 387; Alypius had been beside him at every important hour of his adult life. Alypius had been killed standing on the steps of his own basilica because he had, two years before, in the writing room at Hippo, told Augustine that he was going to be at Tagaste when the time came.

The choosing had been on Alypius and not on Augustine. The choosing had been the choosing of a man of his age and his disposition.

It did not, on the morning, help.`
- **After:** `Alypius had been killed standing on the steps of his own basilica because he had, two years before, in the writing room at Hippo, told Augustine that he was going to be at Tagaste when the time came.

Augustine thought of the bench by the third fig tree. Alypius, nineteen years old, in the spring afternoon, saying: this bench is where a particular kind of life is about to be decided. Alypius had been right. The bench had been right. The steps had been right too, in their way. The choosing had been the choosing of a man who had always chosen correctly.

It did not, on the morning, help.`

### MAJOR (7)

**1. [Developmental Editor] thriller mechanics / artifact stakes** (Section 3 (May 429 CE), final scene — Augustine with the bronze box)
- **Issue:** The disc has been warm in Augustine's hand four times in forty-two years. He has stopped expecting a fifth. This is the chapter's primary artifact beat, and it ends on a deliberately flat note that is tonally interesting but dramatically inert. By Chapter 10, a thriller reader should receive some payoff or escalation on what the four warmings signified. Without it, 'warm four times' reads as mythology the reader cannot decode, and the scene registers as placeholder rather than development.
- **Suggestion:** Let Augustine briefly surface one of the four warmings—a single compressed clause is enough—which rewards close readers and raises the stakes for the box's departure in Section 5. Alternatively, if the chapter insists on withholding the warmings, make Augustine's acceptance of the cold a genuine loss rather than a philosophical conclusion: he gave something up, and the chapter should let us feel its cost.
- **Before:** `He had not warmed in his hands in some years. He had stopped expecting it to. He had stopped, in the last two or three years, attaching any particular significance to the cold. The disc was bronze. Bronze in a closed box on a high shelf in a cool room was cold.`
- **After:** `He had not warmed in his hands in some years. He had stopped expecting it to. There had been a morning at Milan — the morning before the baptism, in the garden — when it had been warm, and for thirty years he had told himself the four warmings had each been on mornings of that kind. He no longer believed this. The disc was bronze. Bronze on a high shelf in a cool room was cold. That was what bronze was.`

**2. [Developmental Editor] narrative drive / protagonist agency** (Sections 3, 4, and 5 overall)
- **Issue:** After Section 1, Augustine makes no consequential decision for the rest of the chapter. He receives news (the crossing, Alypius's death, the scouts), grieves or processes internally, and executes the plan he already made. The thesis — work continues as the world ends — is powerful, but the dramatic cost is real: Augustine is entirely reactive for roughly half the chapter's length, at precisely the point where a thriller structure should be tightening. The plan executes itself without friction.
- **Suggestion:** Give Augustine one active choice in Section 3 or 4 that modifies or complicates the existing plan — a small decision about a third party, a letter written about the box's destination beyond Carthage, a request made of Nikephoros about the sea route. It does not need to become a subplot. It only needs to demonstrate that Augustine is still acting on the world rather than waiting for it to complete its work on him.

**3. [Immersive Experience Editor] scene grounding / sensory detail** (All sections — the writing room setting)
- **Issue:** This chapter spans five years and is set almost entirely in a single room, yet the room is never physically described. We have: a desk, an upper shelf, a window, a door, a cloister audible through it. No dimensions, no temperature, no wall texture, no quality of light beyond 'the brightness of June' filtered through a half-shutter. A reader cannot inhabit a space they cannot see. The room is where Augustine will die. It deserves one grounded description that every subsequent scene can lean against.
- **Suggestion:** In the June 425 opening section, before or after the dispatch-reading, give the room one specific physical moment — the quality of morning light on the plaster, the feel of the desk surface under a palm, the smell of the parchment in the heat. It need not be long. Four sentences would serve all five years.
- **Before:** `He sat at the desk now in the middle of the morning. The window was half-shuttered against the brightness of June and was open enough to let in the harbour smell.`
- **After:** `He sat at the desk in the middle of the morning. The room was small and white and the plaster on the north wall had cracked in two places since the winter and had not been repaired. The window was half-shuttered against the brightness of June; the light that came through the gap fell across the desk in a bar that moved by a hand's width every hour and that Augustine had been watching move for thirty-one years. The half-open shutter let in the harbour smell — salt brine, pitch, the sweetness of dried fish curing somewhere on the quay.`

**4. [Immersive Experience Editor] visceral moment / sensory detail** (Multiple sections — the cough)
- **Issue:** The cough is one of the chapter's structural devices — it marks the passage of disease across five years, and 'the linen took its share' is a fine compression. But the physical experience of slowly dying of a lung condition is never inhabited. We are told the cough happens and grows worse; we are not inside it. In the May 429 section it is described only as 'took longer than the coughs in June 425 had taken.' The body is absent from its own dying.
- **Suggestion:** In one instance — the cough after the Alypius news, where Augustine sits alone with the linen — let the body be specific. Not graphic, but real: the effort of the chest, the taste, the count before it releases him. That one inhabited moment makes every subsequent 'the linen took its share' carry physical weight.
- **Before:** `He picked up the linen square and coughed into it for some time. The linen took its share.`
- **After:** `He picked up the linen square. The cough, when it came, came from the bottom of the left lung — he had learned in the last year to tell which lung — and it went on for a count of twenty and did not finish clean. He tasted copper at the back of the throat. He set the linen down without looking at it.`

**5. [Immersive Experience Editor] showing vs. telling** (Final section (June 430) — 'He held it the way a man holds a small specific weight')
- **Issue:** The image of Possidius holding the box against his chest is already doing its work. The sentence that follows — 'He held it the way a man holds a small specific weight he has decided to carry for the rest of his life' — is the author stepping forward to explain what the image means. This is the chapter's most fully rendered visual moment, and the gloss diminishes it by translating it into abstraction before the reader has had a second to receive it.
- **Suggestion:** Cut the explanatory sentence entirely. Trust the image.
- **Before:** `He picked up the box. The box was small. The box was the size of a man's two cupped hands. The hinges did not give their sound, because Possidius did not open the lid. He did not need to. He held the box. He looked at Augustine.

*Aurelius. I will not be at the gate when you die.*`
- **After:** `He picked up the box. The box was small. The box was the size of a man's two cupped hands. The hinges did not give their sound, because Possidius did not open the lid. He did not need to. He held it against his chest. He looked at Augustine.

*Aurelius. I will not be at the gate when you die.*`

**6. [Accessibility Editor] Concept Accessibility — Theological Stakes** (Paragraph 7 ('The basilica will be re-consecrated by an Arian bishop') and Possidius farewell scene)
- **Issue:** The Arian/Catholic distinction is the engine driving the entire threat — forced conversion, burned basilicas, the impossibility of Catholic clergy surviving — but 'Arian' is never explained. A 10th-grade reader will follow that something religiously bad is happening, but won't grasp why an Arian bishop replacing a Catholic bishop is a theological erasure rather than merely a political inconvenience. The persecution's specific logic (Vandals believe Christ was made, not eternal; the councils have ruled that wrong; the two churches cannot coexist) disappears into a proper noun.
- **Suggestion:** One sentence of dialogue can carry this without disrupting the spare style. Augustine is already explaining the likely fates of the clergy. He can add the theological ground in the same register he uses for everything else.
- **Before:** `The basilica will be re-consecrated by an Arian bishop. The Catholic clergy who are still in Hippo when the city falls will be killed or they will be exiled or they will be forced into the Arian rite.`
- **After:** `The basilica will be re-consecrated by an Arian bishop. The Vandals pray to a Christ they believe was made by God rather than being God — a distinction the councils settled seventy years ago and which the Vandals have not settled to our satisfaction. The Catholic clergy who are still in Hippo when the city falls will be killed or they will be exiled or they will be forced into the Arian rite.`

**7. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation — Geography** (Paragraphs 3–5 (the three dispatches) and throughout all four time sections)
- **Issue:** The chapter deploys roughly twenty distinct place names (Tingis, Mauretania Tingitana, Volubilis, Mauretania Caesariensis, Iol Caesarea, Caesarea Mauretaniae, Calama, Numidia, Madauros, Bagai, Tarifa, the Pillars, Tagaste, Carthage, Hippo). For a reader without a mental map of 5th-century North Africa, these accumulate into an undifferentiated blur. The chapter's central urgency — Vandals moving along the coast toward Hippo — is emotionally clear, but the specific logic of 'scouts east of Madauros means the army reaches Hippo in two weeks' requires spatial knowledge the general reader lacks. The three-year estimate in the 425 section, and the tightening timeline across the chapter, would hit harder with a single orienting anchor.
- **Suggestion:** One sentence establishing Hippo's position in the geography — placed at the first dispatch section — would do it. The actual history supports something clean and thriller-appropriate.
- **Before:** `Tingis had fallen in March. [Geographic accumulation follows with no anchor]`
- **After:** `Tingis had fallen in March. Hippo was the last port of consequence before Carthage; everything between Tingis and Hippo was what stood between the Vandal army and the grain supply of the western empire. [then the dispatches proceed]`

### MINOR (7)

**1. [Developmental Editor] prose tic / structural repetition** (Section 1 (Augustine standing); Section 2 (Alypius sitting, then Alypius rising))
- **Issue:** Rising or sitting 'in two stages' is used three times in the chapter. The first use (Augustine) establishes the motif cleanly. The second (Alypius sitting down on arrival) deepens it — two old men, the same failing bodies. The third (Alypius rising to leave) is mechanical; the image has done its work and the third application reads as formula.
- **Suggestion:** Keep the first two instances. For Alypius's exit, substitute a different physical observation — the staff's sound on stone, a pause before he takes the weight — so the detail reads as variation rather than repetition.
- **Before:** `He rose. The rising was in two stages. He took the staff. He looked at Augustine across the desk.`
- **After:** `He took the staff and came upright with it. He looked at Augustine across the desk.`

**2. [Developmental Editor] character detail / unresolved signal** (Section 1, approximately paragraph 10)
- **Issue:** 'He had taken to sitting that way in the last year. Augustine had been watching it and had not spoken of it.' This clause gives the 'hands flat on knees' detail the weight of a deliberate, loaded observation — Augustine has noticed something and chosen silence. But the chapter never returns to it. When Possidius finally takes the box in Section 5 and leaves, his hands are not mentioned. The suppressed observation goes unpaid.
- **Suggestion:** Either pay it off in Section 5 — when Possidius picks up the box, his hands are no longer flat and still; he is holding something, and Augustine sees it — or strip 'Augustine had been watching it and had not spoken of it' from Section 1, so the detail reads as neutral physical description rather than a marked and withheld signal.

**3. [Developmental Editor] pacing / prose economy** (Section 2 (October 428 CE), second wine paragraph)
- **Issue:** The first wine passage earns its place: Augustine has saved six amphorae for visits of 'particular weight' and judges Alypius's farewell to be among them. The second return to the wine — 'The wine was good. The wine had been good when Augustine had bought the amphorae in the spring. The wine had not gone off in the cellar through the summer; the cellar at Hippo was a good cellar.' — lingers past its purpose. The cellar quality and the wine's preservation deflect from the emotional moment rather than deepening it.
- **Suggestion:** Cut to one sentence and trust the established weight of the scene.
- **Before:** `Alypius nodded. He took another drink of the Gallic wine. The wine was good. The wine had been good when Augustine had bought the amphorae in the spring. The wine had not gone off in the cellar through the summer; the cellar at Hippo was a good cellar.`
- **After:** `Alypius nodded. He took another drink of the Gallic wine. The wine was good.`

**4. [Immersive Experience Editor] sensory detail** (Paragraphs 2 and 8 of the June 425 section; also the Alypius scene)
- **Issue:** 'The harbour smell' is the chapter's primary recurring sensory anchor, used three times as a marker of the living world outside Augustine's window. But the smell is never specified. 'Harbour smell' is a category, not a sensation. A late Roman North African working port in June — pitch, salt brine, dried fish, possibly penned animals, the particular rot of low tide — has a specific smell. The anchor is doing structural work, but it lands as a placeholder rather than as sensation.
- **Suggestion:** On first use, give the smell one specific constituent. Let subsequent uses of 'harbour smell' carry that specificity by association.
- **Before:** `The window was half-shuttered against the brightness of June and was open enough to let in the harbour smell.`
- **After:** `The window was half-shuttered against the brightness of June and was open enough to let in the harbour smell — salt and pitch and the low-tide mud that came up from the quay when the wind was off the water.`

**5. [Immersive Experience Editor] interior life / emotional resonance** (May 429 section — the bronze box passage)
- **Issue:** The disc passage earns its power from the contrast between past warmth and current cold rationalization ('Bronze in a closed box on a high shelf in a cool room was cold. That was what bronze was'). But the rationalization depends on suppressed memory — four moments of warmth over forty-two years — and none of those moments is even fleetingly felt. Without any sense of what the warmth was like, the suppression reads as thought rather than as resistance. The passage is thematically correct but emotionally thin.
- **Suggestion:** A single half-sentence — not describing the supernatural event itself, but a physical detail from one of the four moments (a city, a temperature, a year, a physical context) — would give the rationalization something to push against.
- **Before:** `He had carried the disc for forty-two years and it had been warm in his hand four times. He did not expect a fifth.

He closed the box.`
- **After:** `He had carried the disc for forty-two years and it had been warm in his hand four times — the last time in the garden at Cassiciacum, the winter before the baptism, in his palm in the dark. He did not expect a fifth. He had stopped expecting it the year he turned sixty. The disc was bronze. Bronze in a closed box on a high shelf in a cool room was cold. That was what bronze was.

He closed the box.`

**6. [Accessibility Editor] Jargon Management — Unexplained Text Reference** (Alypius farewell scene, 'Do you remember the Hortensius?')
- **Issue:** The Hortensius is the emotional pivot of the entire Alypius scene — it's the book by the bench by the third fig tree, the text that first put the question that Milan answered. The scene asks the reader to feel the weight of a fifty-eight-year friendship condensed into a shared memory of reading one book at nineteen. But the Hortensius is Cicero's lost philosophical dialogue arguing for the life of wisdom over politics and pleasure — the book that (historically) broke Augustine away from worldly ambition. Without that context, the bench and the fig tree are affecting but abstract. The reader doesn't know what was at stake in that reading.
- **Suggestion:** A single appositive, in Augustine's own voice, would ground it without breaking the dialogue's rhythm.
- **Before:** `Do you remember the Hortensius?`
- **After:** `Do you remember the Hortensius — Cicero's argument that only the philosophical life was worth living?`

**7. [Accessibility Editor] Clarity — Ambiguous Reference** (Final Possidius scene, 'I have given you the chain. The chain is your chain now.')
- **Issue:** 'The chain' appears without antecedent in a scene where only the bronze box has been physically exchanged. It's unclear whether this is a metaphor for the chain of custody (succession of keepers), a literal chain associated with the disc that has not been mentioned in this chapter, or a different object entirely. For a reader of the full series, this may parse immediately. For a reader who has gaps in the earlier chapters, or is returning to the novel after time away, the sudden 'chain' reads as a dropped thread.
- **Suggestion:** Either specify what the chain is or replace with language consistent with what has just been handed over.
- **Before:** `I have given you the chain. The chain is your chain now. The man you give it to in your turn is your choice.`
- **After:** `I have given you the keeping of it. The keeping is yours now. The man you pass it to in your turn is your choice.`

### SUGGESTION (2)

**1. [Developmental Editor] artifact lineage / subplot clarity** (Section 2 (October 428 CE), Alypius's final speech)
- **Issue:** Alypius mentions 'the boy at the harbour who took the bronze cross from the boy at Ostia eighteen years ago.' In a series tracking a physical artifact (the bronze disc in the box), the phrase 'bronze cross' — with its material adjacency to the box and the weight Alypius and Augustine give it — risks sending thriller readers down the wrong interpretive lane. Augustine's emotional response (he hadn't asked in eighteen years, hadn't known the boy was alive) is one of the chapter's quiet triumphs, but the ambiguity about what the 'bronze cross' is clouds it.
- **Suggestion:** If the cross is a separate object — a relic, a pastoral gift, unrelated to the disc — add a single anchoring phrase to distinguish it: 'the small processional cross Augustine had given the boy in the year of the sack.' If it is part of the artifact chain, the fact that Augustine never tracked it raises structural questions about the series' stakes that the chapter doesn't address. Disambiguation here protects both the emotional beat and the thriller architecture.

**2. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation — Unnamed Historical Figure** (Alypius farewell scene, 'We had it from Romanianus's library')
- **Issue:** Romanianus is dropped into a single line without identification. In Augustine's biography, Romanianus was the wealthy patron from Tagaste who paid for Augustine's schooling in Carthage — his presence in a memory about books and formative intellectual decisions is specific and resonant. Without that context, he's just a name attached to a library. The line could do more work.
- **Suggestion:** Three words would close the gap.
- **Before:** `We had it from Romanianus's library in the spring of the year I was nineteen.`
- **After:** `We had it from Romanianus's library — he was paying for our education that year — in the spring of the year I was nineteen.`
