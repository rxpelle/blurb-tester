### CRITICAL (2)

**1. [Immersive Experience Editor] sensory grounding / central mystery object** (All three disc appearances: paragraph 1 (January section, after Mark exits), paragraph 2 (October section, final scene))
- **Issue:** The disc is the chapter's central mystery object — held three times across two years, described only as 'cold.' It has no weight, no size, no surface, no visual character. A reader cannot feel what Augustine feels when he holds it, which means the emotional freight of 'He would be the carrier for whatever years remained. There was no one else' cannot land in the body. Mystery and sensory void are not the same thing. We can know the disc's physical presence without knowing what it is.
- **Suggestion:** Give the disc one stable physical anchor per appearance. Its size, its surface texture, its specific heft. The coldness is already doing work — add one more quality that stays consistent across the three scenes, so that the reader's hand knows this object by the third holding.
- **Before:** `He took it out.

The disc was cold.

He held it for a count.`
- **After:** `He took it out. It was the size of a man's palm, edged, heavier than bronze had any reason to be. He had never found an explanation for the weight.

The disc was cold.

He held it for a count.`

**2. [Accessibility Editor] reader orientation / MacGuffin anchoring** (paragraph beginning 'He went to the upper shelf')
- **Issue:** The bronze box and disc appear without any grounding for what it is, why he checks it, or what 'cold' signifies. It appears three times across the chapter (January 412, October 413, the final scene) as a clearly significant object, yet a reader encounters it as pure opacity. Even if the disc carries meaning from prior books in the series, a chapter spanning two years of story time needs a single re-anchoring thought — one interior line that tells us what Augustine believes the disc to be, or what warmth would mean. Without that, the three check-ins read as mystical gesture rather than thriller stakes.
- **Suggestion:** Give Augustine one brief interior clause — not a lecture, just a thought — that tells us what the object is supposed to do or register. If it is a relic of uncertain provenance, a detection device, a signaling object: name the function. The emotional weight of 'cold again' depends entirely on the reader knowing what 'warm' would have meant.
- **Before:** `He took it out. The disc was cold. He held it for a count.`
- **After:** `He took it out. The disc was cold — the same cold it had been every time he had held it in the seven years since it had come to him, the same cold that meant whatever it was meant to signal had not yet happened. He held it for a count.`

**9. [Accessibility Editor] concept accessibility / covert operation without frame** (Wax tablet passage: 'The matter of the desert has, on Justin's note in the autumn, completed without further complication. The man is in the cell at Sketis where he will finish.')

The lone explanatory sentence — 'a man placed there was, for every practical purpose of the world he had been removed from, gone' — tells us the effect but not the weight. **10. [Accessibility Editor] jargon management / institutional term without gloss** (Marcellinus arrest section: 'He committed Hippo to writing to the proconsul and to the consistory')

General readers will not know what the consistory is — it is not a common term. 'The proconsul' is more familiar by association, and the chapter's handling of the Donatist council and the count of Africa's authority structure is good. But 'consistory' is dropped in without gloss and carries significant weight in the passage (it is the highest court of appeal being invoked).
- **Suggestion:** One brief appositive on first use — 'the consistory, the emperor's inner council at Ravenna' — is all that is needed. It can be phrased as Augustine's own thought.
- **Before:** `He committed Hippo to writing to the proconsul and to the consistory in support of the senior bishop's communications.`
- **After:** `He committed Hippo to writing to the proconsul and to the consistory — the emperor's court at Ravenna, the only body whose authority sat above the count of Africa's — in support of the senior bishop's communications.`

### SUGGESTION (4)

**1. [Developmental Editor] Chapter Hook** (Paragraph 1)
- **Issue:** The opening paragraph is entirely logistical: ship route, ports of call, timing, gear inventory. The one charged detail — the satchel still in the same position under the tunic as when Mark left — is buried at the end of a long sentence. That image is the hook. It should lead.
- **Suggestion:** Invert the paragraph. Open with the satchel detail, which signals immediately that something was at stake in Mark's journey and that it has come back intact. Fold the logistical information into subordinate position.
- **Before:** `Mark came back on the Wednesday on a coastal ship out of Ostia that had touched at Carthage on the Sunday and had come down the harbour road in two days. He arrived at the basilica gate at the second hour of the afternoon, on foot, with the leather satchel under his outer tunic where it had been when he had left...`
- **After:** `The leather satchel was still under his outer tunic, in the position it had been when he had left in October. Mark came to the basilica gate at the second hour on a Wednesday, on foot, off a coastal ship out of Ostia. He was thinner than he had been. He was also smiling.`

**2. [Developmental Editor] Character** (Section 2, Melania's scene in the chapel)
- **Issue:** Melania functions primarily as a project manager in this scene — she tracks what is finished, what remains, who is where. Her competence is well established and appropriate. But she has one moment that opens something more private ('Thank you for coming. He had hoped you would') and nothing else that makes her interior visible. Given that she likely appears across multiple books in the series, this scene is an early opportunity to build a reader relationship with her that is currently being declined.
- **Suggestion:** Add one physical detail or gesture that reveals something beyond administrative precision — not grief performed, but something small and involuntary. The line about her aging at the jaw is good foundation; one more beat built on it would lift her from function to person.
- **Before:** `She was examining the work of the masons and the carpenters with the careful eye of a woman who had spent her family's wealth on every stone and was making sure the stones were what she had paid for.`
- **After:** `She was examining the work of the masons and the carpenters with the careful eye of a woman who had spent her family's wealth on every stone and was making sure the stones were what she had paid for. One hand rested flat against the altar stone. She had not moved it when she turned.`

**3. [Immersive Experience Editor] interior life / embodied grief** (October 413 section — 'He sat with both papers in front of him for a long count.')
- **Issue:** Augustine sits with a living man's letter and a dispatch announcing that man's death. This is the chapter's most devastating juxtaposition. One physical sensation here — the specific quality of what it is to hold or see a dead man's handwriting — would be devastating. The scene earns it. 'He made it steady' in the next sentence is the chapter's best line precisely because it is physical. One sentence before it, inside the sitting, could make the steadying cost something felt.
- **Suggestion:** One concrete sensory detail about the papers themselves, or about the quality of silence in the room, before the pen goes up.
- **Before:** `He sat with both papers in front of him for a long count. Then he took up the iron pen. The hand that held it was steady. He made it steady.`
- **After:** `He sat with both papers in front of him. Marcellinus's letter had been folded and unfolded; the crease at the center had softened to cloth. The dispatch from Carthage lay flat and new beside it. He took up the iron pen. The hand that held it was steady. He made it steady.`

**4. [Accessibility Editor] concept accessibility / philosophical letter** (Marcellinus letter passage: 'the difference between a virtue the state pretends to and a virtue an individual performs under the state's pretense')
- **Issue:** This is the intellectual core of the City of God project and the best candidate for the 'smart friend' treatment. The distinction Marcellinus draws — civic virtue performed sincerely by an individual inside a corrupt state — is genuinely interesting and is the kind of idea that could grip a general reader. As written, it is presented as a summary of a summary, which keeps it at arm's length. The passage immediately following (Augustine's grief at the execution) would land harder if the reader had been briefly excited by the idea that Marcellinus had just been right about something important.
- **Suggestion:** Let Augustine spend one brief sentence unpacking the distinction in his own words before the arrest news comes. Not a lecture — just the recognition that Marcellinus had seen something he had not yet seen.
- **Before:** `The third paragraph began: 'On the question of the Roman virtue you address in the third book — I have been thinking about the difference between a virtue the state pretends to and a virtue an individual performs under the state's pretense, and whether your argument applies equally to both.'`
- **After:** `The third paragraph began: 'On the question of the Roman virtue you address in the third book — I have been thinking about the difference between a virtue the state pretends to and a virtue an individual performs under the state's pretense, and whether your argument applies equally to both.' It was the question Augustine had been circling without naming: could a good man's good act be good if the empire that witnessed it was rotten? Marcellinus had found the exact seam.`
