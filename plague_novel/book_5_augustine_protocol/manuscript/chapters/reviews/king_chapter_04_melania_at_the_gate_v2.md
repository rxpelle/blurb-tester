## Voice & Narrative Persona (Grade: B+)

This is a highly skilled, consistent, controlled voice — spare, precise, intelligent — but it operates almost entirely as an observing camera rather than a speaking person. King's narrators have heat. They lean forward. They have opinions they can't quite keep to themselves. This narrator has opinions but buries them under beautiful restraint. The result is a voice that earns respect but rarely earns intimacy. When it does step forward — 'The bishop had given Rome away,' or 'Eraclius's silence... was the kind of silence that gathered weight for years' — it's electric. Those moments prove the narrator has personality and wit. But they're rationed so carefully that the reader can go entire pages without feeling a human being at the wheel. The long debate sequence is the clearest evidence of the problem: the narrator vanishes into stage direction. King would ask: what does the storyteller think of Volusianus? Of Augustine? The closest we get is the small laugh from the crowd and the deacon smiling into his hand — narrator personality routed through background figures rather than spoken directly. The voice is also, at moments, seduced by its own rhythmic sophistication — the opening color-loop feels like a writer admiring a technique — which is exactly the 'literary affectation' King warns against in On Writing. But the voice is never fake, never verbose, never anachronistic, and at its best achieves a dry authority that suits Augustine perfectly. The bones of a great narrator are here. He just needs to come out from behind the camera.

### Good Examples
> 'Augustine had pointed out that he was fifty-seven and not yet dead. Eraclius had not answered.' — Dry, earned personality. The narrator doesn't editorialize; it doesn't need to. The deadpan gap between those two sentences does all the work. This is King-level compression.

> 'Eraclius's silence, on the matter of the bishop's age and the bishop's hours at the desk, was the kind of silence that gathered weight for years and was eventually expressed in a small written note left on the corner of the desk for the bishop to find.' — The narrator steps all the way forward here. This is a storyteller who has a complete interior model of these people and can't resist sharing it. More of this.

> 'The bishop had given Rome away.' — Four words, enormous editorial confidence. The narrator takes a side, states it baldly, and moves on. This is what the whole chapter needs more of.

> 'His face was the face of a man who had been telling other men what to do for forty years and had not yet, on the evidence of his face, been wrong.' — The phrase 'on the evidence of his face' is the narrator inserting its own forensic intelligence into the description. Personality through precision.

> 'She had chosen the cloak and chosen the hour. She was a woman of careful calculation.' — The narrator draws a conclusion from evidence, out loud, in real time. Exactly right.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The sky was the colour of the sea on a winter morning. The sea, which he could see in a strip between the tile of the basilica roof and the line of the fig trees in the cloister, was the colour of the sky. The two had been the same colour for nine days.' — This is a technique on display. It's beautiful on first read and feels like literary decoration on second. A storyteller who talks to you doesn't open by showing off a color-loop trick; a writer auditioning for a prize does. King calls this 'the closed fist of the show-off.' The information — grey, overcast, nine days of it — could be given in one direct sentence.

> 'He sat with what she had said. He sat with it for a long count.' — The repetition is meant to feel deliberate and weighted, and it does — but it crosses from rhythm into affectation. A real person telling this story would say it once. Saying it twice signals: look how much this moment matters. King's rule: trust the scene. You don't need the second sentence.

> 'in a way that he had felt twice in fourteen months and not before in his life, the bronze disc warm against the parchment in the upper shelf of the writing room three corridors away' — This sentence is tangled. The narrator is trying to do too many things at once: quantify the number of times, establish the novelty, and deliver the sensory fact. Read it aloud and you lose the thread before the disc appears. The narrator has knotted itself trying to be precise. The disc being warm is the sentence. Everything else is scaffolding.

> The entire middle section of the debate — from 'Bishop. The eternal city of Rome stood unconquered...' through 'It will not feed the refugees in the warehouses by the harbour' — is essentially a two-person script. The narrator disappears for roughly a thousand words. King would not accept this. The narrator should be present inside the debate: noting the crowd's body language, Augustine's internal timing, the precise moment Volusianus loses the exchange. The debate is the chapter's climax and the narrator watches it from outside the room.

### Suggestions
- Give the narrator one moment per major scene where it speaks directly about what it thinks is happening — not what the characters think, not what the crowd thinks, but what the storyteller making sense of all this thinks. 'The senator had come to Hippo expecting the bishop to defend Rome. The bishop had not defended Rome. The bishop had given Rome away.' That sentence exists and is excellent. Find three more like it.
- During the debate, restore the narrator inside Augustine's perspective. Not his thoughts — his physical experience of the exchange. The timing of his pauses. What he hears in Volusianus's voice when the hand clenches. The narrator can observe from inside without becoming interior monologue.
- Cut or flatten the opening color-loop. One sentence: 'Nine days of the same grey sky and the harbour road still mud from the October rains.' Direct, specific, moves us forward. Save the rhetorical looping for moments of genuine emotional recursion — like the final paragraphs, where it earns its place.
- The repetition device ('He sat with what she had said. He sat with it for a long count') works when it mimics a character's stunned circling — the bronze disc ending uses it well. But it has to be reserved or it goes stale. Audit every instance and keep only the ones where the repetition carries weight the first use cannot.
- Let the narrator have a small, dry opinion about Volusianus from the start — before the debate. The description of the toga being immaculate because he'd had it laundered is excellent. Follow it with one sentence of narrator verdict, the way you did with Eraclius: something that tells us the storyteller has already made up its mind about this man and is watching to see if it was right.

## Deep POV & Interior Monologue (Grade: C+)

This chapter is doing something deliberate and it's doing it well — but 'deliberate' doesn't excuse the cost. The prose maintains a steady middle psychic distance throughout, which produces a controlled, lapidary elegance and works beautifully for the debate scene and the political choreography. But we almost never get behind Augustine's eyes. We observe him. We are told what he understands, what he notices, what he can see. The filtering is constant. The one section where deep POV ignites — the bronze disc sequence at the end — proves the rule: when the author finally drops into Augustine's nervous system, the whole chapter lifts. The tragedy is that this happens only once, and only after the scene's drama has already passed. Augustine is a man holding a secret object that's warming in a way it's never warmed before, meeting a stranger who names him by name across a generation, feeling the weight of an old woman's dying instruction land in his chest — and we get almost none of his interior experience of any of it. We get his face, which doesn't change. We get his held looks, which he counts. We get 'he understood that.' King's rule applies here: the reader cannot feel what the character does not feel out loud. The debate scene crackles because the dialogue carries it, but even there, Augustine's interiority is blank. We never know what he fears, what surprises him, what it costs him to give Rome away in front of a crowd. The chapter is elegant and cool and kept at arm's length from its own emotional center.

### Good Examples
> "He had felt it warm against the parchment on the upper shelf the way a man feels a fire he is not looking at. He had not gone to it." — This is the chapter's best POV sentence. We're inside the body, not watching from outside. The sensation is rendered first, the action second. This is how deep POV works.

> "He stood in it for the count of three before he remembered which way the cell was, and then he remembered, and he went." — No filtering word. No 'he noticed he was confused.' The cognition itself is disrupted on the page, which signals his interior disturbance through form rather than report.

### Needs Improvement
> "He understood, while he held it, that the thing she had said about her grandmother and the priest in Bethlehem and the work moving south through Africa was the most important sentence anyone had spoken to him since the night of the courier. He did not let his face change." — 'He understood that' is a wall between us and the character. We're told the sentence is important, but we never feel it land. The second sentence compounds the problem: we're told he's suppressing a reaction without being allowed inside the suppression. What does it feel like to hear your name spoken by a dying woman you've never met? We get nothing.

> "Augustine could see, not as good a debater as he had been in his thirties, and the long preparation for this exchange had not been, on the senator's side, a preparation that included the answer he was now hearing." — 'Augustine could see' is a filtering phrase that explicitly places the narrator between us and the character's perception. Cut 'Augustine could see' and you're inside his read of the room. With it, you're watching Augustine watch.

### Suggestions
- Eliminate all instances of 'he understood that,' 'Augustine could see,' 'he felt,' 'he noticed,' and 'he watched.' Every one of these is a locked door. Replace them by rendering the perception or thought directly. Instead of 'He understood that the sentence was the most important he'd heard,' give us the sentence landing: the word 'named' sticking in the chest, the specific thing that shifts.
- The moment Melania says her grandmother named him is the chapter's emotional apex — and it's handled entirely from the outside: 'He held her look.' Give us one sentence of what it's like inside Augustine's body when a stranger tells him a dead woman crossed a continent to say his name. His throat. His hands on the table. Something physical that isn't a managed expression.
- During the debate, Augustine is jousting with a formidable man in front of a crowd on the argument he's been writing for fourteen months. We never get any anticipation, any flash of pleasure when a thrust lands, any moment of doubt. One or two beats of his internal experience of the argument — not summarized, but lived — would make the debate feel dangerous instead of pre-decided.
- The bronze disc section works precisely because it abandons the filtered narrator. Take that same approach into the parlour scene with Melania. Let Augustine's senses carry the psychic weight: what he notices about the cup she doesn't drink from, the specific quality of her steadiness, the warmth of the disc he can feel through the corridor wall while she's still talking. The supernatural intrudes on the mundane through the body, not through narrated significance.

## Backstory & Characterization (Grade: A-)

This chapter earns most of its characterization the right way — through action, physical detail, and behavior under pressure rather than through authorial summary. Augustine is built from habits: the tenth revision, the wax tablet system, the bread-and-water lunch at the desk, nine years of Eraclius's notes. We know who he is before we hear him speak. Volusianus is almost entirely characterized through physical and behavioral tells — the laundered toga, the face that does not change, the hand that closes and opens. Those three details tell us everything. Melania's entrance is a genuine King-style character reveal: 'Her face was not the face of a woman who had been weeping. Her face was the face of a woman who had been listening, all afternoon, to people weeping, and was now done.' That single observation does more characterization work than a page of biography. The five conditions Augustine sets are pure character-through-action — we understand his intelligence, his caution, his theology, and his institutional paranoia entirely through the conditions he names and in what order he names them. The chapter's main weakness is Melania's long speech about her grandmother's teachings, which crosses from character-revealing dialogue into functional backstory delivery. The speech runs five paragraphs and tells us what we need to know, but it tells rather than shows. Augustine's backstory — fourteen months, the courier, the work — is handled better because it comes through texture and habit rather than speech. The ending is close to perfect: standing in the rain, forgetting which direction the cell is. That image earns more emotional weight than any summary of his exhaustion could.

### Good Examples
> 'Augustine had been finding such notes from Eraclius for nine years.' — Nine years of a relationship and an entire personality compressed into one action-based sentence. No character description needed.

> 'The toga was immaculate; he had had it laundered before the service.' — One specific detail tells us Volusianus is theatrical, vain, and calculating. King's specific-detail principle at full power.

> 'Her face was not the face of a woman who had been weeping. Her face was the face of a woman who had been listening, all afternoon, to people weeping, and was now done.' — Melania's entire character delivered in two sentences through what her face reveals about what she has been doing.

> 'Drink the water.' / She drank the water. — The bishop's authority and Melania's decision to yield to it established in five words and four. No description of the power dynamic needed.

> 'He had given Rome away.' — Augustine's entire intellectual move, and the reveal that Volusianus came prepared for the wrong opponent, shown through the senator's perception. Zero exposition.

> The hand that closes and opens — Volusianus loses control in the smallest possible place. The face that does not change three times in a row, then the hand. Perfect ordering.

> 'He stood in it for the count of three before he remembered which way the cell was.' — Exhaustion and the weight of the evening carried entirely through a man forgetting something he has known for years. No authorial summary needed.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He had aged in the fourteen months since the night of the courier in the way men of his age age when they have been the hands of a thing they had not asked to be the hands of for the second time in their lives.' — Stephen (the assistant) is characterized by how he aged, which is good instinct, but the sentence collapses under its own weight. 'The hands of a thing they had not asked to be the hands of for the second time' — this recursion loses the reader. The aging is a good idea; the sentence needs to be cut in half.

> Melania's five-paragraph speech about her grandmother's teachings ('Bishop, my grandmother taught me before she died...') runs as backstory delivery in dialogue form. It is the one extended passage in the chapter that tells rather than shows. We are given the grandmother's philosophy in the grandmother's paraphrased words rather than seeing it refracted through something Melania does. By the fifth paragraph of reported grandmother-speech, we are being informed rather than revealed to.

> 'The educated men of Hippo — three of the magistrates, two retired professors of rhetoric, the deacon of the synagogue who came to the cathedral on the days when the bishop would preach on a matter of philosophy' — this is a light case of roster-listing. The magistrates don't need to be three; the professors don't need to be two. The deacon earns his specificity because he returns as a presence. The others are set-dressing enumerated with false precision.

> 'She had not, in the giving, lost the steadiness of someone who knew what the wealth was for.' — This is the author telling us about Melania's inner quality directly. The sentence is doing work the scene has already done through her behavior. Cut it and the reader has already arrived at the same conclusion.

### Suggestions
- Cut Melania's grandmother-speech by half. Give us one or two of the grandmother's principles in paraphrase, then let Melania demonstrate the rest through her behavior in the negotiation that follows. She names five agreed conditions without blinking and corrects Augustine on her age — that IS the grandmother's teaching made visible. Trust it.
- Rewrite the Stephen sentence. Try: 'He had aged in the fourteen months the way men age when they have been made the instrument of something they did not choose.' The aging-through-instrumentality is the right idea; the sentence needs to breathe.
- The magistrates and professors in the basilica roster don't need numbers. 'Three magistrates, two retired professors' tells us nothing the word 'educated men' doesn't. The deacon keeps his specificity because he acts. Cut the others to type only.
- Consider giving Volusianus one specific physical action during his defeat — not the hand, which you've used — something he does with his body when Augustine says 'the bishop has given Rome away.' The hand closes and opens is excellent; add one more micro-behavior during the killing blow to make the loss land in his body, not just his posture.
- The bronze disc section near the end is strong, but 'in the way that the third warmth of a thing carried for thirty years would be a different warmth' is reaching for meaning the sentence can't quite hold. The warmth is doing its work without the explanation of why this warmth is different. Trust the repetition — warm, warm, different warmth — and cut the gloss.

## Setting & Sensory Detail (Grade: B+)

This chapter contains some of the best 'telling detail' work in the manuscript alongside some significant sensory blind spots that King would not tolerate. When the author lands a physical detail, it does real work — the sky and sea exchanging colors, the warmth of the bronze disc, Volusianus's hand opening and closing. These are master-class touches: one detail, multiple duties. But the chapter is almost entirely anosmic and largely deaf. We are in a fifth-century North African harbor city — a place that would assault the senses with fish, incense, rain-soaked wool, tallow smoke, stone cold, harbor wind, the acoustics of a crowded basilica — and almost none of it reaches the page. The three major settings (writing room, basilica, small parlour) function as backdrops rather than lived-in spaces. King's rule: the reader should be able to close their eyes and be IN the room. Right now they can sketch the room but they cannot smell it, and they cannot hear it. The author is strongest in visual and tactile registers and virtually absent from smell and sound — a lopsided sensory diet that keeps the chapter from achieving full immersion despite its other considerable strengths.

### Good Examples
> 'The sky was the colour of the sea on a winter morning. The sea... was the colour of the sky. The two had been the same colour for nine days.' — Triple duty: establishes November weather, creates a mood of collapse and blurring, and tells us Augustine has been watching this sameness long enough to count the days. That last clause ('nine days') is the King move — the specific number makes it real.

> 'a plain wool cloak the colour of the rain' — Melania's cloak described against the atmosphere itself. It signals her intentional anonymity (wrong garment for her class), connects her to the chapter's governing weather, and does it in eight words.

> 'He had felt it warm against the parchment on the upper shelf the way a man feels a fire he is not looking at.' — The bronze disc's warmth is conveyed through peripheral heat rather than direct touch. This is the right move: the supernatural rendered in a completely physical, mundane register. It makes the reader feel it.

> 'Volusianus's face did not change, but his hand at his side closed and opened.' — One involuntary physical tic. The hand tells us the debate has turned before the senator's face admits it. This is King's principle of the body betraying what the face conceals.

> 'The autumn rain had begun again at the cathedral door. He stood in it for the count of three before he remembered which way the cell was, and then he remembered, and he went.' — The best ending in the chapter. The physical detail (standing in rain), the momentary loss of orientation, the recovery — all of it externalizes Augustine's interior state without explaining it. King ends chapters this way.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The basilica was full... Word that Volusianus would attend had moved through the city by the river of the wine-shops and the harbour warehouses since first light.' — The basilica during the central debate scene has no physical life. A crowded fifth-century North African basilica in November would be loud with shuffling feet on stone, warm with body heat against cold walls, heavy with incense or its absence, acoustically alive in ways that would shape how voices sound. None of this is on the page. We hear about the crowd reacting but we don't hear or feel the crowd.

> 'Come into the small parlour. Eraclius will bring water.' — The small parlour where the most important meeting in the chapter happens is almost completely undescribed. One lamp is mentioned (it gutters once). We get a table and two cups of water. But what is this room? Is it cold? What does it smell like — old stone, lamp oil, bread? The single guttering lamp is the right instinct; there should be five more details like it.

> 'She came at the hour of the lamp. He was at the gate to receive the small delegation of fishermen who had brought a basket of cured tunny for the basilica's relief work.' — Cured tunny has an extremely specific smell. It would be everywhere. The fishermen appear and disappear without a single sensory trace, and they are the setup for Melania's entrance — a perfect opportunity to ground the reader in the physical world of the harbor just before she steps out of it into the lamplight.

> 'She was perhaps thirty. Her face was not the face of a woman who had been weeping. Her face was the face of a woman who had been listening, all afternoon, to people weeping, and was now done.' — The psychological description of Melania's face is clever but it's an abstraction. What does a face that has been listening to grief all day actually look like? Tightness around the eyes? A particular stillness? King would give us something the eye can see, not just the mind can process.

### Suggestions
- Give the writing room a smell. Tallow from the lamp, the particular dry-dust smell of parchment and wax, perhaps damp stone from the rain finding a gap somewhere. One sentence. It will anchor every scene that returns to that room.
- The basilica debate is the chapter's set piece and it needs an acoustic register. Stone echoes. A crowd of this size breathes. When the crowd laughs, the laugh has a shape in that space — it rises and dies in a way that outdoor laughter doesn't. Give us one sentence of what the stone does to the sound of two hundred people.
- The November climate of Hippo Regius is largely absent. You use rain brilliantly at the opening and closing but drop it entirely for the middle sixty percent of the chapter. Is the basilica cold? Does Augustine's breath show? Does the parlour have a brazier? The physical temperature of a scene tells us how long people can stay in it and what it costs them to be there.
- The parlour scene needs one more physical object with history on it. The cup she doesn't drink from is excellent — use that principle again. What is on the table beside the water? What is on the walls? One detail that tells us something about whose room this is and how it is normally used.
- At the gate, before Melania steps forward, let Augustine smell the harbor. The refugees in the warehouses, the cured fish, the rain on salt. He has been inside the written word all day; the harbor is the material world he is supposed to be feeding. The contrast between that smell and the woman who appears out of the lamplight would sharpen her arrival dramatically.
- The scrubbed table mentioned early in the chapter ('the table behind him in a position the table had since been scrubbed of in three separate cleanings') is your single best piece of embedded history-in-object. There should be two or three more of these in the chapter. The bone stylus, the wax tablet, the bronze box — each of these objects has a history. Let one or two of them carry it.

## Tension & Dread Building (Grade: B+)

This chapter builds dread the right way — not through threat but through implication, accumulation, and the specific weight of things withheld. The three-scrubbing detail, the aging of Stephen's face, the bronze disc that warms without explanation: these are the moves of a writer who understands that the reader's imagination is more frightening than any explicitness. The structure is sound — ordinary world (the writing room), public ordeal (the debate), then the private revelation (Melania), then the uncanny payoff (the disc). The final image — a man of iron discipline who momentarily cannot remember the way to his own cell — is the chapter's best moment, small and devastating. The primary structural problem is the Volusianus debate, which is intellectually excellent but runs so long that the chapter's momentum stalls before Melania appears. In King terms, this is the quiet before the storm that goes so quiet the reader stops holding their breath. The warmth of the disc is also slightly pre-telegraphed — told to us during the parlour scene, which means the final discovery scene loses some of its voltage. Otherwise, the tension machinery here is genuinely sophisticated. The withholding strategy — we never learn what the disc is, what the practices are, who 'the man it is for' might be — is exactly correct. Mystery boxes earn their keep when they generate forward pull, and these do.

### Good Examples
> 'the night the courier had been brought in by the carter Lucius and laid on the table behind him in a position the table had since been scrubbed of in three separate cleanings' — Three cleanings. Not one. Not two. Three. The most unsettling backstory exposition in the chapter, and it's accomplished in a subordinate clause. We know something terrible happened on that table without being told what it was, and the triple cleaning tells us whoever cleaned it wasn't sure the first two times had been enough. King's 'ordinary made horrible' in its purest form.

> 'He had aged in the fourteen months since the night of the courier in the way men of his age age when they have been the hands of a thing they had not asked to be the hands of for the second time in their lives' — Dread through a secondary character's body. Stephen has done something terrible before. It aged him then. He was made to do it again. It aged him worse this time. We don't know what it was. We are given only the evidence in the corners of his mouth and the set of his shoulders. Augustine has been watching it and said nothing. The silence between them about it is a third presence in the room.

> 'He could feel, in a way that he had felt twice in fourteen months and not before in his life, the bronze disc warm against the parchment in the upper shelf of the writing room three corridors away. He did not let his face change.' — This is dramatic irony deployed with precision. The reader understands the disc is responding to Melania's presence before Augustine fully processes it. The face that does not change is the tell. We are ahead of the senator but not ahead of the disc.

> 'The bronze disc was warm. / He set the box on the desk. He sat. He did not open the box. He did not need to.' — The restraint here is what earns it. A lesser writer opens the box. This writer understands that opening the box destroys the box. The warmth is enough. The not-opening is the dread.

> 'He stood in it for the count of three before he remembered which way the cell was, and then he remembered, and he went.' — The chapter's best sentence. After forty years of discipline, of arguments held at the rail, of faces that don't change — the man forgets which way his own cell is. He stands in the rain counting. Then he remembers. Then he goes. The 'and then he went' is the most frightening clause in the chapter because of how ordinary it sounds after what precedes it.

> 'You will not negotiate. You will withdraw. You will burn what cannot be moved and you will move what cannot be burned and you will go further south into the desert if the desert is what is left to you.' — Condition Five. Escalation of stakes done through procedural language, which makes it more frightening than if it were dramatic. This is a man describing violence as logistics. The violence hasn't happened yet. It is being planned for. The desert at the end of the sentence is the horizon of the whole story.

### Needs Improvement
> The Volusianus debate — roughly 900 words of theological sparring — runs too long before Melania appears at the gate. The debate is well-written and the arguments are sharp, but tension requires a reader holding their breath, and you can't hold your breath for 900 words of senatorial exchange. By the time Volusianus inclines his head and departs, the reader has exhaled fully. The Melania scene then has to rebuild from zero. King compresses these middle sections mercilessly — the debate could be half its length and do the same structural work (establishing Augustine's public authority and the world's eyes on him) while preserving the momentum the opening correctly creates.

> 'the colour of the rain' appears twice — once describing Melania's cloak ('a plain wool cloak the colour of the rain') and once in the final meditation ('she had walked through the gate of the basilica three hours ago in the colour of the rain'). It's a genuinely good image the first time. The second time it signals the writer's attachment to it rather than the reader's experience of the prose. King's rule: if you love a sentence, kill it. Here, kill the second one.

> 'He could feel, in a way that he had felt twice in fourteen months and not before in his life, the bronze disc warm against the parchment in the upper shelf of the writing room three corridors away.' — This appears mid-parlour-scene, which means when we reach the final scene ('The bronze disc was warm'), we have been told it already. The final reveal loses voltage because we've been spoiled. The disc's warmth should be discovered by the reader at the same moment Augustine goes to touch it, not pre-loaded as interiority during the conversation. Move this sensation to the final scene only, and the ending becomes twice as heavy.

### Suggestions
- Cut the Volusianus debate by half. The public exchange earns its place — Augustine winning in front of the crowd is necessary setup for Melania to arrive knowing she has found the right man. But the back-and-forth currently runs to a full rhetorical KO, and KOs relax the reader. End the debate a round earlier, on a draw or a near-miss, so the reader is still coiled when she appears at the gate.
- Remove the mid-scene bronze disc sensation ('He could feel, in a way that he had felt twice...') from the parlour scene entirely. Let Augustine hear 'the priest in Bethlehem will not last long now and the work is moving south' and let the reader see only 'He did not let his face change.' Trust the reader to understand something enormous is happening. Then when he walks to the writing room and puts his hand on the box and the disc is warm — that's the revelation. Right now you are explaining the explosion before it happens.
- The five conditions are the chapter's sharpest escalation of stakes — but Condition Five ('burn what cannot be moved') arrives and departs without landing. After Augustine delivers it, give Melania one beat of real stillness before she says 'Agreed.' Not hesitation — she has already decided. But a physical beat. She sets down the cup. She looks at him. Then: 'Agreed.' The single action tells us she has just accepted that she may one day be fleeing into the desert, and she is doing it without drama, which is more frightening than drama.
- In the opening, 'The sky was the colour of the sea on a winter morning. The sea... was the colour of the sky. The two had been the same colour for nine days' is doing the work of low atmospheric dread correctly, but the repetition of 'the colour of' three times in two sentences is mechanical. Try: 'The sky had been the colour of the sea for nine days. He had been watching the line between them disappear.' Same content, but the disappearing line adds an image of erasure that rhymes with everything that follows.
- Stephen's aging needs one more beat of payoff later in the chapter. He appears at the top, aged in his shoulders and the corners of his mouth from being 'the hands of a thing' for the second time. Then he delivers the Volusianus letter and exits. He's gone. But when Melania says 'The priest in Bethlehem will not last long now' — that's a death sentence for Jerome, delivered calmly across a table. Augustine is the one who will 'last' or not. Stephen's aged face should return here, as a ghost-image, to connect what the chapter opened with to what the chapter is actually about: who gets used up by the carrying of this work, and who is next.

## Dialogue & Voice (Grade: B+)

This chapter is doing something genuinely difficult: it renders formal, fifth-century ecclesiastical Latin speech in English prose without quotation marks, and it largely works. The compression is superb. The subtext in the Melania scene — an entire negotiation conducted around an unnamed object, an unnamed 'work,' practices never directly described — is exactly what King means when he talks about what's NOT said doing the heavy lifting. The problem is the debate. Volusianus and Augustine deliver prepared orations at each other, and while the content is brilliant, it's speeches, not dialogue. Real people — even in formal disputations, even in basilicas, even in 411 CE — interrupt, adjust, hedge, lose their thread and recover it. Volusianus is a walking objection machine: he has no verbal tics, no unexpected register, no moment where he says something that surprises you. His function is to be answered. The five conditions Augustine names to Melania have the same problem: 'Agreed. Agreed. Agreed.' four times in a row drains a scene of genuine tension. The strongest individual lines are very strong. The weakest stretches feel like historical essays with dialogue tags.

### Good Examples
> '*Put it.*' — One word in response to Volusianus asking permission to pose his question. Augustine's authority, his command of the room, his complete absence of anxiety: all of it in two syllables. This is King-level compression.

> '*It is not a speech, senator. It is a description of what is.*' — The instant, flat reframe. No anger, no performance. Volusianus tries to diminish with 'clever speech' and Augustine doesn't argue the adjective, he rejects the noun. Reveals character in one sentence.

> '*Good. Drink the water.*' — Strange, warm, slightly commanding, completely unexpected after the weight of everything said before it. This is the best line in the chapter. It reveals the whole power dynamic between them — he's been holding something back this whole scene, and this is the first moment of care that breaks through the formality.

> '*He understands. He has been waiting for this for two years.*' — Pinianus, who never appears, is more vivid here than Volusianus is across four pages. Two lines, zero waste. This is how you render an off-page character.

> '*I will help you make it.*' / '*I had hoped you would.*' — The whole scene earns this exchange. No exclamation, no relief, no effusion. She 'had hoped.' Past tense. She came in already knowing. That's a whole character in three words.

### Needs Improvement
> Augustine's unbroken speech from '*Senator. Permit me a question of my own*' through '*they are not the cause of the fall. They are the proof that the fall has occurred.*' runs nearly 400 words without Volusianus doing anything but existing. Even in formal disputations, real people react. A shift in the senator's posture. A half-started objection. A word — '*Bishop—*' — cut off by Augustine pressing forward. The argument is brilliant. The absence of interruption makes it feel written, not spoken.

> '*The first. The community will be your community... Agreed. The second... Agreed. The third... Agreed.*' — Five consecutive conditions, each answered 'Agreed' with minor variation. Dramatically this is a flat line. Melania could have hesitated at the fourth — the one about Possidius, a man she doesn't know. She could have asked a question about the fifth. One moment of real negotiation would make the whole sequence feel like a conversation instead of a document being ratified.

> '*Bishop, you reduce a serious question to mockery.*' — The most predictable thing Volusianus says. Every written debate has this line. It tells us nothing new about him. He had the room and he's losing it — that moment deserved something sharper or stranger, something that showed the man who spent forty years being right about everything.

> Augustine to Stephen: '*This is the man who has been writing the dispatches from Carthage... He is the head of the household that is currently arguing, in Aurelius's parlour and in three private salons in the city...*' — The 'as you know Bob' warning light flickers here. Stephen almost certainly knows some of this. What feels true is Augustine thinking out loud, reading the letter against everything he already knows. But the speech is just expository enough that it reads more like the author briefing the reader than Augustine briefing Stephen.

### Suggestions
- Give Volusianus one line that surprises you — something that makes Augustine pause before answering, rather than flowing straight into his counter. Even one moment of unexpected register (a flash of personal bitterness, an aside that reveals what this debate actually costs him) would make him a person instead of a plot function. Right now he exists to be defeated. King's antagonists always have something the protagonist doesn't expect.
- Break one of Augustine's long speeches. Not to undermine the argument — the argument is excellent — but to let Volusianus's body or voice interrupt at the worst possible moment for him. A sharp intake of breath. '*—Bishop—*' that Augustine talks past. The argument continues but now we feel the crowd feeling the senator get run over, which is more dramatic than watching it on the page.
- In the five conditions, have Melania push back on one — not refuse, but pause, ask a question, show the moment of real consideration before agreeing. The fourth condition (Possidius) is the natural place: she's being asked to trust a man she's never met. Let her want to know more before she says yes. That one crack in the pattern makes all five conditions feel earned rather than ceremonial.
- Augustine's voice in dialogue is nearly identical to Augustine's voice in narration. This is partly intentional — the prose style is doing historical-formal work — but King's characters sound different when they're talking than when they're thinking. Look for one place where Augustine says something slightly ragged, slightly less complete than he means, and then corrects himself. One seam in the formal Latin. The 'Drink the water' moment does this instinctively — build one more like it.
- The Stephen scenes are efficient but Stephen himself is functional rather than present. He 'committed the words to memory' and left — that's good. But in fourteen months of being 'the hands of a thing he had not asked to be the hands of,' he must have a point of view. One line from Stephen that reveals he knows more than he's saying — or that he's afraid of something specific — would make him a character instead of a messenger.

## Pacing & Structure (Grade: B+)

This chapter has real architectural intelligence: three clean movements (writing room → basilica debate → gate meeting), each escalating in stakes, each ending on a beat that pulls you into the next. The writer understands King's core truth — structure is tension delivery. The opening and closing are genuinely excellent, and the chapter's internal rhythm in those sections shows a writer who has internalized the mechanics of pace. The problem is the middle. The basilica debate runs too long. Augustine's argument about Brennus and Hannibal and Sulla is intellectually compelling but pacing-dead — four consecutive long rhetorical paragraphs without a real break in the crowd reaction or a physical beat to anchor the reader. Then the Melania scene compounds this: her grandmother's teaching, delivered in one unbroken speech, followed by five conditions, each at near-equal length, creates a plateau where the chapter should be climbing. King's rule is that the exciting parts earn the slow parts — but when everything runs at the same speed, nothing has speed. The chapter's two best moments, the isolated 'The bronze disc was warm.' and the final disoriented walk to compline, show exactly what the middle lacks: variation. Short sentences after long ones hit like a slap. The writer knows this — uses it brilliantly at the end — but abandons it for twenty pages in between.

### Good Examples
> 'A knock at the door. Three short.' — Textbook King. Fragment sentence as percussive break. The white space before and after does the work. You feel the knock.

> 'The senator had come to Hippo expecting the bishop to defend Rome. The bishop had not defended Rome. The bishop had given Rome away.' — Perfect rhythmic triplet. Each sentence shorter than the last. The compression mirrors the reversal. This is invisible prose.

> 'The autumn rain had begun again at the cathedral door. He stood in it for the count of three before he remembered which way the cell was, and then he remembered, and he went.' — The best sentence in the chapter. Disorientation rendered in rhythm. The doubled 'he remembered' enacts the act of remembering. The flat 'and he went' is exactly right.

> 'The bronze disc was warm.' isolated as its own paragraph — The chapter has been building to this and the writer knows the single isolated sentence is the detonator. Perfect placement, perfect restraint.

> 'Her face was not the face of a woman who had been weeping. Her face was the face of a woman who had been listening, all afternoon, to people weeping, and was now done.' — Melania's introduction earns its space. The repeated structure with the pivot at 'listening' is elegant and the final 'and was now done' lands.

### Needs Improvement
> 'the third warmth was a different warmth from the first two warmths, in the way that the third warmth of a thing carried for thirty years would be a different warmth' — The writer just told us it was the third time. The explanation kills the mystery. King cuts this on instinct. Trust the reader.

> The four consecutive Augustine debate paragraphs beginning 'Brennus the Gaul sacked Rome...' through '...not the cause of the fall' — No physical interruption, no crowd reaction, no beat. Pure lecture. The deacon's smile, Volusianus's clenching hand — these are gold, but they're buried too far down. Scatter them through the argument to keep the scene moving.

> Melania's grandmother speech: 'Bishop, my grandmother taught me before she died that there were certain matters which were best understood...' running to '...because institutions in our age are not lasting.' — This is a single unbroken block of oblique exposition. It needs to breathe. Cut it by a third. Let Augustine react mid-speech. The withholding creates atmosphere but the length undercuts it.

> 'He sat with what she had said. He sat with it for a long count.' — 'A long count' is vague where it should be evocative. King would give us something specific — a sound from the cloister, the lamp, a physical sensation — that carries the weight of the pause rather than naming the pause.

### Suggestions
- Cut the basilica debate by 35-40%. Keep the Brennus setup and the City of God payoff. Cut at least one of the middle exchanges (the tautology argument or the Arian Visigoth argument — pick one, not both). Scatter the crowd and Volusianus physical reactions more evenly so the scene stays embodied, not rhetorical.
- Break Melania's grandmother speech at least twice with Augustine reactions or physical beats. She's building to the most important thing she'll say; let him feel the weight building before she gets there. A single chair-creak or lamp gutter mid-speech does more than ten adjectives.
- The five conditions are structurally smart — they mirror the characters' bureaucratic precision — but conditions three and four can be compressed. Pinianus's consent can be stated in a single sentence. Possidius needs one sentence, not four. The list's power comes from its rhythm; if all five items run the same length, the list becomes a recitation.
- Consider cutting 'He sat with it for a long count.' entirely and replacing it with one concrete sensory detail — the disc's warmth through the shelf above them, the rain restarting outside, anything that externalizes the internal weight. The tell is doing work the show should do.
- The scene break between the basilica and the gate currently uses the standard '---' divider. Earned. But the gate scene's opening paragraph (the fishermen and the tunny) is too long for a transitional beat. Trim it to two sentences max — the fishermen are a mechanism to place Augustine at the gate, not a scene. Get to Melania faster.

## Themes & Subtext (Grade: B+)

The chapter's central theme — that the invisible city outlasts the visible one — is genuinely organic. It earns its weight because the author doesn't argue it once; it plays out twice, at two levels, simultaneously. Augustine makes the argument publicly against Volusianus, then enacts it privately with Melania. The same idea — that what matters is carried person to person, not by institutions or stones — runs through both conversations without the author ever pointing at it. The bronze disc is the chapter's most disciplined symbolic object: felt across rooms, never opened, warming for the third time in thirty years. That's theme as object, not theme as speech. The five conditions Augustine sets are also thematically rich — each one is a lesson in how hidden knowledge survives, and none of it is labeled as such. The subtext about preservation is the thriller engine, and it works precisely because it's never named. The weakest thematic moment is Augustine's long speech at the rail, which crosses from enacting the theme into explaining it. 'The city of God is not the city of Rome. It has never been the city of Rome' is a line Augustine writes in the actual City of God — which is the problem. The reader is getting a lecture, not a story. The refugees sleeping on straw in the warehouses is the theme. The deacon smiling into his hand is the theme. The senator's hand closing and opening is the theme. The chapter already has it. Then Augustine stands up and says it anyway, at length, twice.

### Good Examples
> 'The bishop had not defended Rome. The bishop had given Rome away.' — This is the chapter's sharpest thematic moment, and it's delivered as observation, not argument. Augustine's intellectual move is dramatized, not explained.

> 'His hand at his side closed and opened.' — One physical detail carrying the whole weight of a man's defeat. No interior access to Volusianus required. King would call this the real work.

> 'He felt it warm against the parchment on the upper shelf the way a man feels a fire he is not looking at.' — The bronze disc as thematic anchor: the sacred not displayed but sensed, present in the chapter's margins exactly where hidden tradition lives.

> 'Good. Drink the water.' — Four words closing a scene of enormous consequence. Augustine gives her permission to exist in his world, and he does it through a cup of water. The City of God feeds people. The chapter shows this without saying it once.

> 'She had chosen the cloak and chosen the hour. She was a woman of careful calculation.' — Melania's disguise as thematic statement: the hidden tradition practices concealment at every level, including clothing. The subtext is in the wardrobe choice.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He understood, while he held it, that the thing she had said about her grandmother and the priest in Bethlehem and the work moving south through Africa was the most important sentence anyone had spoken to him since the night of the courier.' — Don't tell us it's the most important sentence. Make it the most important sentence and trust us to feel the weight. This is the author stepping in front of his own scene.

> 'He did not let his face change' appears three times in the Melania section alone. Once is characterization. Twice is a habit. Three times is the writer's hand showing. The first instance earns it; the second and third spend what the first deposited.

> The closing passage of the debate — from 'Senator, the city of God is not the city of Rome' to 'This is what the city of God is' — runs 200 words of Augustine explaining the chapter's central theme. The crowd nodding, the deacon's deep smile, the senator's hand already told us. This speech is the author afraid the reader missed it.

> 'The working of these matters was carried in households, generation by generation, and that the households which carried them were now few and were becoming fewer.' — Melania's grandmother's voice filtered through Melania's report is exposition wearing the clothes of dialogue. The secret tradition is more interesting when we see it operating than when we hear it described.

### Suggestions
- Cut 'He understood, while he held it, that the thing she had said... was the most important sentence anyone had spoken to him since the night of the courier.' End the beat at 'the priest in Bethlehem will not last long now and the work is moving south.' The sentence is already doing its job. Don't summarize it.
- In the debate, end Augustine's final speech at 'It is made wherever those loves are found.' Cut the subsequent paragraph that itemizes where it is made. The specific examples (the basilica, the Aventine houses, the warehouses) are good — the problem is the sentence that follows each one explaining why it matters.
- Find one place in the chapter where someone is actually fed — a name, a face, a piece of bread passing between hands. Augustine says the church has been feeding refugees for fourteen months; we never see it. One concrete image would do what four paragraphs of debate cannot.
- Consolidate 'He did not let his face change' to a single use in the Melania scene, at the moment of highest stakes — when she names the object. The other two instances dilute the technique. Vary the tells: let his hand be still, let him take a slow breath, let him look at the lamp instead of her.
- One of Augustine's five conditions should cost him something — reveal a fear, a wound, a previous failure. Currently they read as procedural wisdom; they should also read as the scars of someone who has been doing this work long enough to know what goes wrong. The fifth condition (burn what cannot be moved) hints at this but never lands it.

## Cultural Specificity & Time Period (Grade: A-)

This chapter is doing something genuinely difficult — anchoring readers in 411 CE Hippo Regius through intellectual and institutional texture rather than costume-drama set dressing — and it largely succeeds. The historical grounding is meticulous and earned. Volusianus is a real senator; Melania the Younger and Pinianus really did flee Italy to Africa after the sack of Rome and really did stay in Tagaste; the martyrs of Scillium really were tried by Saturninus in 180 CE; the 'priest in Bethlehem' who won't last long is unmistakably Jerome, historically on the clock. The debate at the rail is essentially a dramatized origin scene for 'City of God,' and that's not an accident — that's a writer who did the work. The social textures are equally precise: the senatorial toga as theater, the protocol of inclining heads by rank, the deacon of the synagogue who attends only on philosophical days, the fishermen's principle requiring the bishop himself to receive their charity. This is a world with rules, and the author knows them. Where King would push back is on the body. The physical world of a North African harbor city in November — the smell of mud and fish and lamp oil and wet wool, the weight of a basket of cured tunny, the cold stone of the basilica floor — is gestured at rather than inhabited. The chapter trusts its reader to fill in the sensorium from the intellectual texture, which is a gamble. King would say you can't trust that. You have to put the smell in the room. The other weakness: some of the cultural grounding is explained to the reader through Augustine explaining it to Stephen, which means the research is showing. A man like Augustine wouldn't brief his deacon on who Volusianus is the way a professor briefs a seminar. The exposition is mostly well-hidden, but not always.

### Good Examples
> 'He wore the senatorial toga with the broad purple stripe, which on a man of his bearing in a North African basilica in November was a small instrument of theatre. The toga was immaculate; he had had it laundered before the service.' — Perfect. One detail does three jobs: establishes the period, characterizes Volusianus as a man who uses protocol as performance, and shows that he came to Hippo prepared for an audience.

> 'The deacon of the synagogue who came to the cathedral on the days when the bishop would preach on a matter of philosophy — sat in the rows before the altar.' — This one clause tells us more about 5th-century North African pluralism and Augustine's specific reputation than a paragraph of exposition could. Specific, economical, true.

> 'Brennus the Gaul sacked Rome in the year three hundred and ninety before the birth of our Lord. Hannibal and the Carthaginians ravaged Italy for sixteen years...' — The debate passage works because Augustine uses actual dates and names, not vague historical gesture. This is a man arguing from the record, and the record is specific. The reader feels the period in the argument itself.

> 'Twenty-eight, Bishop. I have been giving away the estates for eight years.' — Melania identified in four words as a product of a particular class doing a particular thing that only that class in that era could do. No explanation needed. The reader who knows knows; the reader who doesn't still understands this is a woman with history.

> 'The autumn rains had come early this year, in the second week of October instead of the third, and the harbour road had been mud since.' — Grounds us in the agricultural and seasonal rhythms of North Africa rather than universal calendar time. This rain has consequences: a muddy harbour road, refugees moving slower, grain shipments delayed. It's not decorative weather.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The fishermen handed over the tunny. Augustine blessed them. They went away.' — Three men carrying a basket of cured fish just handed the bishop of Hippo their charity and received a blessing and we get nothing — no smell of the harbor on them, no roughness of their hands, no salt-smell of the fish itself. King would say: the tunny is doing no work here. If you're going to put cured fish in the scene, put the fish in the scene.

> 'He is the head of the household that is currently arguing, in Aurelius's parlour and in three private salons in the city, that the empire fell because the empire became Christian.' — Augustine is explaining to Stephen, his own deacon, who Volusianus is and what he believes. This is the writer briefing the reader through the character. A bishop who has been tracking Volusianus's dispatches for fourteen months doesn't brief his senior deacon as if Stephen has never heard the name. The exposition is wearing a thin disguise.

> 'The work had been going for fourteen months now' / 'since the night of the courier, by the same lamp on the same desk in the same room' / 'the Christian church has been feeding them for fourteen months' — The chapter uses 'fourteen months' as a structural anchor, which is purposeful, but by the fourth or fifth repetition it starts to feel like a verbal tic rather than a deliberate refrain. King would cut two of these without mercy and let the remaining ones carry the weight.

> 'He could feel, in a way that he had felt twice in fourteen months and not before in his life, the bronze disc warm against the parchment in the upper shelf of the writing room three corridors away.' — The bronze disc is the chapter's central mysterious object and we know almost nothing about it — not what it looks like, not its size or weight or patina, not where it came from. For King, mystery objects need physical reality. The warmth is evocative but it's floating in a void. What does the bronze smell like? How heavy is the box? Has it dented the shelf over thirty years?

### Suggestions
- Put one strong sensory detail from the harbor world into the first scene — not the rain, which is already there, but something specific to Hippo: the smell of fish-sauce from the warehouse district, the particular quality of North African mud (red clay under the grey surface), the sound of the harbour at the third hour. King locates his readers through the nose before the eye.
- The fishermen scene is a missed opportunity. Give us one sentence of physical reality — the basket, the smell, the way one of them can't quite meet the bishop's eye because they half-expected Eraclius. Then let them go. That's the texture the chapter is thin on: ordinary people in their ordinary lives, product of a specific time and place.
- Trust Volusianus to make his own argument without Augustine pre-explaining it to Stephen. Cut the briefing in the writing room ('He is the head of the household that is currently arguing...') and let the senator speak for himself at the rail. This removes the expository seam and makes the debate scene land harder because the reader hasn't already been told who wins.
- Give the bronze disc one specific physical attribute — its age, its origin, a mark worn into it by thirty years of handling, its weight in the palm — early in the chapter so that the warmth at the end has something to be warm in. Mystery needs a body to inhabit.
- The meal in the small parlour after the debate is mentioned but skipped ('He went into the small parlour and ate.'). King would say: put one detail from that meal in — the educated men's conversation, something one of them says, the food itself. The contrast between the grandeur of the debate and the ordinariness of bread and olives afterward is period texture you're leaving on the table.

## Prose Polish (King Polish) (Grade: B+)

This chapter has real bones — the dialogue is clean and tagless, the sensory anchors are specific, and the story drives forward with purpose. When it works, the prose is genuinely invisible: you're in the basilica, you're at the gate, you're holding the bronze box. But the writer has a recurring flaw that undercuts the best moments: they don't trust the reader and they don't trust the sentence. They write a good sentence, then they explain it. They find a powerful rhythmic device — the short declarative repetition — and they overuse it until it becomes parody. The opening sky/sea chiasmus is a small vanity. The courier sentence is an act of grammatical cruelty. The warmth chain near the climax takes a devastating image and repeats it into the ground. The chapter needs the 10% cut applied in targeted passes — not evenly, but surgically. Eighty percent of this prose is already at the level it should be. The twenty percent that isn't is concentrated in about six passages, and those passages are the ones the writer was proudest of when they wrote them, which is always the warning sign.

### Good Examples
> 'A knock at the door. Three short.' — Two sentences, five words, complete scene-shift. Perfect Kinglian minimalism. The reader fills the rest.

> 'The senator had come to Hippo expecting the bishop to defend Rome. The bishop had not defended Rome. The bishop had given Rome away.' — The triple structure earns its keep here because each beat is a different idea. The last clause lands like a punch. This is the 10% principle working in reverse: the third sentence does more work than the two before it combined.

> 'She was standing under the arch of the gate, in the half-light of the gate-keeper's lamp, in a plain wool cloak the colour of the rain.' — Character introduction done right. Three prepositional phrases, each adding a layer of sensory and social information, none of them overreaching.

> 'He stood in it for the count of three before he remembered which way the cell was, and then he remembered, and he went.' — The chapter's best sentence. The disorientation, the recovery, the departure — all in one syntactic movement that mirrors the psychology perfectly. Prose disappears entirely here.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The sky was the colour of the sea on a winter morning. The sea, which he could see in a strip between the tile of the basilica roof and the line of the fig trees in the cloister, was the colour of the sky. The two had been the same colour for nine days.' — The reversal is a small literary trick that calls attention to itself as writing. 'The two had been the same colour for nine days' is the worst offender: it adds nothing except the writer's satisfaction at having counted the days. Cut that last sentence.

> 'The work had been going for fourteen months now — since the night the courier had been brought in by the carter Lucius and laid on the table behind him in a position the table had since been scrubbed of in three separate cleanings —' — Passive stacked on passive, and the end of the clause ('a position the table had since been scrubbed of in three separate cleanings') is so syntactically contorted the reader has to read it twice to understand it, then a third time to believe it. Three separate cleanings is a good detail. Bury it in this sentence and it dies.

> 'the third warmth was a different warmth from the first two warmths, in the way that the third warmth of a thing carried for thirty years would be a different warmth' — Five uses of warmth/warm in one sentence. The disc-warmth motif is the chapter's best sustained image. Here it is smothered. The power was in 'The disc was warm' — the simple declarative — and the writer couldn't leave it alone.

> 'She was a woman of careful calculation.' — A tell dropped after the show has already landed. The reader has just been told she chose the plain cloak, chose the hour, chose the gate. We know. This sentence is the writer not trusting what they just wrote.

> 'He was, Augustine could see, not as good a debater as he had been in his thirties, and the long preparation for this exchange had not been, on the senator's side, a preparation that included the answer he was now hearing.' — 'A preparation that included the answer he was now hearing' is the kind of construction that happens when a writer is explaining rather than showing. The senator's closed-and-opened hand already told us everything.

### Suggestions
- Cut the last sentence of the opening paragraph entirely. 'The two had been the same colour for nine days' is an accountant's note appended to a poetic observation. The sky-sea chiasmus either stands on its own or it doesn't.
- Rewrite the courier sentence in active voice and split it. Something like: 'The work had been going for fourteen months — since the night Lucius the carter carried the courier in and laid him on the table. The table had been scrubbed three times since. The position didn't come out.' Shorter sentences, active construction, same information, the three-scrubbing detail now lands.
- In the bronze disc passage near the end, cut the warmth-chain sentence to its core. Keep 'The disc was warm' as the refrain — it's already doing the work. Remove 'the third warmth was a different warmth from the first two warmths, in the way that the third warmth of a thing carried for thirty years would be a different warmth' and replace with nothing, or with one clean clause: 'A different warmth. He had been waiting for it.'
- Cut 'She was a woman of careful calculation.' Full stop. The reader is ahead of you.
- Tighten the five conditions in the agreement — each one currently runs about 30% longer than it needs to. Conditions work as drama only when they're crisp. The rhythm of 'Agreed / Agreed / Agreed' is powerful; the padding around each condition dilutes it.
- The Volusianus interior observation ('He was, Augustine could see, not as good a debater...') wants to be two clean sentences: 'Volusianus had prepared for this exchange. He had not prepared for this answer.' The rest is the writer's working notes left in the draft.
