### CRITICAL (5)

**1. [Developmental Editor] structure / thriller mechanics** (Section 1 (entire Hippo January 412 section))
- **Issue:** The chapter opens on a closed operation. The seven letters have been delivered, acknowledged, and receipted. Mark is 'uninjured in his understanding.' The disc is cold. 'The matter of the desert has completed without further complication.' This is a debriefing scene — it resolves prior tension rather than initiating new tension. For a thriller chapter, opening on resolution is structurally backwards. The reader has nothing to dread or pursue entering the chapter; the dread is retrospective and the operation is already over.
- **Suggestion:** Plant an unresolved thread in Section 1 that runs through the chapter. Options: (1) One of the seven receipts is missing or wrong in some way that Augustine notes but suppresses. (2) The disc is not cold — it is 'less cold than it had been' — and Augustine cannot account for why. (3) The wax-tablet note about Sketis reads slightly wrong on second writing, suggesting something in the operation has completed differently than planned. Any of these gives the reader something to carry forward into the Tagaste and Marcellinus sections, rather than arriving at Section 3's crisis from flat ground.
- **Before:** `He set the disc back. He closed the box. He returned the box to the shelf.`
- **After:** `He held it for a count. The disc was cold. It had been cold the last time. He held it for a second count, which he had not done before, and then set it back. He could not have said what he was checking for.`

**2. [Immersive Experience Editor] visceral moment / showing vs. telling** (Final section — paragraphs beginning 'The four letters went out' and 'Marcellinus and his brother Apringius were executed')
- **Issue:** The chapter's emotional climax — Augustine learning his friend was already dead before he wrote four letters in his defense — is rendered in the flattest possible register. 'He read the dispatch through. He set it on the desk beside Marcellinus's last letter of the previous month. He took up the iron pen. He went back to the fourth book.' The restraint is clearly intentional, but restraint still requires a physical anchor. The reader is told nothing about the act of picking up that pen — what it costs to do it, what the body does or refuses to do. We understand the grief intellectually; we feel none of it.
- **Suggestion:** Keep the iron control of the prose voice. But find one physical detail that makes the discipline of returning to work felt as discipline, not simply reported as fact. The unreplied letter about the third-book qualification — sitting there from a man now headless — is the devastating specific object already in the scene. Use it.
- **Before:** `He read the dispatch through. He set it on the desk beside Marcellinus's last letter of the previous month. He took up the iron pen. He went back to the fourth book.`
- **After:** `He read the dispatch through. He set it on the desk beside Marcellinus's letter — the one about the third book, the qualification about Roman history, the good careful thinking of a man who had been alive when he wrote it. He sat with it for a long count. Then he took up the iron pen. The hand that held it was steady. He made it steady. He went back to the fourth book.`

**3. [Immersive Experience Editor] sensory detail / scene grounding** (All three bronze box passages — paragraphs beginning 'He went to the upper shelf' in each of the three sections)
- **Issue:** The disc is the chapter's central mystery object, appearing at structurally weighted moments — after the courier departs, after Marcellinus's arrest, and in the closing meditation. Each time, the only sensory fact offered is 'The disc was cold.' No weight. No visual description. No texture. No physical response in Augustine's hand or body. The repetition of 'The disc was cold' works as a liturgical refrain only if the coldness is actually felt by the reader — but a single word after an empty gesture ('He took it out') is not felt. The disc is ritually present and sensorially absent.
- **Suggestion:** The mystery of what the disc is can remain total — don't reveal it. But give the hand something to feel. Weight, edge, surface, temperature as it transfers. One sentence of tactile specificity per occurrence is enough to make the ritual real. The third occurrence, with the explicit meditation that follows, needs the most physical grounding because it bears the chapter's thematic weight.

It was cold — the cold of metal that had been in an unheated room since October. The cold moved through his palm. He held it until the cold was gone and it was only the weight of it against his fingers. He set it back.`

**4. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation — MacGuffin With No Frame** (Section 1 (January 412), first disc scene; Section 3 (September 413), second and third disc scenes)
- **Issue:** The bronze box and disc appear three times — clearly the thriller's central object — yet the reader is given zero frame of reference. Not what the disc is, not where it came from, not what 'cold' means (good sign? warning? normal?), not why Augustine is checking it. In a thriller, withholding explanation is legitimate technique. Giving the reader no orientation at all is different. A general reader will register that this object recurs, but they cannot build curiosity without knowing what kind of mystery they are tracking — supernatural? historical? mechanical? The disc is inert across all three appearances because the reader has no hook.
- **Suggestion:** Seed one functional detail at the first appearance — not an explanation, just an anchor. A hint at duration of keeping, or what Augustine is waiting for when he checks it, gives the reader enough to carry the mystery forward. The reader doesn't need to know what the disc is; they need to know what cold means to Augustine.
- **Before:** `He went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box. He opened it. The hinges sounded. The disc was inside. He took it out. The disc was cold. He held it for a count. He set it back. He closed the box. He returned the box to the shelf.`
- **After:** `He went to the upper shelf. He took down the bronze box. He opened it. The hinges sounded. The disc was inside. He took it out. It was a disc of some metal he had not been able to name in the twenty-six years of his keeping it. It was cold. He had been told to check it. He had been told what to look for when he checked it. The disc was cold, which meant it was not yet what it was supposed to be. He held it for a count. He set it back. He closed the box. He returned the box to the shelf.`

### MINOR (5)

**1. [Developmental Editor] register / voice consistency** (Section 2, final paragraph of Tagaste section)
- **Issue:** 'Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer. He lived through the autumn and the winter and the next spring...' This is a proleptic intrusion in an authorial voice that does not match the chapter's cold-reportage mode. It reads as if the author is nervous about the scene's emotional implications and wants to correct the record before the reader finishes the scene. But the cold-reportage style throughout the chapter works precisely because the author trusts the reader to hold uncertainty. This sentence breaks that trust by explaining.
- **Suggestion:** Cut the proleptic passage entirely. Augustine on the road believing he is going home from his last conversation with Pinianus — and carrying that conversation in the slow heat — is more affecting if the reader shares Augustine's uncertainty. The author knows Pinianus lives; the reader does not need to. Alternatively, if the historical correction matters, move it to a brief footnote in whatever apparatus the book uses, outside the narrative voice.
- **Before:** `Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer. He lived through the autumn and the winter and the next spring and the next summer and the autumn after, and he died eventually at Jerusalem in the year Augustine would not be alive to know about. But Augustine, on the inland road back from Tagaste in the slow heat of June, did not know that.`
- **After:** `[cut entirely, ending the section at: 'He carried the conversation with him on the road back.']`

**2. [Developmental Editor] pacing / drafting sequence** (Section 3, four-letter drafting sequence)
- **Issue:** The four-letter sequence — reply to senior bishop, letter to proconsul, letter to consistory, letter to Marcellinus — becomes a list. 'He took up a second sheet... He took up a third sheet... He took up a fourth sheet.' Letters 2 and 3 are functionally identical in prose treatment (same rhythm, same structural summary) and consume space that should belong to letter 4. The letter to Marcellinus in his cell is the only one that matters dramatically, but by the time it arrives the reader has been through two prior drafting cycles and the sequence has lost tension.
- **Suggestion:** Compress letters 2 and 3 into a single sentence each, or combine them: 'He took up a second sheet and a third, and wrote to the proconsul and to the consistory in the formal Latin those offices required, and sealed them and set them in the tray.' Then give letter 4 to Marcellinus the space currently divided across all four.

He took up a third sheet. He drafted the letter to the consistory at Ravenna...`
- **After:** `He took up a second sheet and a third. He wrote to the proconsul and to the consistory in the formal Latin those offices required and sealed both and set them in the tray.

He took up a fourth sheet.`

- **Issue:** The writing room is the chapter's primary recurring space but remains almost unrendered as a physical environment. **5. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation — Accumulated Institutional Vocabulary** (Section 3 (September 413), Marcellinus arrest dispatch and letter-drafting sequence)
- **Issue:** Within a single page the reader encounters 'the senior bishop,' 'the proconsul,' 'the consistory,' 'the count of Africa,' and 'the praetorian compound' as distinct institutional actors with different authority over Marcellinus's fate. The key political fact — that proceedings are being handled by 'the count's household, not the proconsul,' which signals that the ordinary legal process is being bypassed — is the whole point of the sentence, but a general reader cannot catch it without understanding the jurisdictional difference. - **Suggestion:** One functional gloss inside the dispatch letter resolves it without changing the prose's register.

- **After:** `The proceedings are being managed by men of the count's household, not by the proconsul — which is to say outside the ordinary procedure of the courts, and without appeal.`

### SUGGESTION (3)

**1. [Developmental Editor] opening mechanics** (Paragraph 1, sentence 1)
- **Issue:** The opening sentence embeds a long parenthetical on the day-name convention before establishing who Mark is or what his return means: 'Mark came back on the Wednesday — the middle of the week, in the new convention Augustine had taken to using for the day-names since the year of the courier...' This is texture that slows a chapter's first sentence below its proper entry speed. The parenthetical is interesting but it is not load-bearing here. The sentence's real engine is 'Mark came back' and 'he was thinner' and 'he was smiling.' Let those carry the opening.
- **Suggestion:** Move the day-name convention note to the second or third sentence, or drop it if it is not plot-relevant. Open with the arrival and its visible facts.
- **Before:** `Mark came back on the Wednesday — the middle of the week, in the new convention Augustine had taken to using for the day-names since the year of the courier — on a coastal ship out of Ostia...`
- **After:** `Mark came back on a Wednesday in the last week of January, on a coastal ship out of Ostia that had touched at Carthage on the Sunday. He arrived at the basilica gate at the second hour of the afternoon, on foot, with the leather satchel under his outer tunic. He was thinner than he had been in October. He was also smiling.`

**2. [Immersive Experience Editor] sensory writing — model to expand** (Third section — paragraph beginning 'He stood. He went to the window.')
- **Issue:** The sentence 'the light coming off the harbour at the slant the harbour gave it in autumn, the small particular wind off the sea that started at this hour and ran until evening' is the best sensory writing in the chapter. It is specific, seasonal, and felt. It shows what this prose is capable of. It is also one of the shortest sensory passages and arrives at the moment of highest emotional charge — where the reader most needs to be in the body with Augustine — and then ends immediately. The window moment is three sentences. It should be five.
- **Suggestion:** Let the harbour view do more work at this critical moment. Augustine is absorbing the news of his friend's arrest and probable execution while looking at the sea. Two more sentences grounding him in the physical specifics of that view — the actual quality of the light, a ship on the water, a smell — would make this the chapter's most immersive passage and give the subsequent letter-writing its proper weight as return from the edge.
- **Before:** `He went to the window. He pushed the shutter further open. The afternoon was the afternoon of the third week of September, which was the afternoon a North African coastal town gave at this hour of the year — the light coming off the harbour at the slant the harbour gave it in autumn, the small particular wind off the sea that started at this hour and ran until evening. He looked at the harbour for a count.`
- **After:** `He went to the window. He pushed the shutter further open. The afternoon was the afternoon of the third week of September — the light coming off the harbour at the slant the harbour gave it in autumn, the small particular wind off the sea that started at this hour and ran until evening. Below the harbour wall a trading vessel was being worked in by oarsmen who knew the channel. He watched them. The wind brought the smell of brine and the smell of whatever was burning in the lower town. He stood until the trading vessel had tied. Then he went back to the desk.`

**3. [Accessibility Editor] Jargon Management — Untranslated Latin Title** (Section 1 (January 412), Mark's return conversation)
- **Issue:** 'I read the four books of Cicero's *De Officiis* I had taken with me twice through' — the title is not translated. 'On Duties' would take three characters in brackets. The untranslated title is a small thing, but it is one of several small accumulations that slightly raise the register above target. The detail itself — that Mark read it twice and it changed how he thinks about clerical duty — is strong characterization and worth keeping.
- **Suggestion:** Translate in context or in brackets.
- **Before:** `I read the four books of Cicero's *De Officiis* I had taken with me twice through during the wait`
- **After:** `I read the four books of Cicero's *De Officiis* — *On Duties* — I had taken with me twice through during the wait`
