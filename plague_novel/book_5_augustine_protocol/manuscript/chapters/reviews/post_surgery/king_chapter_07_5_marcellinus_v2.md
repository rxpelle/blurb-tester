## Voice & Narrative Persona (Grade: C+)

This is accomplished, disciplined prose with a strong internal logic — but judged against King's core standard for narrative voice, it fails the central test: there is no storyteller in the room. King's narrators are people. They lean in. They say 'here's the thing you need to understand' and 'I'm telling you this because it matters.' This narrator has been deliberately erased, replaced by a kind of late-Roman administrative camera that records without editorializing, observes without feeling, and never once addresses the reader directly. The style is consistent — that counts for something — but consistency of style is not the same as presence of person. The closest the narrator comes to having an opinion is the closing coda on the Marcellinus dedication: 'The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years.' That sentence has a narrator in it — someone who has stepped back, taken the long view, and is telling you what it means. It's the best line in the chapter. The rest of the chapter is filed, not told. The italicized dialogue without quotation marks is a literary affectation King would specifically name: it creates distance between reader and character precisely when you need closeness. The procedural sentence-stacking in the opening paragraph ('on a coastal ship out of Ostia that had touched at Carthage on the Sunday and had come down the harbour road in two days... with the leather satchel under his outer tunic where it had been when he had left, and with a small additional cloth bag') is the kind of thing that looks like voice but is actually the absence of voice — information delivery dressed up as style. The emotional core of this chapter — Augustine learning his friend has been executed before his letters could arrive — is genuinely powerful, but we reach it through the narrator's silence, not through their presence. King would say: the reader needs someone to go through this with. Give them a guide who feels something.

### Good Examples
> 'the conversation of three men who had known each other across long distances for some years and who had agreed, for the hour they had together in the small refectory at Tagaste in the heat of late June, not to speak about the death that was moving through the body of the one of them who would, in the foreseeable accounting of the next decade, not be at this kind of conversation for many further sittings' — the narrator almost steps forward here; the phrase 'the death that was moving through the body' has a teller's judgment in it, not just a recorder's transcription

> 'The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years.' — five words and the narrator finally exists; this is the only moment in the chapter where a human intelligence is visibly behind the sentence making a choice about what it means

> 'He worked until the lamp went out.' — earned, clean, final; the narrator trusts the image and stops; King would keep this exactly as written

### Needs Improvement
> 'Mark came back on the Wednesday — the middle of the week, in the new convention Augustine had taken to using for the day-names since the year of the courier — on a coastal ship out of Ostia that had touched at Carthage on the Sunday and had come down the harbour road in two days.' — King's first rule: hook the reader or lose them; this opening sentence is administrative intake, not storytelling; a narrator with personality would have started with what mattered and let the logistics come later

> The italicized dialogue throughout — '*Father. The deacon Mark.*' / '*Father.*' / '*Mark. Sit.*' — the absence of quotation marks is a literary affectation that signals 'serious fiction' rather than serving the reader; it puts a pane of glass between us and the characters at every exchange; King would say this is the writer showing you the frame instead of disappearing into the picture

> 'He had been corresponding with Marcellinus for two years and three months. He had been writing the City of God to him for two years and one month. He had a letter from Marcellinus on his desk at this hour that was three weeks old and that he had been intending to reply to that afternoon.' — three consecutive sentences of pure timeline accounting; a narrator with personality would have given us one of these facts and then told us what it felt like, not filed all three in sequence

### Suggestions
- Find three places where the narrator can step forward and editorialize — not to explain the history, but to tell us what Augustine's situation means; the Marcellinus execution section is the obvious place; let the narrator say something like: 'The letters had already gone out. They were already too late when he sealed them.' King would give the reader the gut-punch directly, not let them work it out from the timeline
- Cut or replace the italicized-dialogue affectation with standard quotation marks; the spare dialogue itself is good — the punctuation convention is unnecessary signal-flagging that says 'literary' before the reader has a chance to feel anything
- Vary sentence length at emotional peaks; the chapter uses the same long procedural sentence regardless of whether it's describing a receipt being filed or a man dying; King's prose accelerates and fragments at moments of emotional weight — 'He read the dispatch through. He set it on the desk beside Marcellinus's last letter. He sat with it.' is almost there, but the rest of the Marcellinus section needs the same compression; when the news lands, the sentences should shorten until they're almost nothing
- Give the narrator one explicit, direct address to the reader somewhere in the chapter — not necessarily a 'you,' but a moment where the teller's presence is unmistakable; the Pinianus coda ('Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer') is the closest thing to this and it works well; the chapter needs two or three more moments like it where a human intelligence is visibly making decisions about what we're told and when

## Deep POV & Interior Monologue (Grade: C+)

This chapter makes a deliberate stylistic gamble: it writes like a chronicle, stripped and austere, with the flat declarative authority of ancient hagiography. That gamble produces sentences of real beauty and historical texture. But King's question for Deep POV is blunt — are we behind the character's eyes? And the honest answer is: mostly no. The chapter watches Augustine from the outside, records his behavior with documentary precision, and offers interior access in rationed glimpses rather than sustained immersion. The filtering verbs pile up ('Augustine noted,' 'He thought about,' 'He thought, for a small period, about whether'). Thoughts are reported rather than rendered — we're told Augustine thought of Marcellinus, but we get a biographical summary, not a mind working through grief in real time. The psychic distance holds at medium-to-far for the majority of the chapter, with two significant exceptions that prove the author knows exactly how to breach it when they choose to. The dual-listening passage — 'He listened with the part of himself that was a bishop... and he listened with the part of himself that was a man' — is the chapter's interior heartbeat: a consciousness genuinely divided, doing two incompatible things at once, and we feel both. The final disc meditation, with its accumulation of the dead and the living, lands. But the Tagaste section is almost entirely external — we watch Pinianus die by degrees as if through glass. The Marcellinus arrest sequence gives us four letters drafted and a harbour view and 'He did not move for a long count,' which is beautiful restraint but not interior experience. The chapter's discipline is real and the style is earned. The cost is that we understand Augustine intellectually without inhabiting him viscerally. King would say: the bronze box is your horror object, the disc is your tell, and you've handled both at arm's length when you should have your hands shaking.

### Good Examples
> 'He listened with the part of himself that was a bishop receiving a successful courier's report and he listened with the part of himself that was a man hearing the closing seal of an operation he had set in motion in October the consequences of which the courier did not know and would not, on Augustine's intention, ever know. The two parts of himself listened to the same sentences. The two parts heard the sentences differently.' — The chapter's single best interior moment. A split consciousness rendered in motion, not described from outside. This is deep POV: we feel the gap between what Mark hears and what Augustine hears in the same room.

> 'He thought of Marcellinus. He thought of Pinianus at Tagaste. He thought of Mark... He thought of Stephen... He thought of his mother at Ostia and of the words she had said in the second-last sentence of her life. He had been the carrier for twenty-six years. He would be the carrier for whatever years remained.' — The accumulation lands. The anaphora works as interior rhythm, not rhetorical device. The final two sentences are pure rendered interiority — not 'he thought that he had been the carrier' but the thought itself, arriving flat and certain the way a man accepts something he has always known.

> 'The dedication of the City of God was *to my dearest son Marcellinus*. He did not change it.' — Two sentences of exterior fact that carry enormous interior weight because the chapter has built to them. The restraint is earned here. King calls this the iceberg — the eight-ninths below the surface doing the work.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He bowed cleanly and stood. *Father.* *Mark. Sit.* Eraclius, send for hot water and bread. The deacon will eat.' — The entire Mark arrival sequence is conducted at full exterior distance. We get the bureaucratic exchange but nothing of what Augustine is feeling while it happens. What does it cost him to receive this young man who carried letters whose consequences he orchestrated and whose ignorance he is sustaining? The chapter gives us ceremony when it should give us the inside of ceremony.

> 'He thought, for a small period, about whether to take down the bronze box from the upper shelf and check the disc. He decided not to.' — Double-filtered thought. 'He thought about whether' is a filtering phrase wrapped around another filtering phrase. King's fix: cut the scaffolding. Give us the thought: *The bronze box was on the shelf. He left it there.* Or go inside it — what is the temptation? What does he fear finding? 'He decided not to' is a closed door when the door should be open.

> 'He went to the window. He pushed the shutter further open. The afternoon was the afternoon of the third week of September... He looked at the harbour for a count.' — Surface behavior reported as behavior. What does he see when he looks at the harbour? What hits him? The 'small particular wind off the sea' is lovely but it's landscape, not consciousness. Marcellinus is in a cell in Carthage and Augustine is watching the harbour — that gap should be the whole interior of this moment, not a stage direction followed by a count.

> 'Augustine listened. He listened... Mark, on the other side of the desk, heard nothing of the difference; Mark heard the sentences he was reciting and the bishop receiving them.' — The observation about Mark is astute but it arrives as authorial commentary rather than Augustine's perception in the moment. 'Augustine noted' and its cousins ('Mark heard nothing of the difference') are the narrator telling us what Augustine knows instead of being Augustine knowing it. The psychic distance is medium when it should be zero.

### Suggestions
- Cut every instance of 'he thought about' and replace it with the thought itself. 'He thought about Marcellinus. Marcellinus was forty-five' should become 'Marcellinus was forty-five' — the mind going directly to the fact. The reader understands we're in Augustine's head; the filtering phrase only distances us from it.
- The disc scenes need one sustained interior breach. Right now both disc scenes give us behavior (took down the box, held it, set it back) without the experience of holding it. What does Augustine feel when the disc is cold? What does cold mean to him specifically, after twenty-six years of carrying this thing? King's question: what is the worst thought he has in that moment? Give us that thought unmediated.
- The Tagaste section watches Pinianus from outside when it should be inside Augustine watching Pinianus. Pinianus is dying and Augustine knows it and the conversation is deliberately about small things. The interior of that — what it costs to speak of the inheritance dispute when you are watching a man compress to his death — is the chapter's emotional center and it is currently offscreen. One sustained paragraph of close interiority during that 'hour of small things' conversation would make the whole Tagaste section land differently.
- The draft-four-letters sequence after the Marcellinus dispatch is the chapter's most distant passage. We watch Augustine draft letters and are told what they contain in summary. But we're told almost nothing of what is happening to him as he writes. The letter to Marcellinus in the praetorian compound is the emotional detonator of the chapter — yet we get 'He wrote it as a man wrote to a friend who was in a cell on a charge that might or might not end in execution' rather than the actual interior of writing that letter. Pull the camera inside that letter-writing. Make us feel what it is to write about a bad knee to a man who might be heading for the headsman.
- Develop Augustine's unique mental voice more consistently. The dual-listening passage has it — that precise, legalistic interiority ('on Augustine's intention,' 'on the present accounting of the matter') applied to the experience of feeling two things at once. That voice is the chapter's most distinctive feature and it appears in full for perhaps three paragraphs. It should be the temperature of the whole chapter's interior, not a cameo. When Augustine sees Pinianus fold into the chair, what does that juridical-theological mind do with it? Give us the mind working, not just the eyes watching.

## Backstory & Characterization (Grade: B+)

This chapter is doing something genuinely difficult — revealing character through restraint, ritual, and small physical action in a world where the stakes are existential but the surface is procedural. At its best, the characterization is quietly devastating. The changed bow, Pinianus walking to the chapel without letting anyone see his face, Melania checking the mortar joints with the eye of a woman who paid for every stone — these are King-level moments. Specific, physical, irreducible. The problem is structural: the chapter earns its characters in the action scenes and then loses nerve in the connective tissue, reverting to resume dumps and authorial intrusion. The Marcellinus section collapses badly. For two paragraphs we get Augustine's grief shown through paralysis and the iron pen going back to work — perfect. Then a biographical data block lands like a Wikipedia sidebar: his age, his title, the year of the conference, the nature of the friendship, the dedication. Everything we needed to feel has just been explained instead. That's the core failure. The authorial break at Pinianus — 'Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer' — is a second serious wound. King never exits the dream to tell you how it ends. You're writing a thriller. Trust the reader to stay in the room. The disc is working as mystery; the bronze box returns three times and earns its weight. Augustine's inner divisions are observed economically. But the chapter needs surgery on its worst structural habit: stopping to explain who people are the moment they become important.

### Good Examples
> 'He did not, Augustine noted, bow with the small forward step that nervousness had given him in October. He bowed cleanly and stood.' — Pure King. One specific physical detail reveals both men: Mark has changed, Augustine notices everything. Three months of journey compressed into a hinge in a spine.

> 'He walked across the dust to the chapel under the noon sun on his own legs... he did not, in the walking across the dust, let any of the three of them see his face.' — Character under pressure revealed entirely through action. No interior access. The dignity of a dying man is earned in what he refuses to show, not what he thinks about himself.

> 'a man Augustine had known at Tagaste since both of them were boys, who had been the son of the carter who had owned the cart that had carried Monnica's body from Ostia to the harbour for the African ship she had not in the end taken' — Decades of biography delivered in a foreman's identity. Augustine's whole past — his mother's death, the failed journey, the Africa he carries — surfaces through a minor character who doesn't even have a speaking line. This is the chapter at its absolute best.

> 'She was not praying; she was examining the work of the masons and the carpenters with the careful eye of a woman who had spent her family's wealth on every stone and was making sure the stones were what she had paid for.' — Melania characterized in one sentence. We know her class, her disposition, her relationship to this project, and the irony of her religiosity without being told any of it.

> 'He took up the iron pen. He went back to the fourth book.' — After receiving news of Marcellinus's execution, this is the characterization. No grieving paragraph. No interior monologue. He goes back to work. That's who Augustine is.

### Needs Improvement
> 'Marcellinus was forty-five. He had been the imperial commissioner who had chaired the conference at Carthage in 411 that had ended the Donatist crisis on the terms favourable to the Catholic side. He had been a Christian of the careful steady kind that Augustine had recognised, the first time he had met him in the spring of 411, as the kind of Christian who would, in the long arithmetic of Augustine's correspondence with imperial officials over twenty years, become a friend rather than a contact.' — Resume characterization at its most damaging. We've just watched Augustine go still with a four-line dispatch in his hands — that's drama. Then we get a dossier. This is the chapter's worst moment. King would have given us the friendship through a single specific memory, not a biography.

> 'Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer. He lived through the autumn and the winter and the next spring and the next summer and the autumn after, and he died eventually at Jerusalem in the year Augustine would not be alive to know about. But Augustine, on the inland road back from Tagaste in the slow heat of June, did not know that.' — The author exiting the dream to correct the reader's assumptions is fatal to immersion. King's rule: never tell us what happens after the scene. Stay in the car. The power of Augustine carrying that last conversation on the road back is entirely real; the 'in fact' paragraph detonates it.

> 'The two parts of himself listened to the same sentences. The two parts heard the sentences differently.' — Telling us about Augustine's divided nature instead of trusting the scene to show it. The Mark scene has already dramatized this split through the report itself. The sentences after it are unnecessary explanation of what just happened.

### Suggestions
- Cut the Marcellinus biography block entirely. Replace it with one sensory memory — something from the spring of 411, one specific moment between them — then let his paralysis and the four letters do the work of characterizing the friendship. We need to feel the loss before we understand its dimensions.
- Delete 'Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer' through 'he carried the conversation with him on the road back.' Keep only the final sentence. Let the reader believe what Augustine believed. That belief is the story. The correction is the author's anxiety, not the reader's need.
- The 'two parts of himself' sentence can be cut clean. The scene earns the division without naming it — trust the machinery you've already built.
- Mark's De Officiis detail is gold — 'which I have, in the doing of it, found has changed the way I think about the duties of a clerical office' — but it gets one line and is dropped. King would pay this off somewhere. Let Augustine and Mark have that quiet hour. Character is revealed in what people choose to discuss when nothing is required of them.
- The disc needs one more beat — not explanation, but weight. Three appearances and we understand it's charged with meaning Augustine won't examine. That's working. But on the final appearance, after Marcellinus's death, consider giving the disc a single physical difference — temperature, sound, something — that Augustine notices and refuses to interpret. Let the reader feel the cost of not knowing what it means.

## Setting & Sensory Detail (Grade: C+)

The chapter operates in a spare, documentary register that matches its subject — a bishop's administrative world of receipts, dispatches, and careful Latin. That restraint is a legitimate artistic choice. But King's standard isn't maximalism; it's precision. Every setting needs at least one or two details sharp enough to nail the reader to the floor of that room, that road, that building site. This chapter has genuine instincts — 'The disc was cold,' the seasonal brazier, the particular autumn wind off the harbour — but these islands of sensory life are scattered across a vast cognitive desert. We spend most of the chapter in Augustine's head, not in his body, not in his room, not in fifth-century North Africa. Hippo Regius, one of the great cities of late antiquity, sitting on a Mediterranean bay with all the noise and stink and heat that implies, barely registers as a physical place. The writing room where perhaps two-thirds of the chapter unfolds has a desk, a shelf, a brazier, and parchment. That's it. No smell of lamp oil and old parchment, no sound of the harbour coming through the shutter, no feel of cold stone underfoot in January. The monastery section at Tagaste has 'the dust of the building site' and twenty workmen who stop working — but we don't hear the hammers go quiet, don't smell the lime mortar, don't feel the noon sun pressing down on cut stone. The journey on the inland road in early African summer is five days long and essentially textureless. The chapter's sensory world is almost entirely visual and thermal, with smell and sound nearly absent. What saves it from a lower grade is that its few sensory choices genuinely earn their place — particularly 'The disc was cold,' which does triple duty as physical fact, emotional weight, and thematic anchor — and the late-summer harbour light passage, which briefly delivers the specific geographic texture the rest of the chapter withholds.

### Good Examples
> 'The disc was cold.' — Repeated three times across the chapter. A single tactile detail that works as a motif, an emotional register, and a character revelation. Cold metal in a warm hand. The restraint of the repetition is exactly right. This is the chapter's best sensory move.

> 'the afternoon of the third week of September, which was the afternoon a North African coastal town gave at this hour of the year — the light coming off the harbour at the slant the harbour gave it in autumn, the small particular wind off the sea that started at this hour and ran until evening.' — 'The small particular wind' is the kind of specific-but-unnameable detail King loves. The slant of harbour light. This passage briefly puts you in the place.

> 'the brazier was not throwing what it threw in November.' — Concrete, seasonal, double-duty. Tells us the room's temperature, the date, and the domestic order of Augustine's household in a single clause. No abstractions.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He went to the window. He pushed the shutter further open... He looked at the harbour for a count.' — This is the chapter's biggest missed opportunity. Augustine has just learned his friend may be executed. He goes to the window. He looks at the harbour. And we get nothing — no smell of the sea, no sound of the docks, no physical sensation of the shutter in his hands, no image of what Hippo's harbour looked like at that hour. King would have put us in that window. The harbour should hit us the way the cold disc hits us. Instead we get the action of looking with no content of what's seen.

> 'They came to Tagaste on the Saturday afternoon... Twenty workmen were on the site when Augustine and Possidius came up the cart-track from the road. The workmen stopped what they were doing when they saw the bishop's small party.' — A building site in North Africa in June, and we get none of it. No heat haze off the cut stone. No sound of the hammers going silent. No smell of sawdust or mortar. The workmen stopping is a visual note, not a sensory one. The whole monastery arrival is rendered from ten feet above the scene.

> 'The journey took five days... he rode the small mule at the slow pace the small mule preferred.' — Five days on the inland road in early African summer heat that 'had begun three weeks early and was already harder than the previous summer.' Five days, and we get not a single sensory beat of the road — no dust in the mouth, no smell of hot stone and dry grass, no sound of the mule's hooves, no the way the heat sits on you differently at the second hour than at the fifth. The heat is described analytically, via farmers' reports. We never feel it.

### Suggestions
- Give the writing room a smell. Parchment, lamp oil, old wax, cold stone — whatever combination is right for a bishop's scriptorium in a North African winter. One smell planted early will make every subsequent scene in that room feel inhabited.
- The harbour window scene after Augustine learns of Marcellinus's arrest is a major emotional beat. Let the harbour do work. What does he actually see? What does he hear? Is there a ship being unloaded, a smell of fish and salt, the cry of a gull? Ground the emotional paralysis in something physical the reader can touch.
- Sound is almost entirely absent from the chapter. 'The hinges sounded' is the only ambient audio in thirty-plus scenes. The building site, the refectory, the chapel — all silent. Add one specific ambient sound per location, not generic ('hammers'), but particular ('the hollow knock of a mallet against green wood, stopping').
- The foreman's parenthetical about Monnica's body — 'the son of the carter who had owned the cart that had carried Monnica's body from Ostia to the harbour' — is a brilliant telling detail buried where it can't breathe. Give that man a face. Give Augustine a beat of recognition. Let the setting suddenly carry the weight of a twenty-year-old memory. That's King's 'telling detail' doing exactly what it should do — making the place collapse time.
- Pinianus walking across the dust to the chapel 'under the noon sun on his own legs' — this is close to working, but the noon sun in June in North Africa should be a physical presence, not a location marker. Let it press on them. Let it be specific and merciless in the way that makes Pinianus's walk mean what it means.

## Tension & Dread Building (Grade: B+)

This chapter builds dread the hard way — through accumulation, not event. The architecture is correct: the bronze box as ritual object, the scrubbed wax tablet, the gap between what Mark carries and what it means, the letters sent to a dead man. That's the craft. But King's lesson is that mystery without texture is just fog. The disc appears three times and is cold three times and we never know what warmth would mean. That opacity works against dread rather than for it — we feel *curiosity* not *wrongness*. The Marcellinus section is the chapter's best work: four careful letters drafted to a man already beheaded, the nine-day lag, the iron pen going back to the fourth book. That is genuine dread. The dramatic irony is perfectly placed and the weight of 'The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years' lands like a stone dropped from height. The Tagaste section loses tension mid-passage — the Pinianus undercut ('did not, in fact, die that summer') is an interesting narrative choice but it bleeds the air out of the scene at exactly the moment the scene should be tightening. The single most sinister line in the chapter — 'The man is in the cell at Sketis where he will finish' — is also the most isolated. It needs a root earlier in the text for the reader to feel what Augustine has done. Right now it reads as dark but not yet dreadful. The chapter is doing something real and hard, and the bones are right. The flesh needs one or two more points of wrongness.

### Good Examples
> 'The disc was cold.' — Three occurrences, same four words, same ritual. This is King's ordinary-made-uncanny: the object that means something Augustine will not say, checked against a standard (warmth) that is never met. Repetition as dread.

> 'He thought he was going home from his last conversation with Pinianus. He carried the conversation with him on the road back.' — The dramatic irony here is clean and painful. The reader knows Augustine is wrong. The weight of the carrying lands because the wrongness is *his*, not ours.

> 'The four letters went out with the morning post. Marcellinus and his brother Apringius were executed at Carthage on the thirteenth of September — the day before the senior bishop had written...' — The paragraph break between sending and the execution date is perfectly placed. This is the quiet moment *after* the storm the reader didn't know had already passed. Excellent structural dread.

> 'The man is in the cell at Sketis where he will finish.' Then the tablet scraped twice. — The double erasure is the tell. Augustine doesn't trust one scraping. The second scraping is guilt made physical.

> 'He worked until the lamp went out.' — Ending on the extinguished lamp is right. The chapter has been accumulating small darknesses and this closes the aperture completely. Invisible prose doing visible work.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He took out the disc. The disc was cold.' — Three times we check the disc, three times it's cold, three times it goes back. But we never have even one detail that tells us what the disc IS — what it represents, where it came from, what warmth would mean. King gives you *just enough* strangeness to let your imagination do the worst work. Here the disc is so opaque it stays abstract. The reader can't fear what they can't picture.

> 'Pinianus did not, in fact, die that summer. He lived through the autumn and the winter...' — Undercutting the deathbed scene with a prospective correction interrupts the dread at its peak. The reader has just watched Pinianus walk to his chapel under a killing sun. Then the narration steps in and says: actually, no. The correction is interesting as literary technique but it drains the tension that was working.

> 'The man is in the cell at Sketis where he will finish' — This is the chapter's darkest moment — Augustine has condemned someone through those seven letters — but the man has no name, no prior mention, no established weight. A nameless man in an unnamed crime disappearing into a desert cell should be horrifying. Right now it registers as dark but not as dread, because we have nothing to anchor the horror to. Plant a name in the October section. One sentence. Then when this line comes, the reader knows what it costs.

### Suggestions
- Give the disc one specific wrong detail beyond temperature — its weight, its material, a mark on it, something Augustine does *not* look at when he opens the box. Let the reader's imagination do the rest. Right now it has no texture to grab.
- In the October section with Mark, plant the name of the man in the desert — even obliquely, even as 'the matter of the desert' resolving a named person. Then when 'The man is in the cell at Sketis where he will finish' appears, the reader connects it. Right now the line is a door with no frame.
- The Pinianus undercut ('did not, in fact, die that summer') kills the scene's tension. Consider moving the factual correction to after the return journey — let the reader sit with Augustine's grief on the road back before the narration corrects itself. The correction can still be there; it just shouldn't arrive before we've felt the weight.
- The moment Augustine reads Marcellinus's last letter — the one about the third book's qualification — and sets it beside the arrest dispatch is the chapter's emotional center. Give it one more sentence of interiority. Not much. King would give you one specific thought Augustine has about that letter that he will never send the reply to. The specificity of the unsent reply is where the dread lives.
- The ending is right but the lamp going out is slightly expected. Consider: he works until the lamp goes out — then one more line, something small and specific about the dark, or the cold, or the disc on the shelf. The chapter has earned a final note of wrongness. Let it land one beat past where it currently stops.

## Dialogue & Voice (Grade: B-)

This chapter plays an unusual game: nearly all the dialogue is stripped to its bones, formatted without tags, formalized to the point of ritual. It's a deliberate stylistic choice that mostly works — and in two scenes, works brilliantly. The Mark/Augustine exchange about the letters is a masterclass in subtext: two men talking about everything except the thing, one holding power, one sensing its edge. The Pinianus farewell is the emotional peak of the entire chapter, achieved in about four lines. What drags the dialogue down is a near-total uniformity of register: the foreman, Melania, Mark, and Augustine all speak in the same clipped, subordinate-clause-stripped formality. King's rule — every character has a verbal fingerprint, a specific way they come at the world — is violated here. Melania gets a long stretch of dialogue that is essentially a construction status report; it's the one place in the chapter where the author puts information in a character's mouth rather than letting the character speak. Mark's formal oral delivery of the courier's report is interesting as period texture, but it reads more like a memo than a young deacon actually talking. The chapter also leans too hard on narration to do the emotional work that dialogue should be doing — we're told about Augustine's two modes of listening rather than letting the exchange with Mark enact it.

### Good Examples
> 'I will not, on the present trajectory, see you again.' / 'I will write.' / 'Write to her also. She will need the letters more than she will say.' — Three exchanges, no wasted syllable. Pinianus says he's dying, Augustine offers the one thing he can, Pinianus redirects to Melania. Nothing is named. Everything is present. This is how King handles death talk: characters talk around the hole.

> 'The substance of the letters is the kind of substance that a senior bishop carries and a junior deacon does not. The carrying of the letters by a deacon who does not know the substance is the proper division... You have not been told because the not-being-told is what permits a deacon of your office to do this work without complication.' — Augustine answers Mark's question without answering it. He uses the architecture of explanation to close a door. This is power through speech, rendered precisely.

> 'Aurelius. The lady is in the chapel... The husband is in the small refectory. He is not, on the deputy abbess's word, well.' — The foreman's hedging — 'on the deputy abbess's word' — is the mark of a man delivering bad news with deniability. One clause does real character work.

### Needs Improvement
> 'All seven delivered to the named recipients in person. The waiting was convenient at four of the seven; written acknowledgments are here. The other three I waited for an oral acknowledgment because the recipient was either not at the office on the day or was not, on the recipient's representation to me, in a position to write an acknowledgment that day.' — This is a memo. No twenty-four-year-old deacon, however drilled, speaks in completed bureaucratic sentences at the end of a three-month sea crossing. Even one false start, one place where he almost says 'I wasn't sure if—' before correcting himself, would make this a person instead of a report.

> 'The choir wing is behind. It will be finished in October. The lay sisters' wing is finished. There are six women in residence; there will be ten by Christmas. The deputy abbess is from the senior bishop's house at Carthage; she has been at this kind of work for fourteen years.' — This is the clearest 'as you know Bob' problem in the chapter. Melania is giving Augustine a briefing he has no reason to receive as a list. She's conveying information to the reader through a character's mouth. King would break this up, let Augustine ask something, let Melania's anxiety or pride or exhaustion interrupt the sequence.

> 'I would, with your permission, like to discuss the De Officiis with you when there is a quiet hour.' — Written, not spoken. The phrase 'with your permission' is courtly formula and lands as such. The sentence has no breath in it. A young cleric at the end of a long journey, tentatively asking to talk books with the bishop he admires, should sound like a person screwing up courage, not reciting a petitionary form.

### Suggestions
- Give each character one verbal fingerprint that distinguishes them. Mark could have a slight tendency to over-explain when nervous — he's young, he worked hard, he wants credit. Melania could speak in short declarative bursts with a beat before she answers emotional questions. Pinianus could drop formal constructions entirely by the Tagaste scene, stripped down to nouns and verbs by illness. Right now they all sound like the same formally educated voice.
- The Melania status-report speech needs to become a conversation. Break it up. Let Augustine ask about the choir wing. Let him say Sophia's name before she does, showing he's been paying attention to her letters — that small beat of recognition would tell us more about their relationship than two paragraphs of narration.
- Trust your best subtext more and your narration less. The sentence 'The two parts of himself listened to the same sentences. The two parts heard the sentences differently' is doing work the exchange itself is already doing. The reader who followed the Mark scene doesn't need this explained. King's rule: if the dialogue is earning its keep, step back.
- Mark's oral reports are an opportunity the chapter doesn't take. He's been carrying these words for months. Let him slightly misquote one and then catch himself and correct it — one moment of human imprecision that shows us the weight of the carrying without any narration required.
- The Pinianus scene is nearly perfect. The one adjustment: 'I will not, on the present trajectory, see you again' — the phrase 'on the present trajectory' is the only moment in that scene where the author's voice leaks in. Pinianus is a dying sixty-year-old in a chair in North Africa in 412. Find his actual phrase for the same thought. Something blunter. Something that costs him more to say.

## Pacing & Structure (Grade: B+)

This chapter moves like a cathedral — deliberate, architecturally sound in its three-act scaffold (January/June/September), and capable of genuine power when the prose commits to restraint. The structural instinct is strong: each scene escalates mortality and consequence, from a courier mission completed cleanly, to a dying man walking across dust on his own legs, to the news that a friend was already dead before the letters of rescue reached the harbor. The Marcellinus section in particular demonstrates masterful pacing — the repetitive drumbeat of 'He took up a [second/third/fourth] sheet' functions as both procedural realism and emotional containment, grief disguised as duty. King would recognize that move immediately. But the chapter front-loads its worst structural problem: an opening paragraph that dumps date-methodology, ship itineraries, port schedules, and arrival minutiae before we have any reason to care. King's first law is that you earn complexity by giving the reader something to hold. This chapter makes the reader carry the freight first. The Tagaste section is also disproportionate — the foreman's genealogy, the building inventory, the chapel dimensions run long for a scene whose emotional payload (Pinianus refusing the chair, not letting them see his face) arrives efficiently on its own. Cut twenty percent of the approach and that scene breathes. The final Marcellinus movement, however, is nearly perfect in its pacing — the short declarative sentences land like blows delivered quietly, which is the hardest kind to land.

### Good Examples
> 'The letter was four lines.' — A sentence that stops the clock. Classic King compression. Everything before this was bureaucratic noise; this is the knife.

> 'He did not, in the walking across the dust, let any of the three of them see his face.' — This is the Tagaste scene's emotional summit. No adverbs, no exposition of his dignity. The restraint IS the meaning.

> 'He took up the iron pen. He went back to the fourth book.' — Devastating. The two shortest sentences in the chapter carry the most weight. Grief as action. King would put this in a lesson.

> 'The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years.' — Earns its place as the chapter's structural capstone. One sentence that makes the entire enterprise feel cosmically weighted without once reaching for it.

> The repetitive 'He took up a [number] sheet / He sealed the letter / He set it in the tray' — The drumbeat structure perfectly enacts Augustine's condition: duty as the only permissible form of feeling. The pacing IS the characterization.

### Needs Improvement
> 'Mark came back on the Wednesday — the middle of the week, in the new convention Augustine had taken to using for the day-names since the year of the courier — on a coastal ship out of Ostia that had touched at Carthage on the Sunday and had come down the harbour road in two days.' — This is the opening sentence. It explains the calendar system, tracks the ship route, and dates the journey in one breath, before we know or care who Mark is. King: 'A writer's job is to seduce, not instruct. Don't explain the filing system before we've met the man.'

> 'The foreman — a man Augustine had known at Tagaste since both of them were boys, who had been the son of the carter who had owned the cart that had carried Monnica's body from Ostia to the harbour for the African ship she had not in the end taken — came forward and bowed.' — Fascinating genealogy, catastrophically placed. This parenthetical stops a scene mid-arrival for a three-clause biographical footnote. The foreman says one line and disappears. Save it or cut it.

> 'the slow honest accounting that a man performed when he had committed to a thing and recognised that the committing required the keeping of a record' — This sentence explains the psychology of what Augustine is doing while he's doing it. King's rule: trust the action. If he's making secret copies in the middle of the night, we know it's a slow honest accounting. Don't name it.

> 'They spoke of the foundation and of Tagaste and of the inheritance dispute.' — The conversation summary is necessary but it sits inert. King would give us one concrete line from the actual conversation — something small and real — so we feel the duration rather than being told it happened. Right now this is a time-stamp, not a scene.

### Suggestions
- Blow up the opening paragraph. Drop us mid-motion — Mark at the gate, thinner, smiling, the satchel under his tunic — and let the date, the ship, the route come in fragments as context earns them. The chapter's first job is not to explain the calendar system.
- In the Tagaste section, cut the foreman's genealogy entirely or move it to a moment of reflection after Pinianus is settled. It kills the momentum of arrival. The site, the unfinished walls, and Melania at the altar are all you need.
- Vary sentence length more aggressively inside long passages. The prose currently runs at a consistent clause-length within paragraphs, which creates a liturgical hum that occasionally works but more often flattens tension. Punch a short sentence in where you need the reader to land. 'The disc was cold.' works. Use that technique more deliberately throughout.
- The bronze disc appears three times and is never explained within the chapter. If it's series-internal knowledge, it needs one anchoring phrase on first appearance so a reader encountering this chapter isn't adrift. King is meticulous about giving the reader just enough to trust the mystery.
- The final paragraph of the Tagaste section — the correction of Augustine's expectation about Pinianus dying — is a structural masterstroke, but 'He thought he was going home from his last conversation with Pinianus' slightly over-explains the irony. End instead on 'He carried the conversation with him on the road back' and let the preceding correction do all the work.

## Themes & Subtext (Grade: A-)

This chapter does something genuinely difficult: it embeds its themes inside objects, silences, and institutional routine so thoroughly that you feel the meaning before you can name it. The core thematic engine is the tension between what is recorded and what is erased, what is known and what must be withheld, what outlasts its moment and what is scrubbed clean. Augustine writes and scrapes the wax tablet twice. He keeps copies of letters he 'had not been prepared to' keep. He consigns a man to a cell at Sketis and never speaks of it again. Mark carries letters he can't open across the Mediterranean and back. The institutional machinery — receipts, seals, official Latin, the iron chest — runs alongside and against genuine human weight: Pinianus walking across the dust so no one sees his face, Marcellinus's question about the third book that will never be answered. The theme of complicity and necessary ignorance is never stated but is present in every exchange, every scraping of the tablet. Where the chapter stumbles is at the far end of its emotional range, when the author occasionally trusts the reader slightly less than the material deserves and names what the prose has already shown. The bronze disc, however, is a perfect symbolic object: recurring, unexplained, always cold. It does more thematic work than any explicit statement in the chapter. The dedication outlasting Marcellinus by a thousand years lands because the chapter has earned it — we feel the cost before the author tells us the arithmetic.

### Good Examples
> 'The disc was cold. / He held it for a count. He set it back. He closed the box. He returned the box to the shelf.' — The disc appears three times and is never explained. Its coldness is the negative answer to an unspoken question about faith, confirmation, the absence of signs. King would love this: a concrete object carrying the full weight of a man's spiritual condition without a word of explanation.

> 'He had been the carrier for twenty-six years. He would be the carrier for whatever years remained.' — The carrier theme arrives here without fanfare, embedded in the ordinary motion of returning a box to a shelf. It recontextualizes everything that has come before without announcing itself.

> 'The conversation was the conversation of three men who had known each other across long distances for some years and who had agreed, for the hour they had together in the small refectory at Tagaste in the heat of late June, not to speak about the death that was moving through the body of the one of them...' — The subtext is the text. Death is present in the room as a fourth party, and the reader feels it more for being named only as the thing they have agreed not to name.

> 'He went back to the fourth book.' — After receiving word of Marcellinus's execution. No internal monologue, no grief scene. The return to work is the grief scene. The work continuing is the theme. This is the kind of restraint that makes the reader do the emotional labor.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He thought of Marcellinus. He thought of Pinianus at Tagaste. He thought of Mark, who was at the basilica at this hour... He thought of Stephen at the cathedral end of the cloister... He thought of his mother at Ostia and of the words she had said in the second-last sentence of her life.' — This is Augustine's thematic checklist rendered as interiority. It does what the rest of the chapter avoids: it tells the reader what to feel by cataloguing what matters. King would cut this to one image and trust the weight already accumulated.

> 'The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years.' — This line is earned but then over-explained by what follows: 'The man it was made to was dead before the second of the twenty-two books had been finished.' One of these sentences is doing the work. The other is explaining the work. Cut the explanation and let the reader sit with the arithmetic alone.

### Suggestions
- Cut the final meditation inventory — 'He thought of Marcellinus. He thought of Pinianus... He thought of Mark... He thought of Stephen... He thought of his mother' — down to a single image, ideally the one that lands hardest (his mother at Ostia). The chapter has built sufficient weight. The list disperses it.
- Trust the disc entirely. Its coldness is already doing the work. Any temptation in revision to explain it, to give Augustine a thought about what warmth would have meant, should be resisted absolutely. Its power is its silence.
- The subtext of Augustine's moral compromise — 'the man is in the cell at Sketis where he will finish' — is the chapter's richest buried line and is handled beautifully. Consider whether one more oblique reference across the chapter's span would deepen it further, or whether the single appearance and single scraping is exactly right. The current restraint is close to perfect; be careful not to add.
- The moment Pinianus 'did not, in fact, die that summer' is the chapter's most interesting structural risk — the author steps outside the present tense of experience to tell us the future. It works because it embodies the thematic irony (Augustine carries the wrong grief home), but the paragraph explaining what Augustine 'did not know' slightly over-explains the irony. End at 'He thought he was going home from his last conversation with Pinianus' and let the reader feel the dramatic irony without the author confirming it.

## Cultural Specificity & Time Period (Grade: A-)

This chapter is doing something genuinely rare in historical fiction: it earns its period through specificity rather than decoration. The author isn't draping togas on modern people. The institutional hierarchy (deacon, senior bishop, praetorian compound, consistory at Ravenna), the material culture (bone stylus, iron pen, wax tablet, parchment), the reading material (*De Officiis*, which is exactly what a junior cleric in 412 CE would be grinding through on a nine-day wait), the political machinery of late Rome — Heraclian's revolt, the count of Africa bypassing the proconsul, the imperial commissioner role — all of this is the right texture. The historical figures are real: Marcellinus (executed 413), Melania the Younger and Pinianus at Tagaste, Possidius of Calama, Eraclius of Hippo. The dedication of the *City of God* to Marcellinus is historically accurate, as is the approximate dating of the books' drafting. Most impressively, the foreman-as-son-of-Monnica's-carter is the kind of specific invented-but-plausible detail that makes a reader feel the weight of a continuous life rather than a set piece. Where the chapter pulls back from its own strength is in sensory texture — the physical world is occasionally described in category (brazier, lamp, parchment) rather than sensation — and in voice uniformity: Mark, Melania, Pinianus, and Eraclius all speak in the same careful formal register, which flattens the period rather than differentiating it. King's approach would be to find at least one voice that rubs against the grain of the others — the workman who swears, the deacon who stumbles. The mysterious bronze disc is also physically underspecified; repeated three times as 'cold,' it's doing heavy symbolic work with insufficient material grounding. But the bones of this are exceptional. This is a writer who has done the work.

### Good Examples
> 'the son of the carter who had owned the cart that had carried Monnica's body from Ostia to the harbour for the African ship she had not in the end taken' — a minor character defined entirely by his connection to Augustine's real history. Monnica died at Ostia in 387. The foreman has known Augustine since they were boys. This is what lived time feels like.

> 'I read the four books of Cicero's *De Officiis* I had taken with me twice through during the wait, which I had not done before, and which I have, in the doing of it, found has changed the way I think about the duties of a clerical office' — period-exact reading material with an interior response. This is the right book for this character in this year.

> 'The small particular wind off the sea that started at this hour and ran until evening' — the North African coastal geography felt as a lived daily rhythm, not as scenic description.

> 'The man is in the cell at Sketis where he will finish' — Sketis (Scetis) is the specific Egyptian desert monastic community, the real one, not 'a desert monastery.' One word that does enormous grounding work.

> 'The proceedings are being managed by men of the count's household, not by the proconsul' — this single clause carries the factional politics of late Roman Africa: Marinus, count of Africa, running extrajudicial executions through his own household to avoid the legal obstruction of the proconsul's court. Historically accurate and dramatically loaded.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The disc was cold' — repeated three times as the chapter's central object of mystery. After two repetitions we need more than temperature. What does it look like? Bronze? Stone? Carved? Plain? The coldness is a good motif; the physical vagueness undermines it. King would give us the weight, the surface, the smell of old metal.

> 'seven small folded acknowledgments — receipts in seven different hands, in seven different inks, with seven different seals or marks' — we're told seven different seals but never shown one. What did the wax look like on Sixtus's receipt? What was the mark of the master's deputy at Ostia? One specific seal would do the work of all seven.

> Every character speaks in the same careful, formal register — Mark, Melania, Pinianus, Eraclius. 'Father. The deacon Mark.' / 'Bishop. The chapel is what we agreed.' / 'Aurelius. I will go to the cell now.' The uniformity is a stylistic choice, but it erases class, education, and regional texture. In 412 CE North Africa, a Numidian workman and a Roman aristocrat did not occupy the same sentence rhythms. Even one voice that broke the pattern would make the others more legible as authentic period speech.

> 'He wrote it in the careful Latin of a senior bishop addressing an imperial official on a matter of personal weight' — we're told the register of the Latin three times in the letter-drafting sequence, but we never hear a line of any letter. After the excellent specificity of Marcellinus's four-line dispatch ('They are being held in the praetorian compound. The proceedings are being managed by men of the count's household'), reverting to description of Latin register rather than showing it is a missed opportunity.

### Suggestions
- Give the disc a physical description the first time Augustine handles it — not explanation, just the object: its weight, its size, whether it's worn smooth or marked, whether it has a smell. The cold will land harder against something the reader can hold.
- Differentiate at least one secondary voice. The foreman at Tagaste is a boy Augustine has known since childhood, a craftsman's son — let his two lines sound like a man who grew up at the bottom of Roman North Africa, not like the careful diaconal speech of Mark.
- In the letter-drafting sequence (the four letters after Marcellinus's arrest), let us hear one actual sentence from the letter to Marcellinus himself. Augustine is writing to a friend in a cell on a capital charge; the letter is described but not quoted. That letter is the emotional center of the scene and it stays behind glass.
- The 'new convention Augustine had taken to using for the day-names' is excellent historical texture — lean into this kind of observational specificity more. What does the basilica gate smell like in January? What do the seven receipts feel like — thick vellum, thin papyrus, different weights for different offices? King's rule: the specific is always more terrifying (and more true) than the general.
- Consider naming the senior bishop at Carthage (Aurelius of Carthage, Augustine's longtime correspondent and ally) at least once. The deliberate abstraction of 'the senior bishop' is consistent and purposeful, but one named mention would anchor the reader in the real network of North African Christianity without giving anything away.

## Prose Polish (King Polish) (Grade: B)

This prose has a deliberate, Latinate, liturgical rhythm that is its own style — and when it works, it works beautifully. The short declarative sentences that anchor each section ('The disc was cold.' 'He worked until the lamp went out.' 'The dedication remained the dedication.') are exactly right: punchy, earned, invisible. The Pinianus section is genuinely moving. The Marcellinus letter sequence builds dread the way good thriller prose should — through accumulation and restraint. But the writer has developed a set of tics that call attention to themselves and violate King's core rule: you should be reading the story, not watching the writer work. The biggest offender is the 'on [x]'s accounting/representation/intention/word' construction, which appears eight or more times and stops feeling like a character voice and starts feeling like a keyboard shortcut. The opening paragraph is a wreck of nested clauses that buries a simple fact. Several long sentences do the work of short ones badly. The 2nd draft = 1st draft minus 10% rule applies here: cut the parentheticals, kill the hedging constructions, trust the bones — because the bones are strong.

### Good Examples
> 'The disc was cold.' — Three words. Does the work of twenty. This is the whole chapter in miniature.

> 'He worked until the lamp went out.' — Perfect closing line. No adverb, no explanation, no author thumb on the scale. The reader fills the silence.

> 'He was fifty-seven and his right knee was, in this weather, the right knee of a man of seventy; he rode the small mule at the slow pace the small mule preferred and did not, in the five days, complain about the pace once.' — The compression of character here is excellent. One sentence does biography, physicality, and dignity.

> 'He did not, in the walking across the dust, let any of the three of them see his face.' — Restraint as characterization. King would underline this one.

> 'The dedication outlasted him by a thousand years.' — Clean. Devastating. No decoration needed.

> 'He thought of Marcellinus. He thought of Pinianus at Tagaste. He thought of Mark... He thought of his mother at Ostia...' — The litany rhythm is earned by this point and it lands like a bell struck slowly.

### Needs Improvement
> 'Mark came back on the Wednesday — the middle of the week, in the new convention Augustine had taken to using for the day-names since the year of the courier — on a coastal ship out of Ostia that had touched at Carthage on the Sunday and had come down the harbour road in two days.' — This sentence is where the reader almost bounces off the chapter. The parenthetical explains something that doesn't need explaining and the rest is a tangle. King would open with: 'Mark came back on a Wednesday, on a coastal ship out of Ostia.' Full stop. Let the scene build.

> 'He arrived at the basilica gate at the second hour of the afternoon, on foot, with the leather satchel under his outer tunic where it had been when he had left, and with a small additional cloth bag at his shoulder that he had not had when he had left.' — 'where it had been when he had left' and 'that he had not had when he had left' — the same construction twice in one sentence. This is six words doing the work of two each time. 'the satchel still under his tunic, a small cloth bag he hadn't had in October' gets the job done and moves.

> 'Augustine listened. He listened with the part of himself that was a bishop receiving a successful courier's report and he listened with the part of himself that was a man hearing the closing seal of an operation he had set in motion in October the consequences of which the courier did not know and would not, on Augustine's intention, ever know.' — The idea is strong. The sentence is a tunnel. 'on Augustine's intention' is a hedging construction that softens what should be cold. Cut to: 'He listened as a bishop receiving a courier's report, and as a man closing the door on an operation whose consequences the courier would never know.' The reader gets the split without being walked through it.

> 'Mark recited them in the small careful voice he had been carrying them in since November, with the precision of a young man who had been entrusted with a task and who had, performed it in every particular as he had been instructed.' — There's a grammatical error ('who had, performed') and the phrase 'the small careful voice he had been carrying them in' is simply strange. You carry objects, not voices. The precision is a good observation; the construction buries it.

> 'the slow honest accounting that a man performed when he had committed to a thing and recognised that the committing required the keeping of a record' — 'the committing required the keeping of a record' is nine words where 'commitment required a record' is four. This pattern — gerund phrases stacked in series — appears throughout and accumulates into drag.

> 'Marcellinus and his brother Apringius were executed at Carthage on the thirteenth of September — the day before the senior bishop had written, on the date of the dispatch — by beheading at the praetorian compound, on the count of Africa's authority.' — The em-dash insertion here is confusing in its chronology and breaks the sentence's momentum at exactly the moment where a clean, hard sentence would land like a blade. 'Marcellinus and his brother were beheaded at the praetorian compound on the thirteenth of September, by the count of Africa's authority.' The senior bishop's timing confusion can be a separate sentence.

### Suggestions
- Do a single-pass search for every instance of 'on [x]'s [noun]' — 'on Augustine's intention,' 'on the present accounting of the matter,' 'on the present trajectory,' 'on the recipient's representation to me,' etc. Count them. You have at least eight. Keep two. Cut the rest. The first time it reads as period voice; by the fourth time it reads as a keyboard shortcut.
- Apply the King rule: 2nd draft = 1st draft minus 10%. This chapter is approximately 3,800 words. Find 380 words to cut, specifically in the nested-clause sentences and the em-dash parentheticals. The story underneath will get stronger, not weaker.
- Rewrite the opening paragraph. The chapter earns its pace — but the first paragraph has to earn the reader's trust before they'll accept the deliberate slowness. Right now it loses them in the first three lines. Start with a fact. Move forward. Let the detail accumulate as Mark comes through the gate, not before.
- Audit every em-dash parenthetical in the chapter. Most of them are explaining something the reader can infer, or hedging something that should be stated plainly. The best ones can become their own sentences. The rest should be cut.
- In the Tagaste section, 'the small mule at the slow pace the small mule preferred' — repeating 'small mule' twice in one clause is a minor thing, but King would catch it. 'he rode the mule at its preferred pace' loses nothing.
- Trust the reader on the Marcellinus exposition. 'He had been corresponding with Marcellinus for two years and three months. He had been writing the City of God to him for two years and one month.' — the precision is right, but the block of background that follows is long. Consider whether the reader needs all of it to feel the weight of the letter on the desk. Some of it can be earned by the City of God dedication appearing later.
