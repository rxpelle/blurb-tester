## Voice & Narrative Persona (Grade: B+)

The prose here is accomplished — controlled, rhythmically precise, structurally confident. But King's core test is: does this feel like a person telling you something? And the honest answer is mostly no. The narrator is nearly invisible, functioning as a high-resolution camera rather than a storyteller with a seat at the table. This is Hilary Mantel's approach — the deliberate erasure of narratorial presence as a style — and it's executed with genuine skill. But King's narrators lean in. They editorialize. They have opinions about what they're showing you. They say things like 'You wouldn't have thought a man that size could move that fast, but he did.' This narrator never does that. The voice is consistent because it is consistently absent. Where the chapter earns real King-level marks is in the Augustine sections, specifically when the narrator slips inside a character and tells us something true and uncomfortable about that character's inner life. 'It came out of him in his bishop's voice, which was a voice he had built over twenty years for moments like this one and which he was not, by tonight, sure he still believed' — that's a narrator with a point of view. That's someone telling you a story. The surgical scene that opens the chapter is technically masterful but reads like forensic documentation. The narrator watches Cyprian work with a clinical detachment that has no warmth, no dread, no personality. A King narrator would let you feel what it means that a man can do this so calmly. The reader needs to feel the narrator's presence in the room. Right now, the room is empty except for the characters.

### Good Examples
> 'He brought the road in with him — dust on his face, the particular sweat of a man who had been riding hard since dawn' — narrator has personality in the observation; the phrase 'brought the road in with him' is a narrator making a choice, not just describing

> 'It came out of him in his bishop's voice, which was a voice he had built over twenty years for moments like this one and which he was not, by tonight, sure he still believed.' — this is the chapter's best voice moment; narrator is inside the character and editorializing, doing real work

> 'She said it the way one reports a result to oneself.' — six words, but they're the narrator having an opinion; this is exactly what's missing from most of the surgical scenes

> 'It came out the way a coin from his hand to a beggar came out — necessary, sincere, insufficient.' — narrator renders a judgment; you feel someone in the room with you for a moment

> 'The kind of clientele who came in carrying nothing they could not carry out' — the narrator's personality is in that word 'clientele'; it's dry, it's a choice, it gives the narrator texture

### Needs Improvement
> 'He cut the courier's tunic up the front, from the navel to the chest, with the courier's own knife. The skin of the belly was tight and warm. He pressed two fingers into the soft place above the right hip' — the surgical scene is documentary; the narrator watches this procedure without temperature, without dread, without any signal about what it means that a man can do this to another man; King would have the narrator in the room, not above it

> 'The magistrates appointed without examination by the magistrates of the previous year. The granaries reporting full and being empty. The coinage debased to two parts in five.' — the narrator disappears entirely here; we're reading a list and the list has no guide; a storyteller narrator would give us a heartbeat in the middle of this, a line that says what the list adds up to before Augustine has to think it

> 'The disc did not light, did not move, did nothing visible.' — the supernatural element of the bronze disc arrives and then gets a sentence designed to deflate it; a narrator with personality would use this moment; instead the narrator performs restraint at exactly the wrong time; we need to feel wonder or unease or something — not a camera report

### Suggestions
- Find three places in the surgical opening where the narrator can step into frame with a single sentence that tells us something about what we're watching — not what Cyprian feels, but what the narrator thinks about a man who can do this; King would not let a scene this cold run for four pages without a human voice somewhere in it
- The bronze disc moment is where the narrator needs to stop restraining and lean in; 'The warmth was not the warmth of a thing that had been against another warm thing' is good, but the narrator then retreats; add a beat where the narrator acknowledges what we're both thinking — that this is wrong, that metal doesn't do this — and hold it one beat longer before moving on
- The listing of the forty-one conditions in the papyrus needs a narrator voice threading through it; 'He read on. By the time he reached Carthage, the entries he was reading were the entries of the city he was sitting in' is the right instinct, but it comes too late and too briefly; the narrator should be present throughout the reading, translating not just the words but the weight
- Study the girl's confession scene and notice that the narrator has more personality there than anywhere else in the chapter — 'the thought of that — a child finding water in a refugee warehouse and washing her hands before asking a bishop whether God had permitted her grandmother to be burned alive on her own table — closed something in his throat' — this is the narrator speaking; find the equivalent moments in the Cyprian sections and the document reading and build them in
- The ending — 'The hand stayed cold' — is correctly voiced, but the chapter earns it only halfway; the narrator needs to be more present in the middle hundred pages so the restraint of that last line reads as earned rather than as more of the same documentary distance

## Deep POV & Interior Monologue (Grade: B+)

This chapter operates on two distinct psychic registers, and the contrast is both its greatest strength and its central weakness as a POV exercise. The Augustine sections are genuinely accomplished — at their best, they put us directly behind the bishop's eyes, rendering thought as it forms, sensation as it lands, and moral crisis as a physical event. The Cyprian sections are deliberately opaque: pure behavior, no interiority, a camera watching a man through glass. That choice is defensible as craft — Cyprian's inscrutability is the point — but it leaves the opening third of a long chapter without an emotional anchor. The reader watches rather than inhabits. King would argue you can withhold plot and still give us the inside of a skull: what Cyprian smells, what his body knows, what his hands do without his mind asking. The absence of any interior access to Cyprian means the chapter doesn't fully breathe until Augustine walks the cloister with a bad knee in the fog. Once we're inside Augustine, the chapter becomes something special. The prose finds a character-specific mental voice — scholastic, precise, slightly exhausted, self-auditing — and the deep POV moments are earned and piercing. 'He had a sentence for this also. It was smooth from use.' That is the interior of a man who has been a bishop for twenty years, rendered in nine words. The chapter's final image — 'The cold of a child's hair was on the palm of his hand through the canticle' — is a masterclass in how sensory memory IS thought. There are filtering constructions throughout ('he understood,' 'he felt,' 'he noticed') but they are sparse and the author mostly avoids the standard distancing traps. The larger issue is structural: the chapter runs at medium psychic distance for long passages when it should be running close.

### Good Examples
> 'He had a sentence for this also. It was smooth from use. He felt it open in his mouth and come out.' — Not reported thought, not 'he thought about what to say.' We are inside Augustine's relationship with his own pastoral language, inside the fatigue of a man who has answered this question so many times the answer has become mechanical.

> 'Neither do I. It was out of him before he could check it.' — Perfect. The character surprises himself. This is the gold standard of deep POV: thought not as something the narrator reports, but as something that happens to the character before he can stop it. King does this constantly.

> 'Augustine kept his hands on his knees. They wanted to do other things. They wanted to lift to his face.' — The body as separate agent, in conflict with the will. This is interior experience rendered through the body, not through stated emotion. No filtering word. No 'he felt the urge.' We feel it.

> 'The cold of a child's hair was on the palm of his hand through the canticle, the way a small physical sensation will outlast every effort to set it down.' — This is the ending of the chapter and it earns its place. A sensory memory that IS the interior state. No 'he was still thinking about her.' The cold is still there. That's deep POV.

> 'The recognition arrived in his body the way a fever arrives — first in the back of the throat, then in the chest, then everywhere — and after it came the man who had cut the courier and sent him.' — Intellectual realization rendered as physical event. This is how King writes revelation. The body knows before the mind names it.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He had read what was in the packet. He understood now why he had been sent and not killed himself.' — Reported thought. The most important psychological moment in Cyprian's arc and we get a summary from outside. King would put us inside that understanding. What did the reading feel like? What specific line made the killing make sense? We get nothing.

> 'He was watching the road.' — This is a filter construction at the bottom of a paragraph that has already established he's watching the road through behavioral detail. The sentence adds psychic distance instead of removing it. Cut it, or replace it with the sensory texture of the watching itself — what his eyes are actually tracking, what his body is doing while he waits.

> The entire Cyprian surgical scene: 'He did not hear it. He was past hearing.' — This is the most intimate act in the chapter and we are completely locked outside it. What does Cyprian think about while he works? What does he feel when his fingers find the packet? One sentence of interior would transform this from clinical procedural into something the reader carries. The deliberate coldness reads like the author protecting the character's mystery at the cost of the reader's access.

> 'Something moved in Augustine's chest that he did not have time to name.' — This is close but it's a half-measure. 'Something moved' is a filter on an emotion the prose is afraid to name. The refusal to name it reads as writerly restraint rather than character interiority. King would name it, or he would let it be so physically specific that naming was unnecessary. 'He did not have time to name' tells us it was unnamed without giving us the unnamed thing.

### Suggestions
- Give Cyprian one interior sentence — just one — in the opening section. Not his plan, not his reasoning. Something sensory and specific: the weight of the warm cup against his palm, the particular way the mule smell comes up through the boards, some physical fact his body registers that the narrator isn't reporting to us from outside. This would anchor the reader without revealing anything about his identity or purpose. Hitchcock said suspense requires that the audience care about the person in danger. We need one thread of interior life to care.
- Audit for 'understood,' 'noticed,' 'felt,' and 'realized' throughout. Each one is a filter you can cut by moving one step closer. 'He understood now why he had been sent' → render what the understanding is. 'He felt it land' (regarding the pious answer to the girl) → this one actually works because the landing is physical. But most filter words here create distance that the surrounding prose has already earned the right to close.
- The wax tablet note Augustine writes — 'The City of God to be in twenty-two books...' — is a tremendous moment, but we get the note itself without the decision process that preceded it. The reader watches Augustine pick up the stylus. Where is the moment he decides he cannot refuse? The prose says 'He could not refuse' but gives it to us as conclusion, not as experience. Let us be there when the refusal becomes impossible — what specific thing in the document, or the disc, or the girl's face, tips him past the point of choice?
- The Augustine-Marcus scene is the most psychic-distance-variable passage in the chapter. It goes close when it counts ('They wanted to do other things. They wanted to lift to his face.') but then the pious answer he gives Marcus is rendered at full distance: 'It came out of him in his bishop's voice, which was a voice he had built over twenty years.' This is reported interior. King would let us hear the voice from inside the building — the slight mechanical quality of it, the way it moves through his chest. The writer knows what's wrong with Augustine's answer; the prose needs to let Augustine know it in real time, not in retrospect.

## Backstory & Characterization (Grade: A-)

This chapter does something rare and difficult: it introduces two major characters, a nameless operative and Augustine of Hippo, without a single resume-style paragraph, and both feel completely real by the end. The method is almost pure King: character revealed through action, through the body doing things before the mind has decided, through small physical details that carry enormous freight. The warm cup prepared an hour before murder — a kindness and a weapon simultaneously — tells us everything about the operative in one image. Augustine's hands wanting to lift to his face during Marcus's account, and the discipline of keeping them on his knees because 'a man who has come from a sacked city watches the bishop's hands,' gives us twenty years of episcopal authority in a single gesture. The child washing her hands in the warehouse before asking whether God let her grandmother burn is devastating and flawless — no explanation needed, none given. Where the chapter stumbles is in Augustine's reasoning about the archive: the strategy is articulated fully in consecutive paragraphs, giving us the conclusion before we've watched him arrive at it. King would have let those decisions emerge through Augustine picking up the stylus, through the body moving before the mind had finished arguing. There are also two moments — both involving Augustine's surgical hands — where the prose does the reader's work for them when restraint would have been sharper.

### Good Examples
> "In his right hand was a different cup, smaller, of his own — a Falernian he had been warming for an hour against his palm. Warm wine was a kindness offered to a tired man. He intended to offer one." — Character as irony: the warmth of preparation, the weapon disguised as hospitality, all in three sentences with no explanation.

> "He had asked for the inn's wine and bread and a bowl of olives. The bread had gone hard at the crust. The olives sat untouched in their bowl. The cup of the inn's wine sat at his elbow; he had drunk none of it." — Four sentences of untouched things that make a portrait: patience, caution, discipline.

> "She had found water somewhere in the warehouse before coming and had washed them, and the thought of that — a child finding water in a refugee warehouse and washing her hands before asking a bishop whether God had permitted her grandmother to be burned alive on her own table — closed something in his throat that did not want to be closed." — The specific action tells us who this girl is more completely than any physical description could.

> "Neither do I." / "It was out of him before he could check it." — Involuntary speech as character revelation. Augustine's faith is complicated in two words and one sentence. King's principle exactly: characters revealed by what escapes them, not by what they choose to say.

> "The stylus was the bone one Monnica had bought him in Carthage when he was a student and which he had used for thirty-eight years." — One object: his age, the length of his career, his dead mother, his continuity. No biography required.

> "He found, inside the cavity, something not of the man." — Backstory-as-action. We understand the operative's entire plan in that phrase without a word of exposition.

> "The same door by which Crispus had been carried thirteen years and four months earlier." — We don't know who Crispus is. We don't need to. The specificity of four months tells us Augustine has been counting. The counting is the character.

> "His fingers — which had not done this kind of work in thirteen years and which had assumed, all those years, that they had forgotten — moved along the underside of the gut without his asking. They had not forgotten." — Backstory delivered through the body. Not 'he had once been a physician' but the hands that remember.

### Needs Improvement
> "The institution of the next century in this part of the world would be the Church or it would be no one. Papyrus did not survive. No one in Africa would copy this on its own merits... The Church would not copy this on its own merits. The Church would copy it only if it could not be separated from a thing the Church could not afford to lose. He needed a wrapping." — Augustine's strategic reasoning is handed to us in full before we watch him make the decision. King would have cut from 'he read the papyrus a third time' directly to Augustine's hand reaching for the stylus. The decision revealed through the action, not laid out before it.

> "He understood now why he had been sent and not killed himself." — The chapter earns this moment and then explains it. The sentence before it — 'The cargo would wait.' — is perfect. This sentence tells us the realization rather than trusting the reader to arrive there. Cut it and the paragraph gets sharper.

> "The cut had been small enough not to kill quickly. The stitching had been competent enough to keep the cargo in place. The men who had done it had wanted the courier to be found alive, or nearly alive, by whoever would be required to open him." — This is the Augustine POV section. The deduction is correct and interesting, but 'The men who had done it had wanted the courier to be found alive' articulates the conclusion Augustine is reaching. Better to end on the physical observation — 'the stitching had been competent enough to keep the cargo in place' — and let Augustine's next action (not hesitating, proceeding to open the wound) show us the deduction without stating it.

### Suggestions
- In Augustine's archive reasoning section, cut from 'He read the papyrus a third time' to the moment his hand reaches for the stylus — let the decision exist in the physical act of reaching, not in the paragraph of strategic logic preceding it. The logic is good; it just belongs slightly later, compressed into action.
- The courier's characterization at the inn (drinks water before wine, removes belt, eats before speaking) is a masterclass. Give Stephen the same treatment. He appears late, he buries the dead without speaking, his fist has been clenched for minutes without his noticing — these are good details but brief. The line 'Stephen had been the hands of a thing in a baptistery thirteen years before' is doing enormous work very late. Either trust it to do its work alone or give it one more physical anchor earlier in the chapter so it lands with full weight when it arrives.
- The document content itself (the forty-one markers, the seven dead cities) is delivered at length in Augustine's reading. King's instinct here would be to give us three specific entries — unforgettable ones — rather than the full summary. The granaries reporting full and being empty is strong. Cut most of the rest and let the reader's imagination complete the list. What Augustine says at the end — 'he was reading the entries of the city he was sitting in, and he stopped reading them as Carthage and read them as Rome' — is the moment. Get there faster.
- "He had said this sentence two hundred times. He had not, before tonight, said it to a child whose grandmother's mark was on a leather cord against her chest under her tunic." — The first sentence is strong. The second over-explains the dramatic irony the reader has already understood. Trust the setup. Cut from 'He had said this sentence two hundred times' directly to the girl's next question.

## Setting & Sensory Detail (Grade: A-)

This chapter operates at a high level. The author has internalized King's core principle that the best sensory details do double duty — grounding the reader while simultaneously revealing character, advancing mood, or carrying thematic weight. Temperature is used as a structural device: the warm Falernian cup becomes a murder instrument, making tenderness and killing simultaneous; the warm interior of the dying courier's cavity against the cold hands of the man working inside him; the cold of a child's hair that refuses to leave Augustine's palm through the final canticle. These thermal contrasts embody the chapter's central tension between the living and the dying, the past and what it costs the present. Smell is equally precise — the opening stratification of inn odors (mule, dung, straw below; watered wine above) maps the building's moral and social geography in two clauses. The surgery scene's smell 'that was not yet the smell it would be in eleven days but was the start of it' is extraordinary: smell as calendar, as quiet horror. Sound functions architecturally — the bridge's bowed plank becomes a surveillance instrument; a gull's complaint voices the silence at the chapter's most agonizing confession. Objects (the bone stylus, the bronze disc, the girl's cross) are consistently loaded, never decorative. The weaknesses cluster in transitional and receptive spaces: the small parlour where Marcus delivers world-changing news, the inn's upstairs room before the surgery, the walk north to Rome. These are places where the author trusted the drama to carry weight that physical reality should carry. The chapter is close to excellent but uneven in its coverage.

### Good Examples
> 'The smell of the place came up through the floorboards from the stable below — mule and dung and old straw — and across this rode the smell of the wine, which was a vintage the keep had watered twice.' — Stratified smell as architecture. The verb 'rode' makes the wine smell active, aggressive. The watered-twice detail is both sensory and moral.

> 'A smell came up out of the cavity that was not yet the smell it would be in eleven days but was the start of it.' — Smell as timeline and doom. King would call this perfect because it does three things simultaneously: it's viscerally real, it confirms the surgery's fatal design, and it ties the two story halves together before we've reached the second half.

> 'The smell had not waited to be remembered. It had already stepped into the room.' — This is the chapter's single best line. Memory as ambush, smell as agent. The personification is earned because it's precise, not decorative.

> 'The hinges gave their small specific sound when the lid lifted — he had heard that sound twice in the last thirty years, the night his mother died at Ostia and the night his son died at Hippo, and the sound carried both nights with it.' — Sound as compressed grief. The word 'specific' is the key — it's not any hinge sound, it's the sound of those two particular deaths, and the reader understands Augustine's hand slowing before it reaches the lid.

> 'The wax buckled at the first attempt — he had pressed too hard, the way a man presses when his hand is unwilling' — A single physical sensation that makes an emotional state legible without naming it. King's invisible prose in practice.

> 'The cold of a child's hair was on the palm of his hand through the canticle, the way a small physical sensation will outlast every effort to set it down.' — The chapter ends here, effectively. Temperature as moral residue. The meta-observation ('the way a small physical sensation will outlast every effort to set it down') trusts the reader to recognize the truth of it because they've just felt it happen.

> 'His back was against the pine of the wall, which was badly cured and still a little wet at the joins.' — A telling detail that gives us the inn's poverty and, by contrast, the quality of Cyprian's attention. He notices what a man notices when he has put his back to walls for years.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The community met at the third hour in the small house off the cloister. There were nine of them.' — After a chapter of rigorous sensory grounding, this is a vacuum. The small house off the cloister is a description. We don't know if it's cold, crowded, whether voices echo. The number nine is census data. We need one detail that makes this room exist — stone floor, dust in a morning window, the smell of men in close space. The absence is felt.

> 'He sat across from him and let it run. After some minutes Marcus wiped his face on his sleeve.' — The small parlour where the fall of Rome is announced has almost no physical existence. We have no floor, no smell, no light quality, no temperature. Marcus's bread is mentioned but barely. This is the chapter's most consequential scene and it unfolds in white space. The author gave the surgical table more reality than the room where a man first hears that civilization has ended.

> 'He had found the courier in a ditch beside a still-living mule three miles outside the western wall, and had recognized that the man on the mule had been put on the mule, not riding it.' — The ditch, the mule, the courier draped across the saddle — this deserves a moment of physical reality. What does a man look like who has been sewn shut and tied to a mule for eleven days? We need one beat of image before the body comes through the door.

> 'He walked north, toward Rome, where the gates were open and the smoke was four days settling.' — This is the chapter's emotional hinge: the assassin walking back into a burning city. The smoke is right but it's handled in ten words and then abandoned. The road to Rome at night after a murder, with the city burning on the horizon, is a setting that should live.

### Suggestions
- Give the small parlour scene with Marcus two or three sensory anchors before the news arrives — what does the room smell like, what is the quality of the fog-filtered light coming through, is the bread on the table still steaming or cold? The contrast between the ordinary physical world and the news that breaks into it is where the real horror lives.
- Expand the walk north. The smoke is four days settling but Cyprian has just committed a precise, premeditated murder. Let the road be physical. Let the smoke have a smell, let the darkness have a quality. One paragraph of concrete sensory detail here would make the final 'The road to Rome was empty' land harder.
- The inn's upstairs room before the surgery needs one more sensory detail beyond the lamp and the shuttered window. The way the floorboards feel underfoot, the mule sounds from below, the heat trapped under the roof in summer — something that makes the room exist before the man begins to work in it.
- The girl's testimony about her grandmother — 'They put her on her own table and they made the table burn' — is stark and effective, but one physical specific from the girl's telling would sharpen it further. Not gore — something small and concrete that proves she saw it or heard it from someone who did. The Phrygian woman got out by the wall. What did she see first?
- Trust the bronze disc less. Its warmth is introduced, described, and analyzed in a way that's slightly over-explained. The hinges of the box already do the emotional work. The warmth needs to be felt once and trusted to carry — the extended meditation on 'not the warmth of a thing that had been against another warm thing' edges toward telling the reader what to think about a sensation instead of letting the sensation do its work.

## Tension & Dread Building (Grade: A-)

This chapter is a masterclass in slow-burn dread, operating on two registers simultaneously — body horror and civilizational horror — and having the intelligence to let each validate the other. The opening inn sequence is nearly flawless: Cyprian's patient counting of cart crossings, the wine warming in his palm for an hour, the untouched olives, the closed book — all of it screams wrongness before a single act of violence occurs. King's core principle is at work here: the ordinary made sinister through precision. The surgical kit laid out 'in the order he would need them' is as chilling as any monster because it implies a calm that the reader's body refuses to accept. The circular structure — courier sent out, courier comes back dying — is elegant thriller architecture, and when Augustine smells the wound eleven days later the chapter pays off a debt planted in the first act with ruthless economy. The weaknesses are structural: the document-reading passage (the granaries-and-coinage list) bleeds tension into intellectual analysis at the exact moment the thriller should be tightening its grip, and the long confessional sequence — emotionally devastating, yes — runs nearly 1,500 words with no thriller wire kept live beneath it. King never lets you exhale completely. Two places here let you exhale more than you should. The chapter recovers beautifully — 'the smell had not waited to be remembered. It had already stepped into the room' is the kind of sentence that makes a reader put the book down and look at the wall — but the lapses are there. Overall: a work of exceptional craft that a surgical revision would push from very good to unforgettable.

### Good Examples
> 'He was watching the road.' — Four words as their own paragraph. Full stop. King's most reliable tool: the declarative sentence dropped like a stone. Everything before it has been scene-setting; this sentence tells us what kind of scene it is.

> 'a roll of waxed linen thread, a bronze probe, a small set of bone-handled blades wrapped in oiled silk, a clay flask of vinegar, a copper bowl. He counted the spool. There was thread for thirty sutures. He would use ten.' — The inventory is the horror. Not blood, not screaming — a man counting how much thread he will need. Clinical precision as the delivery mechanism for dread.

> 'the way a man stitches a sack he means to open again' — The single most chilling phrase in the chapter. Terror through domestic metaphor. The human body compared to a sack. The implication that the sack will travel and be opened somewhere else. Everything you need to know about Cyprian in ten words.

> 'The courier reached for the knife at the back of his belt and the knife was not there. The man held it up, briefly. He had taken it on the stair. The courier did not remember the stair.' — Textbook dramatic irony in micro. The reader understands exactly what 'did not remember the stair' means for the rest of the night. The word 'briefly' is doing enormous work — it signals a man so unbothered he barely pauses.

> 'the smell had not waited to be remembered. It had already stepped into the room' — Personifying the smell of a gutted dying man is the move of a writer at full stretch. The smell as autonomous entity, arriving before Augustine can prepare himself. This is King-level: the horror that doesn't announce itself, it just shows up.

> 'They wanted to lift to his face. They wanted to find the wood of the bench and grip. He kept them on his knees, because Marcus was watching them, and a man who has come from a sacked city watches the bishop's hands for what those hands tell him about whether the news will be carried.' — Physical suppression of panic. King always runs horror through the body first. Augustine's hands as a seismic instrument. The double dread: the news of Rome, and the cost of absorbing it visibly.

> 'He had read what was in the packet. He understood now why he had been sent and not killed himself.' — The chapter's most efficient dread sentence. One revelation withheld (the document's contents), one revelation delivered (Cyprian's own expendability). A two-sentence hook that pulls the reader through the entire Augustine section.

> 'The cold of a child's hair was on the palm of his hand through the canticle, the way a small physical sensation will outlast every effort to set it down.' — The closing image. King knows this truth viscerally: horror lives in the body longer than in the mind. The child's cold hair as the chapter's final ghost. Perfect.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The magistrates appointed without examination by the magistrates of the previous year. The granaries reporting full and being empty. The coinage debased to two parts in five...' — The document-reading section (approximately 400 words of listed civilizational collapse markers) drops the reader into essay mode at the chapter's climactic moment. The body on the table behind Augustine recedes entirely. King would keep one thin wire of body horror live through this passage — a sound from the corpse, a smell worsening, Augustine's hand going involuntarily to his mouth — so the intellectual horror and the physical horror vibrate together rather than trading off.

> 'A steward who had cheated his master of grain. An apprentice fuller who had struck his sister. A soldier on his way to Carthage who had taken the wife of his cousin...' — The confessions of the first five penitents run approximately 200 words with no thriller element at all. As emotional texture these confessions work; as tension architecture they are dead weight. By this point the reader knows a dying courier is eleven days out from Rome with something sewn inside him. The ticking clock should be audible even in the confessional. One small intrusion — Augustine hearing the gate, or a shadow passing the curtain, or his eye going involuntarily to the window — would keep the chapter's two registers in simultaneous play.

> 'He was past hearing.' — This line, meant to signal Cyprian's cold professional dissociation during the surgery, is slightly muddied. Is he unable to hear, or choosing not to? The ambiguity lands flat rather than chilling. King would sharpen it: 'The man had stopped listening at the moment the knife entered the skin' or simply cut it entirely and let the silence of the scene do the work.

> 'He sat with the choice. The choice did not get smaller. The brazier was at his elbow. He could feel the unlit charcoal in it. He could see the lamp in his hand putting it to the kindling. He could not refuse.' — The refusal-to-refuse passage is thematically important but takes slightly too long to arrive at its destination. The three sentences before the final one ('He could not refuse') dilute it. King compresses these pivots — the moment of decision should feel like a door swinging shut, not a man walking slowly toward it. Cut to: 'He sat with the choice. The brazier was at his elbow. He could not refuse.' Three sentences, same beat, double the velocity.

### Suggestions
- During the document-reading sequence, keep Augustine physically tethered to the dying man in the room. One line — the smell shifting, a sound from the body, Augustine noticing the lamp is lower than it was — would prevent the intellectual horror from eclipsing the body horror entirely. The reader should feel both at once.
- Plant one small thriller needle in the first or second confessional (before the girl) — Augustine's eye going to the shuttered window, a sound he can't identify, his hand pressing flat against the bench. The girl's cross reveal is the payoff; give us one beat of unease to pay off from.
- The transition from the inn to Augustine's fog-walk is the chapter's weakest seam. Consider ending the Cyprian section on the withheld document — what he read — rather than the Falernian wine. The reader arrives at Augustine already knowing something terrible is traveling toward him, which puts dramatic irony to work from the first word of the second section rather than eleven days into it.
- The line 'He was past hearing' during the surgery wants one more word of specificity: what was Cyprian past hearing? The courier's words? The courier's body? His own conscience? King would choose the most unsettling of these and name it.
- Stephen's closing question — 'Will there be others?' — is excellent. Consider letting it end the chapter, or near-end it. Everything after it (Augustine sitting at the desk with the cold parchment, the psalm, the cold palm) is beautiful but it softens a perfect knife-edge exit. King ends chapters on hooks, not on comfort, even cold comfort.

## Dialogue & Voice (Grade: A-)

The dialogue here operates at two levels simultaneously and mostly succeeds at both. At the surface it moves information. Underneath it moves character — which is the only level King cares about. The inn exchange between Cyprian and the courier is a clinic in subtext: every line means something other than what it says, the recognition phrase embedded so naturally you almost miss it, the whole transaction running on silence between the words. The confession scene with the nine-year-old girl is the chapter's crown jewel — four exchanges that strip theology to the bone and leave Augustine naked in a way no introspective passage could. 'Neither do I' may be the two most important words in the chapter. The Marcus scene carries more weight because the dialogue is doing heavy expository lifting, which it mostly disguises through Marcus's wine-merchant vocabulary, but the Rome speech is a concentrated information delivery that strains the seams. The dying courier's fragmented speech is handled with restraint and care. Stephen's closing question — 'Will there be others?' — arrives from a character who has been a shadow all chapter and suddenly becomes fully human in five syllables. Voice differentiation is strong: Cyprian speaks entirely in imperatives and declarations, zero warmth, no hesitation; Augustine shifts registers — the bishop's voice, the man's voice, and the crack between them where the real character lives. The girl speaks in the flat, literal grammar of a child processing the impossible. These are distinct voices. You would know them in the dark.

### Good Examples
> '*You. The wine.*' / '*Yes.*' / '*Network does not.*' / '*This is not the network.*' — The drugged courier's speech fragmenting in real time. Not 'I have been poisoned' but the brain losing its grammar before it loses consciousness. This is how a frightened man sounds when the connection between thought and word is going. Absolutely earned.

> '*Then I do not understand love.*' / '*Neither do I.*' — The confession scene's fulcrum. Two words from Augustine that demolish twenty years of theological scaffolding. The child says it as a reported result, not a challenge. Augustine answers before he can stop himself. The room doesn't move. A gull complains above the cloister. King would be proud of that gull — the mundane world carrying on while the bishop's certainty cracks.

> '*Father, a man at the gate from Italy.*' followed immediately by 'Augustine waited for more. The old man could not provide more, because his throat had closed.' — The gate-keeper's dialogue is only ten words but the prose around it does the work of fifty. We know everything about what those ten words cost to say.

> '*Tell me.*' — Augustine's response to Marcus beginning his report. Two words. The bishop stepping aside so the man can speak. It is a professional silence dressed as a sentence, and it reveals character entirely.

> '*He said: the houses on the Aventine are empty. He said: not all the burning was done by the Goths.*' — A message-within-a-message delivered through a wine merchant who doesn't know what he's carrying. The repetition of 'He said' has the flat rhythm of a man reciting something memorized in case it mattered. It matters.

### Needs Improvement
> '*Rome is taken. The Goths came in by the north gate before midnight on the twenty-fourth of August. They were inside three days. They burned. They took. They killed where they were resisted and many places where they were not. There are Christians among them who spared the basilicas of the apostles...*' — Marcus's speech is the chapter's worst dialogue moment. A wine merchant who has just crossed a war zone and wept at the sight of bread is not going to deliver a structurally organized report with subordinate clauses. He'd start in the middle. He'd jump. He'd say 'my cousin' five words in and then not say anything intelligible for thirty seconds. This is the author writing, not Marcus talking.

> '*It means God has permitted what He has permitted. It means the city of stones may fall, and the city which is not of stones cannot be reached by the Goths. It means we will mourn, and we will continue, and we will give shelter to those who come.*' — The text flags this as a performance: 'It came out of him in his bishop's voice, which was a voice he had built over twenty years.' That framing rescues it, but the speech itself is too polished for a man who has just decided, mid-sentence, to give the lesser answer. Real suppressed honesty has a stammer in it somewhere. One snagged word. One breath taken where no breath should be needed.

> '*Brother. I am going to open the wound. There is a thing inside you. I am going to take it out. Then I will give you absolution. Do you understand?*' — This is strong in concept but the four sentences have an almost liturgical evenness that makes them sound rehearsed. Augustine could still land every beat if one of those sentences broke — 'I am going to — there is a thing inside you' — because the bishop is also a man pressing his hands into a dying stranger at midnight, and men doing that do not speak in perfect parallel structure.

### Suggestions
- Break Marcus's Rome speech into fragments with Augustine pulling it out of him. Let Marcus start with the wrong detail — his cousin's face, or a smell — and have Augustine ask the direct questions: 'When did they enter?' 'How many days?' The information arrives the same way but through the texture of two people, not a briefing.
- In the dying courier scene, let Augustine mishear or misinterpret once before he gets it. 'Cy —' could go wrong before it goes right — Augustine first bending close thinking 'si' or trying 'city' — because the scene's emotional weight comes from the effort of being heard, and one failed attempt before the breakthrough makes the connection feel earned rather than efficient.
- Give Stephen one more line somewhere in the middle of the chapter — even a functional line, even three words — so his final 'Will there be others?' lands with more accumulated weight. He's been a shadow-functionary until that moment. A single earlier moment of presence would make the question feel like it comes from a person who has been dreading asking it.
- Consider one moment of dialect or idiosyncrasy in the girl's speech to separate her from the grave formality that, while effective, occasionally makes her sound older than nine. Children ask the devastating theological question and then, in the same breath, say something specific and strange. One small wrong-footing would make her more real without losing the weight of what she's asking.
- The Cyprian dialogue at the inn is exceptional — protect it. Do not add warmth or hesitation to his voice. His coldness is the voice. The contrast with Augustine's register-shifting humanity is doing structural work across the whole chapter.

## Pacing & Structure (Grade: A-)

This chapter is doing something genuinely hard — two POVs, two time signatures, a complex document mystery, and historical exposition across roughly 8,000 words — and it mostly pulls it off. The Cyprian section is a clinic in controlled menace. The pace perfectly mirrors the character: patient, still, watching. Sentence length earns every word. The surgical scene slows into a kind of terrible clinical beauty, and that slowing is the horror. Then the chapter cuts clean to Augustine and the rhythmic shift is earned. The problem is the second half runs long and loses the discipline of the first. The Augustine sections accumulate beautiful individual moments but the pacing loosens after the confession with the girl — which is the real emotional center of the chapter — and the chapter keeps finding new endings and failing to take them. The Cyprian section is A+ material. The Augustine back half is B work dragging the grade down. The chapter ends three times after its real ending.

### Good Examples
> 'The wayside inn had two rooms, a stable for four mules, and the kind of clientele who came in carrying nothing they could not carry out.' — Perfect King-style scene-setting. One sentence, complete world, the final clause doing all the atmospheric work without commentary.

> 'He was watching the road.' — Four words, own paragraph. This is the chapter's first gear-shift. Everything before it has been patient accumulation; this beat isolates the chapter's governing tension. King uses the paragraph break as a held breath.

> 'He turned the bolt.' — Isolated single sentence between the social scene and the surgical one. The chapter's pacing clicks from slow-burn to something colder here, and this is the exact sentence where it happens. Three words of white space.

> 'Neither do I.' — Standalone paragraph. This is the chapter's emotional climax and it lands because the author doesn't explain it, qualify it, or run from it. The pace stops here. Everything after is denouement.

> 'The cut was steady and not deep. He went through the second layer of the belly at the second pass and into the cavity at the third.' — The surgical scene's short declarative sentences match the character's calm. The horror is in the pace: methodical, unhurried, professional.

### Needs Improvement
> 'A steward who had cheated his master of grain. An apprentice fuller who had struck his sister. A soldier on his way to Carthage who had taken the wife of his cousin. A woman of thirty whose husband had returned from a campaign two years older than he had left and was beating her in the new manner. A deacon who had spoken sharply to his bishop...' — This inventory before the girl confession is a pacing mistake. King would cut it to one confessor, or cut it entirely. The reader is being made to wait for what they can already feel coming, and the waiting isn't charged — it's just waiting.

> 'He could not refuse. The wax was in front of him. He took up the stylus.' — The interior deliberation about burning the papyrus is told at summary distance when it needs to be lived. We get the conclusion without the sensation of arriving at it. Where's the weight of the charcoal in the brazier? The heat of the lamp in his hand? King would put us inside the choice, not above it.

> 'He set the disc down on the desk. His hands were not steady. He waited until they were. They took some time.' — The bronze disc arrives too late, does too much work, and this beat — which should feel seismic — lands soft because the chapter has already been closing for several pages. The rhythm has diffused the impact.

> 'The archive would survive. In some century not yet his, the man who had cut the courier would return for it.' — The chapter has a real ending at 'The hand stayed cold.' The final-paragraph summary after the burial sequence is the author explaining what the reader has already understood. This is the pacing equivalent of a film that keeps going after the music has swelled.

### Suggestions
- Cut or collapse the pre-girl confession inventory to a single line — 'He heard five in the usual run of things' — and move straight to 'The sixth was a girl of nine.' The reader's patience is a resource; spend it on the girl, not the steward.
- The chapter's real ending is 'The hand stayed cold.' Build to it and stop. Everything from 'Augustine sat at the desk' after the burial to 'The hand stayed cold' is three pages — cut it to one. The detail of the stylus, the bronze box sound carrying both dead, the burning linen: these are beautiful but they're bleeding out a chapter that's already spent its tension.
- The seven-cities document reading needs compression. Give us the structure — 'Forty-one entries. Seven headings. Seven dead cities.' — then let two or three of the most chilling entries land in full, and cut the rest. As written, the pace stalls for three paragraphs of inventory when the reader has already understood the implication by the second entry.
- The bronze disc feels grafted on and its introduction in the final third introduces a new mystery at the moment when the existing mysteries should be crystallizing. Consider moving its introduction earlier — plant it in the cloister scene, or have Augustine reference it during the Marcus conversation — so its appearance in the writing room feels prepared rather than sudden.
- The Cyprian section ends perfectly with 'The cargo would wait.' Consider whether the chapter should be split: Cyprian's POV as Chapter 1, Augustine's as Chapter 2. Two taut chapters of 3,500-4,000 words each will maintain the first section's pace throughout, rather than asking the reader to carry a chapter that runs nearly 8,000 words with a loosening second half.

## Themes & Subtext (Grade: A-)

This chapter is doing several things simultaneously beneath its surface plot, and most of them work. The dominant subtext — that truth survives only by hiding inside something else — is structurally embedded rather than announced. The document travels inside a man's body cavity; the knowledge will travel inside a twenty-two-book theological argument; the network's mark travels inside cheap bronze crosses sold for copper. The author never says this. It accumulates as a felt pattern. Even stronger is the running subtext about institutional faith versus personal belief. Augustine gives 'the pious answer' as professional duty — he has a bishop's voice 'he had built over twenty years' — and the chapter never once editorializes about whether this is hypocrisy, cowardice, or mercy. That ambiguity is where the theme lives. The confession scene earns the chapter's emotional and philosophical weight: a nine-year-old forces Augustine into an admission he cannot walk back, and the reader feels the floor shift under him as much as he does. The 'neither do I' lands as genuine revelation because it emerges from a child's logic, not the author's. Where the chapter wobbles is in Augustine's analytical monologue after reading the document — he moves too quickly into thesis-mode, thinking in complete institutional paragraphs when the prose has taught us he thinks in sensation and reluctance. The warm bronze disc is handled with appropriate restraint until its thematic function is slightly overexplained. These are minor problems in a chapter that handles subtext with genuine sophistication.

### Good Examples
> 'Then I do not understand love.' She said it the way one reports a result to oneself. Some answer was forming in him. He waited to see what it would be. 'Neither do I.' — The chapter's thematic center emerges from collision between two characters, not from authorial statement. The doubt is real, the admission involuntary, and it carries the weight of a man's lifetime of professional certainty.

> 'He had been a bishop for fourteen years and he had given the first answer to nine adults out of every ten. He had given it to a child four times. The shape of it sat ready in his mouth.' — Institutional faith rendered as muscle memory. The subtext about performance versus belief arrives through accumulation, never declaration.

> 'He had not been sent the courier. He had been sent to be the archive.' — The instrumentalization theme lands through plot revelation rather than statement. Augustine recognizes he is being used the same way the courier was used, and the reader makes the connection without being asked to.

> 'The cold of a child's hair was on the palm of his hand through the canticle, the way a small physical sensation will outlast every effort to set it down. He sang the verses. He went on with the morning. The hand stayed cold.' — The body's memory as the chapter's final image. Thematic without being symbolic in the announcing way. Just true.

### Needs Improvement
> 'The institution of the next century in this part of the world would be the Church or it would be no one. Papyrus did not survive... The Church would copy it only if it could not be separated from a thing the Church could not afford to lose.' — Augustine is thinking in complete analytical paragraphs here, reasoning through institutional logic with the clarity of a man writing an essay. The chapter has taught us he thinks in sensation, reluctance, small physical details. This passage is the author thinking through Augustine, not Augustine thinking.

> 'He who reads this document is to do with it what he can. The writer is no one of consequence. The matter is of consequence.' — The document's closing line is the chapter's thesis delivered as dialogue. It does the thematic work too openly, too neatly. King's rule: if a character states the theme, cut it in half. If it still feels like the theme, cut it again.

### Suggestions
- The analytical monologue that begins 'The institution of the next century' runs for six sentences of clear-eyed institutional reasoning. Break it up with physical sensation — Augustine's cold hands, the smell of the room, the sound of the courier breathing — so the thinking feels like it's happening to a man, not being delivered by an author.
- The closing paragraph of the embedded document ('He who reads this document is to do with it what he can') announces its own importance. Consider cutting the final two sentences entirely and letting the forty-one conditions land without editorial. The reader who has been reading Augustine will supply the weight.
- The warm bronze disc is handled well until: 'The markers in the papyrus arranged themselves in his head differently with the disc on the desk than they had arranged themselves an hour before without it.' This explains the disc's function. The chapter would be stronger if Augustine simply noticed it was warm and moved on — let the reader carry the strangeness forward without interpretation.
- The confession sequence is the chapter's best thematic material. Consider whether the chapter needs everything that comes after it. Augustine's writing-room revelations are intellectually rich but emotionally thinner than the girl's question. The cold of her hair on his palm at the end is the right note to end on — make sure the route to it doesn't dilute it.

## Cultural Specificity & Time Period (Grade: A-)

This chapter is doing something genuinely difficult — grounding fiction in a specific week of late Roman history, using real people, real events, and real material culture — and it pulls it off at a level that most historical fiction doesn't reach. The Sack of Rome on August 24-27, 410 CE is precisely dated, and every named figure checks out: Augustine of Hippo, Possidius (his biographer, correctly placed in Calama), Marcellinus (the imperial commissioner to whom Augustine actually dedicated City of God), Eraclius (his actual successor). The genesis of City of God is dramatized with fidelity — twenty-two books, ten books of refutation, the dedication to Marcellinus, and an opening sentence that closely renders Augustine's actual Latin. The material culture is meticulous: Falernian wine, wax tablets, bone stylus, oil lamps, papyrus judged by fibre and laid-down lines, oiled silk, waxed linen thread, the cursus publicus belt-removal habit, an imperial courier's callus from a wax stylus. The social world is calibrated correctly — wayside inns above their own stables, slave-boy grooms paid in silver, Numidian carters, the hierarchies of the episcopal household. The theological texture is specifically Augustinian: the typological use of Psalm 78, the refusal of platitude in the confessional, the Polybius and Sallust marginalia in a Alexandrian Latin glossing hand. The one significant gap is that Augustine's encounter with the warm bronze disc — a theurgic object, an object with autonomous heat — does not engage his specific theological crisis about such things. This is a man who wrote at length against divination and magic, who had himself practiced astrology before his conversion. A 5th-century Christian bishop holding a warm bronze object of unknown origin would not be mildly puzzled; he would be in a precise, named theological emergency. That emergency is absent. The chapter is otherwise among the more rigorously grounded pieces of historical fiction in its handling of time.

### Good Examples
> 'He drank water before he drank wine, which is what a man who has been riding does first.' — A behavioral detail that belongs to 410 CE the way a brand name belongs to 1958. It's not explained. It doesn't need to be.

> 'an old callus at the base of the index finger that messengers got from carrying a wax stylus for years against the same place' — This is the King method exactly: a body detail that dates a character's occupation to a specific technology. No callus like this exists after paper.

> 'The stylus was the bone one Monnica had bought him in Carthage when he was a student and which he had used for thirty-eight years.' — Monnica is Augustine's actual mother. Carthage is where he actually studied rhetoric. Thirty-eight years back from 410 is 372 CE, which tracks. The detail is historically load-bearing.

> 'The chant had been written for the burning of Jerusalem, centuries before, and it would outlast Hippo as it had outlasted Jerusalem.' — This is Augustine's actual typological mode of thought, not just color. The 78th Psalm was used for lament over fallen cities. The chapter is thinking in Augustine's categories.

> 'non in Romae historicis sed in archivis ipsis' — The glossator read in the archives of the cities themselves, not the standard historians. The Latin is correct, the Alexandrian provenance is plausible, and the detail separates this document from generic ancient wisdom writing.

> 'The institution of the next century in this part of the world would be the Church or it would be no one.' — This is Augustine's actual historical position, stated plainly. Not editorializing. Fact.

> 'The Goths came in by the north gate before midnight on the twenty-fourth of August. They were inside three days.' — Alaric entered through the Salarian Gate (north Rome) on August 24. Three days is accurate. The chapter does not footnote this. It just says it.

### Needs Improvement
> 'He received the box from the hands of the priest Simplicianus in Milan the night he was baptized.' — Augustine was baptized by Ambrose at Easter 387, not Simplicianus. Simplicianus was Ambrose's predecessor and mentor and a crucial figure in Augustine's conversion — he told Augustine about Victorinus — but he did not officiate at the baptism. This is a historically traceable error in a chapter that otherwise earns its credibility.

> 'The reading was from Cassian, on patience.' — John Cassian's Institutes and Conferences were written between roughly 415-430 CE. In August 410, they likely do not yet exist. A five-year anachronism in a chapter that's otherwise calibrated to the week is a small but real slip.

> 'Something moved in Augustine's chest that he did not have time to name.' — This is the one place where the interiority goes generic. Augustine is one of the most articulate men who ever lived about interior states. 'Something he did not have time to name' is the language of a protagonist who hasn't been individuated. His interiority elsewhere in the chapter is specific. This sentence could belong to anyone.

### Suggestions
- When Augustine holds the warm bronze disc, give him his specific theological crisis. He knows the vocabulary: theurgy, divination, the Neoplatonist arguments for animated objects he encountered through Porphyry and rejected. He should name what is happening to him in 5th-century terms — and then decide to proceed anyway. That decision is the character.
- Push the Numidian carter, Lucius, one specific cultural beat further. He crosses himself, he's 'not particularly devout' — but what does devout mean in 410 CE Hippo for a Numidian carter? Is he Donatist? That schism was live and local and would have mattered to Augustine in a precise way. One line would do it.
- The reading from Cassian: either move it to a post-415 setting, replace it with a text Augustine demonstrably read before 410 (Ambrose, Jerome, the Psalms commentary of Hilary), or make it a letter or early draft rather than a formed work. The anachronism is small but correctible.
- The seven fallen cities are the strongest piece of cultural invention in the chapter. Trust them more. Let Augustine sit with the specific resonance between Carthage and Hippo — his adopted city and the city that Rome destroyed — before he reaches the Rome entries. That comparison was alive in 410 in a way that would have been personally destabilizing for an African bishop.
- The fog at Hippo Regius (modern Annaba, Algeria) is atmospheric but underspecified. The harbour smells, the specific heat and quality of North African August light breaking through Mediterranean sea fog, the sound of the port — these could be two sentences that couldn't belong to any other city.

## Prose Polish (King Polish) (Grade: A-)

This is exceptional prose — among the cleanest historical literary thriller writing I've seen at this length. The writer has genuinely internalized King's core doctrine: prose as glass, not as decoration. The observational specificity is consistently excellent (the callus at the base of the index finger, the warm Falernian held against the palm for an hour, the child washing her hands before confession). Dialogue is clean and mostly untagged without chaos. Short punch sentences land at exactly the right moments. The surgical sequence is a small masterpiece of procedural precision that never lets the mechanics overtake the dread. The girl-and-the-bishop scene is the kind of writing King would dog-ear. The failures, where they exist, cluster in two zones: the metaphysical reasoning passages (the Church-as-archive section, the warm bronze disc), where the writer tries to explain what should be felt; and a handful of phrases that call attention to themselves as writing — moments where you notice the sentence instead of the story. The 10% trim rule has not been fully applied to the abstract interior passages. But these are edge-case problems in an otherwise A-level manuscript. The prose earns its length in almost every paragraph.

### Good Examples
> "He drank water before he drank wine, which is what a man who has been riding does first." — King-perfect. Character through observed physical behavior, zero adjectives, no authorial intrusion. You know everything about this courier in one sentence.

> "He turned the bolt." — Alone on its line. The bolt turn is the story. Every word earns its place because there are only three words.

> "It came out the way a coin from his hand to a beggar came out — necessary, sincere, insufficient." — Three words after the dash doing the work of a paragraph. This is what earned simile looks like.

> "He did not eat at the noon meal. He did not eat at the evening meal either, but that was later." — The 'but that was later' is a small masterpiece of laconic grief. It creates time, sadness, and deadpan restraint in five words.

> "the thought of that — a child finding water in a refugee warehouse and washing her hands before asking a bishop whether God had permitted her grandmother to be burned alive on her own table — closed something in his throat that did not want to be closed." — Long but every word earns its place. The specificity is devastating. This is the kind of sentence that makes readers stop and sit.

> "The hand stayed cold." — Last line of the chapter. Three words. All the theology and grief and unanswerable questions compressed into a physical sensation that will outlast the morning. King would frame this on a wall.

### Needs Improvement
> "and across this rode the smell of the wine" — The prose becomes visible here. 'Rode' is a self-conscious verb choice that makes you notice the sentence. 'Over this the wine's smell, a vintage the keep had watered twice' — or just: 'and over it the wine, watered twice.' The stable setting makes 'rode' feel clever rather than invisible.

> "Something moved in Augustine's chest that he did not have time to name." — King would cross this out in red. You don't name the emotion, fine — but 'something moved' is a flag of avoidance. Either physicalize it (a drop of cold below the sternum, a lurch) or cut the sentence and let the silence do it. Waving at an unnamed feeling is not the same as showing it.

> "that was a comfort of a small kind" — Five words where two would do. 'A small comfort' is the sentence. The inversion 'of a small kind' calls attention to itself as period prose styling at the exact moment when the prose should be invisible.

> "The absence of the body was a thing he could feel against his face." — 'A thing he could feel' is soft and labored. What did it feel like? Cold? Weight? Pressure? King's rule: if you can name it, name it. If you can't, physicalize it. 'A thing he could feel' is neither.

> "His right knee was worse in damp weather; it was worse this morning than it had been all season." — The double 'worse' in one sentence is the kind of clumsy repetition that belongs in a first draft. 'His right knee ached in damp weather; this morning it was the worst it had been all season' — or simply trust the reader: 'His right knee was worse in damp weather, and this was a bad morning.'

### Suggestions
- Apply the 10% rule specifically to the Church-as-archive reasoning passage ('The institution of the next century...'). This section explains what Augustine is thinking rather than showing it. Cut 20% of it and let the action — putting papyrus next to disc, waiting for hands to steady — carry the weight.
- The warm bronze disc section overexplains the mystery: 'The warmth was not the warmth of a thing that had been against another warm thing.' Trust the reader. 'The bronze was warm. It had been on a high shelf for ten years. It should not have been warm.' Done. King's rule: don't explain the supernatural, just report it and move on.
- Search every instance of 'something moved,' 'something shifted,' 'something happened in his chest/throat/stomach' and either cut or replace with a physical sensation. These are placeholders for emotion, not emotion itself.
- The repeated structure 'The cut had been small enough not to kill quickly. The stitching had been competent enough to keep the cargo in place.' appears twice in the chapter — once in Cyprian's section and once in Augustine's. The first time it's chilling. The second time it's a technique becoming visible. Cut one or vary the language significantly.
- Trim 'across this rode the smell of the wine' — this is the chapter's opening and the one spot in the first paragraph where the prose announces itself. Open clean.
- The chapter's length is justified by its ambition, but the final Augustine interior passages (from 'He sat at the desk' through the City of God composition) could lose 10% of the connective reasoning tissue. The physical details — the stylus Monnica bought him, the bronze box's hinge sound carrying both deaths — are perfect and should stay. The sentences that explain what those details mean should be cut.
