### CRITICAL (1)

**1. [Immersive Experience Editor] emotional resonance / visceral moment** (Paragraph beginning 'He did not weep. He did not pray.')
- **Issue:** The chapter's single highest-stakes emotional moment — Augustine reading his own twenty-seven-year-old letter signed 'Aurelius, who loves you' immediately after learning Una has died — passes in three sentences that enumerate what he is NOT doing. The fire simile is the prose reaching for something true, but it stays at simile distance. We understand the moment intellectually but we do not feel it in the body. The negation list (did not weep, did not pray) describes the absence of expected responses rather than the presence of the actual one.
- **Suggestion:** Anchor the moment in one specific physical sensation — the weight of the parchment, the temperature of it, the way old parchment feels different from new. He has already decoded the crease pattern (brilliant passage). Let the body continue that work. Something in the hands or the chest or the throat. The 'watches a fire' comparison is doing real emotional labor but is generic — specify what watching a fire does to a person who cannot look away.
- **Before:** `He did not weep. He did not pray. He watched the small piece of his own younger handwriting on the desk as one watches a fire. After a quarter-hour he heard, or thought he heard, the bell for the third hour.`
- **After:** `He did not weep. He did not pray. He kept his hands flat on the desk, one on each side of the page, and looked at the closing line. The parchment was the temperature of the room, not the temperature of a hand, and he understood that she had not been holding it when she died. He looked at the line for a long count. After some time the bell for the third hour began, or he heard it begin, and he let it ring.`

**2. [Developmental Editor] thriller thread / plot momentum** (October 410 surveillance scene through December coda (final two lines))
- **Issue:** The lock-trying subplot is introduced with genuine menace, escalates beautifully through the all-night vigil, and delivers a superb payoff: Augustine sits up the whole night, no one comes, but two new scratches appear in the morning. This is thriller craft at its best — the antagonist is patient, aware, and undeterred. But the subplot then closes in the final coda ('tried twice more in December, and not again') without any development toward identity or motive. An adversary sophisticated enough to know Augustine was watching, withdraw, and probe again twice before going silent is not a subplot that should end with 'not again.' As written, the line reads as resolution when it should read as escalation in a new key.
- **Suggestion:** The December coda should plant unease, not closure. Either: Stephen reports something that differentiates the December attempts from the earlier ones (timing, method, something left behind); or the cessation itself becomes ominous when Augustine recognizes it coincides with an external event. The goal is to leave the reader asking 'why did they stop?' rather than 'good, that's over.'
- **Before:** `The lock at the writing-room door was tried twice more in December, and not again.`
- **After:** `The lock at the writing-room door was tried twice more in December — both times in the same night, one hour apart — and not again. Stephen, who had kept the watch, had seen no one. Augustine, when Stephen told him in the morning, said nothing. He thought about what it meant that the attempts had stopped.`

**8. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation / Thriller Mechanism** (October 410 section, paragraphs 2–3 (beginning 'He had thirty-one paragraphs'))
- **Issue:** The thriller's central MacGuffin — Augustine encoding a secret map inside City of God — is introduced in dense shorthand that assumes prior knowledge. 'Forty-one markers,' 'seven Akkad markers,' 'the matching key,' 'the first of the seven cities' arrive in quick succession with no grounding in what an Akkad marker is, what the seven cities represent, or why embedding codes inside a theological treatise is the right vessel for whatever Augustine is hiding. A general reader can register that Augustine is concealing something coded and that someone is trying to reach it, but they cannot understand the elegance or the stakes of the system. This matters because the danger (the lock being tried) cannot land emotionally if the reader doesn't know what's inside the room.
- **Suggestion:** Give Augustine one internal beat — even three sentences — that explains the system's logic in terms a reader can hold: there is an ancient record in his possession; he is hiding a map to it inside the most important book of his era; whoever holds both the book and the matching key can find what he's hidden. Make the 'why this book' visible through his own reasoning — the City of God will be copied, translated, preserved across centuries precisely because Rome has fallen. The catastrophe is the guarantee of the vessel.
- **Before:** `The first book did the chapter on Brennus and the Gauls; the chapter would carry, beneath the proper teaching about the city of stones falling and the city of God standing, the first of the seven Akkad markers, in the form of an apparently incidental aside about the appointment of magistrates without examination. A reader four hundred years from this writing, holding the matching key, would read the apparently incidental aside as the first marker of the first city.`
- **After:** `What he had in the bronze box was a record of something buried in a sequence of seven ancient cities — a record made by men who believed the knowledge should outlast any single empire. He was going to outlast an empire too, and he was going to do it the only way he knew how: inside a text too important to destroy. Into the chapter on Brennus and the Gauls he had built what he called a marker — a sentence that would read, to any ordinary reader, as an aside about the appointment of magistrates. To a reader holding the matching key, the same sentence would locate the first of the seven cities. A reader without the matching key would read it as the aside.`

**9. [Accessibility Editor] Historical Context / Concept Accessibility** (Julian the clerk section (November 410, 'It was a writ of confiscation under the Edict of Unity'))
- **Issue:** The Donatist confiscation sequence assumes the reader knows what the Donatist schism is, what the Edict of Unity of 405 was, and why a Catholic bishop is countersigning writs to seize grain from a Christian village. The moral horror of the scene — which is the chapter's sharpest knife — depends on understanding that Augustine is enforcing a coercive unity policy against Christians who differ from him on ecclesiology, and that he chose this role, argued for it in letters, and theologically justified it. Without that context, the scene reads as 'bishop signs a government document.' The reader gets that people will die; they don't get that Augustine is enabling the persecution of his own faith's dissenters while the Magnificat plays in his throat.
- **Suggestion:** One sentence of internal context during Augustine's reading of the writ — not a history lesson, but enough to locate the reader in the situation: the Donatists are a rival Christian church, in schism for a century over a question of who may legitimately administer sacraments; the Edict has made their refusal to reunite a civil offense; and Augustine has argued in writing that compelled return is an act of mercy. The reader then understands that the 'according to mercy, confirmed' is not bureaucratic formula but the most precise possible self-indictment.
- **Before:** `It was a writ of confiscation under the Edict of Unity of February 405 — for the seizure of the grain stores held in the storehouse attached to the Donatist place of worship at the village of Tibilis Minor in the territory of Calama.`
- **After:** `It was a writ of confiscation under the Edict of Unity of February 405. The Donatists had been in schism from the Catholic church for over a century — a dispute about which clergy had betrayed the faith under Roman persecution, and whether sacraments administered by such men were valid. The Edict had made their continued refusal to reunite a civil offense: their properties could be seized, their clergy exiled. Augustine had argued for the Edict in letters; he had written that a compelled return to truth was better than a voluntary persistence in error. The writ before him was the Edict made concrete: the seizure of the grain stores attached to the Donatist place of worship at Tibilis Minor.`

### MINOR (7)

**1. [Developmental Editor] character interiority / moral weight** (November 410 writ scene — 'He did not put the figure into words. He took up the iron pen and dipped it.')
- **Issue:** Augustine calculates six to eight deaths before signing, which is devastating. Then he signs, with no interior moment between the arithmetic and the act. This is almost certainly deliberate — the blankness is the characterization, the man who turns the page — and the cold mechanics of the countersignature are correctly handled. The risk is that the blank reads as authorial oversight rather than character revelation, because the chapter gives rich interiority everywhere else. The Latin formula 'secundum misericordiam confirmavit — According to mercy, confirmed' is the chapter's most bitter irony, but it needs one more ounce of Augustine's awareness of it to land at full force.
- **Suggestion:** One concrete physical detail between the arithmetic and the signing that activates the reader's awareness of what the hand is doing — not an interior prayer, not self-justification, just the body registering a continuity. The bone stylus is already on the desk. The iron pen is the other implement. Let the distance between them be perceptible.
- **Before:** `He did not put the figure into words. He took up the iron pen and dipped it.`
- **After:** `He did not put the figure into words. The iron pen was across the desk from the bone stylus. He reached past the stylus and took the iron pen up and dipped it.`

**2. [Developmental Editor] character function / structural anchoring** (Possidius arrival scene — paragraphs 1–12 (August 410 section))
- **Issue:** Possidius's arrival effectively establishes that he suspects what happened (the salt bloom, the brazier residue, the bronze box displaced) and that both men will proceed in mutual silence. But Possidius then disappears entirely. He is not present for — and makes no visible response to — any of the chapter's major events. His opening appearance therefore functions as pure setup for a conversation that does not occur in this chapter, and his investment (two extended paragraphs of close observation, the three pauses 'still in the room like things that had been put down') is not returned. This is fine if his arc extends through Chapter 3, but as written the reader's expectation of him exceeds his presence.
- **Suggestion:** Either (a) give Possidius a brief reappearance — even one wordless exchange at vespers — that reactivates the complicity thread and signals the silence between them is ongoing, not resolved; or (b) trim the opening by one paragraph to reduce the reader's investment in a character whose payoff is deferred. Option (b) is safer if his arc pays off later in the book.

**6. [Accessibility Editor] Jargon Management / Period Vocabulary** (November 410 section, Una letter — 'A freedman, then, or a man of the working middling sort')
- **Issue:** 'Freedman' is used without definition to characterize Anthony and to contextualize his deference throughout the letter. Roman manumission is not general-reader knowledge. A 10th grade reader likely reads 'freedman' as simply 'free man' (i.e., not a slave), missing the specific status — formerly enslaved, still attached by social obligation to the master's household network — that explains both Anthony's tone and the institutional framework of Una's arrangement ('the records of our late master's household').
- **Suggestion:** Add three words in apposition. This costs nothing in pace.
- **Before:** `A freedman, then, or a man of the working middling sort who had paid a few coppers for a clerk's hour.`
- **After:** `A freedman — once enslaved, now released — or a man of the working middling sort who had paid a few coppers for a clerk's hour.`

**7. [Accessibility Editor] Reader Orientation / Object Significance** (October 410 section — 'He took down the bronze box from the upper shelf. He opened it. The disc was inside. The leather packet with the papyrus was inside.')
- **Issue:** The disc and the leather packet with the papyrus are referenced throughout the chapter as the objects Augustine is hiding and protecting, but their nature is never identified. A reader who does not carry prior-book context cannot understand what these objects are, where they came from, or why they are worth setting an overnight trap over. They are treated as known quantities when they are not. The reader can intuit 'these are important things being hidden' but cannot understand the specific danger — and cannot feel the threat of the lock being tried with any precision.
- **Suggestion:** One brief parenthetical identification at the first significant handling — not a full explanation, but enough to attach the objects to a history: something that names the objects' origin or the cost of their acquisition. The author knows the actual backstory; the example below is illustrative of the type of addition needed, not the specific content.

- **After:** `He took down the bronze box from the upper shelf. He opened it. The disc was inside — the thing the Alexandrian had died to give him, or so he had come to believe — and beside it the leather packet with the papyrus, which he could half-read and could not destroy and could not explain to any living person. Both were in the position he had left them.`

### SUGGESTION (3)

**1. [Developmental Editor] chapter opening / thriller hook** (Paragraph 1 — Possidius arrival)
- **Issue:** The chapter opens at summary distance: 'Possidius came back from Calama in the middle of the week, two days early, having heard the news of Rome on the road.' Three sentences of circumstance before any dramatic moment. The chapter's most immediate thriller question — what does Possidius know, and what will he do with it — is best answered by his eyes landing on the bronze box or the salt bloom. That moment of recognition is the chapter's real first beat; the arrival logistics are preamble.
- **Suggestion:** Open on Possidius's gaze — mid-scan, mid-recognition in the writing room — then pull back to establish the arrival. This places the reader inside the complicity before the chapter explains the circumstance, and creates immediate forward lean without requiring any exposition to precede it.

**2. [Immersive Experience Editor] scene grounding / emotional destination** (Final coda paragraphs: 'The youngest was a girl of eleven months...')
- **Issue:** The unnamed girl is the moral endpoint of the chapter — the most distant human cost of the abstract writ Augustine signed — and she exists almost entirely as documented non-existence: no name, no marker, no record. The prose achieves something real with this, but she is still entirely abstract. One specific physical detail — not from a document, since documents are part of the point — would make her more than a figure in a ledger.
- **Suggestion:** The mother's name is given: Anna. The chapter might allow itself one gesture toward the physical reality of the child without undercutting the archival coldness — something that registers before the administrative language closes back over her. This is a fine line and the current version isn't wrong; this is a suggestion, not a correction.
- **Before:** `The youngest was a girl of eleven months whose mother, a Donatist woman of twenty-six named Anna, had not yet given her a name.`
- **After:** `The youngest was a girl of eleven months who could stand if she held the edge of something. Her mother, a Donatist woman of twenty-six named Anna, had not yet given her a name.`

**3. [Accessibility Editor] The Smart Friend Test / Character-Driven Concept** (October 410 section — 'The method was working. The method depended on the matching key surviving as well as the wrapper')
- **Issue:** The paragraph describes the coded message system as if summarizing a process rather than showing a brilliant mind appreciating his own solution. Augustine was one of the great strategic intellects of late antiquity — the man who argued that the fall of Rome proved Rome was never what it claimed to be. The system he has devised is elegant precisely because the catastrophe that makes the concealment necessary also guarantees the vessel's survival: City of God will be copied, debated, and preserved for centuries because Rome has fallen. If Augustine sees this, even in a clause, the reader feels the genius of the plan. As written, the passage gives us the mechanics without the elegance.
- **Suggestion:** Add one beat of Augustine recognizing why this vessel and this moment are exactly right for each other — the irony that Rome's fall is the best thing that could have happened to his hiding place.
- **Before:** `The method was working. The method depended on the matching key surviving as well as the wrapper, and the matching key was a single small roll of parchment in the lining of his tunic.`
- **After:** `The method was working. The beauty of it, which he had not fully seen until this autumn, was that the more Rome's catastrophe made men desperate for the book — the more they copied it and argued over it and carried it across the sea — the more securely the markers travelled with it. The disaster was the guarantee. The method depended on the matching key surviving as well as the wrapper, and the matching key was a single small roll of parchment in the lining of his tunic.`
