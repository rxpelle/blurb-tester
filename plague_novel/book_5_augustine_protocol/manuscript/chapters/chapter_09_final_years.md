## SCENE 1: The Vandals Are Coming

**427 CE, Spring - Bishop's Residence**

The reports arrived weekly, each one more alarming. Vandals crossing from Hispania into Mauretania. Cities falling—Tingis, Volubilis, Caesarea. An entire barbarian nation on the move, pushed by Huns in the east, flooding into North Africa.

And heading toward Hippo.

Augustine was seventy-three, increasingly frail, but his mind remained sharp. He read the intelligence reports Possidius brought from traveling merchants and refugees.

"How long?" he asked.

"Two years," Possidius said quietly. "Maybe three. They're consolidating territory as they advance. But they're coming."

Augustine nodded slowly. He'd seen this pattern in genetic memory—civilizational collapse followed predictable trajectories. Rome's western provinces were fragmenting. Power was localizing. Barbarian kingdoms were forming in the vacuum.

The empire was dead. It just hadn't stopped moving yet.

"The books," Augustine said. "We need to distribute copies of everything. Not just *City of God*—all the encoded works. *Confessions*, the anti-Pelagian treatises, the biblical commentaries."

"Already in progress," Possidius confirmed. "Melania funded a scriptorium in Carthage. They've been copying everything for the last three years. We have complete sets going to: Jerusalem, Alexandria, Constantinople, Rome, Lérins, and Cassian's monasteries in Gaul."

"Not enough. We need more redundancy."

"Teacher, there are limits. Trained scribes, materials, transport—"

"I know." Augustine stood, walking to the window overlooking the harbor. Fewer ships these days. Trade declining. Infrastructure collapsing. "But the Vandals will burn libraries. Destroy codices. They're Arian Christians—they'll see Nicene theological works as enemy propaganda."

"So we need copies in regions they won't reach."

"Yes. And we need to prepare the network for what comes after."

Possidius waited.

"After I die," Augustine said bluntly. "Which is soon. And after Hippo falls. Which is also soon. The North African network has been stable for forty years because we've had institutional cover—I'm the bishop, you're the bishop of Calama, Melania has Church funding. But that's ending."

"We go underground."

"We adapt," Augustine corrected. "Not hiding—adapting. The Vandals will establish their own church hierarchy, Arian instead of Nicene. Which means new institutional cover. New encoding strategies. New survival methods."

"You want us to infiltrate the Vandal church?"

Augustine smiled slightly. "I want you to recognize that 'Vandal church' and 'Roman church' are both temporary political structures. The pattern transcends both. Defensive carriers can work within either framework."

"That's... controversial."

"That's survival." Augustine turned from the window. "Constantine converted Rome to Christianity. We encoded within Roman Christianity. Now Rome is falling and Vandals are Christianizing differently. We encode within that. The surface form changes. The deep pattern continues."

"Jerome would have hated this."

"Jerome *did* hate this," Augustine agreed. "That's why I wrote Book XIX of *City of God* the way I did—showing that political alliances are temporary, all earthly cities fall, loyalty to pattern transcends loyalty to any institution."

Possidius was quiet, processing.

"You'll be the senior carrier in North Africa when I die," Augustine continued. "Generation 55, trained in full methodology, trusted by the network. Your task is maintaining continuity through the transition. Roman to Vandal. Nicene to Arian. Imperial to tribal. Whatever that requires."

"And the Pattern Eye?"

Augustine glanced toward the basilica, where the bronze artifact remained hidden. "That's the immediate problem. Hippo will be besieged. The basilica might be destroyed. We need to move it."

"Where?"

"You decide. Based on what's safest when the time comes." Augustine smiled at Possidius's expression. "I'm serious. I won't be alive when Hippo falls. You'll have to assess the situation—which cities are stable, which network nodes are secure, where the next generation is forming. The Pattern Eye goes wherever serves the network best."

"That's enormous responsibility."

"You're ready for it. You've been training for this for thirty years."

Possidius exhaled slowly. "What else needs preparing?"

"Succession plans for every network role. Training protocols for Generation 56—they're coming into their own now, need systematic instruction. Financial planning—Melania won't live forever, we need sustainable funding models. Encoding standards—making sure the method stays consistent across regions as communication breaks down."

"You're planning for total imperial collapse."

"I'm planning for what's already happening," Augustine corrected. "The empire collapsed when Rome fell in 410. We're just watching the corpse finish dying. Our task is ensuring the network survives the death."

## SCENE 2: Teaching the Next Generation

**428 CE, Winter - Melania's Monastery**

Augustine taught his last formal class in the monastery's library. Twenty students, all Generation 56, ranging from eighteen to thirty-five. They were the future—if the network survived the next decade.

"The Pattern Eye," he said, holding the bronze artifact for the first time in public instruction. He'd retrieved it from the basilica specifically for this lesson. "Seven of these exist. Or existed—we're not sure. Each one amplifies genetic memory access, allowing carriers to experience ancestral knowledge more vividly."

The students leaned forward, fascinated.

"But don't misunderstand what it is," Augustine continued. "It's not magical. It's not divine. It's a tool—Bronze Age technology that interacts with genetic memory in ways we don't fully understand."

He passed it around. Each student held it briefly, feeling the weight, the inscriptions, the strange resonance.

A young woman—Helena, local aristocrat turned carrier—asked: "How does it work?"

"We don't know," Augustine admitted. "The instructions were encoded three thousand years ago and partially lost. What we know: It amplifies existing abilities but doesn't create them. Carriers with genetic memory can access more information, more clearly, more reliably. Non-carriers experience nothing."

"Is it dangerous?" asked Marcus, former Roman soldier now training as monk.

"Potentially. Overwhelming memory influx can be disorienting. Some carriers become lost in ancestral experience, struggle to maintain present identity." Augustine took the artifact back. "That's why we train contemplative practice first—learning to observe memory without being consumed by it. The *nous* technique from the Gospel of Mary, which I encoded in *Confessions*."

Recognition rippled through the group. They'd all studied *Confessions*, learned the contemplative methods. Now they understood the deeper purpose.

"The keys are tools," Augustine emphasized. "Useful, but not essential. The real treasure is the method—pattern recognition, systems thinking, encoding. These can be transmitted without artifacts. Taught person-to-person. Preserved in texts. The keys help, but losing them doesn't destroy the network."

"But we should still protect them," Helena said.

"Yes. Because future generations might need them. Especially if genetic memory weakens over time, or if network knowledge is suppressed and carriers need help recovering it." Augustine set the Pattern Eye on the table. "That's why I encoded their locations in *City of God*. Redundancy. If guardians are killed, if hiding places are forgotten, future carriers can find them."

"How?" Marcus asked.

Augustine smiled. "By learning to read for pattern. The same skill we teach for everything else."

He opened a codex to Book XV, the Jerusalem section. "Here's how it works. I'm describing Solomon's temple—orthodox historical background, required for theological argument. But watch the detail level."

He read aloud: *"The temple's foundation was laid in courses of dressed stone, each stone precisely measured. The third course on the eastern side, beneath the priests' chambers, was particularly notable..."*

"Excessive detail for theological argument," Helena observed.

"Exactly. Which signals: this contains secondary information. For readers trained in pattern recognition, excessive detail flags encoding." Augustine continued: "Now translate proportionally. Solomon's temple becomes Hippo basilica. 'Third course on the eastern side' becomes a specific location. 'Beneath priests' chambers' means the sacristy area."

"You've created a coordinate system disguised as historical description," Marcus said slowly.

"Yes. Which the Church will preserve because historical accuracy in scriptural commentary demonstrates scholarly rigor. Orthodox readers think I'm being thorough. Carriers recognize I'm providing coordinates."

He closed the codex. "This is the method at every level. Orthodox surface meaning, complete and satisfying. Pattern meaning beneath, accessible only to those asking different questions. Same text, different readings, both valid."

A young monk named Fortunatus—North African Berber, brilliant but cautious—raised his hand. "What happens if the offensive network realizes what you've done? If they decode the *City of God* and find the key locations?"

"Then they find five bronze artifacts," Augustine said calmly. "Which they can't use, because they don't have genetic memory. The keys only work for defensive carriers—that's literally genetic. They could destroy the keys, but that costs them political capital—destroying 'apostolic relics' would be controversial. And we'd still have the method, which matters more."

"So the encoding is self-protecting."

"All good encoding is. It survives hostile reading because multiple meanings are genuinely present. I'm not lying about theology to hide coordinates. The theology is real, complete, orthodox. The coordinates are additional meaning in the same text."

The class continued for hours. Augustine taught encryption methods, recognition signals, institutional navigation, survival strategies. Everything he'd learned in forty-three years as carrier and bishop.

Finally, as sunset approached, he stood carefully—age making movement difficult.

"Remember this," he said. "The network survives not because we're stronger than the offensive network, but because we're more distributed. They build vertical power structures—efficient but fragile. We build horizontal networks—less efficient but resilient. When institutions fall, they collapse. We adapt and continue."

"Even through empire's collapse?" Helena asked.

"*Especially* through empire's collapse. That's what we're designed for." Augustine smiled. "Rome is dying. The Vandals are coming. Political structures are fragmenting. This is exactly the situation the defensive network was created to survive."

"The City of God," someone murmured.

"Always," Augustine agreed.

## SCENE 3B: The Seeds of Future Methods

**429 CE, Late Summer - Scriptorium Discussion**

The evening before Augustine sealed his final letters, Possidius found him in the scriptorium examining a beautifully illuminated manuscript—a Psalter from Constantinople, decorated with elaborate margin work. Gold leaf, vermillion, azure blue, intricate geometric patterns framing the text.

"The Eastern monasteries produce remarkable work," Possidius said, admiring the craftsmanship.

"They do." Augustine traced one of the decorative elements—a series of small colored dots arranged in a triangular pattern. "Look at this. The decorator has created precise geometric arrangements. Triangles, squares, circles. Each dot placed with mathematical accuracy."

Possidius leaned closer. "I've seen similar work in manuscripts from Ireland. The monks there have developed extraordinary precision in decorative elements."

"Precision that serves beauty," Augustine agreed. "But precision that could serve other purposes." He looked up at his student. "What if we marked margins themselves? Small marks, colored inks, geometric patterns that appeared to be decoration but actually encoded information?"

Possidius considered carefully. "The risk is visibility. Any systematic marking draws attention. Wouldn't the offensive network recognize patterns?"

"Not if everyone expects beautiful manuscripts to have colored marks." Augustine gestured to the Psalter. "This manuscript is orthodox—copied in an imperial scriptorium, approved by bishops. Yet it contains hundreds of precisely placed colored dots. If such decoration becomes standard..."

"Then systematic patterns would be invisible," Possidius finished. "Hidden in plain sight among all the other decorative elements."

"Exactly." Augustine set the manuscript down. "But that culture doesn't exist yet. Not widely. Constantinople and Ireland have this tradition, but most scriptoriums in the West produce plain text. No elaborate decoration, no precise margin work."

"So we can't use this method?"

"Not yet." Augustine's tone was thoughtful. "For now, we encode in words themselves—safer, more distributed, survives copying variations. Semantic encoding works regardless of how precisely a scribe reproduces decoration."

"But someday..." Possidius traced the triangular dot pattern in the margin. "If manuscript culture evolves. If elaborate decoration becomes standard practice across all scriptoriums. If colored inks and geometric precision become expected rather than exceptional..."

"Then yes," Augustine agreed. "Then you could hide precise information in decoration. Locations, coordinates, dates, connections. A properly trained scribe could encode vast amounts of data in what appears to be ornamental flourishes."

"We'd need physical templates," Possidius said, mind working through the implications. "Some kind of key or guide that shows which marks to read, which to ignore. Without it, the patterns would look random."

"Metal templates," Augustine suggested. "With apertures or viewing windows. Align the template with specific anchor points in the text, and only the meaningful marks appear through the openings. Simple iron or bronze, disguised as bookmarks or reliquary decorations."

They both studied the illuminated Psalter in silence.

"You're giving this to the next generation," Possidius finally said. "As possibility, not instruction."

"I'm observing that technology creates opportunities," Augustine corrected. "My generation encodes in meanings—double-layer semantics that survive any copying method. Your generation will have access to tools we don't. Printing might someday make manuscripts obsolete, or decoration might become standardized, or some other development we can't predict."

"Adapt the encoding to the available technology."

"Always." Augustine smiled slightly. "That's the core principle. The offensive network tries to control technology to maintain power. We adapt *to* technology to preserve knowledge. They centralize. We distribute. They build rigidity. We build flexibility."

"The City of God," Possidius said softly.

"Rebuilding itself with whatever materials each generation has available." Augustine closed the Psalter carefully. "If your generation—or Generation 56, or 57—finds that manuscript decoration has become standardized and precise, then yes. Develop encoding methods that use that precision. Create physical keys. Train scribes to place marks with geometric accuracy."

"But only if the cultural context supports it."

"Exactly. Otherwise you're creating patterns that stand out rather than blend in." Augustine stood slowly, age making movement difficult. "The encoding method must match the era. What works in Constantine's empire won't work in barbarian kingdoms. What works when manuscripts are rare won't work when they're common. Adapt always."

Possidius carefully stored the Psalter. "I'll remember this. When the time comes—when manuscript culture has evolved enough—I'll ensure the network explores this method."

"Or your students will. Or their students." Augustine moved toward the door. "The pattern continues. Each generation adds what their era makes possible. That's how knowledge survives across centuries."

As they left the scriptorium, Possidius paused, looking back at the shelves of manuscripts. Someday, he thought, these decorated margins might carry more than beauty. They might carry the locations of every hidden archive, every network contact, every piece of knowledge the offensive network tried to suppress.

Someday, when the technology and culture aligned.

Someday, when illuminated manuscripts filled every monastery library in Christendom.

Someday, the dots themselves would speak.

But not today. Not yet.

Today, they still encoded in words. In meanings. In the interpretations that arose from asking different questions of the same text.

Tomorrow belonged to the next generation.

And the generation after that.

The pattern continued.

## SCENE 4: The Vandals Arrive

**430 CE, May - Siege of Hippo**

The Vandal army arrived in spring. Thirty thousand warriors under King Gaiseric—disciplined, organized, terrifying. They surrounded Hippo, cutting off supply routes, settling in for long siege.

Augustine watched from the basilica tower. The army stretched to the horizon—tents, siege engines, cavalry units. This was no raiding party. This was conquest.

"They'll offer terms," Possidius said, standing beside him. "Convert to Arianism, accept Vandal authority, maintain local administration."

"Terms we can't accept," Augustine replied. "I'm too visible, too connected to Nicene orthodoxy. They'll want me gone—killed or exiled."

"Then we evacuate you. Ship to Sicily or Carthage."

"No." Augustine's voice was firm. "I'm seventy-six. Dying anyway. Running would demoralize the city and accomplish nothing. I stay."

"Teacher—"

"I stay," Augustine repeated. "But you prepare to leave. When the city falls—not if, when—you take the Pattern Eye and go. Build the next phase."

"I can't just abandon—"

"You're not abandoning. You're continuing." Augustine turned from the tower view. "This is what succession means. I hold position while I can. When I can't, you take over. Clean transfer, no gap in network continuity."

Possidius was silent, jaw tight.

"Where will you go?" Augustine asked.

"Calama initially. Assess from there. The Vandals might install their own bishops in major cities but ignore smaller sees. I could maintain cover as bishop while rebuilding network connections."

"Good. And the Pattern Eye?"

"Hidden until things stabilize. Then either to Constantinople with Atticus or to Gaul with Cassian—whichever route is safer when the time comes."

"Good," Augustine repeated. They descended from the tower, Augustine moving slowly. His breathing was labored—the lung infection was getting worse.

In the basilica, he conducted liturgy as always. The church was packed—people seeking comfort as the siege tightened. He preached on Psalm 46:

*"God is our refuge and strength, a very present help in trouble. Therefore we will not fear though the earth gives way, though the mountains be moved into the heart of the sea..."*

Orthodox meaning: Trust God despite disaster.

Network meaning: The pattern persists through collapse. Physical cities fall, but the City of God continues.

After liturgy, he met with the city council. They wanted his advice—resist or negotiate?

"Negotiate," Augustine said quietly. "You can't win militarily. Gaiseric has numerical advantage and will outlast any siege. Better to get terms now, while you have leverage, than after starvation forces surrender."

"But they're Arian heretics!" one councilor protested.

"They're Christians who disagree about Christology," Augustine corrected. "Theological difference, not pagan opposition. You can live under Arian rule. Thousands already do in other captured cities."

"What about you?" another councilor asked. "Will they let you remain as bishop?"

"No. And I'm dying anyway. Don't factor me into your calculations." Augustine looked around the table. "Your task is protecting the people of Hippo. Mine is protecting the legacy. Those require different strategies."

He saw them process this—the famous Augustine of Hippo, calmly discussing his own irrelevance.

"The books," he continued. "The library. The liturgical objects. Whatever can be evacuated, evacuate now. Before surrender terms are negotiated. Get it to Carthage, then disperse to other cities. Don't let knowledge be destroyed."

"The Vandals might see that as provocation—"

"The Vandals will be too busy looting to track library materials," Augustine said bluntly. "And books aren't military threat. Get them out."

The council agreed. Over the next week, ships quietly evacuated codices, church treasures, essential records. The Vandals, focused on military encirclement, didn't stop the sea traffic.

Augustine's complete works went out in multiple copies. *Confessions*, *City of God*, all the treatises, all the commentaries. Distributed to Constantinople, Jerusalem, Rome, Gaul.

The encoding survived.

The institution was dying, but the text persisted.

## SCENE 5: Final Conversation

**430 CE, August - Bishop's Residence**

Augustine was bedridden now. The lung infection had progressed to pneumonia. He could barely breathe, certainly couldn't leave his room.

Possidius stayed close, managing the diocese while Augustine died.

One afternoon, in a moment of clarity, Augustine called him closer.

"The biography," he whispered. "How far?"

"Nearly complete. Your childhood through the *City of God*. Still need to write the ending."

"Write it carefully," Augustine managed. "Make it work on both levels."

"I will."

Augustine's breath rattled. "The mistakes. Include them."

"I said I will."

"No—listen." Augustine gripped Possidius's hand with surprising strength. "Include the *cost*. What it costs to encode. What it costs to compromise. What it costs to serve institution while protecting pattern."

"I'm listening."

"Pelagius..." Augustine coughed, wincing. "...haunts me. He was right about free will, about responsibility, about human capacity. I condemned him to protect the network. But the cost was truth. Some truth, anyway. Nuance."

"You preserved his methodology in the encoding—"

"I know what I did!" Augustine's voice was sharp, then softened. "Sorry. But don't let carriers think encoding is clean. It's not. It's compromise. Necessary compromise, but compromise nonetheless. They need to know that."

Possidius nodded, throat tight.

"Jerome haunts me too," Augustine continued, quieter. "He spent seventy years building Christendom. Believed Christian empire would preserve truth. And it failed—collapsing within a century. But his Vulgate survived. His translations persist. He's not wrong that texts survive institutions. Just wrong about which institutions matter."

"You wrote that in the *City*—"

"I wrote it for him," Augustine confirmed. "Knowing he'd read it. Knowing it would break his heart. And knowing he'd agree, because he was honest enough to see truth even when it hurt. That's what made him defensive carrier despite everything."

He coughed again, longer. Possidius held a cloth to his mouth—it came away bloody.

"Write this," Augustine whispered. "That encoding works because multiple meanings are real. I'm not lying when I write orthodoxy. It's genuinely orthodox. And I'm not lying when I encode pattern. It's genuinely pattern. Both meanings inhabit the same words."

"The City of God."

"And the City of Man. Both real. Both present. Woven together until judgment separates them." Augustine smiled slightly. "If judgment comes. Might just continue forever—two cities, eternal wrestling match. Would be very like God to make it permanent tension rather than final victory."

"That's heresy," Possidius said, smiling.

"That's realism," Augustine countered. "Offensive and defensive networks have coexisted for three thousand years. Why would it suddenly end? More likely it continues—dialectic, driving both sides to evolve."

His breathing became more labored. Possidius gave him water.

"The Pattern Eye," Augustine said after a moment. "You know where it is."

"Yes."

"Take it when I die. Not before—I want to die with it nearby, full genetic memory active. But after, take it immediately. Don't wait for burial or ceremony. Just take it and go."

"Where?"

"You'll know when the time comes. Trust your pattern recognition." Augustine's eyes were distant now, accessing genetic memory. "I can see... so many versions of this moment. Carriers dying, transmitting to successors. Back to the beginning. Tausret teaching Nefer. Nefer teaching her daughter. Forward to... I can't see clearly. Future branches. But the pattern continues. Always continues."

"Teacher," Possidius said carefully. "Should I activate it now? Let you access memory more fully before—"

"No." Augustine's voice was firm despite weakness. "Natural process. I'm accessing what I need. More would be overwhelming, and I don't have strength." He paused. "But stay close. When I'm gone, activate it once. Full power. See what it shows you. My final gift."

"What will it show?"

"I don't know. Different for each carrier. But it will show you something you need for what comes next."

They sat in silence as afternoon became evening. The sounds of siege drifted through the window—distant shouts, cavalry movements, the machinery of war encircling the dying city.

"Tell me," Augustine whispered. "The next generation. Helena, Marcus, Fortunatus. Are they ready?"

"Yes. You trained them well."

"Not me. We trained them. Network trained them." Augustine smiled. "That's the difference. Offensive network depends on individual genius—Constantine, Jerome, whoever. When the genius dies, crisis. We distribute training. Any carrier can teach any other carrier. I die, you continue. You die, Generation 56 continues. Pattern persists regardless of individuals."

"The City that rebuilds itself."

"Exactly." Augustine's breathing was very shallow now. "Possidius?"

"Yes?"

"Thank you. For learning. For staying. For continuing."

"Thank you for teaching."

"Not me. Monica taught me. I just transmitted." Augustine's eyes closed. "Pattern continues. Tell them pattern continues."

His breathing grew irregular. Possidius held his hand, keeping vigil.

Outside, the Vandals tightened their siege lines.

Inside, the greatest theologian of the age died quietly, genetic memory fading back into the ancestral chain.

Augustine of Hippo. Generation 54. Defensive carrier for forty-three years.

The encoding was complete.

The transmission was finished.

The pattern continued.

## SCENE 6: The Transfer

**430 CE, August 28 - The Night Augustine Dies**

Augustine died at sunset. Possidius stayed with the body, keeping vigil as tradition required. But after midnight, when the household slept, he moved.

The basilica was dark, empty. Under siege, night vigils had ceased—too dangerous. Possidius entered through the bishop's private entrance, carrying a lamp.

The reliquary was in the side chapel. He knew exactly which stone—third course from the floor, eastern wall, beneath the window. He'd watched Augustine check it periodically over the years, never asking questions until he needed to know.

The stone was loose, designed to be removed by hand. Behind it, a hollowed space lined with lead—protection from moisture and decay.

The Pattern Eye rested inside, wrapped in silk.

Possidius took it carefully, feeling the weight, the inscribed bronze surface. Even through the silk, he could sense something—not quite vibration, not quite warmth. Presence.

He'd never held it before. Augustine had been reluctant to let anyone handle it, protective of the artifact that Monica had trusted to him.

Now it was Possidius's turn.

*Activate it once,* Augustine had said. *Full power. See what it shows you.*

Possidius unwrapped the silk, holding the bronze cylinder directly. Cold metal, surprisingly heavy. The inscriptions were archaic—Egyptian hieroglyphs mixed with something older, pre-literate symbols that predated writing.

He closed his eyes and focused, using the *nous* technique Augustine had taught him. The contemplative state between soul and spirit. Where genetic memory surfaced.

The Pattern Eye activated.

Memory flooded in.

He saw himself—Possidius, thirty years earlier, first meeting Augustine. Hearing about the network for the first time. Deciding to commit.

He saw Augustine—younger, at his mother's deathbed, receiving the Pattern Eye. Monica's hand trembling as she transferred the artifact.

He saw Monica—as a young woman, learning from Marcus Publius. The first time she accessed genetic memory. Her shock and wonder.

He saw Marcus Publius—in Rome, writing while Augustine of Dacia dictated. Taking notes on Jesus's teaching. Preserving methodology.

He saw Jesus—teaching Mary Magdalene, showing her how to access forty-two generations of memory. "The *nous* sees the vision."

He saw Mary Magdalene—young, brilliant, learning pattern recognition from Jesus. Understanding the seven collapse modes. Teaching others.

He saw the bloodline stretching back—generation after generation, carriers preserving pattern through Roman expansion, Greek philosophy, Persian wars, Bronze Age collapse.

He saw Nefer—first defensive carrier, bronze age Egypt, learning from Tausret. Recording everything. Creating the methodology that would persist three thousand years.

He saw Tausret—Pharaoh of Egypt, 1177 BCE, standing over bronze artifacts. "Seven keys. Seven lines. The pattern must survive."

The memory crashed back to present.

Possidius gasped, releasing the Pattern Eye. His hands shook.

Fifty-five generations. Three thousand two hundred thirty-six years. Continuous transmission from Tausret to him.
