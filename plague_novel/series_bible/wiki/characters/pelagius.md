# Pelagius

British monk; defensive network teacher who taught the methodology **openly** (free will, human capacity for self-transformation) and was made a martyr to it by [Augustine](augustine_of_hippo.md)'s strategic public condemnation. His "Pelagianism" was direct defensive teaching; Augustine's anti-Pelagian works were the encoding-survival counter-move.

- **Bloodline:** Gen 57. Weak genetic memory — "fragments, flashes, intuitions."
- **Dates:** ~360–418 CE. Exiled after Council of Carthage (416 CE); dies in Alexandria (418 CE).
- **First appears:** Book 5 chapter 3 (background); major in chapter 6 — "The Pelagian Controversy"
- **Raw sources:** `../../book_5_augustine_protocol/manuscript/chapters/chapter_06_pelagian_controversy.md`

## Book 5 role

- **Open teaching:** "Humans can choose to prevent collapse through free will and moral effort" — defensive methodology delivered without theological cover
- **Strategic threat:** his openness draws offensive network attention to all defensive operatives, including Augustine
- **Augustine's public condemnation (411–418 CE):** maintains Augustine's cover inside the Church; allows Pelagius's *teaching* (re-encoded as "grace doctrine") to survive embedded in Augustine's anti-Pelagian treatises
- **Two letters to Augustine** (chapter 6, scenes 3 and 5):
  - First letter: confronts Augustine with understanding of the betrayal
  - Second letter (death letter, 418 CE): forgiveness, recognizes the dialectic — *"I forgave you"*
- **Legacy:** letters preserved in sealed container with Pattern Eye, hidden by Augustine for future carriers. His students go underground, preserve teachings.

## Continuity notes

- Augustine's later assessment to Possidius: *"Pelagius's sacrifice buys you time to do real work."*
- The condemnation was theater for survival, not theological enmity. Augustine privately agreed with Pelagius's substance; the encoding through "orthodox grace theology" preserved the methodology while Pelagius's name was sacrificed.
- Pelagius's weak genetic memory means he understood the pattern more through intellectual recognition than ancestral access — making him in some ways braver and in some ways more naive than Augustine.
