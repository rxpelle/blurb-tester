## THE YEAR 1177 BCE

### Historical Significance
- **1177 BCE**: Year Ramesses III defeated the Sea Peoples in decisive naval/land battle
- **Iconic date** in Bronze Age collapse scholarship (Eric Cline's "1177 B.C.: The Year Civilization Collapsed")
- **Tausret's reign**: c. 1191-1189 BCE (ruled near end of Egypt's New Kingdom)
- **Novel timeline**: Tausret dies 1177 BCE, triggering Nefertari to create Genesis Protocol

### The Collapse Context
- **Sudden and violent**: Sharp material decline across Eastern Mediterranean
- **Culturally disruptive**: Affected Egypt, Anatolia, Aegean, eastern Libya, Balkans
- **Perfect storm**: Drought, famine, earthquakes, internal rebellions, Sea Peoples invasions
- **Not single cause**: Modern scholars reject simple explanations (Sea Peoples as scapegoat)

## THE SEA PEOPLES

### Who They Were
- **Confederacy of naval raiders**: Harried Mediterranean coast c. 1276-1178 BCE
- **Groups**: Shardana, Shekelesh, Tjekker, Denyen, Weshesh, Peleset (Philistines)
- **Victims AND aggressors**: Likely refugees themselves fleeing collapse elsewhere
- **Evidence**: Egyptian texts/illustrations (Medinet Habu temple), Hittite sources, archaeological data

### Military Threat
- **1177 BCE battle**: Ramesses III fought both land and naval engagements
- **Egyptian victory**: Egypt defeated large onslaught but suffered long-term destabilization
- **Propaganda element**: Egyptian sources one-sided; portrayed threat as existential

**Sources**: [Sea Peoples - World History Encyclopedia](https://www.worldhistory.org/Sea_Peoples/), [Sea Peoples - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_Peoples), [ANE TODAY - Sea Peoples](https://www.asor.org/anetoday/2016/09/who-sea-peoples/)

## REFUGEE CRISIS AND POPULATION DECLINE

### Scale of Movement
- **Large-scale migration**: Waves of displaced people across Mediterranean
- **Cyprus example**: Mycenaean refugees arrived ~1200 BCE fleeing Aegean turmoil
- **Resource strain**: Refugees exacerbated demands on local communities (land, food, water)
- **Already vulnerable**: Populations suffering from malnutrition, environmental stress

### Demographic Collapse
- **Egyptian evidence**: Bas-reliefs show armies clashing, famine-ravaged cities, depopulated countrysides
- **Crop failures**: Nile flow disruptions caused widespread famine
- **Political instability**: New Kingdom Egypt fell into chaos following population reduction
- **Abandoned settlements**: Archaeological evidence of sudden city abandonment

**Visual Evidence**: Egyptian hieroglyphic texts portray graphic suffering

**Sources**: [Late Bronze Age collapse - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age_collapse), [Environmental Roots of the Late Bronze Age Crisis - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3743901/), [Bronze Age Collapse - World History Encyclopedia](https://www.worldhistory.org/Bronze_Age_Collapse/)

## PI-RAMESSES: THE CAPITAL CITY

### Overview (1177 BCE Context)
- **Built by**: Ramesses II (1279-1213 BCE) at Qantir (near old Avaris site)
- **Population**: Over 300,000 (one of largest cities in ancient Egypt)
- **Status 1177 BCE**: Still functioning as capital (36 years after Ramesses II's death)
- **Later decline**: Pelusiac Nile branch silted up c. 1060 BCE; capital moved to Tanis

### City Layout
- **Size**: 18 km² (6 km long × 3 km wide)
- **Grid pattern**: Rigid street layout in mansion precinct (western riverside area)
- **Disorderly east**: Houses and workshops less organized
- **Central temple**: Huge religious complex at city center

### Infrastructure
- **Venice of Egypt**: Many canals and lakes (Austrian archaeological team)
- **Four quarters**: Each dedicated to deity (Amon, Wadjet, Seth, Astarte)
- **King cult**: Dominant worship in city center
- **Buildings**: Temples, palaces, administrative offices, residential areas

**Novel Use**: Nefertari witnesses refugee suffering in this massive, canal-laced capital

**Sources**: [Pi-Ramesses - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi-Ramesses), [Pi-Ramesses - World History Encyclopedia](https://www.worldhistory.org/Pi-Ramesses/), [Per Ramessu - Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Per-Ramessu)

## ANCIENT EGYPTIAN PHYSICIANS (19th Dynasty)

### Professional Status
- **19th Dynasty (1292-1189 BCE)**: Physicians at Houses of Life (Per Ankh) enjoyed:
  - Medical insurance
  - Pensions
  - Sick leave
- **Remarkably advanced** system for physician welfare

### Specialization
- **Highly specialized**: Herodotus wrote "each physician is a healer of one disease and no more"
- **Specialists included**:
  - Ophthalmologists (eye diseases)
  - Gastroenterologists (digestive disorders)
  - Proctologists
  - Dentists
  - "Doctor who supervises butchers"
  - "Inspector of liquids"

### Medical Knowledge
- **Anatomical understanding**:
  - Aware of pulse and its connection to heart
  - Vague idea of cardiac system (Edwin Smith Papyrus)
  - Advanced knowledge of anatomy, surgery
- **Treatment areas**: Dental, gynecological, gastrointestinal, urinary disorders

### Medical Texts
- **Edwin Smith Papyrus**: Textbook on surgery with anatomical observations, examination, diagnosis, treatment, prognosis
- **Ebers Papyrus (1550 BCE)**: 328 ingredients, 876 prescriptions
- **Blend of approaches**: Scientific methods + spiritual practices (herbal remedies, surgery, incantations)

### Health Practices
- **Prevention advice**: Washing/shaving body to prevent infections, dietary care, avoiding raw fish and "unclean" animals

**Novel Use**: Nefertari is highly trained physician-scribe with specialized knowledge (genetic memory encoding would be within her theoretical wheelhouse given understanding of heredity, anatomy, medical texts)

**Sources**: [Ancient Egyptian medicine - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_medicine), [Traditional ancient Egyptian medicine - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8459052/), [Egyptian Medicine - World History Encyclopedia](https://www.worldhistory.org/Egyptian_Medicine/)
