### 2. Epistemology Accessibility Barrier (Lines 186-192)

**Issue**: "Empirical vs. ideological epistemology" is dense jargon that stops readers cold.

**Current Text** (approximate location):
> "The question is one of epistemology. Should genetic memory encode ideology—defensive values, predetermined lessons—or just empirical data? Do we trust future carriers to learn from experience, or do we guide what they learn?"

**Revised Approach**: Show the debate through concrete examples FIRST, name it SECOND.

**Suggested Rewrite**:
```
Aristomache's question cut to the heart of everything. "When our children dream of Pylos burning, what should they inherit? The fact that the palace fell—or our interpretation that centralized systems always fail?"

Silence in the safe house.

Taharqa spoke carefully. "If we encode our conclusions, we're no different than Amenhotep. We're controlling what future generations can think."

"But if we encode raw experience without context," another carrier countered, "they might learn the wrong lessons. They might see Mycenae's fall and conclude that literacy itself is dangerous, that writing systems cause collapse."

"Then they'd be wrong," Aristomache said. "And they'd discover that error when they tried to rebuild without records and found themselves repeating our mistakes. Learning from failure is learning."

Nefertari listened, her ancient eyes tracking the debate. This was the question she'd wrestled with for seventy-seven years. Empirical versus ideological encoding. Did you preserve evidence and trust interpretation—or did you preserve lessons and hope future carriers wouldn't need to learn the hard way?
```

**Why This Works**:
- Concrete example (Pylos burning) grounds abstract concept
- Characters debate using accessible language
- Technical term appears LAST, after readers understand the stakes
- Still efficient (one page), but dramatically clearer

### 3. Emotional Weight: Taharqa's Grief (Add One Scene)

**Issue**: Three palace collapses = Holocaust-level trauma, but chapter treats it matter-of-factly.

**Location**: After Taharqa meets Nefertari, before network introduction (around line 100-120)

**Suggested Addition** (200-250 words):

```
Nefertari studied him across the rough wooden table. "You've lost everyone."

Taharqa's throat closed. He'd been holding it together—the voyage, the arrival, the genetic memory dreams—by focusing on survival logistics. But hearing it stated plainly broke something loose.

"Not everyone," he managed. "Two hundred seventeen made it to the ships. Out of..." He couldn't finish. Twenty thousand? Thirty? How many had lived in Tiryns when the end came?

"The palace scribe who taught me Linear B," he said instead. "He was seventy-two. He couldn't run fast enough when the burning started. I heard him screaming."

Nefertari's hand moved across the table, not quite touching his. The gesture of someone who'd seen too much loss to offer empty comfort.

"My wife." Taharqa hadn't meant to say it. "She was in the women's quarters when the walls came down. I never found her body."

The safe house was quiet except for the distant sound of Egyptian market vendors and the cry of unfamiliar birds. Greece was three weeks behind them by ship. It might as well be three lifetimes.

"That memory," Nefertari said quietly, "will encode. Your children will inherit the grief, the terror, the moment you heard screaming and couldn't help. They won't know whose screaming. They won't know why. But they'll know the shape of collapse."

"Is that supposed to comfort me?"

"No," she said. "It's supposed to teach them."
```

**Why This Works**:
- One scene, 250 words, doesn't derail pacing
- Makes stakes visceral through specific detail (palace scribe, wife)
- Connects to genetic memory theme
- Shows Nefertari's philosophy through action
- Readers now FEEL why the epistemology debate matters
