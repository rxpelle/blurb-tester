**Generation:** 27 (Theophrastus of Athens, age 35) and 27 (Aristotle of Stagira, age 49)
**POV Character:** Theophrastus

## SCENE 1: The Wrong Teacher

Theophrastus had been watching Aristotle for twelve years, waiting for the ancestral dreams to surface.

They never would.

The network's models had been wrong again—just like with Socrates three generations ago. They'd predicted someone in Athens' philosophical schools. One of their own. Someone with the instinct for collapse. Systematic thinking. The ability to feel disaster coming before it arrived.

All the signs had pointed to Aristotle.

But Aristotle wasn't one of them. He was just... brilliant. Frighteningly, impossibly brilliant without needing ancestral trauma written in his blood. His empirical method, his categorical logic, his systematic analysis of everything from politics to biology—all arrived at through pure intellectual discipline.

No inherited dreams. No bloodline. No secret purpose.

Just philosophy.

Theophrastus stood now in the Lyceum courtyard, watching his teacher pace while lecturing. Aristotle's students—dozens of them, from across the Mediterranean—hung on every word about systematic observation and logical deduction.

Teaching anyone who could afford tuition to see the world clearly.

Exactly what Xanthe had predicted seventy years ago, when she'd studied with Plato and refused recruitment. Philosophy schools were democratizing the knowledge. Making foresight teachable without genetic privilege.

Their whole way of working was becoming obsolete.

But that wasn't what worried Theophrastus today.

What worried him was the young man who'd just entered the courtyard.

Macedonian. Sixteen, maybe seventeen, but moving with the economy of a trained fighter—no wasted motion, weight centered, eyes tracking threat vectors before they settled on anything else. Two guards flanked him and both looked nervous, which told Theophrastus everything. Men paid to protect this boy were afraid of him.

He crossed the courtyard and the students parted without being asked. Not deference—instinct. The way a crowd parts for a predator moving through it, bodies reading danger before minds catch up.

Theophrastus' blood sang with recognition. Not the warm hum of finding a fellow carrier. Something sharper. The ancestral alarm that said *this one is different, this one is dangerous, this one will reshape the world or burn it.*

*One of us. But which side?*

The young man approached Aristotle, spoke quietly. Even his voice carried that quality—low, controlled, the kind of voice that didn't need volume because no one would dream of talking over it. Theophrastus caught fragments: "...my father Philip... tutoring position... prepare me to rule..."

Alexander. Prince of Macedon. Sixteen years old.

And unmistakably one of them.

## SCENE 2: The Offensive Network's Prize

**Two weeks later. Mieza, Macedon.**

Theophrastus traveled north under false pretenses—officially to study Macedonian flora for Aristotle's botanical research, actually to investigate Alexander.

What he found terrified him.

Alexander wasn't just a carrier. He was the other side's masterpiece. Generation 27, descended from Amenhotep's carefully managed bloodline, genetic memory activated at age twelve, trained in strategic thinking since childhood.

And they had positioned him perfectly.

Theophrastus discovered this in the royal library, where Alexander kept his private scrolls. Genetic memory recorded in precise detail: Bronze Age collapses, Nefertari's founding protocol, twenty-seven generations engineering survival through empire and control.

But also newer dreams. Fresher ones, from his own father's generation.

*How to build an empire that endures.*

*How to unify fragmented city-states under single authority.*

*How to use military conquest to stop the systemic failures that killed Bronze Age civilization.*

Alexander wasn't learning to protect civilization through compassion and early warning. He was learning to protect it through conquest and command. Through building an empire so vast, so unified, so tightly governed that regional crises couldn't cascade into civilizational death.

Amenhotep's philosophy on a scale never attempted before.

"You're from the other side. Athens."

Theophrastus spun. Alexander stood in the doorway, blocking it completely. Sixteen years old, but built like a weapon—compact, coiled, a hunting knife already in his hand, held low against his thigh the way someone trained in close combat holds a blade. Not threatening. Just ready.

The recognition between them hit like a physical blow. Theophrastus felt his pupils dilate, felt every muscle in his body lock tight. Carrier facing carrier. The ancestral dreams screaming warnings through eight hundred years of blood.

Neither of them moved. Neither breathed.

Alexander's eyes—one brown, one pale blue, a detail the histories would obsess over—tracked Theophrastus the way a lion tracks movement in tall grass. Calculating distance. Measuring threat. Behind those mismatched eyes, Theophrastus could feel the genetic memory churning, recognizing him as *other*, as *enemy*, as something that needed to be dealt with.

Theophrastus' gaze flicked to the window. Three body-lengths. Stone ledge, then a fifteen-foot drop into the garden. Survivable. Barely.

"Don't." Alexander's voice was quiet, almost gentle. He shifted his weight slightly and the doorway shrank. "I'd catch you before you reached it."

He would, too. Theophrastus could see that clearly.

"Generation twenty-seven," Theophrastus said, keeping his hands visible. His voice came out steadier than he deserved. "Studying with Aristotle. Interested in how philosophy teaches what our bloodlines carry."

"And spying on me." Alexander smiled, and the smile was the worst part—warm, boyish, the kind of smile that made men love him. The knife hadn't moved. "Your people want to know why mine put me on the Macedonian throne."

"Are you going to tell me?"

Alexander studied him for a long moment. Then, with deliberate slowness, he tucked the knife into his belt. Not sheathed. Tucked. The difference mattered.

"I'm going to show you. Because eight hundred forty-two years into this experiment, I'm going to prove which approach actually works." He gestured to the scrolls with the hand that had held the blade. "Nefertari believed in scattering knowledge. Compassion. Quiet intervention before disaster struck. My ancestors believed in unity. Strategy. And I'm going to demonstrate that on a continental scale."

"By conquering the known world?"

"By *unifying* it." Alexander moved into the room, and Theophrastus had to fight the instinct to step back. The young prince occupied space the way a fire did—drawing everything toward him even as it consumed. "One empire. One system. One authority that can see the cracks forming and seal them before everything fragments into regional catastrophes." His voice dropped, intimate and absolute, the way a man speaks when he's describing something he's already decided. "The Bronze Age Collapse happened because no one was in charge. City-states competing instead of cooperating. Regional failures cascading because no one had the authority to act."

He stopped close enough that Theophrastus could see the old scar on his jawline, a training wound from childhood. Close enough to smell iron and horse sweat and something underneath—the particular scent of someone whose body runs hot, whose blood is always up.

"I'm going to fix that."

Theophrastus felt cold settling into his bones. Not from the words. From the certainty behind them. This was not a boy making plans. This was a force of nature describing what was already in motion.

"The compact says both sides operate in parallel. Neither interferes with the other's work. You're planning to—"

"I'm planning to win. Definitively." Alexander reached out and gripped Theophrastus' shoulder—hard, the fingers digging in, a gesture that was half fraternal and half territorial. "Prove that unity holds things together better than scattered goodwill. And the beautiful part? Aristotle's teaching me to do it. A philosopher with no bloodline at all, training me to think. Philosophy and inheritance combined."

He released the grip, leaving bruised warmth behind.

"We're unstoppable."

## SCENE 3: The Teacher's Dilemma

Theophrastus found Aristotle that evening in the garden, cataloguing plant specimens by moonlight. The philosopher wore his usual work clothes—a worn himation draped loosely over his shoulders, a simple chiton beneath, both stained with ink and plant sap. His fingers were dirty from handling specimens, and a half-eaten dinner sat forgotten on the stone bench: bread and olives, some cheese, a cup of watered wine that attracted moths. Aristotle worked like this most evenings, preferring the garden's quiet to the symposium conversations happening inside where his wealthier students drank and debated.

"Alexander is a carrier," Theophrastus said without preamble. "Amenhotep's line. Generation twenty-seven. Bred specifically to inherit Macedonia and impose order through conquest."

Aristotle looked up slowly. "I know."

"You—what?"

"I've known for three years. Since he was thirteen and the dreams first surfaced—you could see it in the way he thought, the way he saw threats before anyone else." Aristotle set down his stylus. "Did you think I wouldn't notice? I've been teaching carriers for decades. I know the signs."

Theophrastus stared. "You're training a conqueror. Do you understand what that means for the experiment?"

"I understand I'm teaching the most brilliant student I've ever encountered to think systematically, observe empirically, and see crises before they become catastrophes. Whether he uses that knowledge for conquest or compassion isn't my responsibility."

"Yes it is! You're a philosopher. You teach ethics. And you're preparing him to violate the compact—to prove force superior to cooperation."

Aristotle was quiet for a long moment. "The compact says both sides operate in parallel for three thousand years. But it doesn't say they operate in isolation. Alexander will conquer. That's inevitable—carrier or not, dreams or just ambition, Macedonia is expanding regardless. I can either teach him to conquer wisely, with foresight, or let him conquer blindly. Which serves humanity better?"

"Neither! You should be teaching him not to conquer at all!"

"Should I?" Aristotle's voice hardened. "Athens tried democracy. Failed. Sparta tried military discipline. Failed. Persia tried imperial administration. Failed. Every system your people carefully tended through quiet intervention eventually fragments anyway. Maybe Alexander's approach—one empire, one authority, real stability at continental scale—maybe that's what humanity actually needs."

Theophrastus felt the ground shifting under his assumptions. "You sound like you agree with him."

"I sound like a philosopher who's spent forty years studying political systems and watching them fail in the same patterns. Your side stops small failures. Congratulations. But you haven't stopped the *cycle itself*—just delayed the individual instances. Alexander thinks he can break it entirely through unification. Maybe he's right."

"Or maybe he'll create the biggest collapse in history when his empire fragments after his death."

"Then that's an experiment worth running." Aristotle returned to his plant specimens. "Eight hundred forty-two years of quiet tending. Time to test whether unity at scale actually works. Let the experiment continue. See which approach survives."

## SCENE 4: The Betrayal (The Twist)

**Six months later. Athens.**

Theophrastus reported back to the council with devastating news.

"Aristotle isn't just training Alexander. He's actively supporting the other side's strategy. Believes a unified empire might work better than anything we've done. He's abandoned neutrality."

The council chamber went quiet for exactly three heartbeats. Then it detonated.

"Kill him." The Phoenician carrier stood so fast his chair scraped stone. "Before he crosses into Persia. Before this becomes unstoppable."

"We don't kill. The Protocol Amendment—"

"The Protocol Amendment was written when the worst threat was a regional famine. This boy is going to burn the entire eastern Mediterranean." The Phoenician's hand slammed the table. Wine cups jumped. "Can we stop Alexander's conquest any other way? Military intervention? We don't have armies. Diplomatic pressure? His father just died and his generals are hungry for war. There is no other way."

"He's one of us. The compact forbids—"

"The compact assumes both sides honor it!" A Cretan carrier—a woman named Phila who'd spent twenty years embedding physicians in Aegean trade routes—was on her feet now too. "If they're using Alexander to prove conquest works, they've already violated the spirit of the agreement. We owe them nothing."

"This is exactly how it starts," Theophrastus said. "One exception. One justified killing. And then what? We become an assassination network?"

"We become *alive*." The Phoenician leaned across the table, veins standing in his neck. "Every city I've spent my life protecting sits in the path of that army. Every healer, every teacher, every safe house from Tyre to Babylon. You want to sit in Athens and philosophize while he rolls over everything we've built?"

A Sicilian carrier, old and scarred, spoke from the back. "I'll do it myself. I don't need the council's permission. Give me two men and access to the Macedonian court. The boy drinks. He celebrates. One night is all I need."

The room fractured. Half the carriers were talking at once—some backing the Sicilian, some shouting him down. Phila grabbed Theophrastus' arm and hissed, "If we don't vote now, he'll act alone. Others will follow. We lose control entirely."

"ENOUGH."

The voice cut through the noise like a blade through cloth. An older carrier—Generation 25, Egyptian branch—rose from his seat with the deliberate calm of a man who had seen this exact argument play out in his ancestral dreams.

"Sit down. All of you."

The Phoenician didn't sit. "With respect, elder, you're not the one whose cities will burn."

"My cities burned three hundred years before yours were built." The Egyptian held his gaze until the Phoenician looked away. Then he turned to the Sicilian. "You. You'll do it yourself. And when the other side retaliates—because they will—whose cities burn then?"

The Sicilian said nothing.

"I need to tell you something about Alexander. Something the other side hasn't disclosed."

The room went still. Not calm—taut, the way a rope goes still before it snaps.

"Alexander isn't just their prize student. He's their test. They *want* us to kill him. They want to see if we'll violate the Protocol Amendment to stop him. If we'll murder one of our own to keep his side from winning. Because if we do—if we embrace assassination—then we prove their philosophy correct. That force beats compassion. That when catastrophe really threatens, everyone abandons principles for pragmatism."

"Horseshit." The Phoenician, still standing. "That's a story we tell ourselves to justify cowardice."

"Is it?" The Egyptian turned to face him fully. "Then tell me—you've dreamed the futures. What happens after Alexander dies by our hand? Do the Macedonian generals go home? Does the army dissolve? Or does someone worse pick up the crown, someone without the dreams, without even the *pretense* of unification—and conquer anyway, out of pure revenge?"

The silence that followed was ugly. Theophrastus watched the Phoenician's jaw work, watched him searching for an answer that wasn't there.

"It's a trap," Theophrastus said quietly. "Let Alexander conquer, and they win through demonstration. Kill Alexander, and they win philosophically by proving we're no different from them."

"So what do we do?" Phila asked. "Just watch?"

"I'm calling a vote," the Egyptian said. "Act, or observe. But know this—if we act, we become something we cannot un-become."

The vote was close. Closer than Theophrastus wanted to admit—nine to seven, with two abstentions. The Sicilian voted to kill. The Phoenician voted to kill. Phila hesitated with her hand raised, then lowered it. Observe.

The Phoenician walked out without speaking. The Sicilian followed.

In the silence that remained, the Egyptian spoke to those who'd stayed. "We remember that the experiment runs for three thousand years. Not eight hundred forty-two. Alexander will conquer. His empire will fragment—I dream that future clearly enough. And when it does, we'll be there. Catching what falls. Saving the knowledge his failure scatters. Proving that patience outlasts ambition, even when ambition seems to win in the short term."

"You're saying we let him build the empire knowing it will fail?"

"I'm saying we let the experiment run. Both approaches tested at scale. And we prepare to show that when Alexander's empire collapses—and it will—our people will still be working. Still teaching. Still carrying the knowledge forward. That's the real test. Not who wins this generation, but whose approach persists for thirty centuries."

## SCENE 5: The Philosopher's Confession

**One year later. Alexander had just become king. Philip was dead. Conquest was beginning.**

Theophrastus visited Aristotle one final time before Alexander marched east.

"I'm inheriting your school," Theophrastus said. "The Lyceum. You're leaving Athens to follow Alexander's campaigns."

Aristotle didn't answer immediately. He was standing at the window, and Theophrastus noticed his hands were trembling. The philosopher picked up a wine jug from the table, poured, spilled a dark ribbon across the wood. He didn't wipe it up. Drank standing, which Theophrastus had never seen him do.

"Not to follow. To observe." He set the cup down too hard. "I need to see if my teaching produces a world-unifier or another Bronze Age catastrophe. Need to know what I built."

"You sound uncertain."

"I am." Aristotle turned from the window and began pacing—three steps to the wall, three steps back, the restless circuit of a man arguing with himself. He looked older than his fifty years. Thinner. "Philosophy teaches me to question everything. Including my own certainty that empire is the answer."

He stopped pacing. Picked up the cup again. Put it down without drinking.

"Can I tell you a secret? About the real reason I agreed to teach Alexander?"

"Tell me."

"The other side approached me. Ten years ago." The words came out clipped, bitten off. "Offered me everything—funding, position, influence—"

"In exchange for what?"

"Training Alexander to conquer wisely. They knew his dreams would surface. Knew he'd have the instinct." Aristotle's voice cracked on the next word. "Wanted philosophy to sharpen what his blood already carried."

"And you accepted." Not a question. Theophrastus let the accusation sit.

Aristotle flinched. He turned away, braced both hands on the windowsill. "I accepted because I wanted to see what would happen. Pure empirical curiosity."

"Curiosity." Theophrastus stepped closer. "You armed a conqueror out of *curiosity*?"

"If both sides have been running parallel experiments for eight hundred forty-two years, neither proving superiority—" Aristotle's knuckles were white against the stone. "Maybe we needed a definitive test. Let one approach run at maximum scale. See what happens."

"That's not science. That's gambling with civilization."

Something broke in Aristotle's composure. He swept the wine cup off the sill. It shattered against the far wall, dark liquid running down the plaster like blood.

"Yes. It is." His voice was raw now. "And maybe that's what the experiment always required. Nefertari and Amenhotep split philosophically but never truly tested their approaches at civilizational scale. Just tended small crises or managed small populations. Alexander forces the real test."

"How long have you been telling yourself that?"

Aristotle turned to face him. His eyes were red-rimmed. "Every day since I watched that boy pick up a sword at thirteen and *dream* his way through a combat drill he'd never been taught. Since I realized what I was sharpening." He pressed the heels of his hands against his eyes. "I'm not on either side, Theophrastus. I don't carry the dreams. I'm a philosopher who believes knowledge requires testing. And I'm testing whether empire or quiet stewardship actually works when civilization itself hangs in the balance."

"And if you're wrong? If Alexander's conquest creates catastrophe?"

"Then I write that failure into everything I leave behind." Aristotle's hands dropped. He looked gutted. Hollowed out. "Make sure future generations learn from the results. Pass the knowledge forward through philosophy instead of blood. Let institutions preserve what families might lose."

He stepped over the broken cup. Knelt, almost absently, and picked up the largest shard.

"Xanthe was right, three generations ago. Ideas persist better than DNA. So I'm recording this experiment in text. Making sure someone remembers what happens when conquest runs at continental scale." He turned the shard in his fingers, watching the wine residue catch the light. "Even if what happens is my fault."

Theophrastus understood then: Aristotle wasn't betraying their side. He was creating a third path. Philosophy as independent observer, recording both approaches, preserving the experiment itself for future analysis.

"Will you teach me?" Theophrastus asked. "How to observe without interfering? How to record the experiment even when I'm part of it?"

"That's why I'm giving you the Lyceum. Not as a safe house. As a philosophical institution recording three thousand years of history from outside both sides." Aristotle gripped his shoulder. "Alexander will conquer. His empire will fragment. Both sides will claim victory or defeat. But you—you'll write what actually happened. Preserve the truth for generations who come after both our bloodlines are dust."

## SCENE 6: The Conquest Begins

**334 BCE. One year later.**

Alexander crossed into Persia with thirty-five thousand men, a hunting knife on his belt, and ancestral dreams showing him exactly how every previous empire had died—and how his wouldn't. At the Hellespont crossing, he drove a spear into Asian soil and called the continent a gift from the gods. The generals cheered. The carriers in his retinue—and there were more of them than Theophrastus had feared—exchanged looks of quiet, predatory satisfaction.

Theophrastus watched from Athens, managing the Lyceum, training students in systematic observation regardless of bloodline.

And he wrote.

*Year 843 of the Genesis Protocol. Alexander of Macedon, Generation 27, began systematic conquest of the Persian Empire. Strategy: unify fragmented city-states, eliminate regional competition, create a single authority strong enough to hold civilization together.*

*Our response: observation without interference. Let the experiment run. Prepare to show what quiet stewardship is worth when (if?) empire fails.*

*The question being tested: Does conquest protect civilization more effectively than compassion? Can a unified empire outlast a scattered network of teachers and healers?*

*Time will tell. The experiment continues. And I will record what happens for those who dream this moment thirty centuries from now.*

He sealed the scroll in the Lyceum's archive, encoded it using both sides' cipher systems, and marked it for long-term preservation.

Philosophy recording what blood carried. Institutions preserving what families might forget. The third path Xanthe had envisioned, now real.

## SCENE 7: The Death of Empire (Epilogue - 323 BCE)

**Twelve years later. Athens.**

The messenger arrived at the Lyceum gates at dawn, his horse lathered and half-dead beneath him. He'd ridden relay from Babylon—fresh mounts every thirty miles, the imperial post system Alexander himself had built, now carrying the news that killed it.

Theophrastus heard the commotion from his study. Footsteps. Raised voices. A student running across the courtyard shouting something he couldn't make out.

Then the silence. That particular silence that spreads through a crowd when the news is too large for noise.

He knew before the messenger reached his door. Felt it in his blood—a sudden absence, like a hand releasing a string that had been pulled taut across half the world. The offensive network's greatest instrument, gone. The dreams would already be rewriting themselves.

Alexander was dead at thirty-two. Empire stretching from Greece to India. Dreams still showing him fractures he'd never have time to seal.

By midday, the streets of Athens had gone strange. Groups of men gathered at intersections, talking fast, gesturing east. A merchant closed his shop and began loading a cart—already calculating which trade routes would collapse first. Two Macedonian soldiers from the garrison walked past the Lyceum with their hands on their sword hilts and their eyes on every doorway, sensing that the authority behind their uniforms had just evaporated.

By evening, runners were arriving from the harbor with contradictory reports. Perdiccas had the ring. No—Ptolemy had already seized Egypt. The generals were meeting. The generals were already fighting. The army was holding together. The army was splitting into factions.

The empire was dying the way Alexander's ancestors had always feared—not from external attack but from internal fragmentation, the exact pattern the Bronze Age Collapse had burned into their bloodline's memory. One authority holding everything together, and then the authority was gone, and everything flew apart at once.

Theophrastus, now fifty-seven and head of the Lyceum for fourteen years, stood in the courtyard and watched history repeat itself in real time. Carriers from both sides found him there over the following days, arriving separately, keeping distance from each other even in grief.

"His empire will fragment within a generation," Nefertari's heirs predicted. "The classic pattern—one ruler holding everything together until he can't. It failed."

"His empire unified more territory than any previous civilization," Amenhotep's heirs countered. "Created administrative systems, trade routes, cultural exchange that will persist for centuries. Knowledge spread farther in twelve years than quiet stewardship achieved in eight hundred fifty-four. It worked."

Both were right. Both were wrong.

A week after the news arrived, a Lyceum student found Theophrastus at his desk in the small hours, still writing by lamplight. The old carrier's eyes were bloodshot, his hand cramped around the stylus, but the words kept coming—the record that Aristotle had charged him to keep.

*Year 854 of the Genesis Protocol. Alexander died. Empire fragmenting. Both sides claiming victory.*

*Truth: Neither approach proved superior. Empire unified a continent, held crises at bay through authority, but couldn't outlast its creator. Stewardship preserved knowledge through the collapse, kept it from cascading into another Bronze Age catastrophe, but couldn't stop the empire from forming or falling apart.*

*Conclusion: The three-thousand-year experiment requires both approaches. Empire creates large-scale stability when it works. Stewardship preserves knowledge when it fails. Neither is sufficient alone.*

*Final observation: Aristotle was right. Philosophy can teach what blood carries. But blood provides motivation philosophy can't match. Carriers feel it in their bones. Philosophers only understand it intellectually. Maybe the real answer requires combining both—inherited instinct AND philosophical training. Family AND institution. The dreams AND the discipline.*

*We are adapting. Recruiting from philosophy schools. Training carriers in both ancestral knowledge and systematic thinking. Building something new that preserves Nefertari's compassion while learning from Amenhotep's ambition.*

*Eight hundred fifty-four years into the three-thousand-year experiment, I predict: The side that wins won't be purely one thing or the other. It will be the one that learns to hold both truths at once. Scattered AND unified. Compassionate AND strategic.*

*May those who dream this moment learn from it.*

Theophrastus sealed the scroll and set down the stylus. His hand ached. Outside, Athens was restless—he could hear distant shouting from the agora, the democracy faction already testing whether Macedonian authority would hold without its founder.

It wouldn't. He dreamed that much clearly enough.

He was reaching for the lamp when a young carrier he didn't recognize appeared in his doorway. Macedonian clothes. Alexander's sigil on his cloak pin.

"Theophrastus of Athens?"

"Who sent you?"

"No one sent me. Alexander's network—the offensive carriers embedded in his court, his generals, his administration." The young man's eyes were steady. "We're still operational. We don't need Alexander to continue the work."

Theophrastus felt the cold settle back into his bones. The same cold he'd felt in that library in Mieza, twenty years ago, when a sixteen-year-old boy had blocked a doorway and shown him what the future looked like.

"The empire is fragmenting," he said carefully.

"The empire was a vehicle. The network is the engine." The young carrier smiled—and for a horrible, vertiginous moment, the smile was Alexander's. The same warmth. The same iron underneath. "Alexander built something that doesn't require Alexander. We're in every successor court. Every general's staff. Every satrapy from Egypt to Bactria."

He left without waiting for a response. His footsteps faded down the colonnade, unhurried, confident—the walk of a man who believed the future belonged to him.

Theophrastus sat alone in the lamplight for a long time. Then he broke the seal on the scroll he'd just finished, unrolled it, and added a final line:

*Correction to final observation. The experiment is not proceeding as theorized. Alexander is dead. His network is not. Prepare accordingly.*
