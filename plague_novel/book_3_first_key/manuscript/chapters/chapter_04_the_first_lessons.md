# BOOK 3: THE COLLAPSE - CHAPTER 4
## "The First Lessons"
## CHAPTER 4: THE FIRST LESSONS

---

**Twenty-two years after the Protocol creation (1155 BCE)**

The screaming woke her before dawn.

Tirzah was on her feet and through the doorway before her mind caught up with her body, knife in hand, scanning the darkness for raiders. But the sound wasn't coming from the settlement walls. It was coming from inside the house. From her son's room.

Ephraim was sitting upright on his sleeping mat, eyes wide and unseeing, his small body rigid with terror. His mouth was open and the sound pouring out of him was not a child's cry—it was a grown woman's scream, pitched wrong in his seven-year-old throat, ancient and raw.

"Ephraim." Tirzah dropped the knife and grabbed his shoulders. His skin was burning. "Ephraim, come back."

He didn't see her. His eyes were tracking something across the far wall—watching things move that weren't there. His hands clawed at the blanket.

"The water," he gasped. "The water's on fire. The boats are—" He choked on it. "Everyone's in the water and the water's burning—"

Pi-Ramesses. The harbor. The night the Sea Peoples came.

Tirzah's hands went cold. She'd had these dreams herself—fragments, shadows, echoes of what Tausret witnessed. But never this clear. Never at seven years old.

She pulled him against her chest and held him until the shaking stopped. It took a long time. Outside, a dog barked twice at the darkness and went quiet. The settlement slept on, unaware.

When his breathing finally slowed, Ephraim said in a voice scraped thin: "It happens every night now, Mama."

Tirzah pressed her lips to the top of his head so he wouldn't see her face. Because what she felt wasn't comfort. It was fear—a cold, spreading recognition that what Nefertari had planted in their blood was growing faster than anyone predicted, and she had done this to her son by the simple act of bearing him.

"I know," she said. "I know it does."

---

They'd walked to the hillside after the morning meal, though Ephraim had barely eaten—pushing barley porridge around his bowl, dark circles under his eyes. Tirzah wore the clothing of a Canaanite farmer's wife now—wool tunic, linen head covering, leather sandals worn thin at the soles. Twenty-two years since fleeing Egypt and she'd almost forgotten what it felt like to wear pleated linen and faience jewelry. Almost.

Below them, terraced fields where drought-resistant barley grew in careful rows. The Canaanite hill country had recovered somewhat since the worst of the collapse—population stabilizing, settlements taking root, trade routes cautiously re-emerging. But the scars were everywhere if you knew where to look. The charred foundation stones of a village that never rebuilt. Terraces half-collapsed where there weren't enough hands to maintain them. A field left fallow not by design but because the family that worked it had starved two winters ago.

Everyone who'd survived knew it could all fall apart again.

"What do you see?" Tirzah asked her son.

The boy—Ephraim, she'd named him, meaning "fruitful" in the local language—was picking at a scab on his knee instead of looking at the fields. Seven years old. Bright. Exhausted from dreams that weren't his.

"Ephraim."

"Dirt," he said flatly. "Farmers. I don't want to do this today."

"I know you're tired. Look anyway."

He sighed the way only a child being asked to do something pointless can sigh, but he looked. After a moment, grudgingly: "They're planting in different places than last year. Moving the crops around."

"Why?"

"Because..." He picked up a beetle crawling over a rock, cupping it in his hands, watching its legs work. "Because the dirt gets tired? I don't know. You told me this already."

"I told you the word for it. Tell me what you actually see."

He set the beetle down with exaggerated care. "If you plant the same thing in the same place the earth stops giving. You have to move it around. Let it rest." He scratched at the scab again. "Can we go home? My stomach hurts."

Tirzah felt her heart quicken despite the resistance. That was pattern thinking—the way physicians learned to see. Noticing how actions circle back. Understanding how overuse depletes and the failures pile on each other. He saw it even when he didn't want to.

"That's called a feedback loop," she said. "The system—soil, crops, harvest—it responds to what you do to it. Overuse creates depletion. Rotation creates renewal. What else? Then we can go."

Ephraim looked down at the fields with renewed purpose now that freedom was at stake. "Some families are planting together. They didn't used to do that."

"Why do you think?"

"So if somebody's food dies they don't starve? Like when Miriam's family lost their wheat and Yosef's family gave them some?"

There it was. Not abstract reasoning—*lived observation, turned into principle.* Better than any clean answer.

"That's exactly right."

"Mama, there's a really big beetle over here—"

"Ephraim. One more question, then beetles." Tirzah knelt beside him. "Do you remember the stories I've told you? About your grandmother Tausret?"

His face changed. The fidgeting stopped. "The collapse," he said, quieter now. "When everything broke."

"Yes. And do you know why it happened?"

He shook his head, but his hand had found hers and was holding it tight.

"Because people stopped seeing the patterns you just described. They kept planting the same crops in the same soil until the earth couldn't feed them anymore. They kept building cities that required trade networks, but didn't notice when those networks started failing. They saw each problem as separate—drought here, invasion there, crop failure somewhere else—instead of seeing how everything connected."

Tirzah placed her hand on Ephraim's small shoulder. "Your grandmother Tausret understood. So did Nefertari, the woman who saved us. They created something called the Genesis Protocol. A way of weaving that sight into our bloodline. Into you."

"Is that why the dreams—" He stopped. Swallowed. "Is that why I see the burning city?"

Tirzah's chest tightened. She kept her voice steady. "Yes. What the Protocol carries is deeper than memory. More like an instinct. The ability to see connections others miss. To feel when small problems are symptoms of something bigger breaking. And sometimes that means seeing what your grandmother saw."

"I don't like it." His voice was small and fierce. "I don't want to see the city burning every night."

"I know, love."

"Can you make it stop?"

The honest answer was no. The honest answer was that it would get worse—that each generation the visions sharpened, that his children would carry them even more vividly, that she had passed him a gift wrapped in barbed wire. Tirzah felt the weight of it settle on her shoulders like a physical thing.

"I can teach you to understand it," she said. "So it frightens you less. So you know what you're seeing and why."

He looked unconvinced but didn't argue. A seven-year-old's grudging trust.

"Because someday—maybe not in your lifetime, maybe not for a thousand years—it will threaten again. And when it does, our descendants need to see it coming. They need to spot the signs early enough to stop it."

"Can it be stopped?"

Tirzah thought of Amenhotep, wherever he was now. Egypt, probably. Building his offensive network. Teaching his people that collapse was inevitable and control was the only answer.

"I believe it can," she said. "But only if we see it in time. That's why you need to learn this. Not just with your mind—with your blood. With what you inherited from Tausret."

Ephraim was quiet. Then, with a directness that pierced her: "Is that why it hurts? Because she was hurting when she put it in?"

Tirzah pulled him close. Not to comfort him—to hide the tears she couldn't stop. Because he was right. Tausret had been in agony when Nefertari pressed the Protocol into her dying blood, and that agony echoed forward, and her son was carrying it at seven years old, and she could not take it back.

"Trauma marks the body in ways that pass forward," she said when she could trust her voice. "Heartbeat, stress responses, even memories themselves. Nefertari amplified those markers, wove the ability to see connections into our blood. You're not imagining Pi-Ramesses. You're remembering it through Tausret's eyes, through my eyes, through three generations of inherited witness."

She turned his face toward hers. "And yes, sometimes it hurts to carry them. But that pain has a purpose."

"What purpose?"

"To make sure we never forget how bad it can get. So we stay vigilant. So we keep watching for the signs." She touched his chest. "Your grandmother Tausret was a pharaoh. She could have run when it all started falling apart. Saved herself. But instead she stayed and tried to save everyone. She died doing it. And Nefertari—the physician who created the Protocol—she spent her whole life pressing Tausret's compassion into our blood. The ability to see suffering and refuse to accept it."

"Did it work?"

"You tell me. When you see those farmers below sharing fields, helping each other survive—what do you feel?"

Ephraim thought about it. Kicked a pebble. Thought some more. "Like I want to help too. Even though I don't know them very well."

"That's the Protocol working. That's defensive mode."

Seeing what's building—not to control it, but to hold things together for everyone.

---

**That evening, in Tirzah's stone house**

The defensive network had grown slowly over twenty-two years.

Tirzah sat now with five others—refugees and survivors who'd found their way to the Canaanite hill country after everything fell apart. They gathered monthly, these survivors. Sharing knowledge. Teaching the next generation. Building practices that could endure even if institutions failed.

The Phoenician trader spoke first—older now, graying, but still sharp. A scar from a roadside ambush ran white along his jaw; he'd nearly died on the route from Byblos, and his left hand still shook from the fever that followed the wound going septic. "The Pattern Eye is secure. I visited Byblos last month. The priestesses remember their oath. They're teaching it to their apprentices—there's a sacred bronze disc in the foundation vault, not to be touched until someone from Tausret's line claims it."

"How long will they remember?" the Cypriot priestess asked. She looked thinner than last month. Everyone did—the late rains had stunted the barley, and the settlement was stretching stores.

"As long as the temple stands. And that temple's stood a thousand years already." He paused, studying trade reports on the table. "I noticed something, though. Grain prices in Sidon are rising faster than drought conditions explain. Could be merchant hoarding. Could be an early warning sign—resource anxiety triggering selfishness."

Tirzah felt it—a flash of something like pride. The trader had no inherited visions, no blood connection to Tausret. But twenty-two years of training had taught him to see. The defensive network wasn't just about inheritance—it was about *teaching* the way of seeing.

"You've learned well," Tirzah said quietly.

The Phoenician looked embarrassed. "I'll never see things the way you do. No ancestral dreams. No instinct in the blood. But you taught me to pay attention. To look for how actions circle back to their source. To notice when small problems connect into something bigger."

"What about the Memory Bridge?"

"Still sealed in the archive wall," the priestess confirmed. "Theia died three years ago, but she trained her successor. The location is recorded in their coding system. It'll persist."

"And the Distribution Network fragments?"

The Egyptian physician spoke. He was the oldest among them, hands gnarled with joint sickness, and he did not soften the news. "Four of seven families still hold their fragments. One family died out during the drought. Another lost theirs when bandits burned their village last season—we don't know if the fragment survived or was destroyed. The seventh family has moved south and stopped responding to messengers."

Silence. The lamplight flickered against the stone walls.

"Four of seven," the Ugarit scribe repeated. "That's not redundancy. That's attrition."

"The network adapted before," Tirzah said carefully. "When the first family—"

"The network *survived* before." The physician cut her off, something he rarely did. "There's a difference between adapting and slowly bleeding out. We've lost three families in two years, Tirzah. At this rate we'll have nothing left to distribute by the time your grandchildren are grown."

The room shifted. The scribe leaned forward. The priestess looked at the table.

"What are you suggesting?" Tirzah asked.

"I'm suggesting that patience isn't working." The physician's voice was steady but hard. "Twenty-two years we've been doing this your way. Teaching. Waiting. Hiding keys in temples and hoping priestesses we've never met keep their oaths. Meanwhile, we're losing ground. People are dying. Fragments are disappearing. And Amenhotep—" He stopped himself, then pushed through. "Amenhotep's network isn't losing fragments. Because they don't *ask* families to carry them. They place them under guard. They use force and structure. And their network is growing while ours crumbles at the edges."

"You want us to become them." Tirzah's voice was flat.

"I want us to survive long enough to matter. What good is compassion if our entire network collapses before Generation 10?"

The others watched. This wasn't new tension—Tirzah could see it in how the Phoenician trader stared at his hands, how the priestess's jaw was tight. They'd been talking about this without her.

"If we use force to maintain the network," Tirzah said slowly, "we become indistinguishable from what we're fighting against. The whole point is that distributed trust outlasts centralized control. If we abandon that—"

"If we abandon nothing and lose everything?" the physician pressed. "Is that the better outcome?"

Tirzah looked around the circle. These were people who had followed her for two decades. Trusted her. And they were tired, and hungry, and scared, and some of them were beginning to doubt. She could feel the room tilting.

"I hear you," she said. "And you're right that we need to adapt. But not by becoming Amenhotep." She held the physician's gaze. "We find the seventh family. We rebuild the sixth fragment—Nefertari designed redundancy into the encoding. We recruit new carriers. And we do it faster than we have been, because you're right—we don't have the luxury of patience anymore."

The physician studied her for a long moment. Then he exhaled through his nose—not agreement, exactly, but a willingness to wait one more season before pushing harder.

"What about your son?" the scribe asked, breaking the tension. "Is he showing signs?"

"Yes." Tirzah felt pride and fear tangled together. "He sees patterns. Grasps how changes circle back through systems. He's having the dreams—seeing Pi-Ramesses through Tausret's eyes. Waking up screaming."

"That's early. You didn't start having those until you were twelve."

"It's getting stronger," Tirzah said. "Each generation, the visions get clearer. Nefertari predicted this—trauma markers stacking, the inheritance reinforcing. By Generation 10, our descendants might remember the fall as vividly as if they'd lived it themselves."

"Is that good?" the priestess asked quietly. "Burdening children with ancestral trauma? Your boy looks half-starved from lack of sleep."

Tirzah had wrestled with this question for years, but hearing it said aloud—by someone who'd seen Ephraim's hollowed eyes—struck differently tonight. "Only if they understand it's a tool, not a curse. The pain has purpose—to keep us vigilant, to make us compassionate. I'm teaching Ephraim that what the blood carries isn't a burden. It's a warning."

"And if Amenhotep's network is teaching the opposite?" the scribe pressed. "If they're using the inheritance to justify control instead of compassion?"

"Then we're in a race," Tirzah said. "Whose philosophy persists longer. Whose descendants stay truer to the original purpose. Which approach actually prevents the next one."

Somewhere in Egypt, Amenhotep's people were learning different lessons. Recognize the signs early, yes—but to seize control, not to save anyone. Identify who's valuable, engineer their survival, let the rest die. Efficiency. Ruthlessness. Planning. The same sight, opposite purpose.

Two experiments running in parallel across three thousand years.

"You're gambling on human goodness," the physician said. It wasn't concession. It was a label. "Amenhotep's gambling on human nature requiring management. Three thousand years is a long time to maintain faith in people. Especially when the people are hungry."

"I know." Tirzah looked around the circle—at their drawn faces, their calloused hands, the evidence of twenty-two years of survival wearing them down to bone. "That's why we need to plant it deeper than knowledge. We need to make it culture. Make it practice. Make it so fundamental to our descendants' identity that even if they forget the Genesis Protocol's name, they still carry its purpose."

"How?"

"By teaching them what I'm teaching Ephraim. That seeing what's coming isn't about control—it's about compassion. That this way of thinking isn't a weapon—it's a responsibility. That the dreams, the visions—they're not a burden. They're a gift that lets us learn from our ancestors' mistakes."

She paused. "And by building a network that survives even when we don't. Priestesses who protect keys for families they'll never meet. Archivists who preserve knowledge for descendants a thousand years away. Families who pass down fragments without fully understanding what they're protecting. We're creating something that outlasts individual lives." She looked at the physician. "But we do it faster. Starting now."

The Phoenician trader broke the silence. "Nefertari would tell us to stop arguing and get to work."

"Nefertari is probably still alive somewhere, doing exactly that," Tirzah said. "She's only fifty-six. I wouldn't be surprised if she's training others in Phoenicia, building redundant networks, weaving the Protocol into a dozen different lines as backup."

"Do you miss her?"

Tirzah considered. "Every day. But she taught me well. Taught me that the work matters more than the worker. That knowledge has to survive even if we don't." She looked at her weathered hands—thirty-four years old, already showing the wear of hill country life. "I'll probably die here. My son will grow up Canaanite, not Egyptian. By Generation 5, our family won't even remember we were royalty once. But the Protocol will persist. What the blood carries will persist. And when it threatens again, someone in our line will see it coming."

"You really believe that?" the priestess asked.

"I have to. Otherwise Tausret died for nothing. Otherwise we're just refugees hiding in hills instead of people building something that lasts."

---

**Three days later: The first test**

Ephraim came running from the village, seven years old and breathless.

"Mother! Mother, something's wrong!"

Tirzah looked up from grinding grain. "What is it?"

"The traders. The ones who come from the coast every month. They didn't come this time. And I heard the elders talking—three villages to the south stopped sending grain to the temple. Everyone's keeping their surplus."

Tirzah's pulse quickened. "Show me."

She followed Ephraim to the village center where elders were arguing. Raised voices. Fear beneath the anger.

"—can't trust the coastal cities anymore—"

"—if Sidon won't trade, we're cut off—"

"—better to hoard what we have—"

Tirzah listened. Trade networks disrupting. Resource hoarding starting. Local cooperation breaking down. Small problems. Seemingly isolated.

But she'd seen this before.

Not personally—she'd been twelve when they fled Egypt, too young to read the signs. But through the genetic memory. Through Tausret's eyes. This was how collapse started. Not with invasion. Not with catastrophe. With small breaks in the system that people dismissed as temporary problems until the whole structure failed.

She stepped into the circle of arguing elders.

"Stop hoarding," she said clearly.

They turned to her. Tirzah—the refugee woman from Egypt, healer and midwife, generally respected but not powerful. What did she know about trade policy?

"If we hoard now, we break faster," Tirzah continued. "The coastal traders haven't come because Sidon's probably having the same conversation—whether to keep surplus or trade it. If we hoard, they'll hoard. If they hoard, the network breaks. If the network breaks, we're all isolated. Isolated villages can't survive drought or invasion or crop failure."

"That's speculation," one elder said.

"No. I've seen this before." Tirzah felt the genetic memory sharpening her sight. "Twenty-two years ago, this whole region fell apart because everyone made local decisions without seeing the system. Each choice made sense locally—hoard food, close borders, protect your own. But when every city does it, trade networks fragment. Then drought hits and no one can share resources. Then invasion comes and no one has allies. One failure feeds the next. That's how civilizations die—not from one catastrophe, but from a thousand rational choices that don't account for the system."

"You were a child twenty-two years ago."

"But my grandmother wasn't. Pharaoh Tausret watched this exact pattern destroy Egypt. And she taught me—through blood, through memory—how to recognize it early."

The elders looked skeptical but uncertain.

Tirzah pushed forward. "Send the grain to the temple anyway. I'll go to the coast. I'll find out what's stopping the traders. And I'll make sure they understand: cooperation is how we survive. Breaking the network is how we die."

"And if they won't listen?"

"Then we adapt. Find new trade routes. Build new networks. But we don't break cooperation ourselves. That's how we lose."

---

**The coastal road, six days later**

Tirzah traveled alone. The Phoenician trader had offered to accompany her but she'd refused—he was too well known on the coastal routes, and the new men running Sidon were suspicious of anyone who looked like competition. A hill country healer, though. A refugee woman carrying a bag of dried herbs and asking to trade remedies for information. That was invisible.

The road was worse than she remembered. Two years ago there had been waypoints—stone shelters every half-day where travelers could rest and water their animals. Three of them were burned shells now. At the second ruin she found a family camped in the rubble, a woman and two children with the hollow look of people who'd stopped expecting help. The woman watched Tirzah pass with eyes that tracked like a predator's, one hand on a sharpened stake.

Tirzah left a bundle of dried figs at the edge of their camp without stopping. You didn't approach desperate strangers on an empty road. She'd learned that lesson the hard way, years ago. The scar on her left forearm was proof.

She slept in a shepherd's cave the first night, knife across her lap, listening to something large move through the brush outside. On the second day she passed a merchant's wagon overturned in a ditch, its goods picked clean, dark stains on the roadside stones that could have been wine or could have been blood.

Sidon was worse.

The city still stood—its walls intact, its harbor functional—but it had turned inward. Guards at the gate who hadn't been there a year ago. Markets half-empty. And a new ruling council that had seized power during a food riot and now controlled the granaries with an iron grip.

Tirzah talked her way past the gate by offering to treat a guard's infected tooth. Inside, she spent two days working as a healer in the lower market, treating fevers and dressing wounds, listening. Learning who controlled what. Mapping the fear.

The problem was a man named Abdi-Milkutti—a former grain merchant who'd leveraged the food shortage into political power. He'd convinced the council that Sidon's survival depended on hoarding surplus, that the inland settlements were a drain on resources, that the trade routes were charity the city couldn't afford.

Classic fear-driven thinking. And from his position, it was rational. Every sack of grain that left Sidon was a sack his people couldn't eat. The logic was airtight if you couldn't see past the city walls.

Tirzah got an audience by healing his wife's persistent cough—a reputation for competence opened doors that power and wealth could not. She sat across from Abdi-Milkutti in his requisitioned merchant's house and watched him eat dates while she spoke.

"You're killing your own city," she told him.

He laughed. "I'm the only reason it's still alive."

"For now. Cut the trade routes and you lose the copper supply from the hill settlements. Lose the copper and your smiths can't make tools. Lose the tools and your farmers can't work the fields efficiently. Within two seasons you'll need more grain, not less, and you'll have no one left to trade with."

"The hill settlements need us more than we need them."

"They needed you. Past tense. Three villages have already started trading with Akko instead. Two more are talking to Damascus. You're not protecting Sidon by hoarding—you're making it irrelevant."

She watched it land. Watched the calculation behind his eyes. Men like Abdi-Milkutti didn't respond to compassion, but they understood leverage—and the prospect of their city being bypassed entirely was a language he spoke fluently.

The negotiation took another full day. Tirzah slept in a healer's quarters near the harbor, listening to rats in the walls and the sound of waves against the seawall, and in the morning she sat down with Abdi-Milkutti again and hammered out terms. Limited trade resumption. Grain for copper. A trial period of three months.

It wasn't trust. It was mutual need dressed up as cooperation. But it was enough.

She left Sidon on the third morning with a small trading party—three wagons and an armed escort as far as the foothills. The road back felt longer. At the burned waypoint, the woman and her children were gone. Whether they'd moved on or something worse had found them, Tirzah couldn't say. She tried not to think about it, and failed.

---

**Three days later**

The traders resumed their routes. The grain flowed to temples and back out to villages. The network held—barely, provisionally, by the thinnest of threads.

It was a small victory. Barely noticed. But Tirzah understood: survival meant catching these small breaks before they cascaded. It meant seeing the pattern early. It meant intervention before crisis, even when intervention meant walking alone into a hostile city with nothing but a healer's bag and the ability to see what others couldn't.

This was defensive mode working.

When she returned home, Ephraim was waiting at the settlement gate. He'd been sitting there since morning, the neighbor woman told her—had refused to go play, refused to eat, just sat and watched the road.

"You stopped it," he said, grabbing her hand. "The thing that was starting."

"I slowed it," Tirzah corrected. Her feet were blistered and her back ached and she wanted nothing more than to sleep for a week. "Collapse is always there, waiting. We just have to keep recognizing it in time."

"Will I have to do this too? When I'm grown?"

Tirzah knelt beside her son, feeling every mile of the journey in her joints. "You and your children and your children's children. For as long as the bloodline persists. That's what being the Living Key means. You carry the knowledge. You pass it forward. You teach the next generation to see."

"It sounds hard." He was looking at her blistered feet.

"It is hard. But it's also important. And Nefertari planted something else in our blood besides the sight. She planted the belief that we *can* make a difference. That seeing it coming isn't futile—it's powerful. It's the difference between dying passively and fighting back."

Ephraim thought about this. Then, with seven-year-old seriousness: "I'll learn. I'll practice seeing. And when I have children, I'll teach them too."

Tirzah hugged him. He smelled like dust and sunlight and the goat pen where he liked to sit. "That's all I ask."

---

**That night**

Tirzah sat in her house, grinding herbs by lamplight, when Ephraim screamed again.

She was beside him in seconds. But this time was different. He wasn't thrashing. He was sitting perfectly still, eyes open, speaking in a low monotone that didn't sound like a child's voice at all.

"There's a man," Ephraim said. "He's watching us."

"In the dream?"

"No." Ephraim's eyes were focused on something far away. "Not in the dream. In the real. He's been watching for a long time. He knows about the blood. He knows about the keys."

Tirzah's skin went cold. "Ephraim, you're dreaming. You're—"

"He has the same sight we do, Mama. But it's different in him. It's cold." Ephraim blinked, and his eyes refocused, and he was a seven-year-old boy again, confused and frightened. "I saw—I don't—" He started to cry. "There was a man and he was looking right at me. Through me. Like he could see what's in my blood."

Tirzah held her son and told him it was just a dream, a bad dream, nothing more. She rubbed his back and hummed the song she'd learned from Nefertari until his breathing slowed and he slept again.

Then she sat in the dark for a long time.

Because Ephraim had never seen Amenhotep. Had no way of knowing what the man looked like, what his network was, what his purpose was. Tirzah had never described him—had been careful never to describe him, wanting to keep her son's childhood free of that particular shadow for as long as possible.

But the boy had described cold sight. Sight turned inward, turned to purpose rather than compassion. The offensive mode, seen from the inside.

Either the Protocol was evolving in ways Nefertari never predicted—the blood carrying not just Tausret's memories but awareness of the other line, the opposite experiment, the enemy growing in parallel—

Or Amenhotep's network had found them.

Tirzah rose and checked the door. Checked the window. Walked outside into the cold night air and scanned the hillside, seeing nothing, hearing nothing except wind through dry grass and the distant bleat of a goat.

The settlement was quiet. The stars were indifferent.

But she stood there until dawn, watching the road south, her hand on the knife she'd stopped putting away.
