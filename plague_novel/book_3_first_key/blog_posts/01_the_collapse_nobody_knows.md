# The Biggest Civilizational Collapse You've Never Heard Of

Around 1177 BCE, the world ended. Not the whole world — but every major civilization in the Mediterranean collapsed within a few decades. The Mycenaeans. The Hittites. The Kassite Babylonians. Ugarit. Gone.

We're talking about a collapse more total than the fall of Rome. Rome took centuries to unwind. The Bronze Age Collapse happened in a generation.

And most educated people have never heard of it.

## What Actually Happened

Picture a network. Egypt trades grain to the Hittites. The Hittites trade tin to the Mycenaeans. The Mycenaeans trade finished bronze to everyone. Phoenician ships carry it all. Every palace economy depends on every other palace economy.

Now imagine that network failing. A drought kills the harvests. Dead harvests kill the trade. Dead trade kills the armies that protect the trade. Refugees flood from burned cities into surviving ones, overwhelming their resources. Disease follows the refugees.

The Mycenaean palaces at Pylos, Tiryns, and Mycenae burned one after another. The Hittite capital Hattusa was abandoned so completely that no one even remembered where it was until archaeologists found it in 1906. Ugarit — a wealthy port city that had invented one of the first alphabets — was destroyed and never rebuilt.

In Greece, the collapse was so complete that literacy itself vanished. The Mycenaeans had a writing system called Linear B, but they only used it for palace bookkeeping. When the palaces burned, no one needed to write anymore. Greece went dark for about 400 years. Historians literally call it the Greek Dark Ages.

## The City You've Never Heard Of

In the middle of this catastrophe sat Pi-Ramesses, the Egyptian capital in the Nile Delta. One of the largest cities in the ancient world — over 300,000 people. Temples covered in turquoise faience tiles. Canals lined with lotus.

And you've probably never heard of it.

Pi-Ramesses was the pride of Ramesses II, but after the collapse era, the Nile branch it sat on silted up. The city was abandoned. Its stones were literally dismantled and hauled to a new city nearby. Its location wasn't confirmed until the 1960s.

A city of 300,000 people — one of the largest on Earth at the time — just... erased.

## Why This Matters Now

The Bronze Age Collapse is a case study in what happens when interconnected systems fail simultaneously. Drought. Supply chain disruption. Mass migration. Disease. Political instability. Each problem feeds the next.

The Phoenician trading cities — Byblos, Tyre, Sidon — survived better than anyone else. Not because they were stronger, but because their economy was based on flexible trade networks rather than rigid palace systems. They adapted. Everyone else broke.

There's a reason I set the beginning of *The First Key* in Pi-Ramesses during the collapse. It's the moment when everything the ancient world built over centuries fell apart — and a handful of people had to decide what was worth saving.

Some of them chose to save knowledge. How they did it is the story.
