# Genesis Protocol Book 2 - Voice & Style Guide

**Purpose:** Maintain consistent King-style voice across all chapters.

---

## Core Voice Principles

### 1. **Intimate, Not Clinical**

❌ **Wrong:** "Dr. Morrison observed that the genetic markers indicated resistance patterns."
✅ **Right:** "Morrison looked at the data and thought: these people were built to survive and they had no idea."

**Rule:** Write like you're telling a story to a friend, not writing a research paper.

---

### 2. **Show Don't Tell (Especially Emotions)**

❌ **Wrong:** "Sarah was terrified and felt guilty."
✅ **Right:** "Sarah's hands wouldn't stop shaking and somewhere in the back of her mind a voice that sounded like her mother kept saying *you did this you did this you did this*."

**Rule:** Physical sensations + intrusive thoughts = emotion. Never name the emotion directly.

---

### 3. **Specific Beats Abstract**

❌ **Wrong:** "Many people died."
✅ **Right:** "There was this woman—pregnant, maybe, walking to market—and Sarah couldn't stop seeing her face."

**Rule:** One specific person is more powerful than a million abstract deaths.

---

### 4. **Dark Humor During Crisis**

❌ **Wrong:** "The situation was dire. She had to escape immediately."
✅ **Right:** "The situation was dire and her bladder had opinions about it and wasn't that just perfect."

**Rule:** Real people think absurd things during trauma. Use it to make characters human.

---

### 5. **Body Betrayal**

❌ **Wrong:** "Her hands shook."
✅ **Right:** "Her hands had their own ideas about staying still and those ideas didn't involve any cooperation whatsoever."

**Rule:** Body as separate entity = more visceral.

---

## Sentence Structure

### During Tension/Action:
- Short. Sentences. Work.
- One word sentences for impact.
- No complex clauses during chase scenes.

**Example:**
> "She ran. The alarm shrieked. Morrison's voice over the intercom. Her bare feet slapping cold tile. Thirty seconds to the exit. Twenty-nine. Twenty-eight."

### During Reflection/Grief:
- Long, rambling sentences that spiral
- Let thoughts derail mid-sentence
- Parenthetical asides

**Example:**
> "Sarah sat there and thought about the twenty-three million people (not people, not really, just a number too big to mean anything) and how she'd killed them by saying no (except Morrison had killed them, Morrison had deployed Lagos, but he'd given her a choice and she'd chosen principles over lives which meant—didn't it mean?—that she'd killed them too)."

---

## Dialogue Rules

### People Don't Talk in Complete Sentences

❌ **Wrong:** "I understand your position, but I cannot cooperate with your plan."
✅ **Right:** "I get it. I do. But I can't— no. I won't help you."

### Profanity (Strategic Use Only)

- Use sparingly for emotional peaks
- Characters who don't normally curse → shows how broken they are when they do
- Morrison never curses (control)
- Sarah curses when pushed past breaking point
- David curses once in the entire book (when Carlos dies)

### Subtext

What they say ≠ what they mean

**Example:**
> "I'm fine," Sarah said.
>
> (She wasn't fine. Morrison knew she wasn't fine. They both knew what "I'm fine" actually meant: *please stop asking before I break*.)

---

## Character Voice Consistency

### Sarah Chen
- **Baseline:** Analytical, precise, tries to stay controlled
- **Under stress:** Thoughts spiral, notices irrelevant details, dark humor
- **When broken:** Fragments, repetition, body betrayal
- **Never:** Clinical detachment (that's Morrison's voice, not hers)

### Morrison
- **Always:** Calm, patient, clinical
- **Even when angry:** Controlled voice (which makes him scarier)
- **Never:** Loses composure (until final breakdown)
- **Trick:** His calmness discussing genocide is the horror

### David Rivera
- **Baseline:** Quiet, observant, gentle
- **When passionate:** Rambling sentences, physics metaphors, can't quite say what he means
- **His letter:** Vulnerable but still using timeline/quantum metaphors as emotional armor

---

## Things to NEVER Do

1. ❌ **Explain genetics/science at length**
   - Cut technical exposition by 80%
   - If you must explain: character explains wrong, gets corrected, move on

2. ❌ **Use filter words** (felt, thought, saw, heard, seemed, wondered)
   - Just show us what they experience

3. ❌ **Describe emotions directly**
   - Show physical sensations instead

4. ❌ **Clean, articulate dialogue**
   - People interrupt themselves, say wrong things, repeat

5. ❌ **Abstract stakes**
   - One specific person > millions abstract

6. ❌ **Passive voice during action**
   - "The door was opened" → "She opened the door"

7. ❌ **Adverbs** (very, really, quite, just, etc.)
   - Usually weakens the prose

## Recurring Motifs

### Coffee
- Symbol of humanity, connection, normalcy
- David brings terrible coffee = he cares
- Becomes emotionally loaded by end

### Parenthetical Asides
- King's signature move
- Use for:
  - Sudden realizations: "(and that was the moment she understood)"
  - Contradictions: "(she wasn't fine)"
  - Dark humor: "(which was just great)"

### Numbers/Math
- Morrison uses numbers to distance himself from humanity
- Sarah's horror = when she can't stop doing the math
- "23 million" repeated until it becomes numbing = the point

### Time Distortion
- During trauma, time gets weird
- "Seven seconds or seven hours"
- Split-second = paragraph of thought
- Hours = single sentence

## Chapter Opening Rules

Every chapter must hook in **first 3 sentences**. No exceptions.

**Good hooks:**
- Action mid-scene
- Shocking statement
- Specific visceral detail
- Voice that demands attention

**Bad hooks:**
- Setting description
- Backstory
- Waking up
- Weather

---

## When Voice Slips

**If you notice the prose getting clinical again:**
1. Stop
2. Read paragraph aloud
3. Does it sound like someone TELLING you a story?
4. If no → run through Master Voice Prompt again

**Red flags voice is slipping:**
- Sentences all same length
- Using "felt/thought/seemed"
- Explaining instead of showing
- Characters being too articulate
- No dark humor for 2+ pages
- Abstract instead of specific

---

## Testing Your Voice

**Read aloud test:**
- Does this sound like someone telling you a story at 2 AM?
- Can you hear a human voice saying these words?
- Are you FEELING it or just understanding it?

**Reader test:**
- "That was interesting" = NOT WORKING
- "Holy shit that hurt" = WORKING
- "I couldn't stop reading" = WORKING

---

**Remember:** First draft won't be perfect. That's why we have multiple prompts and revision passes. Get it done, then polish. Voice consistency comes with practice.

---

## Quick Reference Checklist

Before marking a chapter "done":
- ☐ Opening hooks in first 3 sentences
- ☐ No filter words (felt, thought, seemed)
- ☐ Emotions shown through physical sensations
- ☐ Dialogue has interruptions/fragments
- ☐ At least one moment of dark humor
- ☐ Cut technical exposition by 80%
- ☐ Specific details beat abstract descriptions
- ☐ Read aloud - sounds like storytelling?
- ☐ Character voices consistent?
- ☐ No passive voice during action scenes
