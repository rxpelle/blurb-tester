# CHAPTER 5 - COFFEE AND TIMELINES

**Modern Timeline - GenVault Archive, Sublevel 3**
**November 18, 2019 - 4:23 PM**
**13 days until THRESHOLD deployment**

---

The elevator going down to sublevel 3 smelled like recycled air and the kind of institutional cleaning products that probably gave cancer to lab rats somewhere.

Sarah counted floors.

Three.

Four.

Five.

Her stomach doing that sinking thing that elevators always caused except this time it wasn't the elevator, it was the fact that she was voluntarily descending into the heart of a facility that bred people like livestock and planned to murder four billion of them.

(This was fine.)

(She was gathering intelligence.)

(Morrison thought he was recruiting her and she was letting him think that while Martin and Maya watched from three miles away ready to activate bioterrorism protocols if she disappeared.)

(Totally fine.)

Six.

Seven.

The elevator stopped.

Sublevel 3.

The doors opened to reveal a man holding two paper cups of coffee like he'd been waiting for her.

Mid-thirties. Latino. Tired eyes behind wire-rimmed glasses. Lab coat with "Dr. David Lagos, Lead Geneticist" embroidered over the pocket in letters so small you'd have to get close to read them.

(Sarah was suddenly very aware of personal space boundaries and how this man could probably kill her seventeen different ways using molecular biology.)

"Dr. Chen," Lagos said. Voice quiet. Careful. Like someone who'd learned that speaking too loud in this building attracted wrong kinds of attention. "Morrison said you'd be joining us. I brought coffee."

He held out one of the cups.

Sarah stared at it.

Coffee.

Offered by a man who probably helped engineer the pathogen that would kill billions.

"It's not poisoned," Lagos said. Then, with the

kind of dark humor that only worked when you were already in hell: "I mean, it's GenVault break room coffee, so it's definitely toxic. But not lethally."

Sarah took the cup.

It was warm.

Real. Tangible. The kind of normal human gesture that should not exist in a building where genocide got optimized during PowerPoint presentations.

"Thank you," she said.

Lagos smiled.

Small. Sad. Genuine.

Like he knew exactly how absurd it was to be offering coffee while genocide timelines counted down overhead.

"The archive is this way." He started walking down a hallway that looked like every other institutional hallway Sarah had ever seen—fluorescent lights, beige walls, the subtle hum of HVAC systems that never quite managed comfortable temperature.

Except the doors they passed had security keypads that probably required retinal scans and blood samples.

Sarah sipped the coffee.

Lagos was right. It tasted like institutional despair and burnt beans.

"Morrison says you have defensive Protocol markers," Lagos said. Not looking at her. Just walking. Casual. Like he was discussing weather instead of genetics that made her a target for breeding programs. "Twenty-eight generations from Maria of Toledo. That's... remarkably rare."

"Lucky me," Sarah said.

Her voice came out flatter than intended.

Lagos stopped walking.

Turned.

Really looked at her.

His eyes were brown. Deep brown. The kind of brown that held exhaustion and something else Sarah couldn't quite identify. Guilt? Regret? The specific look people got when they'd made terrible choices and couldn't figure out how to unmake them?

"I'm sorry," he said quietly.

Sarah's brain hiccupped.

"What?"

"For the breeding program comment. Morrison mentioned it upstairs, didn't he? His 'voluntary genetic contribution' pitch." Lagos's jaw tightened. "He does that. Frames eugenics as opportunity. Makes collaboration sound like saving the world."

"Isn't that what you all think you're doing?" Sarah gripped her coffee cup harder than necessary. Paper crinkled under her fingers. "Saving the world through optimized extinction?"

Lagos was quiet for a long moment.

Then he resumed walking.

"The archive," he said, "contains three thousand years of defensive Protocol implementation data. Every iteration. Every success. Every failure." He stopped at a door marked with biohazard symbols and security warnings Sarah didn't want to translate. "You wanted complete transparency. Here it is."

He pressed his palm to the scanner.

Retinal scan. Palm print. Security keypad requiring a code so long Sarah stopped counting digits.

The door opened.

---

The archive looked like someone had mashed together a medieval library, a modern genetics lab, and a climate-controlled museum exhibit, then filled it with evidence of the apocalypse.

Floor-to-ceiling shelves. Climate-controlled cases. Manuscripts in languages Sarah recognized (Latin, Arabic, Hebrew) and languages she didn't (something that looked like medieval Catalan? Old Norse? Her brain was too overwhelmed to process linguistics).

And between the manuscripts: hard drives. Servers. Digital displays showing genetic sequence data scrolling past too fast to read.

Three thousand years of pattern.

Three thousand years of the Order manipulating civilizations through controlled genetics.

Sarah's coffee cup was shaking in her hand.

She set it down on the nearest table before she spilled it.

Lagos was watching her. Not threatening. Just... observing. Like a scientist studying a reaction.

"Overwhelming?" he asked.

"Slightly," Sarah managed.

Her voice sounded distant. Like someone else was using her vocal cords and she was just along for the ride.

Lagos pulled up a chair. Gestured for Sarah to sit.

She sat.

The chair was metal. Cold. Uncomfortable. Nothing like the leather trap in Morrison's office.

(Good. She didn't want comfort. Comfort felt like manipulation.)

"Where do you want to start?" Lagos asked. "Bronze Age Collapse data? Black Death implementation? Spanish Flu?" He paused. "THRESHOLD deployment protocols?"

Sarah's throat was doing that closing thing again.

"Timeline," she said. "Show me the timeline. When prevention became impossible and Morrison decided optimization was better."

Lagos's expression shifted.

Something complicated crossing his face. Grief? Recognition? The look people got when someone finally asked the right question?

He pulled up data on a tablet. Turned it toward her.

A graph.

Simple. Devastating.

Defensive Protocol implementation attempts. Starting 1989. Thirty years of Morrison trying to make prevention work at modern scale.

Every simulation ending the same way: collapse anyway. Seven point two billion dead.

"He really tried," Lagos said quietly. "I know what Morrison is. What he's become. But... he really did try prevention first. Spent fifteen years building models, running simulations, recruiting people who specialized in crisis response and distributed resilience."

Lagos pulled up more data.

Personal files. Morrison's research notes. Pages and pages of someone genuinely trying to save everyone.

"Emily died in 1992," Lagos continued. "Morrison started defensive Protocol research in 1989—three years before she got sick. He wasn't trying to save her. He was trying to save everyone's daughters." He paused. "And then Emily died anyway and he... broke. Decided prevention was impossible and optimization was the only option left."

Sarah stared at the timeline.

1989-2004: Defensive Protocol research. Fifteen years of trying.

2004-2019: THRESHOLD development. Fifteen years of giving up.

"You believe him," Sarah said. Not a question. Statement. "You think prevention really is impossible."

Lagos was quiet.

Then: "I think prevention at modern scale requires resources we don't have and time we've already lost." He met her eyes. "But I also think Morrison's math is wrong. THRESHOLD won't save civilization by killing four billion people. It'll destroy the social cohesion that makes civilization possible."

Sarah's heart rate kicked up.

Ninety-seven beats per minute.

Too fast.

"Then why are you here?" she asked. "Why help him engineer genocide if you think it won't work?"

Lagos smiled.

Saddest thing Sarah had seen since Morrison's coffee cup with the hairline crack.

"Because," he said quietly, "I'm trying to sabotage it from the inside. And I'm very bad at it. And in thirteen days, four billion people die anyway and it'll be partially my fault because I wasn't brave enough to blow the whistle earlier."

Sarah's coffee cup was empty.

She didn't remember drinking it.

Her brain was processing Lagos's words. Trying to categorize them. Ally? Trap? Morrison's next level of recruitment where the lead geneticist pretends to be sabotaging THRESHOLD to make her trust him?

(Probably that last one.)

(Definitely that last one.)

(But what if it wasn't?)

"Prove it," Sarah said.

Lagos reached into his lab coat.

Pulled out a thumb drive.

Set it on the table between them.

"Complete THRESHOLD deployment data," he said. "Hub locations. Pathogen specifications. Timeline. Genetic targeting parameters. Everything Morrison showed you upstairs plus the parts he didn't mention. Like how the targeting isn't as precise as he claims. How estimated casualties range from four billion to five point three billion depending on variables we can't control."

Sarah stared at the drive.

Small. Black. Innocent-looking.

Containing evidence that could bring down GenVault and prevent genocide.

Or evidence that would get her killed if Morrison discovered she had it.

"Why?" Sarah asked. "Why give this to me?"

"Because you're the first person Morrison's recruited who actually argued with him instead of just nodding and accepting the mathematics." Lagos leaned forward. "You told him THRESHOLD destroys social cohesion. You identified the critical flaw. And you walked into this building wearing GPS trackers broadcasting to teams who'll burn it down if you disappear."

Sarah's hand went to the tracker behind her ear.

Touched it.

Warm. Pulsing. Martin watching.

"You know about the trackers," she said flatly.

"I helped Morrison recruit twenty-three people over the past five years," Lagos said. "You're the first one who came prepared for extraction. The first one treating this like intelligence gathering instead of job interview." He paused. "You're also the first one who might actually be able to stop this."

Sarah's fingers closed around the thumb drive.

Cold. Solid. Containing either salvation or trap.

"Morrison will know," she said. "The moment I leave this building with classified data, he'll know."

"He'll know anyway," Lagos said. "You're not leaving this building, Dr. Chen. Morrison's already decided. Seventy-two hours from now, you either join THRESHOLD voluntarily or you join it as a breeding program participant. Those are the only two options he's offering."

Sarah's stomach dropped.

"He said I could walk away."

"Morrison says lots of things." Lagos stood. Walked to one of the manuscript cases. Touched the glass like he could feel the weight of history through climate control. "The defensive Protocol was never about genetic optimization. It was about people brave enough to see collapse coming and choose different timelines. Choose to help instead of hoard. Cooperate instead of compete. Build instead of destroy."

He turned back to her.

"THRESHOLD removes those people. Kills everyone with the genetics that make cooperation possible. Four billion murders to prevent collapse except it guarantees collapse by destroying the humans who prevent it." His voice cracked. Just slightly. "I've been trying to sabotage it for two years. All I've managed is slowing deployment by six months and getting my research assistant killed when Morrison figured out she was helping me."

Sarah's hands were shaking again.

"Her name was Ava," Lagos continued. Voice flat now. Dead. "Twenty-six years old. Brilliant geneticist. Found discrepancies in the targeting parameters and tried to fix them. Car accident three months ago. Brake failure." He met Sarah's eyes. "Morrison doesn't let people leave once they know too much. And you, Dr. Chen, know everything."

The thumb drive was warm now.

Sarah's hand had been clenching it so hard the plastic had absorbed her body heat.

"Why tell me this?" she asked. "Why not just let Morrison recruit me, let me think I have choices?"

"Because," Lagos said quietly, "I'm tired of watching people die because I was too scared to act. And because you walked in here prepared to die if necessary. That's defensive Protocol thinking. Crisis response. Making hard choices." He paused. "I can get you out. Not today. Morrison's watching too closely. But in seventy-two hours, during shift change, there's a forty-three second window where sublevel security protocols overlap. Fire alarm system test. Electronic locks disengage. Forty-three seconds to the medical bay exit."

Sarah's brain was doing math.

Forty-three seconds.

Sublevel 3 to medical bay exit.

Possible. Barely. If everything went perfectly.

(Nothing ever went perfectly.)

"What about you?" she asked.

Lagos smiled.

That sad, brave smile again.

"Morrison knows I helped you the moment you run. Knows I gave you the data. He'll execute me for treason." He shrugged. "But four billion people survive if THRESHOLD doesn't deploy. My death saves more lives than my genetic research ever could."

Sarah's throat was doing that thing where it forgot how to work.

"I can't—"

"You can," Lagos interrupted. "You already are. The defensive Protocol chose you for pattern recognition and crisis response. You see the collapse coming. You're gathering the intelligence to prevent it. All you need is the courage to run when the moment comes."

He checked his watch.

"Morrison expects us upstairs in twenty minutes. You should spend that time reviewing the deployment data. Memorize what you can. Verify independently. Make sure I'm not lying or manipulating you into trusting me."

"Are you?" Sarah asked. "Lying or manipulating?"

"Probably," Lagos said. "That's what Morrison trained me to do. Recruit brilliant people through whatever means necessary. Build rapport. Earn trust. Extract cooperation." He paused. "But Ava's death was real. My guilt is real. And my desire to stop being complicit in genocide is real."

He moved toward the door.

Stopped.

Looked back.

"In seventy-two hours," he said quietly, "I'll trigger the fire alarm during shift change. Forty-three seconds. Medical bay exit leads to parking garage level B2. Your people will be waiting?"

Sarah's hand touched the tracker behind her ear.

Martin watching.

Maya ready.

Both of them three miles away and powerless to extract her right now.

But in seventy-two hours?

"They'll be there," Sarah said.

Lagos nodded.

"Coffee waiting," he said. Strange phrase. Weighted with meaning Sarah didn't quite catch.

Then he left.

Left Sarah alone in an archive containing three thousand years of evidence that humanity kept almost destroying itself and kept getting saved by people brave enough to choose different timelines.

She looked down at the thumb drive in her hand.

Then at her coffee cup.

Still warm.

Still real.

Still the kind of normal human gesture that shouldn't exist in buildings where genocide got optimized.

Sarah plugged in the thumb drive.

Started reading deployment protocols.

Behind her ear, the tracker pulsed.

Steady.

Alive.

Counting down seventy-two hours until she either escaped with evidence to stop THRESHOLD or became a permanent resident of Morrison's breeding program.

(Definitely escaping.)

(Probably dying trying.)

(But four billion people survived if she got the data out.)

Sarah opened the first file.

Started memorizing deployment hub locations.

Started seeing the pattern.

Started choosing which timeline she wanted to live in.
