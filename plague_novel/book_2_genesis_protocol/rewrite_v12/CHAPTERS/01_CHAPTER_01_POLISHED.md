# CHAPTER 1 - THE BURNING

**November 9, 2019 - New Haven, Connecticut**
**1:47 AM**

---

Sarah Chen stood in Linda Marsh's driveway watching the house burn.

Too late. Again.

Flames ate through the second-floor windows. Black smoke poured into November cold. Fire trucks screamed three blocks away but the house was gone. Everything inside was gone.

(Linda Marsh. CDC epidemiologist. The woman who'd sent Sarah an encrypted message eighteen hours ago: *"Found something at GenVault. Genetic research. Wrong numbers. Need pattern analysis ASAP."*)

(The woman who was now dead inside that house because Sarah had been eighteen hours too slow.)

Sarah's phone buzzed in her pocket. Martin Reeves. FBI contact. The man who'd connected her with Marsh two weeks ago.

She answered without looking away from the flames.

"Linda's house is on fire," she said.

"I know." Martin's voice was tight. Controlled. "New Haven FD got anonymous tip twenty minutes ago. Electrical fire. Except Linda's breaker box was inspected last month and passed perfectly."

"Where's Linda?"

"Unknown. Not answering phone. Car's in the driveway. Sarah—"

"She's inside." Sarah's throat closed. "She's dead inside and this wasn't electrical and you know it."

Martin was quiet. Then: "Get out of there. Now. If this was orchestrated, they're watching to see who shows up."

Sarah ended the call.

Walked toward the house anyway.

(Because Linda Marsh had found something worth killing for and Sarah needed to know what.)

The front door hung open. Kicked in or blown out by pressure—hard to tell. Heat rolled through the doorway in waves. Sarah pulled her jacket over her mouth and went inside.

The entryway was an oven. Wallpaper peeling. Paint bubbling. Smoke so thick she couldn't see three feet ahead.

"Linda!" Sarah shouted. No answer. Just fire crackling and wood groaning as support beams weakened.

The stairs were impassable—flames blocking the second floor entirely. Sarah dropped low where smoke thinned and crawled toward the study. Linda's workspace. Where an epidemiologist would keep research.

The study door was closed. Sarah grabbed the handle—hot but not searing—and shoved it open.

Linda Marsh lay on the floor beside her desk.

Not burned. Not trapped. Just dead.

Single gunshot wound. Back of the head. Execution style.

(So this was murder dressed as electrical fire. This was someone making sure Linda Marsh's "wrong numbers" stayed buried.)

Sarah's hands shook as she checked for pulse. Nothing. Body still warm. Maybe dead twenty minutes. Maybe less.

The desk was empty. Computer gone. Filing cabinets forced open and ransacked. Everything Linda had found—stolen or destroyed.

The ceiling groaned. Flames eating through from above. Sarah had maybe thirty seconds before the whole structure collapsed.

She scanned the room. Nothing. Then her foot hit something under the desk. A fireproof lockbox. Small. Heavy. Hidden.

Sarah grabbed it and ran.

Out through the study. Through the entryway. Past wallpaper fully engulfed. Out the front door as the second floor collapsed inward with a sound like the world ending.

She made it to the street as fire trucks arrived.

Paramedics surrounded her. "Ma'am, are you hurt? Were you inside?"

"I heard screaming," Sarah lied. "Thought someone was trapped. Couldn't find anyone."

(Linda Marsh's body would burn now. Evidence consumed. Investigation concluding electrical fire, tragic accident, single casualty.)

A sedan idled across the street. Black. Tinted windows. Engine running but lights off.

Watching.

Sarah met the driver's gaze through the windshield. Couldn't see features. Just a silhouette.

Then the sedan pulled away.

Slow. Deliberate. Making sure she saw them leave.

(That was the message: We know you came. We're watching. You're next if you keep looking.)

Sarah walked to her car three blocks away. Got inside. Locked the doors. Set the lockbox on the passenger seat.

Her hands were shaking. Smoke smell thick in her hair. Linda Marsh dead eighteen hours after reaching out.

(And Sarah had been too slow. Too careful. Too worried about protocol to just drive to New Haven immediately when the message arrived.)

The lockbox was cool. Fireproof. Combination lock.

Sarah tried Linda's birthday. Wrong.

Tried her lab ID number. Wrong.

Tried the date she'd sent her last CDC report. Wrong.

Two attempts remaining before the lock froze.

Sarah closed her eyes. Thought about the encrypted message. "Wrong numbers." Pattern analysis. GenVault.

She tried: 11-09-19. Today's date.

The lockbox opened.

Inside: A thumb drive. A handwritten note. A photograph.

The note read:

*"Sarah - If you're reading this, I'm dead. GenVault Pharmaceuticals is running genetic research that doesn't match their public filings. Casualty projections. Deployment schedules. I found the numbers. They're planning something. The thumb drive has everything I could copy before security caught me. Find the pattern. Stop this. - LM"*

The photograph showed three men in a conference room. Linda had written names on the back:

- **Dr. James Morrison** - GenVault Director of Genetic Research
- **Dr. David Lagos** - Lead Research Coordinator
- **Martin Reeves** - FBI Regional Director

Sarah stared at Martin's name.

(The man who'd connected her with Linda. The man who'd told her to run. The man whose face was in a photograph Linda had hidden in a fireproof box with a warning about GenVault.)

Her phone rang.

Unknown number.

Sarah answered.

"Dr. Chen." The voice was male. Older. Precise. "My name is Dr. James Morrison. I'm calling about Linda Marsh."

Sarah said nothing.

"You're holding her lockbox right now," Morrison continued. "Sitting in your car three blocks from the fire. You opened it using today's date. Smart. Linda knew you'd figure it out."

"Who are you?"

"The man in the photograph. GenVault's director of genetic research. The man Linda was investigating before she died." Morrison paused. "And the man offering you a choice."

"What choice?"

"Linda found partial data. Incomplete picture. She died before finishing analysis. You're a pattern recognition specialist—CDC's best mathematical epidemiologist. You can finish what she started. See the complete pattern. Understand what GenVault is doing."

"Why would I do that?"

"Because in seventy-two hours, 262 million people will die in Phase One deployment. In ninety-six hours, another 7.2 billion die in Phase Two. Linda found evidence of Phase One. You're holding it. The question is whether you'll use it."

Sarah's stomach dropped. "You're confessing to genocide."

"I'm offering you time. Seventy-two hours to investigate. GenVault's main facility is in Boston. Security credentials are on that thumb drive—Linda copied my access codes before she died. You can walk in freely. Review research. Gather evidence. Understand exactly what's coming."

"And then?"

"Then you decide whether to stop it or let it proceed. Whether 262 million deaths are acceptable to prevent 7.2 billion deaths. Whether genocide now prevents extinction later." Morrison's voice was steady. Clinical. "That's the choice, Dr. Chen. Linda died investigating partial data. You can investigate complete data. See the full pattern. Make an informed decision."

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because Linda deserved better. Because someone should see the complete mathematics before deployment. Because if there's another way—if your defensive Protocol theories actually work—I want to know before 262 million people die."

Morrison paused.

"Seventy-two hours, Dr. Chen. GenVault Pharmaceuticals, Boston. Sublevel 2 research archives. Everything Linda found is there. Everything she died for. Come investigate or don't. Your choice. But the countdown started at midnight."

He hung up.

Sarah sat in her car watching smoke rise from Linda Marsh's house. Fire trucks pumping water into a structure already lost. Paramedics packing equipment. Police establishing perimeter for investigation that would conclude accidental electrical fire.

She looked at the thumb drive.

Seventy-two hours.

262 million casualties.

7.2 billion more.

(And a man confessing genocide while offering her time to investigate.)

Her phone buzzed. Text from Martin Reeves: **Are you out? Are you safe?**

Sarah looked at Martin's face in the photograph. FBI regional director photographed with GenVault researchers. Connected to Linda Marsh's investigation. Maybe complicit. Maybe not.

She typed back: **I'm out. Need to process. Will call tomorrow.**

Then she opened her laptop and plugged in the thumb drive.

The files loaded. Dozens of documents. Research papers. Casualty projections. Deployment schedules. A folder labeled "THRESHOLD - Phase One Targets."

Sarah opened it.

The screen filled with maps. Cities. Population density overlays. Genetic distribution data. Casualty estimates by region.

47.3 million: Atlanta

67.2 million: Tokyo

89.4 million: Lagos

(Wrong numbers. Linda had called them wrong numbers. These were genocide calculations.)

Sarah's hands shook as she scrolled through projections. 262 million casualties total in Phase One. Targeted genetic elimination. Specific populations removed. Precision genocide.

The document was titled: "THRESHOLD Protocol - Population Optimization Through Genetic Targeting."

(Population optimization. That's what they called killing 262 million people.)

Sarah pulled up Linda's note again. "Find the pattern. Stop this."

She looked at the countdown on her dashboard clock: 1:47 AM. Seventy-two hours until November 12th. Seventy-two hours until Phase One deployment.

Morrison had given her credentials. Access to GenVault. Time to investigate.

(Time to see if defensive Protocol—cooperation, pattern recognition, prevention—could stop genocide before it started.)

Sarah started her car.

Typed in GPS coordinates for GenVault Pharmaceuticals, Boston.

Three-hour drive. She'd arrive at dawn. Seventy-two hours to investigate. Seventy-two hours to decide if one person could stop 262 million deaths.

(Linda Marsh died trying.)

(Sarah would finish it.)

She pulled onto the highway heading north.

Behind her: smoke rising from New Haven. Linda Marsh's body burning. Evidence consumed.

Ahead: GenVault Pharmaceuticals. Complete data. Full pattern. 262 million lives in seventy-two hours.

(This was the choice: investigate or run.)

(This was the pattern: genocide dressed as optimization.)

(This was the countdown.)

Sarah drove north through November darkness toward whatever truth was waiting in Boston.

Coffee would taste terrible in three hours.

But she'd drink it anyway.

(Because that's what you did when you were seventy-two hours from stopping genocide.)

(Or failing to.)
