# CHAPTER 1 - THE PATTERN

**Modern Timeline - New Haven, Connecticut**
**November 15, 2019 - 1:47 AM**

---

The smell hit Sarah before she saw the flames.

Not wood smoke—she'd smelled that before, camping trips in Vermont with Linda, back when her biggest problem was whether to accept GenVault's job offer or stay in academia. This was wrong. Chemical. The kind of wrong that bypassed her PhD brain entirely and went straight to the part that still lived in caves and knew fire meant death.

Accelerant mixed with something organic burning.

Her legs were already moving before her conscious mind caught up.

Marsh's house.

Sarah ran and her lungs screamed and her laptop bag slammed against her hip with each stride and somewhere in the logical part of her brain that still worked she was thinking: *you're too late you're too late you're always too late*.

Fifty yards away. Forty. The gravel driveway crunching under her sneakers loud enough to wake the dead except nothing was waking the dead tonight, nothing was waking anyone in that house.

Orange light painted the pine trees. Wrong colors. Fire-colors that didn't belong in Connecticut woods at 1:47 AM on a Friday when Sarah was supposed to be meeting Dr. Marsh and getting the cipher key and proving GenVault was engineering genocides not preventing them.

The front door hung open like a mouth screaming.

Smoke poured out thick and black and alive, rolling across the porch like it was hunting for something to choke.

"MARSH!"

Sarah's voice cracked. Too loud. Too desperate. The smoke alarm inside was shrieking—that particular pitch designed to wake you up, to make you move, to save your life if you could be saved.

It wasn't saving Marsh.

Sarah hit the porch steps and heat slammed into her face like a physical thing. The kind of heat that evaporated sweat instantly and left her skin feeling too tight, shrinking, about to split. Her eyes watered. Her throat closed. Every instinct she had was screaming *back away back away you can't help her she's already dead*.

She pulled her jacket over her nose. It didn't help. Smoke found ways in—down her throat, into her lungs, tasting like burning plastic and something underneath it, something sweet and terrible that her brain refused to identify because identifying it meant admitting what was burning.

"MARSH! WHERE ARE YOU?"

Movement. Inside. Ten feet from the door.

A shape on the floor. Not moving right. Just the slump of a body positioned wrong, one arm bent at an angle that living people didn't make, head turned away like she'd been trying to crawl toward the door and hadn't made it.

Sarah took two steps into the house and the heat hit her like a wall made of pain.

Her skin prickled. Her eyes felt like they were boiling. The smoke was so thick she couldn't see three feet and her lungs were doing this thing where they kept trying to cough but there was nothing to cough, just more smoke pouring in.

Five feet from the body. Four. Three.

An old woman. White hair matted with blood. Face turned away. Not moving. Not breathing. Definitely not breathing because Sarah had done CPR training and you could tell when someone's chest was supposed to rise and fall and this chest wasn't doing anything.

Sarah's hands reached for her anyway. Grabbed an arm. Started pulling.

The sleeve came apart in her hands.

The skin underneath was—

Sarah dropped it. Stumbled backward. Her stomach heaved and she bent over coughing and somewhere in the distance sirens were wailing and she needed to get out, needed to get out *now*, this house was coming down and she was going to die in here with Marsh if she didn't—

She ran. Back through the smoke. Through the door. Across the porch. Down the steps. Onto the lawn where she fell to her knees and vomited into the grass and couldn't stop coughing and her eyes were streaming and everything tasted like smoke and death.

Behind her, something inside the house exploded. Glass shattered. More flame. The entire second floor lit up like a funeral pyre.

Sarah knelt in the grass and watched Dr. Evelyn Marsh's house burn and thought: *six hours ago Aris gave me this address.*

*Twelve hours ago I triggered Level Three security alerts.*

*They found her first.*

---

Six hours ago, Dr. Otto Aris had given her this address. Scrawled it on a napkin in a coffee shop that smelled like burnt beans and old grease, his hands shaking so badly he could barely hold the pen.

*The only surviving member of the original translation team. She has the source documents. The cipher key that proves everything.*

Now the house was an inferno and Marsh was dead and Sarah was kneeling in wet grass watching evidence burn.

A fire truck screamed up the driveway. Red lights. Men shouting. Hoses unfurling. Sarah sat there and watched them work and couldn't make herself move because her legs had forgotten how.

"Ma'am?" A firefighter appeared in front of her, face shield pushed up, concern in his eyes that looked practiced. "Were you inside? Do you need medical attention?"

Sarah shook her head. Her throat hurt too much to talk.

"When did it start?" Her voice came out as a croak.

"Call came in at 1:34 AM." The firefighter was already turning away, speaking into his radio. "Neighbors reported explosion, immediate spread—"

Explosion.

Sarah's stomach dropped through the ground.

Not an accident. Not faulty wiring. Not a knocked-over candle or an old woman who forgot she'd left the stove on.

They'd found Marsh first.

Found her and eliminated her and made it look like an accident and Sarah had walked straight into it like an idiot who thought she was clever, who thought downloading files and running would be enough, who'd led them right to the one person who could prove what GenVault was doing.

A second fire truck arrived. More shouting. Water arcing through the night. Steam rising where it hit flames. The house was too far gone—they were just containing the spread now, keeping the fire from jumping to the trees, protecting structures that could still be saved.

Marsh's house was already dead.

Just like Marsh.

A paramedic appeared at Sarah's elbow. Young. Efficient. The kind of efficient that came from seeing terrible things and learning not to feel them. "Ma'am? Were you a family member?"

"No." Sarah's voice sounded wrong. Distant. Like someone else was talking through her mouth. "Colleague. Researcher. Is she—has anyone—"

The paramedic's expression told her everything before his mouth opened.

"They found a body?" Sarah whispered.

"I can't comment on an active investigation. But if you knew the resident, you should speak with Detective Morrison. He's—"

"Morrison?"

The name hit like ice water.

Like waking up.

Like realizing you'd been asleep this whole time and now you were awake and everything was so much worse than you thought.

The paramedic gestured toward a man standing near the fire chief. Tall. Mid-fifties. Expensive wool coat over a suit that probably cost more than Sarah's monthly rent. Taking notes. Speaking into a phone.

Not watching the fire.

Watching *her*.

Their eyes met across the chaos of burning house and emergency vehicles and firefighters shouting coordinates.

Dr. James Morrison smiled.

And Sarah's brain screamed: *run run run run run*.

She ran.

---

## The Chase

Sarah didn't think. Thinking was what got Linda killed. Thinking was what got Marsh killed. Thinking was what got you noticed by people like Morrison who smiled while houses burned with old women inside.

She just turned and sprinted back toward the rental car she'd parked crooked in the middle of the street three houses down. Keys in her jacket pocket. Laptop bag slamming against her hip. Lungs still burning from smoke.

"Miss?" Morrison's voice behind her. Calm. Professional. The voice of authority that made you stop and listen and cooperate because surely police officers were here to help. "We need to ask you some questions—"

Sarah reached the car. Fumbled the key fob. Her hands were shaking so hard she dropped it. Picked it up. Pressed the button. The locks clicked.

Footsteps accelerating behind her. Getting closer. "Ma'am, stop. Detective Morrison, New Haven PD. I need you to—"

She threw herself into the driver's seat. Slammed the door. Locked it. Engine starting. Hands shaking so badly she could barely grip the wheel.

Morrison appeared at her window. Badge out. Looking patient. Looking amused. "Miss Chen. Turn off the vehicle. We need to talk about Dr. Marsh and what you were doing at her house at 1:47 AM."

He knew her name.

She hadn't told the paramedic her name.

She hadn't told anyone her name.

Sarah's foot hit the accelerator.

The rental shot backward, tires screaming on pavement. Morrison jumped aside—not fast, casual, like he'd been expecting this. Sarah cranked the wheel. Shifted into drive. Floored it.

The car fishtailed. Straightened. Accelerated down Elm Street past houses with lights coming on, past neighbors in bathrobes watching the fire, past everything Sarah had thought she was running toward when she'd gotten on that bus in Boston.

In her rearview mirror: Morrison standing in the middle of the road. Phone to his ear. Not running. Not calling for backup. Not even looking upset.

Just watching her go with that patient, predatory smile.

Like he'd wanted her to run.

Like he'd *planned* for her to run.

Like this whole thing—Marsh's death, the fire, Morrison showing up—was exactly what he'd wanted to happen.

## Thirty Minutes Earlier

(Because Sarah's brain kept going back there, kept trying to figure out where she'd made the mistake, where she'd done the thing that got Marsh killed.)

She'd stepped off the Greyhound at 1:15 AM into cold November air that smelled like diesel and urban decay. New Haven bus station. Nearly empty. Just her and a sleeping drunk and a maintenance worker mopping floors with industrial disinfectant that was supposed to smell like pine but mostly just smelled like chemicals lying about being pine.

The address Aris had given her was written on a napkin: *147 Elm St. Back door only. Don't call ahead.*

But Sarah had called anyway because she was smart, because she'd been trained to verify things, because showing up unannounced at a stranger's house at 1 AM was how you got the police called on you.

She'd found a payphone. The kind that still existed because bus stations were where time went to die. Dialed the number Aris had scrawled below the address.

Four rings. Five.

Then a woman's voice, cautious: "Yes?"

"Dr. Marsh? My name is Sarah Chen. Dr. Otto Aris sent me—"

"Hang up." The voice was sharp. Afraid. "Hang up right now."

"Please, I have information about the Genesis Protocol, the medieval manuscripts that—"

"I said hang up. They monitor calls. If you're real, if Otto actually sent you, come to the address. Back door. Not the front. Don't call again. Don't—"

The line went dead.

Sarah had stood there in fluorescent glare listening to dial tone and deciding.

Then she'd made the second mistake: she'd gotten into a taxi.

Given the driver the address.

Watched New Haven slide past through scratched windows—empty streets, closed shops, Yale campus silent and dark like everyone sensible was asleep.

The taxi had dropped her two blocks from Elm Street. She'd paid cash. Walked the rest. Watched for surveillance.

And then she'd seen the glow.

The orange light painting trees.

The fire.

Too late. Always too late.

---

## The Pattern

Sarah drove north on I-91 with no destination except *away*.

Hands white-knuckled on the wheel. Checking mirrors every three seconds. Speedometer creeping past eighty. Heart hammering so hard she could feel it in her ears.

No pursuit.

The road stretched empty behind her—just darkness and the occasional truck and her own paranoia reflected in the rearview mirror.

Dr. Marsh was dead.

The only person who could prove what GenVault was doing. The only surviving translator who understood the medieval cipher system. The only witness who'd been there in 1982 when the Order decided to weaponize a defensive protocol.

Dead.

Twelve hours after Sarah triggered Level Three security alerts by accessing restricted databases.

Six hours after Aris gave her Marsh's address.

Ninety minutes after Sarah called from a payphone that probably wasn't as secure as she'd thought.

They'd found Marsh. Eliminated her. Burned the evidence. And Morrison had been there—not as GenVault security director but as *Detective Morrison, New Haven PD*. Using her name. Smiling.

The Order wasn't just a pharmaceutical company running secret experiments.

It was everywhere.

Police. Hospitals. Universities.

Anywhere someone might notice the pattern.

---

## Six Months Earlier
## (The Reason Sarah Couldn't Stop)

Linda.

Dr. Linda Hwang. Sarah's MIT advisor. The woman who'd recruited her to GenVault. Who'd mentored her through doctoral defense and career uncertainty and impostor syndrome and all the ways academic science tried to convince you that you weren't good enough.

May 2019. GenVault's Cambridge lab. Routine project meeting. Linda had been presenting population genetics data when she'd stopped mid-sentence. Hand to her head. Confused expression. Then collapsed.

Cerebral aneurysm. Dead before paramedics arrived. Forty-seven years old. No warning. No family history. No reason.

Statistically improbable.

GenVault HR had been efficient. Cleared Linda's office within forty-eight hours. Reassigned her projects. Sent flowers to the funeral that Sarah hadn't been able to attend because she'd been too busy hyperventilating in a bathroom stall thinking about how Linda had been fine, had been *fine*, had been laughing about her daughter's soccer tournament twenty minutes before her brain exploded.

They'd asked Sarah to take over Linda's disease resistance mapping research.

Sarah had said yes because she didn't know what else to do with the grief.

Three weeks later, she'd found Linda's private notes.

Backup files Linda kept on a shared server that HR hadn't known about. Notes showing escalating concern about genetic markers appearing in population datasets before they should exist. CCR5-Delta32 variants in pre-1348 European populations. Plague resistance markers pre-dating the plague by centuries.

Statistically impossible unless selection pressure occurred earlier than recorded history suggested.

Linda's final entry, dated two days before her death:

*"GenVault blocking archive access. Morrison requested meeting tomorrow. Something wrong. Sarah—if you're reading this, it means I didn't make it to the meeting. Don't trust Morrison. Don't trust anyone at GenVault. The pattern is real."*

No further entries.

Just Linda's death.

And Morrison's signature on the autopsy report declaring it natural causes.

Sarah had spent six months following Linda's research trail. Finding the same impossible correlations. Noticing that every researcher who got too close—Marsh's colleague in 1982, Aris forced into hiding, Linda dead at forty-seven—disappeared or died or stopped asking questions.

And tonight, Marsh had burned.

Sarah's hands tightened on the wheel.

Linda's last words to her—actual last words, not the note—had been: "Coffee tomorrow? I want to show you something interesting."

Tomorrow had been the meeting with Morrison.

Tomorrow Linda had died.

And Sarah had spent six months pretending it was coincidence because admitting it wasn't meant admitting she worked for people who killed anyone who got too close to the pattern.

---

Sarah's burner phone—bought with cash at the bus station, never connected to anything, supposed to be untraceable—buzzed in the cup holder.

Unknown number.

She almost didn't answer.

Then she thought: they already know where I am. They let me run from the burning house. They've been tracking me since I left GenVault. Answering can't make this worse.

(It could definitely make this worse.)

She pulled onto the shoulder. Hazards blinking. Answered. "Yes?"

"Dr. Chen." Morrison's voice. Calm. Almost gentle. Like he was calling about a dinner reservation. "I apologize for the dramatic introduction. The fire was... unfortunate timing."

"You killed her."

"Marsh died of smoke inhalation approximately twelve minutes before you arrived. Natural gas leak. Faulty furnace. Tragic accident for a woman who lived alone and probably couldn't smell the leak." A pause that felt calculated. "The timing was unfortunate. For both of us."

Sarah's hands were shaking again. She pressed them against her thighs to make them stop. "How did you get this number?"

"The same way I knew you'd call Marsh from that payphone. The same way I knew you'd run when you saw me." Morrison's tone shifted—still calm, but with an edge of something that might have been respect if respect didn't make Sarah's skin crawl. "Dr. Aris warned you that the Order is everywhere. He wasn't exaggerating."

"What do you want?"

"To talk. To show you what you're really dealing with. To offer you a choice before you make decisions that can't be unmade." Traffic sounds in the background—he was driving, following her probably, tracking her location through the phone she was an idiot for answering. "You think you're exposing a conspiracy. What you're actually doing is confirming that you're exactly the person we've been waiting for."

"I'm not joining you."

"I'm not asking you to. Not yet." Morrison paused and Sarah could hear him smile through the phone. "I'm asking you to listen. To see the complete picture. To understand what you're really part of before you try to stop something you don't understand."

"Like Dr. Marsh understood?"

"Marsh died because she refused to understand." Morrison's voice hardened. "She had terminal cancer. Six months to live. We offered her a choice: help us refine the Protocol, or watch Phase I deploy without her input. She chose ideology over humanity."

"You're insane."

"I'm a scientist. Like you. Like Dr. Hwang was, before she found the pattern and refused to see what it meant."

Linda's name in his mouth made Sarah's stomach heave.

"Don't—don't talk about her."

"Linda Hwang spent six months investigating the same genetic correlations you've been tracking. She found exactly what you found: that the Black Death survivors weren't random. That someone engineered resistance markers into specific bloodlines centuries before the plague arrived. She was brilliant." Morrison paused. "She was also idealistic. When I showed her the collapse models, the resource depletion curves, the mathematical certainty that humanity was heading toward civilizational failure—she rejected the solution because the math was uncomfortable."

"She died before you could kill her for refusing."

"She died because she had a pre-existing aneurysm that GenVault medical screening flagged three years before her death. We monitored it. We knew the risk. We hoped she'd join us before it became relevant." Morrison sounded genuinely sad. "The timing was unfortunate. For both of us."

Sarah couldn't breathe. Her throat was doing that thing again where it wanted to close up and her brain was showing her Linda collapsing and she could taste smoke even though she was fifty miles from the fire.

"The Genesis Protocol isn't what you think it is," Morrison continued. "It wasn't designed to cause harm. It was designed to prevent collapse. To optimize humanity's survival through catastrophic bottlenecks. The medieval cipher-makers weren't monsters—they were trying to save civilization."

"By killing millions of people."

"By preserving billions." Morrison's voice carried absolute conviction that made Sarah want to vomit. "The Black Death killed ninety million. But humanity survived. Rebuilt. Thrived. Because the Protocol ensured that the survivors carried resilient genetic traits. That's not genocide, Dr. Chen—that's directed evolution."

"You're talking about eugenics."

"I'm talking about optimization. About giving humanity a future." Morrison paused. "THRESHOLD deploys in seventy-two hours. Phase I will reduce Homo sapiens population to sustainable levels. You can't stop it. Aris can't stop it. But you can choose whether you understand it when it happens."

Seventy-two hours.

Three days.

"You're going to release something," Sarah said. "A bioweapon."

"We're allowing nature to select for resilience. The difference is precision." Morrison's tone didn't change. "Initial models project 262 million casualties across Phase I. Tragic. But Phase II—which would occur organically over the next thirty years as climate collapse and resource depletion compound—would kill 7.2 billion. The math is uncomfortable. But it's certain."

262 million.

The number was too big. Sarah's brain couldn't process it. Couldn't convert it to actual people with names and lives and futures.

262 million.

That was the entire population of the United States.

Gone.

"You're talking about genocide."

"I'm talking about triage. About choosing which version of catastrophe we can live with." Morrison exhaled. "Dr. Chen, you carry defensive Protocol markers in your own genome. CCR5-Delta32 variant. Crisis-response genetics. Twenty-eight generations from Maria of Toledo, one of Nefertari's original bloodline carriers. You, personally, are a product of successful Protocol implementation. You exist because someone three thousand years ago made the choice to engineer survival."

That couldn't be true.

But if it was—

If her own bloodline was a result of this pattern—

"I'm giving you two weeks," Morrison said. "To verify the data. To understand what we're actually trying to accomplish. To see that the Order isn't evil—it's necessary. After that, THRESHOLD deploys whether you've joined us or not."

"And if I refuse?"

"Then you'll watch 262 million people die in Phase I, knowing you could have helped make it more humane. You'll watch 7.2 billion die in Phase II, knowing you could have helped prevent it by joining us instead of fighting us." Morrison's voice softened like he actually cared. "Or you can help us. Refine the Protocol. Reduce casualties. Minimize suffering. We need people like you, Sarah. People brilliant enough to understand the mathematics and ethical enough to care about harm reduction."

The line went dead.

Sarah sat on the highway shoulder. Hazards blinking. Staring at the phone.

Seventy-two hours until THRESHOLD deployed.

And Morrison thought he was the good guy.

And somewhere deep in the part of Sarah's brain that still worked, the part that had gotten her through MIT and doctoral defense and Linda's death, a voice was whispering: *what if he's right?*

She wanted to throw up.

## The Key

Sarah's hands were shaking too badly to drive.

She pulled into a rest stop. Parked under flickering lights that made everything look like a crime scene. Locked the doors. Watched a semi pull in two spots over, driver climbing out for a cigarette, not looking at her rental car or caring that someone was sitting inside at 3 AM hyperventilating.

She needed to think.

Morrison had let her escape GenVault. Let her run from Marsh's burning house. Given her seventy-two hours. Called her personally instead of just disappearing her like Linda.

Why?

Because he wanted her to join them. To recruit her. To show her "the complete picture" and convince her that genocide was actually optimization and murder was actually mercy.

Or because he wanted to see where she'd run. Who she'd contact. Whether there were other resistance members he didn't know about yet.

Both, probably.

Morrison didn't do anything without three backup plans and a spreadsheet.

Sarah pulled out the materials Aris had given her six hours ago in that coffee shop that now felt like a different lifetime. Spread them across the passenger seat.

Photocopied manuscripts. Genetic charts. Population maps showing CCR5-Delta32 distribution in pre-1348 Europe.

And the iron key, wrapped in cloth.

*Don't open this until you're with Dr. Marsh. It only works once.*

Marsh was dead.

The source documents were ash.

But the key remained.

Sarah unwrapped it slowly. Her hands were still shaking but she forced them steady because Linda had taught her: when you're scared, do the science. When the world doesn't make sense, look at the data.

Cold iron. Heavy in her palm. Geometric symbols etched along the shaft—the same symbols from the manuscript margins, the same cipher marks that appeared in Bronze Age texts and medieval plague documents and Linda's research notes.

What was it for?

A safe deposit box? A hidden archive somewhere?

She turned it over. On the back, barely visible, scratched letters that looked like they'd been added later:

**YUL - 1912 - ARIS**

YUL. Yale University Library.

1912. The year someone had catalogued something.

ARIS. Otto Aris. Or someone with the same name. Or a reference to something else entirely.

Sarah's pulse quickened—not the panicked racing from before, but the sharp focus that came from finding a pattern, from seeing the next step.

Marsh's house was destroyed. But if she'd hidden backup documents in Yale's archives—documents that had been there since 1912, long before GenVault existed, long before Morrison could burn them—

Then Morrison was wrong.

She still had a chance to prove everything.

Sarah checked the GPS. Sterling Memorial Library, Yale University. Six miles south.

She glanced at the clock: 3:17 AM.

Closed until morning.

But Sarah had spent years doing graduate research at MIT. She knew how university libraries worked. Knew the back entrances grad students used for all-night research sessions. Knew that rare manuscripts were stored in climate-controlled basements with card-catalog access terminals that never got fully updated to digital systems.

Knew that security guards at 3 AM mostly cared about sleeping through their shifts.

If she was right—if this key unlocked something in Yale's archives—

Then she had leverage.

Evidence.

Proof.

Something Morrison couldn't burn.

Sarah started the car. Pulled back onto the highway. Headed south toward New Haven, toward Yale, toward whatever the key would unlock.

Seventy-two hours until THRESHOLD.

Marsh was dead.

Linda was dead.

But Sarah was still alive.

And she still had the key.

That had to mean something.

(It had to mean something because if it didn't, if Linda and Marsh had died for nothing, if Sarah couldn't stop this—)

(She couldn't think about that.)

(Think about the science. Look at the data. Find the pattern.)

(Prove Morrison wrong.)

The highway lights slid past overhead like a countdown.

Seventy-two hours.

Sarah drove faster.
