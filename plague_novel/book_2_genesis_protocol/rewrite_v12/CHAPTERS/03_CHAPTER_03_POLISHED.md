# CHAPTER 3 - THE TRAP

**24 Hours After Marsh's Death**
**Martin's Safe House - Vermont**

---

Sarah had been staring at the same document for three hours.

Seventy-eight pages. Single-spaced. Everything she'd learned in the past forty-eight hours about Pattern Eye, genetic marker analysis, Otto Aris's historical evidence proving the Genesis Protocol was originally defensive, the Order's perversion of defensive protocols into offensive genocide.

She'd titled it "The Aethelred Cipher" because historians loved obscure Anglo-Saxon references and maybe that would get it past the Order's monitoring algorithms for five extra minutes before they found it and buried it and buried her.

(Buried her.)

The cursor blinked at the end of page seventy-eight.

Waiting for her to write the conclusion.

Waiting for her to decide what happened next.

Sarah's coffee had gone cold. Again. She couldn't remember making coffee. Couldn't remember the last time she'd slept. Her brain was doing this thing where it tried to categorize exhaustion into neat boxes—*this is caffeine crash, this is adrenaline withdrawal, this is what happens when you watch someone's house burn with them inside and realize the people hunting you have institutional reach and murder on their to-do lists*—

"The exposé is ready," Sarah said quietly.

Martin looked up from the window. He'd been standing there for an hour. Watching the tree line. Rifle propped against the wall within arm's reach. The kind of man who'd spent years expecting trouble and knew what trouble looked like when it showed up.

"And then what?" His voice was flat. Analytical. The tone of someone who'd seen this movie before and knew how it ended. "They deny it. Call it conspiracy theory. We've seen this before. Dr. Lisa Park had evidence three years ago. Published it. The Order buried her story in forty-eight hours."

He paused. Something flickered across his face.

"Then buried her."

Elena stopped pacing. She'd been wearing a path in the hardwood between the fireplace and the kitchen for the past twenty minutes. Phone in hand. Coordinating with resistance contacts across three continents. Sarah had heard fragments—encrypted chatter about safe houses and document verification and how many people were still alive who remembered what they were fighting.

Not many.

"Lisa didn't have Pattern Eye documentation," Elena said. "She didn't have Otto Aris's historical proof. This is different."

"Is it?" Martin turned from the window. Looked at Sarah. "You have documentation. You have proof. What you don't have is hub locations. Deployment protocols. The tactical intelligence that would let us *stop* this instead of just exposing it."

Sarah knew he was right.

(She hated that he was right.)

Marsh had given her the cipher that revealed Pattern Eye's existence. But he'd died—*smoke inhalation twelve minutes before you arrived, tragic accident*—before sharing the tactical intelligence. Hub locations. Deployment schedules. Order infrastructure.

Everything they needed to *prevent* Phase I instead of just writing a thorough exposé that would get them all killed.

"Morrison has that intelligence," Sarah said slowly.

The words hung in the air.

Martin nodded. Once. Sharp. Like he'd been waiting for her to say it. "Site Seven. Their primary research facility in western Massachusetts. We've known about it for six years. Never been able to get inside."

"They'd never let someone infiltrate," Elena said. "Security is—"

"They'd let me in."

Sarah's voice was steady. Calm. The decision crystallizing as she spoke even though her stomach was doing complicated things that involved nausea and terror and the coppery taste of panic that she was learning to identify as "the flavor of bad ideas that might be the only option."

"If I contacted Morrison. If I said I was reconsidering cooperation."

Martin's expression didn't change.

(That was worse than if he'd reacted. The stillness meant he'd thought of this. Done the math. Decided whether it was tactical suicide or regular suicide with extra steps.)

Elena's eyes widened. "You want to get captured."

"I want to get *inside*," Sarah corrected. "There's a difference."

"Is there?"

Sarah stood. Moved to the window beside Martin. Outside, early spring was fighting through Vermont's last snow. Dead grass showing through in patches. Trees that looked skeletal and cold. The kind of landscape that made you wonder if winter was ever over or just temporarily distracted.

"Phase I deployment is coming soon," Sarah continued. Her reflection in the window looked exhausted. Haunted. Twenty-eight years old but her eyes looked older. "Morrison said they're on an accelerated timeline. If we publish the exposé now, they'll just deploy early. We prevent nothing."

"But if you're inside Site Seven," Martin said slowly, "with access to their systems..."

"I gather real intelligence. Hub locations. Deployment schedules. Order infrastructure. Everything we need to stop Phase I, not just write a convincing article that gets buried under cat videos and conspiracy theories about lizard people."

Elena crossed the room. Fast. Angry. "Sarah, once you're in there, we can't extract you. Morrison isn't stupid. He'll know you're gathering intelligence."

"He'll suspect," Sarah agreed. "But he'll also think he can control me." She turned from the window. Met Elena's eyes. "Morrison believes humans are predictable. Pattern-able. Optimize the inputs, control the outputs. That's his weakness."

Martin was quiet for a long moment.

Then he opened his laptop. Pulled up surveillance photos of Site Seven—concrete building that looked like a corporate office park had a baby with a military bunker, surrounded by fencing that had armed guards and cameras and maybe landmines because why not go full supervillain.

"If you do this," Martin said, "we need to be smart about it. The facility has external monitoring. If you contact Morrison, he'll analyze the communication. Look for signs of resistance coordination."

"So I make it convincing," Sarah said. "I'm scared. Isolated. Marsh is dead. Aris is in hiding. Maya can't help without burning her CDC career. I'm alone. I'm reconsidering cooperation because I have no other options."

(She wasn't alone. That was the lie Morrison needed to believe.)

(The lie she needed him to believe badly enough that she'd walk into a building full of people who murdered retired professors and called it optimization.)

Elena's jaw tightened. Muscle jumping. "You're describing yourself as bait."

"I'm describing myself as tactical."

Sarah moved back to her laptop. Closed the exposé document. Opened a new file. Started typing notes—insertion strategy, intelligence priorities, extraction contingencies that sounded more confident than she felt.

"Marsh spent forty years fighting the Order. He died buying me time to escape." Her voice cracked. Slightly. "Yuki risked her life getting me Pattern Eye documentation. I'm not wasting those sacrifices by publishing an exposé that gets buried in two days while Morrison deploys Phase I on schedule and kills two hundred sixty-two million people."

(Entire US population. Gone.)

(She needed to stop thinking about it in those terms. Made it too real. Too visceral. Easier to think "262 million" than "every person in every city you've ever visited.")

"You'll need a tracking device," Martin said.

Planning now. Analyzing probabilities. The tactical part of his brain engaging while the human part—the part that wanted to tell her this was insane—got shoved into a box labeled "deal with later."

"Something Morrison won't find immediately. We need to know where you are. Be able to coordinate."

"And you'll need an exit strategy," Elena added. "Even if you gather intelligence, you're useless if you die in there."

Sarah thought about Marsh.

About how he'd spent four decades preparing for a moment that never came. About how he'd died in a burning house with documents that proved everything and Morrison had been *there*, watching, smiling—

Her stomach turned.

She swallowed hard. Tasted copper. Had she bitten her cheek again?

(Her body needed to stop doing that. Stress-biting was a terrible habit. She'd bitten through her entire cheek by the time this was over.)

"I'll have an exit strategy," she said. "Once I have the intelligence. Once I know we can stop Phase I."

Martin closed his laptop. "This is insane."

"This is tactical."

"Those aren't mutually exclusive."

"I know."

Sarah returned to her laptop. Opened a new message draft. "Elena, I need you to create a secure email account that looks like it was set up in panic. No sophisticated encryption. Just enough security to look like I'm trying to hide but don't know what I'm doing."

Elena nodded. Pulled out her phone. Fingers moving across the screen with practiced efficiency.

"Martin, I need you to build a tracking device. Something small. Something I can implant subcutaneously." Sarah's voice was steady even though her hands wanted to shake. "Transmits location but not audio—Morrison will sweep for audio surveillance."

"Subdural implant behind the ear," Martin said. No hesitation. Like he'd been planning this. "Two millimeter chip. GPS tracker. Broadcasts to encrypted satellite receiver. I can have it ready in six hours."

Sarah's phone buzzed.

Maya.

**"Where are you? Need to talk. Urgent."**

Sarah showed the text to Martin and Elena.

Martin nodded. "She helped you find the pattern. She should be part of this."

"Sending her location," Elena said.

---

Twenty minutes later, headlights cut through the trees.

Sarah watched from the window as a CDC vehicle pulled into the driveway. Government plates. Official enough to get through checkpoints. Unmarked enough to not draw attention.

Maya Chen stepped out carrying a medical kit.

She looked exhausted. Dark circles under her eyes. Hair pulled back in a ponytail that had seen better days. The kind of tired that came from too much coffee and not enough sleep and the weight of knowing millions of people were about to die and you were one of maybe five people who could stop it.

"You're planning something insane," Maya said as she entered. Not a question.

"How did you know?" Sarah asked.

"Because I've been monitoring GenVault's communications since you escaped." Maya set down her kit. Heavy. The kind of weight that meant serious medical equipment. "Morrison sent encrypted messages to six facility coordinators this morning. Subject line: 'Chen recruitment protocols.' He's expecting you to make contact."

Sarah exchanged glances with Martin and Elena.

"He's that confident?"

"He's that prepared." Maya pulled out her laptop. Opened files. "Which is why you're going to need more than Martin's tracker. Morrison's security will sweep for electronics. Standard GPS devices. Anything broadcasting on normal frequencies."

She opened her medical kit.

Inside: a subcutaneous tracker the size of a grain of rice.

Sarah's stomach did that dropping thing again.

(A grain of rice. She was going to have a grain of rice embedded under her skin. This was her life now. This was fine. Everything was fine.)

"CDC biocontainment protocol," Maya explained. "We use these to track field personnel in outbreak zones. Encrypted transmission piggybacking on cellular towers. Looks like environmental noise." She held up the tiny device. Smaller than a Tic Tac. "They won't find it unless they're scanning for this frequency."

"Two trackers," Martin said slowly. "Redundancy."

"One behind the ear," Maya confirmed. "One on the hip. Different frequencies, different transmission protocols. If Morrison finds one, you still have the other."

Sarah looked at the two of them.

Martin with his tactical gear and rifle and six years of fighting the Order from the shadows.

Maya with her CDC resources and medical equipment and the kind of determination that came from watching pandemic models and deciding to act instead of just documenting the collapse.

Elena coordinating resistance networks across three continents, keeping people alive who'd been marked for death decades ago.

"I'm not doing this alone," Sarah said quietly.

"You never were," Elena replied.

---

## The Implants

Six hours felt like six minutes and six years simultaneously.

Sarah sat at the kitchen table while Martin finished building his tracker. Tiny circuit board. GPS chip. Battery that would last seventy-two hours before needing recharge. He worked with steady hands and the focused silence of someone who'd done field medicine in places where mistakes got people killed.

Maya sterilized her medical equipment. Alcohol swabs. Scalpel. Local anesthetic. Dissolving sutures. The smell reminded Sarah of hospitals—antiseptic and fear-sweat and something underneath that her brain labeled "the smell of things about to hurt."

Elena stood watch at the window. Still pacing. Phone in hand. Checking updates from resistance contacts who were monitoring GenVault facilities across six continents.

"This is going to hurt," Martin warned.

He positioned the subdural implant behind Sarah's right ear. Showed her the device—two millimeters, flat disk, larger than a watch battery.

"Two millimeter incision. Local anesthetic first. Then I slide it under the skin. Takes ninety seconds."

Sarah nodded.

Her heart was doing things. Fast things. Complicated things.

(This was fine. People got piercings all the time. This was just a piercing except instead of jewelry it was a GPS tracker and instead of a mall kiosk it was a resistance fighter's kitchen and instead of looking cool it was going to get her killed.)

Martin injected the numbing agent.

Cold spread through her scalp. Weird sensation—like her head was there but also not there. Present but distant. The kind of numbness that made her hyper-aware of every sensation around it.

Then pressure.

Sharp. Quick. Gone.

"Incision done. Implanting now."

More pressure. Deeper. Sarah's fingers gripped the edge of the table. Not pain—the anesthetic was working—but the *knowledge* of what was happening. Foreign object sliding under skin. Body that wanted to reject it but couldn't because biology didn't work fast enough.

"Done," Martin said. "Give it five minutes to initialize, then it starts broadcasting."

Sarah touched the spot behind her ear. Felt the tiny ridge where the tracker sat beneath skin. Invisible if you weren't looking. Obvious if you knew it was there.

Her body's early warning system saying *foreign object foreign object something wrong*—

(She ignored it. Her body had been saying that for forty-eight hours straight. At this point body opinions were background noise.)

Maya was next.

"Hip tracker goes subcutaneous on your left side. Lower quadrant. They won't find it during standard searches unless they're looking for implants."

Maya's hands were steady. Professional. The hands of someone who'd done field medicine in outbreak zones where steady hands meant the difference between containing infection and watching it spread.

She swabbed Sarah's hip with alcohol. Cold. Sharp smell.

"Deep breath."

The injection stung.

Sharp pain that radiated outward—not the surgical pain of Martin's implant but the biological pain of something small and foreign being pushed through tissue that didn't want it there.

Sarah's breath hissed through her teeth.

"Almost done," Maya said. Steady pressure. "There."

The pain faded to dull ache. Throbbing. Present.

"Both active," Maya confirmed, checking her laptop. "I'm reading both signals. Clean transmission. No interference."

"Same here," Martin said from his encrypted receiver. "Signal strength good. GPS lock acquired."

Sarah touched both spots. Behind her ear. On her hip.

Two trackers. Two teams monitoring her location. Multiple redundancies.

Tactical.

(And insane.)

(But mostly tactical.)

Her phone buzzed. She checked it on autopilot.

Maya's laptop showed a map. Two glowing dots representing Sarah's location. Real-time tracking. Latitude and longitude updating every few seconds.

Proof that this was happening.

That she was about to walk into GenVault headquarters and let Morrison believe he'd recruited her.

"Now we need to decide how you make contact," Elena said. "Morrison is expecting you to reach out. What's your approach?"

Sarah thought about the email she'd been composing in her head. About Morrison's recruitment protocols. About walking into the trap with eyes open and trackers broadcasting and a team ready to extract her or avenge her depending on how badly this went.

"I don't want a meeting," Sarah said slowly. "Meetings give him time to set up elaborate scenarios. Control the environment. Play psychological games where he shows me collapse models and Emily's coffee cup and makes me feel guilty for not helping him murder a quarter billion people."

"Then what?" Martin asked.

"I want to walk straight into GenVault headquarters in Boston." Sarah's voice was steady even though her stomach was staging a protest about this entire plan. "Call their recruitment contact—Dr. Preston Jenson—and tell him I'm ready to see the complete picture. No games. No negotiation. I come in voluntarily, they give me full access to verify everything independently."

"They'll imprison you," Elena warned.

"Maybe," Sarah said. "Or maybe Morrison sees it as proof I'm reconsidering. Someone walking in voluntarily, demanding transparency—that plays into his recruitment fantasy." She pulled up Jenson's contact info. "He thinks he's been cultivating me. Waiting for me to discover the truth myself and choose to join because I understand the mathematics."

Maya nodded slowly. "Jenson recruited you originally. If you call him, tell him you want to verify everything independently before making a decision—that's the psychology Morrison wants. Someone *choosing* to join after seeing evidence, not being coerced."

"And if they just lock you up?" Martin asked.

Sarah touched both trackers.

Behind her ear. On her hip.

Two signals broadcasting to two teams who'd burn GenVault to the ground if Morrison tried to disappear her.

"Then you know where I am," she said. "And I start gathering intelligence from inside."

The room was quiet for a long moment.

Wind outside. Trees creaking. The silence of people standing at the edge of a decision that couldn't be unmade.

Then Martin stood. "When do you go?"

"Tomorrow," Sarah said. "I call Jenson first thing. Tell him I'm in Boston, I want to come in, I need to see the complete archive before I decide anything."

"I'll monitor both trackers," Maya said. Professional voice. Clinical. The voice that kept panic at bay. "If you disappear—if signals go dark—I activate CDC bioterrorism protocols. Full facility raid."

"I'll coordinate with resistance networks," Elena added. "If you send a distress signal, we'll be ready."

Sarah looked at the three of them.

Her team. Her backup. The people who'd make sure her sacrifice—if it came to that—wouldn't be wasted.

The people who'd turned "this is insane" into "this might work."

"Okay," she said. "Tomorrow morning, I walk into GenVault. Voluntarily. And I start gathering the intelligence we need to stop Phase I."

"Not a trap," Martin said quietly. "Tactical infiltration."

"Right," Sarah agreed.

(Definitely both.)

(Definitely mostly trap.)

(She was going to die doing this, wasn't she?)

(But at least she'd die trying instead of hiding while Morrison killed 262 million people and called it optimization.)

## The Call

The next morning, Sarah stood outside GenVault's Boston headquarters.

Glass and steel tower. Forty-three stories. Executive entrance on Causeway Street that cost more than her entire graduate education. Security visible through the windows—guards with earpieces and suits that didn't quite hide the weapons underneath.

Sarah's hand trembled as she pulled out her phone.

(Her hands needed to stop doing that. Professional competence was harder when your body was advertising terror.)

She dialed Jenson's number.

The phone rang.

Once.

Twice.

Three times.

Sarah counted her heartbeats. Eighty-two beats per minute. Too fast. Calm people had heartbeats in the sixties. She was in the "about to have a panic attack" range.

(This was fine. Everything was fine.)

"Jenson."

His voice. Warm. Professional. The voice that had recruited her two years ago with promises of meaningful research and making a difference and no mention of genocidal optimization protocols.

"Dr. Jenson, it's Sarah Chen."

A pause.

Long enough for Sarah's heart to do several complicated things.

Then warmth flooded his voice. Too much warmth. The warmth of someone who'd been *expecting* this call. "Dr. Chen. I'm so glad you reached out. Are you somewhere safe?"

"I'm downstairs," Sarah said. "In your lobby. I want to see the complete archive. The original manuscripts. The genetic data. Everything Morrison showed you when he recruited you. I need to verify this independently before I make any decisions."

Silence on the line.

Sarah counted it. Three seconds. Four. Five.

Her brain was doing this thing where it tried to interpret the silence. *Surprised. Analyzing. Calculating whether this is genuine or a trap.*

(It was a trap. Just not the trap he thought it was.)

Then: "You're here? Right now?"

"Executive entrance. I'll wait five minutes." Sarah made her voice steady. Calm. The voice of someone who'd thought this through and wasn't panicking. "If someone doesn't come down to escort me up, I walk away and you never hear from me again."

She ended the call.

Stared at her phone.

Her reflection stared back from the glass tower. Twenty-eight years old. Exhausted. Terrified. About to walk into a building full of people who thought genocide was mathematics.

Sarah touched the tracker behind her ear. Tapped twice—signal for Martin and Maya that she was moving. That this was happening. That if she disappeared they should assume the worst and activate every contingency.

Three states away and one city over, two encrypted receivers registered her signal.

Maya Chen in a CDC office, watching a glowing dot on her screen.

Martin in a Vermont safe house, rifle within reach, ready to coordinate extraction or revenge.

The lobby doors opened.

A security guard approached. Young. Professional. The kind of person who thought they worked for a legitimate pharmaceutical company and had no idea their job involved protecting genocidal architects.

"Dr. Chen? Dr. Morrison is waiting for you. Twelfth floor."

Sarah's stomach dropped.

(Twelfth floor. Morrison. This was it.)

She walked through the doors.

Glass lobby. Marble floors. The kind of corporate elegance that screamed "we have money and power and nothing to hide."

(Except the ancient conspiracy to engineer human evolution and the plan to kill 262 million people and the three-thousand-year-old cipher system that made eugenics look like amateur hour.)

The elevator was waiting.

Empty.

Just Sarah and a security guard and mirrors on three walls that showed her reflection from every angle—exhausted, terrified, walking into the trap with eyes open because it was the only way to get the intelligence they needed.

The guard pressed the button for the twelfth floor.

Doors closed.

Sarah watched the numbers climb.

Three. Four. Five.

Her heart was doing things. Complicated things. Variations on "this is a terrible idea" and "you're going to die" and "at least Maya will avenge you."

Six. Seven. Eight.

The tracker behind her ear felt heavy. Foreign. A reminder that she wasn't alone even though she was about to be alone in a building full of people who thought she was choosing to join them.

Nine. Ten. Eleven.

Sarah took a deep breath.

In. Out. Oxygen was useful.

Her body should keep doing that.

Twelve.

The elevator dinged.

Doors opened.

Dr. James Morrison stood in the hallway. Expensive suit. Patient smile. The smile of someone who'd been expecting her and had planned how this would go.

"Dr. Chen," he said. "Welcome to GenVault. I'm so glad you decided to see the complete picture."

Sarah stepped out of the elevator.

Into the Order that had engineered humanity for three thousand years.

Into the trap with her eyes wide open and two teams tracking her every move.

*I'm coming*, she thought. *And I'm going to burn you from the inside.*

Morrison's smile widened.

"Let me show you why we do what we do," he said.

The elevator doors closed behind her.

No going back now.

Sarah Chen was going to finish what Marsh had started.

Or die trying.

(Die trying.)

(But at least she'd die gathering intelligence instead of hiding in Vermont writing exposés that would get buried under conspiracy theories and cat videos.)

"Lead the way," she said.

Morrison gestured down the hallway.

Sarah followed.

And somewhere in Vermont, Martin watched her signal move deeper into the building.

And somewhere in a CDC office, Maya prepared bioterrorism protocols just in case.

And somewhere in her chest, Sarah's heart counted beats that might be numbered.

But she was *inside*.

And that was the first step.
