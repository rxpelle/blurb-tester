# PROLOGUE: THE ARCHITECT

**GenVault Security Command Center - Site Seven**
**Sublevel 4, Northern Virginia**
**Friday, November 14, 2019 - 11:47 PM**

---

Seventy-two days until Dr. James Morrison saved two billion people by killing four billion others.

He stood three stories underground watching seventeen monitors glow in the dark like the world's worst aquarium—except instead of fish you got climate models showing exactly when humanity started dying and resource curves that looked like someone's EKG flatlining and political fragmentation indexes that basically said *we're fucked* in seventeen different mathematical languages.

Morrison sipped his coffee. It had gone cold three hours ago and tasted like pennies and regret.

His reflection stared back from the monitors—fifty-four years old, suit that looked like he'd slept in it (he had), eyes that had stopped being surprised by terrible news sometime around 2007. Thirty years preparing for this moment and his coffee was still shit.

Sometimes at 3 AM he wondered if there was another way.

(There wasn't. He'd checked. God knew he'd checked.)

Then he looked at the models again and the doubt passed like it always did because mathematics didn't care about your moral comfort. The collapse curves were certain. Seven point two billion dead over thirty years if they did nothing. Civilization gone. Everything gone. Just slow screaming chaos until there was nothing left to collapse.

The central display ticked forward:

**PHASE I (TEST DEPLOYMENT):**
**T-minus 72 days, 11 hours, 13 minutes**

Two hundred sixty-two million dead in Phase I. Another three point seven billion in Phase II. Six airports. Sixteen days to global spread. Mortality rate four point three percent which sounded almost reasonable until you remembered four point three percent of eight billion was a lot of corpses.

Not evil.

Not genocide.

Engineering.

(He'd been telling himself that for fifteen years and it still sounded like horseshit.)

Morrison's tablet chimed—that particular chime that meant someone had done something stupid and now it was his problem.

Security alert. Level Three.

He pulled it up and felt ice water in his veins which was a cliché but there it was, cold spreading through his chest like he'd been dunked in a frozen lake:

**ALERT: UNAUTHORIZED DATABASE ACCESS**
**User:** Dr. Sarah Chen (GenVault Research Division, MIT-recruited 2017)
**Access Time:** 11:31 PM
**Files Accessed:** Population Genetics Archive (RESTRICTED)
**Search Parameters:** "CCR5-Delta32 pre-1348 distribution" / "Black Death survivor correlation" / "Genesis Protocol cipher marks"
**Security Classification:** LEVEL 3 BREACH
**Recommendation:** IMMEDIATE CONTAINMENT

Morrison read it twice.

Sarah Chen.

He knew the name. Brilliant pattern recognition specialist, twenty-eight years old, recruited from MIT two years ago with three published papers that had made the Order's targeting algorithm sit up and bark. They'd hired her for disease resistance mapping—legitimate science that gave them cover while the Order used her data to figure out who lived and who died when the killing started.

She wasn't supposed to access the restricted archives.

She definitely wasn't supposed to search for Genesis Protocol cipher marks.

(Well. Hell.)

Morrison pulled up her personnel file. PhD at twenty-four which meant she was smarter than him and that was a problem. Clearance level Yellow—legitimate researcher, completely unaware the Order existed, probably thought she was doing actual science instead of helping them build a better genocide. Psychological profile: idealistic (useless), brilliant (dangerous), tendency toward pattern obsession (oh that's just perfect).

Dangerous combination.

Her search history showed six months of escalating curiosity. Started with legitimate disease resistance research. Then noticing things. Statistical impossibilities. Genetic markers appearing in populations centuries before selective pressure should have created them. Pre-dating the Black Death. Pre-dating everything.

She'd found the pattern.

And tonight she'd confirmed it by accessing files only twelve people in the entire organization were supposed to read.

Morrison ran her genetic profile through the targeting algorithm, already knowing what it would say.

Green flag. CCR5-Delta32 variant. Cooperative behavior markers. Crisis-response predisposition.

She'd survive Phase I.

Emily wouldn't have.

(And there it was. There was always Emily.)

His daughter had died at seven—pediatric leukemia, before THRESHOLD existed, before Morrison could engineer who survived and who didn't. Twenty-seven years ago. The event that had turned him from researcher who wanted to save the world into architect who'd decided saving some of the world meant killing most of it.

If the defensive Protocol had worked back then—if crisis-response genetics had been optimized across populations earlier—maybe Emily's immune system would have fought harder.

Maybe she'd still be alive.

Maybe he wouldn't be standing in a bunker planning to kill four billion people to prove her death meant something.

That's what he told himself. That THRESHOLD would save other people's daughters. That two hundred sixty-two million deaths prevented seven point two billion. That Emily's random, meaningless, stupid death from faulty cells could mean something if it drove him to save humanity from its own faulty cells.

(And if you believed that, Morrison had a bridge to sell you.)

But Sarah Chen would survive. She'd live through Phase I with barely a sniffle. And she'd hate him for every single corpse it took to get there.

His phone buzzed. Encrypted call. Catherine Wells.

He answered. "I'm looking at it."

Wells's voice was cold and clinical and sounded like a scalpel cutting through bullshit. She ran the Order from Geneva, making policy decisions Morrison turned into corpses. "Dr. Chen triggered every alarm we have. She accessed the Bronze Age translation archives. Downloaded Genesis Protocol documentation. If she understands what she's looking at—"

"She understands." Morrison pulled up Sarah's search queries. "Pattern recognition scores off the charts. That's why we recruited her. She's connecting Bronze Age cipher marks to modern genetic data. She knows it's not coincidence."

"Then we have a problem."

"We have an opportunity."

Silence on the line.

Then Wells: "Explain."

Morrison pulled up Sarah's genetic profile again like looking at it would make the math different. "Dr. Chen carries defensive Protocol markers. Twenty-eight generations from Maria of Toledo, one of Nefertari's original bloodline carriers. She's exactly the kind of person the defensive Protocol was designed to create—brilliant pattern recognition, systems thinking, crisis response capability."

"Which makes her the exact person we can't allow to interfere."

"Or the exact person we recruit." Morrison studied Sarah's psychological profile. "She's not a zealot. She's a scientist. Show her the mathematics. The collapse models. The certainty of extinction if we do nothing versus managed survival if we act. She might understand."

Wells laughed and it sounded like broken glass. "You want to recruit the woman who just stole our operational files?"

"She hasn't stolen anything yet. She accessed restricted databases from inside our facility. She's still sitting at her workstation in Cambridge right now." Morrison pulled up the surveillance feed—there she was, downloading files to a flash drive, hands shaking so hard she kept missing the USB port. "If we move fast, we contain this before it becomes a problem."

"Or we eliminate her. Permanently."

Morrison watched Sarah on the security camera. Terrified. Checking over her shoulder every three seconds. Downloading files like her life depended on it (it did). Not a spy. Not trained. Just a brilliant researcher who'd found something that shouldn't exist and couldn't ignore it because that's what brilliant researchers did—they poked things until they understood them even when understanding would get them killed.

"Dr. Chen isn't the primary target," Morrison said. "She's been communicating with Dr. Otto Aris."

Wells's breath caught and Morrison knew he had her. "Aris. You're certain?"

"Seventeen encrypted emails over three months. Our systems cracked them. Aris has been feeding her information. Confirming her suspicions. He gave her the access codes for the restricted archives."

"That old fool." Wells's voice went sharp. "We should have eliminated him twenty years ago when he left the translation project."

"He's seventy-three. Terminal cancer. Six months to live at most. Killing him draws attention." Morrison pulled up Aris's medical records. "But there's someone more dangerous. Dr. Evelyn Marsh."

"I thought Marsh was retired."

"She is. But she's the only surviving member of the original 1970s translation team. She has the source documents—actual Bronze Age manuscripts with complete cipher keys. If Chen reaches Marsh and gets those documents, she'll have proof. Not just data anomalies. Actual historical evidence that the Order has been engineering human survival for three thousand years."

Wells was quiet for a long moment and Morrison could practically hear her doing the math.

Then: "Where is Marsh?"

"New Haven, Connecticut. Passive surveillance for thirty years. Hasn't been active since the translation dispute in 1982." Morrison pulled up Marsh's address—small house, retired professor, probably kept the source documents in her study like academic trophies. "But Aris gave Chen her contact information. Phone records show Chen called Marsh's number eighteen minutes ago."

"Did Marsh answer?"

"Yes. Conversation lasted forty-seven seconds. Chen mentioned Genesis Protocol. Marsh told her to come to the address. Back door. Don't call again."

Wells's voice went cold. "Then we have twelve hours at most before Chen reaches Marsh, gets the source documents, and has everything she needs to expose us."

"Agreed."

"Morrison." Wells paused and Morrison knew what was coming before she said it. "I'm authorizing a cleanup operation. Marsh first. Then Chen. Then Aris. Simultaneous. Tonight. No survivors. Make it look like accidents."

Morrison looked at the surveillance feed. Sarah Chen shutting down her computer. Grabbing her coat. Flash drive clutched in trembling hands. Twenty-eight years old. Brilliant. Terrified. About to run straight into a burning building or maybe into their arms, depending on how the next twelve hours went.

He made his decision.

"I'll handle Marsh personally," Morrison said. "Natural gas leak. House fire. Tragic accident. Source documents burn with her."

"And Chen?"

"Let her run." Morrison smiled and it felt like something cracking. "Let her find Marsh's burning house. Let her realize the Order is real, is everywhere, is not bound by normal rules like thou shalt not murder retired professors in their sleep. Then I'll offer her a choice: join us and help refine THRESHOLD to minimize casualties, or watch three point seven billion people die knowing she could have helped make it more humane."

"That's a gamble."

"She carries defensive Protocol markers. Pattern recognition. Strategic thinking. We could use someone like her—someone brilliant enough to understand the mathematics and ethical enough to care about reducing harm." Morrison closed the surveillance feed. "Best case: we recruit her. She helps optimize THRESHOLD, reduces projected casualties significantly. Maybe we only kill two hundred million instead of two hundred sixty-two."

(Sixty-two million lives. That was what passed for optimism in his line of work.)

"And worst case?"

"She refuses. Tries to expose us. We eliminate her like we would have anyway. We lose nothing."

Wells considered. "You have twelve hours. Marsh dies tonight. If Chen can be turned, turn her. If not..." She paused and Morrison could hear her lighting a cigarette three thousand miles away. "Morrison, we're seventy-two days from Phase I deployment. The window for interference is closing. I don't care if Chen carries every defensive marker ever encoded—if she threatens THRESHOLD, she dies. Understood?"

"Understood."

"Then proceed."

The line went dead.

Morrison set down his phone. Pulled up deployment protocols. Double-checked the timeline like checking would make it different.

**T-minus 72 days, 10 hours, 47 minutes**

Seventy-two days until the greatest act of mercy in human history.

Or the greatest act of murder, depending on whether you believed there was a difference.

Morrison had stopped caring about the distinction somewhere around year fifteen when he'd realized the mathematics didn't care about his moral comfort or his daughter's corpse or the fact that he woke up at 3 AM wondering if there was another way. The collapse was coming. Climate crisis. Resource depletion. Political fragmentation. Migration pressure. War. Famine. Pandemic. All the horsemen showing up at once because humanity had decided to cram eight billion people onto a planet that could sustainably support two.

The Bronze Age Collapse had taken fifty years and killed ninety percent of Mediterranean civilization.

The modern collapse would take thirty years and kill ninety percent of global civilization.

Unless someone had the courage to do the math.

Unless someone was willing to engineer survival instead of allowing chaotic extinction.

(Unless someone was willing to play God and then spend the rest of their life wondering if God felt this empty.)

Morrison pulled up his private files—the ones he showed recruits when they needed to understand why a good man would do terrible things. The scenarios loaded: uncontrolled collapse versus managed intervention.

Scenario A: Do nothing. Seven point two billion dead over thirty years. Civilization collapses. Survivors regress to pre-industrial conditions. No infrastructure. No medicine. No hope. Slow screaming darkness until there was nothing left that remembered what humanity used to be.

Scenario B: THRESHOLD deployment. Four billion dead over ten years. Civilization intact. Survivors selected for crisis-response genetics, cooperative behavior, adaptive capability. Humanity survives. Diminished. Traumatized. But alive.

That was mercy. Fewer dead. Faster. With something left worth saving.

Morrison closed the files. Checked his watch. 11:52 PM.

Marsh would be asleep by now. Old woman. Alone in a house full of books and dead languages and source documents proving the Genesis Protocol existed, proving the Order had been engineering human survival for three thousand years, proving Morrison wasn't crazy—he was just carrying on a tradition that started with Nefertari and never stopped.

One house fire. One retired scholar. One set of irreplaceable documents.

All gone by morning.

And Dr. Sarah Chen would arrive tomorrow to find ash and questions and a very patient man offering her a choice: join us or die trying to stop us.

Morrison stood. Grabbed his coat. Checked his sidearm—standard procedure for field operations, though he wouldn't need it for an old woman who probably couldn't run if the house was burning around her.

(She wouldn't be able to run. He'd make sure of that.)

On the monitors behind him, the world continued its slow march toward extinction.

Climate models trending worse than projected.

Resource curves accelerating.

Political fragmentation increasing.

Right on schedule.

Morrison walked to the elevator. Pressed the button for ground level. The doors closed on the command center—seventeen monitors showing humanity's approaching doom and one man willing to do terrible things to prevent something worse.

(Or one man telling himself he was preventing something worse because the alternative was admitting he was just a murderer with a spreadsheet.)

His phone chimed. Text from the tactical team:

**MARSH LOCATION CONFIRMED. READY TO PROCEED.**

Morrison typed back:

**AUTHORIZED. MAKE IT CLEAN. NO WITNESSES.**

He pocketed the phone.

Stepped out into cold November air that smelled like winter coming.

And drove toward New Haven to burn an old woman's house and begin the recruitment of Dr. Sarah Chen—the brilliant pattern recognition specialist who'd just made the worst mistake of her life.

Finding the truth.

**END OF PROLOGUE**
