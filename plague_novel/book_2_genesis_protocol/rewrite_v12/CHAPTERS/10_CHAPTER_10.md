# CHAPTER 10 - THE FINAL HOURS

**Modern Timeline - GenVault Sublevel 3 Archive**
**November 20-21, 2019 - 11:47 PM → 7:52 AM**
**11 days until THRESHOLD deployment**
**8 minutes until fire alarm testing**

---

Sarah couldn't sleep.

Midnight. The archive empty except for her and nine notebooks containing genocide. Eight hours until the fire alarm. Eight hours until she ran or got detained trying.

(Five percent probability. Lagos said five percent. Down from ten percent after Wells positioned seventeen guards. Five percent she made it to the medical bay. Five percent she reached parking garage B2. Five percent Martin's extraction team got her out before Wells locked down the building.)

(Ninety-five percent she got caught.)

She reviewed Hub 1 again. Atlanta. For the seventh time tonight. Memorizing storage location (CDC Sublevel 4, Biological Containment Wing C). Security protocols (biometric + keycard + armed response). Deployment vectors (calibrated for Southeastern US population density). Casualties (47.3 million).

(She knew it already. Had memorized all forty-seven hubs three days ago. But reviewing helped. Kept her brain occupied. Kept her from thinking about tomorrow morning when she'd run and either escape with complete intelligence or get detained and prove Morrison right—that defensive Protocol failed when tested against optimization.)

The door opened.

Sarah looked up.

Lagos stood in the doorway. Carrying two cups of coffee.

(GenVault coffee. Still tasted like shit. Sarah had developed opinions about it over four days in the archive—it was consistent, at least. Consistently terrible.)

"Thought you might be awake," Lagos said. He set one cup on her desk. Pulled up a chair. "Big day tomorrow."

"Can't sleep."

"Me neither." Lagos sipped his coffee. Made a face. "I've worked here eight years and this coffee never gets better."

"It's the consistency I appreciate." Sarah drank hers. Cold already. She'd been so focused on memorizing Atlanta that she hadn't noticed Lagos arrive. "Consistently awful is better than unpredictably awful."

"That's dark philosophy for midnight."

"It's been a dark week."

They sat in silence. Sarah reviewing Hub 1. Lagos drinking terrible coffee. The archive quiet except for the ventilation system and the hum of servers storing THRESHOLD deployment data.

"Morrison called me into his office this morning," Lagos said finally.

Sarah stopped typing. "What did he want?"

"To offer me a deal." Lagos set down his coffee. "Transfer to Geneva facility. Non-operational site. Full pension. Relocation assistance. Leave tomorrow morning at 6 AM—two hours before the fire alarm."

"Immunity."

"Survival." Lagos smiled. Sad. Broken. "He offered me the choice I didn't give Ava. Sign the transfer, leave, live. You run tomorrow whether I'm here or not. My presence changes nothing about your odds."

"But you didn't sign it."

"No."

"Why not?"

Lagos was quiet for a long moment.

Then: "Because Ava stayed. She found evidence THRESHOLD was worse than Morrison claimed. Found proof the pathogen wouldn't kill selectively—that Morrison's 'precision genocide' would cascade beyond the targeted populations. She tried to warn me. Tried to get me to help expose it. I told her to stay quiet. Told her the Order would kill her if she pushed too hard."

He pulled out his phone. Showed Sarah a photo.

Ava Rodriguez. Twenty-six years old. Dark hair. Bright smile. Standing in a genetics lab wearing a lab coat, holding a DNA sequencing result like it was a trophy.

"Three days after I told her to stay quiet, she died. Brake failure. Car accident. The Order eliminated her and I was complicit because I chose comfort over courage. Because I told her to stop looking when she should have looked harder."

Lagos closed the photo.

"Morrison offered me the same choice this morning. Optimize for survival or choose principle. I'm not making his choice. I'm making Ava's choice. The one I didn't give her. Staying when leaving would be easier. Fighting when silence would be safer."

Sarah looked at him.

At this man who'd spent two years sabotaging THRESHOLD. Who'd given her the thumb drive. Who'd helped her document all forty-seven hubs. Who was refusing survival to prove that cooperation didn't end when self-preservation became an option.

"You're going to die tomorrow," Sarah said quietly.

"Probably." Lagos sipped his coffee. "Wells has seventeen guards positioned. Kill authorization if you resist. When I help you escape—when I trigger the fire alarm or disable the locks or distract Morrison—Wells will identify me as your coordinator. I'll get detained for treason. Executed."

"Then don't help me. Transfer to Geneva. Live. You've already given me everything I need."

"That's what Morrison said."

"Morrison's right."

"Morrison's offering me absolution for choosing comfort when Ava needed courage." Lagos met her eyes. "I can't accept it. Can't sign a transfer and leave while you run alone. Can't optimize for survival when cooperation requires staying."

He stood. Walked to the whiteboard where Sarah had been mapping hub locations. Picked up a marker. Drew a diagram.

"Defensive Protocol isn't individual action," Lagos said. "It's cooperative networks. Multiple people making small choices that cascade into crisis prevention. You gathering intelligence. Me helping you document hubs. Martin coordinating extraction. Maya alerting authorities. Each person contributing what they can."

He drew another branch.

"Morrison thinks I'm data—proof that defensive Protocol scales through cooperation. He's right. But cooperation doesn't mean staying until it's convenient. It means staying until it costs something. Until self-preservation says run and you stay anyway because the network requires it."

Lagos turned to face her.

"I'm staying because defensive Protocol requires irrational sacrifice. Because if I leave—if I take Morrison's transfer and optimize for survival—I prove him right. That cooperation ends when the cost gets personal. That people only help each other until it threatens their own life."

"And if you stay, you prove what?"

"That some people choose cooperation over self-preservation even when the mathematics say it's stupid." Lagos smiled. "Five percent chance you escape. Zero percent chance I survive helping you. One hundred percent certainty that staying proves defensive Protocol works through people who don't calculate odds before helping."

Sarah was quiet.

Thought about Lagos refusing Morrison's transfer. Thought about him staying when leaving would save him. Thought about five percent probability and zero percent survival and the mathematics that said cooperation ended when self-preservation became an option except Lagos was proving it didn't.

"Thank you," she said quietly.

"For what?"

"For staying. For helping. For making Ava's choice instead of Morrison's."

Lagos erased the diagram from the whiteboard. "Get some sleep, Sarah. Eight hours until the fire alarm. You need rest."

"I can't sleep."

"Neither can I. But we should try anyway." He walked to the door. Stopped. Looked back. "Tomorrow morning at 8 AM. Fire alarm testing. Forty-three seconds. Medical bay exit to parking garage B2. Don't stop running. Don't look back. Martin will be waiting."

"And you?"

"I'll be in Morrison's office. Keeping him distracted. Making sure he doesn't override the escape window." Lagos opened the door. "Five percent, Sarah. Not great odds. But enough to choose when the alternative is four billion deaths."

He left.

The door locked behind him.

Sarah sat alone in the archive.

Midnight. Eight hours until the escape. Eight hours until she ran or got detained or got killed trying to reach parking garage B2.

(Five percent.)

(Ninety-five percent failure.)

(One hundred percent certainty that trying was better than not trying.)

She pulled out her phone. Typed a message to Martin's encrypted number:

**8 AM fire alarm. 43 seconds. Medical bay to B2. Be ready. If I don't make it, tell Maya the intelligence died with me.**

Martin's response came three minutes later:

**We'll be there. 3 miles out. Extraction coordinates locked. Don't stop running.**

Sarah deleted the messages.

Looked at the nine notebooks on her desk. At forty-seven hubs documented in handwriting that got progressively worse as exhaustion accumulated.

(All that intelligence. All those hub locations. All that casualty data. Only mattered if she got out with it.)

(If she got caught, THRESHOLD deployed on schedule and 4.2 billion people died and Morrison knew his thirty years of mathematics had been necessary because defensive Protocol failed when tested against optimization.)

Sarah closed the notebooks.

Turned off the lights.

Lay down on the small couch in the corner of the archive.

(Couldn't sleep. Brain too active. Tomorrow too close. Five percent probability echoing in her head like a countdown: five percent, five percent, you're probably going to die tomorrow but you're trying anyway because cooperative behavior prevents collapse better than optimized genocide.)

She closed her eyes.

Tried to sleep.

Couldn't.

---

**6:47 AM - November 21, 2019**

Sarah woke to someone shaking her shoulder.

Lagos stood over her. Exhausted. Wired. The expression of someone who'd spent the night not sleeping and was running on caffeine and adrenaline.

"One hour," he said. "Fire alarm testing at 8 AM. Wells positioned guards at every exit. Morrison's in his office watching surveillance. Martin confirmed extraction team is ready."

Sarah sat up. Her neck hurt from sleeping on the couch. Her eyes burned from four days of archive work. Her hands still cramped from documenting forty-seven hubs.

"How many guards?" she asked.

"Seventeen. Wells has kill authorization. If you resist detention, they shoot." Lagos pulled up security footage on his tablet. Showed her. Guards at the ground-level exits. Guards in the stairwells. Guards in the medical bay corridor leading to parking garage B2.

"I can't get past seventeen guards."

"You don't have to get past all seventeen. Just the three between sublevel 3 and medical bay." Lagos zoomed the footage. "During fire alarm testing, electronic locks disengage for forty-three seconds. Emergency protocols. That's your window. You run from here to ground level. Hit the medical bay exit before locks reengage. Reach parking garage B2 before Wells realizes you're running."

"Three guards."

"Three guards in forty-three seconds. If you're fast."

Sarah looked at the route on his tablet. Sublevel 3 archive to ground-level stairs. Stairs to medical bay corridor. Medical bay to parking garage exit. Approximately eight hundred feet total.

Forty-three seconds.

Three guards.

Five percent probability.

"What happens to you when I run?" Sarah asked.

"Morrison knows I helped you. Wells will detain me for treason. I get executed." Lagos's voice was flat. Dead. Matter-of-fact. "But Morrison will be distracted. I'll keep him in his office during the fire alarm. Buy you extra seconds before he realizes you're running."

"Lagos—"

"Don't." He set down the tablet. "Don't tell me not to do this. Don't tell me to save myself. I refused Morrison's transfer for a reason. I'm staying because cooperation requires it. Because Ava stayed and I should have helped her instead of telling her to stay quiet."

He pulled out a thumb drive. Different from the first one. Handed it to Sarah.

"This has the access codes for the medical bay exit. Biometric bypass. Security override. Everything you need to reach parking garage B2 without triggering alarms."

Sarah took the drive.

Small. Black. Containing the codes that either got her out or got her killed depending on whether she was fast enough.

"One hour," Lagos said. "8 AM. Fire alarm starts. Locks disengage. You run. Don't stop. Don't look back. Martin will be waiting in parking garage B2 with extraction coordinates."

He walked to the door.

Stopped.

Looked back one more time.

"Sarah, listen to me: You're going to hear screaming. Guards yelling. Morrison on the intercom demanding you stop. Wells ordering lethal force. You ignore all of it. You run. You don't stop until you're in Martin's car and three miles away from GenVault."

"What if I don't make it?"

"Then you die trying. And Morrison learns that defensive Protocol doesn't fail—people just choose courage even when the mathematics say it's stupid." Lagos smiled. Sad. True. "Five percent, Sarah. Not zero. That means it's possible. Run like hell and make it real."

He left.

Sarah sat on the couch.

Looked at the thumb drive in her hand.

At the nine notebooks on her desk.

At the surveillance camera in the corner where Morrison was watching.

(One hour until the fire alarm.)

(Forty-three seconds to escape.)

(Three guards between freedom and detention.)

(Five percent probability she made it out.)

She stood. Walked to her desk. Picked up the first notebook. Started reviewing Hub 1 one more time.

(Atlanta. CDC Sublevel 4. 47.3 million casualties. She knew it already. But reviewing helped. Kept her focused. Kept her from thinking about the seventeen guards and the kill authorization and the five percent probability that felt more like hope than mathematics.)

Her phone buzzed.

Martin: **Team positioned. Garage B2. Ready for extraction. 8 AM. Don't be late.**

Sarah deleted the message.

Checked the time.

7:52 AM.

Eight minutes until the fire alarm.

Eight minutes until she ran or died trying.

(Five percent.)

She closed the notebook.

Stood.

Walked to the door.

Waited.
