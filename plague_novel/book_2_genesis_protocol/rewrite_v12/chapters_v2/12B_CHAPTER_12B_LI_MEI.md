# CHAPTER 12B: THE PATIENT

**Date:** November 19-24, 2019
**Location:** Wuhan, China - Huanan Seafood Market / Wuhan Central Hospital
**POV:** Li Mei (Age 34, Market Vendor)

---

## NOVEMBER 19, 2019 - TUESDAY MORNING

Li Mei arrived at Huanan Seafood Market at 4:47 AM like she had every Tuesday for eight years.

Forty-seven minutes early. Time to arrange the produce displays before the morning rush. Time to make sure the bok choy looked fresh and the winter melons were stacked pyramid-style because Qiang said pyramid displays sold thirty percent better and Qiang was always right about numbers.

(Qiang was an engineer. Engineers knew about angles and pyramids and thirty-percent improvements.)

She unlocked her stall—Section C, Row 12, Vendor Position 47. Lucky number. Her mother had told her forty-seven was lucky because it added to eleven and eleven meant balance. Li Mei didn't believe in luck but she believed in her mother so she'd paid extra for Position 47 eight years ago when Jian was still nursing and money was tighter than winter skin.

Now Jian was seven. Xiao was four. Money was still tight but less so.

The market was quiet. Dawn vendors setting up. The fish smell from Section A mixing with chicken smell from Section B and that chemical cleaner smell the maintenance crew used on the concrete floors every Monday night.

(Except this Monday they hadn't used it. The floors still had Saturday's grime tracked across them.)

Li Mei started unloading crates. Twenty kilos of bok choy. Fifteen kilos of winter melon. Eight kilos of—

Movement in her peripheral vision.

Section D. The back section. The one that had been closed for "renovations" since last Thursday.

Workers in white hazmat suits carrying equipment out through the loading dock.

(Why hazmat suits for renovations?)

She watched them for thirty seconds. One worker dropped a metal case. It clattered on concrete. Another worker barked something sharp in Mandarin she couldn't quite hear. They loaded the case into an unmarked white van. Drove away.

No company logo on the van. No construction permits posted. No renovation debris.

(Strange.)

Li Mei returned to her bok choy. Not her business. Probably health inspection or pest control or one of those government things Qiang always complained about during dinner. Regulations and permits and forms in triplicate.

She arranged the bok choy into neat rows. Started building the winter melon pyramid.

Base layer: six melons. Second layer: three melons. Top: one perfect melon with minimal bruising.

(Thirty percent better sales. Qiang's numbers never lied.)

By 6:15 AM the market was filling. By 7:00 AM she'd sold four winter melons and eight bundles of bok choy. By 8:30 AM her hands were cold from handling produce and her feet hurt from standing on concrete and her throat felt scratchy in that way that meant she was getting Jian's cold from last week.

(Kids brought home everything. Daycare was germ factory. Qiang said they should pull Jian out but they couldn't afford private school and someone had to watch him while Li Mei worked the market and Qiang worked the engineering firm.)

She coughed. Dry cough. Throat-tickle cough. The kind that went away after hot tea.

A customer wanted three winter melons. Li Mei loaded them into bags. Took payment. Counted change. Coughed again.

(Definitely Jian's cold. Maybe she'd go home early. Make ginger tea. Sleep before Qiang got home from work.)

By noon her head hurt.

By 2:00 PM she was shivering despite the November warmth.

By 4:00 PM when she locked up Position 47 and rode her electric scooter home through afternoon traffic, her whole body ached.

(Just a cold. Jian had the same thing last week. He was fine in three days.)

---

## NOVEMBER 21, 2019 - THURSDAY AFTERNOON

The cold wasn't going away.

Two days. Usually colds went away in two days. Jian's cold had gone away in two days. But Li Mei's cold was getting worse.

She'd stayed home Wednesday. Qiang had taken the day off work to watch the kids and complained the entire time because missing work meant missing a project deadline and project deadlines determined quarterly bonuses. Li Mei had slept fourteen hours. Woke up Thursday feeling worse.

Fever now. Not high fever. Just persistent low fever that made her skin feel too tight and her eyes feel too dry.

She went to work anyway. Had to. November was busy season—winter vegetable season—and missing two days meant losing two days of sales. They needed the money. Xiao's daycare tuition was due. Jian needed new shoes because seven-year-olds grew like weeds.

Position 47. Four-forty-seven AM. Same as always.

(Except her hands shook unpacking the bok choy.)

(Except her breath felt short climbing stairs to storage.)

(Except the scratchy throat had become sharp throat pain every time she swallowed.)

By 10:00 AM she'd sold three winter melons and sat down twice between customers because standing made her dizzy.

Mrs. Chen from Position 52 noticed.

"You look terrible," Mrs. Chen said. Blunt. Mrs. Chen was always blunt. Sixty-three years old and no patience for politeness. "Go home. You're sick."

"Just a cold," Li Mei said.

"That's not cold. That's flu. Go home before you give it to customers."

Li Mei wanted to argue but another cough interrupted. This one hurt. Deep chest hurt. Like something was sitting on her ribs.

She closed Position 47 at 11:30 AM. Rode home on her scooter going fifteen kilometers per hour instead of the usual thirty because going faster made her dizzy.

Qiang was at work. Jian was at school. Xiao was at daycare.

Li Mei collapsed into bed still wearing her market clothes and slept until 3:00 PM when her phone alarm went off reminding her to pick up Xiao.

(Couldn't pick up Xiao. Could barely stand.)

She texted Qiang: *Can you get Xiao? I'm sick.*

Qiang: *I have meeting until 6. Ask your mother.*

Her mother lived forty minutes away and didn't drive.

Li Mei forced herself upright. Drank water. Took ibuprofen. Rode her scooter to daycare going ten kilometers per hour and picked up Xiao at 3:47 PM.

Xiao was four years old and talked nonstop about the finger painting he'd made and the boy who'd stolen his crackers at snack time and whether they could get McDonald's for dinner.

"Not tonight," Li Mei said. Voice hoarse. "Mama's sick."

"You sound funny," Xiao said.

"Just a cold."

They got home at 4:15 PM. Li Mei heated Xiao a bowl of instant noodles and put on cartoons and collapsed on the couch.

Jian got home at 5:30 PM from after-school program. Qiang got home at 6:45 PM and immediately asked why dinner wasn't ready.

"I'm sick," Li Mei said.

"Still? You've been sick three days."

"It's getting worse."

Qiang felt her forehead. Frowned. "You're burning up. Did you take anything?"

"Ibuprofen at three."

"Take more."

She took more.

That night she slept in the guest room so she wouldn't give Qiang the flu. Woke up at 2:00 AM soaked in sweat. Fever breaking, she thought. That's good. Fever breaking means getting better.

(Except the cough was worse.)

(Except breathing hurt now.)

(Except when she coughed something rattled in her chest that shouldn't rattle.)

---

## NOVEMBER 22, 2019 - FRIDAY MORNING

Li Mei woke up at 6:00 AM and couldn't breathe.

Not couldn't breathe like out-of-breath. Couldn't breathe like someone was sitting on her chest and her lungs weren't expanding right and every inhale ended too soon.

She sat up. That helped. Sitting up meant gravity wasn't pressing on her lungs.

(Or maybe sitting up meant she was awake enough to panic and panic meant breathing felt harder.)

Qiang knocked on the guest room door. "You okay?"

"Can't breathe right."

He opened the door. Looked at her. His face did that thing faces do when someone sees something scary they're trying not to show is scary.

"Hospital," he said.

"I'm fine. Just—"

"Hospital. Now. I'll call in sick. Get dressed."

She got dressed. Moved slowly. Breathing felt like work. Like every inhale required decision and effort instead of happening automatically.

(Breathing wasn't supposed to require thinking about it.)

(Why was she thinking about breathing?)

They dropped Jian and Xiao at her mother's apartment at 7:15 AM. Her mother took one look at Li Mei and started crying which meant it was worse than Li Mei thought because her mother never cried.

Wuhan Central Hospital emergency entrance at 7:52 AM.

Triage nurse took her temperature: 39.4 Celsius.

Oxygen saturation: 91%.

"That's low," the nurse said. Not to Li Mei. To the computer screen while typing notes.

They put her in a wheelchair. Took her to examination room. Put oxygen mask on her face.

(Breathing easier with oxygen. That was bad sign. If breathing was easier with oxygen that meant her lungs weren't working right without oxygen.)

Doctor came in. Young doctor. Maybe thirty. Tired eyes like he'd been working overnight shift.

He listened to her lungs with stethoscope. Frowned. Listened again. Frowned harder.

"How long have you been sick?"

"Four days. Maybe five."

"Any recent travel?"

"No."

"Contact with anyone who's been sick?"

"My son had a cold last week but he's fine now."

"Where do you work?"

"Huanan Seafood Market. I sell produce."

Something changed in the doctor's face. Not much. Just a small tightening around his eyes.

"Which section?"

"Section C. But I saw workers in Section D earlier this week. Tuesday morning. They were—"

"Wait here," the doctor said.

He left.

Came back ten minutes later with older doctor. Woman. Fifty-something. Serious face.

"I'm Dr. Wang," she said. "We're going to admit you for observation and run some tests."

"What kind of tests?"

"Chest X-ray. Blood work. Viral panel."

"I can't stay. I have kids. I have—"

"Mrs. Li, your oxygen saturation is ninety-one percent. Normal is ninety-seven to one hundred. You need to stay."

They admitted her at 9:30 AM.

Chest X-ray at 10:15 AM.

Blood draw at 11:00 AM.

By 2:00 PM she was in isolation room on fourth floor and the doctors' faces had gone from concerned to carefully neutral which was worse than concerned.

Dr. Wang came in wearing mask and gloves and gown like Li Mei had plague.

"Your chest X-ray shows bilateral pneumonia," Dr. Wang said. "Both lungs. We're starting you on antibiotics and antivirals but I need to ask you more questions about Huanan Market."

"What about it?"

"Section D. The back section. When did you see workers there?"

"Tuesday. November nineteenth. Early morning. They were wearing hazmat suits. Loading equipment into white van."

Dr. Wang wrote notes. Didn't look up. "Anyone else at the market sick?"

"Mrs. Chen said I looked terrible but I don't know if she's sick. Why?"

"We're seeing other cases. Similar symptoms. All connected to Huanan Market."

"How many cases?"

Dr. Wang didn't answer.

That was answer enough.

---

## NOVEMBER 23, 2019 - SATURDAY AFTERNOON

Li Mei was dying.

She didn't know it yet. Not consciously. But her body knew.

The antibiotics weren't working. The antivirals weren't working. Her oxygen saturation kept dropping—91%, then 89%, then 87%—and they'd increased her oxygen flow twice and talked about ventilator in hushed voices outside her room thinking she couldn't hear.

(She could hear.)

She called Qiang at 2:00 PM.

"How are the kids?"

"Fine. Jian's playing video games. Xiao misses you. When are you coming home?"

"Soon." Lie. Lies were easier than truth right now. "Let me talk to them."

Jian got on phone first.

"Hi Mama. Are you still sick?"

"Little bit. But I'm getting better." Another lie. "Are you being good for Baba?"

"Yeah. He let us get McDonald's for lunch."

"That's good. I love you."

"Love you too. Here's Xiao."

Xiao's voice: "Mama?"

"Hi baby."

"When are you coming home? I made you a drawing."

"What's it a drawing of?"

"Our family. You and Baba and Jian and me. And our apartment. And the market where you work."

Li Mei's chest hurt. Not from pneumonia. From something else.

"That sounds beautiful. I can't wait to see it."

"Are you coming home tonight?"

"Not tonight. But soon."

"Promise?"

"Promise." Third lie. She was getting good at lying.

After she hung up she cried for twenty minutes until her oxygen saturation dropped to 85% and alarms went off and nurses came running.

---

## NOVEMBER 24, 2019 - SUNDAY, 11:47 AM

Qiang came to visit at 11:00 AM.

He wore mask and gloves and looked scared.

"They won't let me bring the kids," he said. "Something about isolation protocol."

"That's okay." Li Mei's voice was whisper. Talking hurt. Everything hurt. "Probably better they don't see me like this."

"You look—" He stopped.

"Terrible. I know."

"They're saying there are seventeen cases now. All from the market. Three people died."

Li Mei didn't respond. Couldn't. Breathing was taking all her concentration.

Qiang held her hand. His hand was warm. Hers was cold despite the fever.

"What did the doctors say?" he asked.

"They don't know what it is. They're calling it 'unusual pneumonia.' Running more tests."

"But you're getting better, right? The antibiotics—"

"Qiang."

He stopped.

"Take care of the kids," she said.

"Don't talk like that. You're going to be fine."

"Please. Just promise me."

"Li Mei—"

"Promise."

"I promise. But you're going to be fine. You'll be home by next week and this will just be scary story we tell at dinner and—"

She squeezed his hand. Couldn't squeeze hard. Didn't have strength. But enough for him to understand: Stop.

He stopped.

They sat in silence for forty-seven minutes. Then visiting hours ended and Qiang had to leave and Li Mei watched him walk out thinking that was probably the last time she'd see him.

(Turned out she was right.)

---

## NOVEMBER 24, 2019 - SUNDAY, 2:47 PM

Li Mei died at 2:47 PM.

Not dramatically. Not like movies. Just quietly stopped breathing despite the oxygen mask and the ventilator they'd put her on an hour earlier and the doctors working on her chest trying to restart what couldn't be restarted.

Her last thought wasn't profound.

It was: *I didn't tell Jian where his new shoes were. They're in the closet. Top shelf. Qiang won't think to look there.*

Then nothing.

---

Thirty-four years old.

Mother of two.

Produce vendor at Huanan Seafood Market, Section C, Row 12, Position 47.

Patient Zero in what would become global pandemic.

One of seventeen cases reported by November 24, 2019.

One of three deaths.

(There would be many more.)

The doctors wore full hazmat suits removing her body. Sealed her in medical waste bag. Cremated her within eight hours to prevent spread.

No funeral. Just cremation and urn and Qiang standing in crematorium at 9:30 PM on Sunday night holding ceramic jar that used to be his wife and trying to understand how four days turned into this.

In Section C, Row 12, Position 47 at Huanan Seafood Market, Mrs. Chen from Position 52 would find Li Mei's vendor key on Tuesday morning.

She'd try calling Li Mei.

No answer.

She'd call Qiang.

He'd tell her.

Mrs. Chen would start coughing that afternoon.

Seventeen cases on November 24.

Fifty-three by November 30.

The numbers would keep growing.

But Li Mei wouldn't know that. She'd died believing she was unlucky. Believing she'd caught bad flu. Believing Qiang would raise their kids and remember to check the top shelf of the closet for Jian's new shoes.

She never knew about THRESHOLD.

Never knew about Hub 47.

Never knew she was murdered by mathematics designed to optimize humanity by killing thirty-five million people just like her.

Never knew Sarah Chen was watching news reports in Hartford safe house three days later seeing Li Mei's death as data point—*Patient Zero, Age 34, Huanan Market, November 24*—and understanding that partial prevention meant millions dead.

Li Mei just died.

Age thirty-four.

Position 47.

Lucky number.

(Except it wasn't.)
