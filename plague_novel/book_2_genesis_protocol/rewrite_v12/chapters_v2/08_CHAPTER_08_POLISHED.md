# CHAPTER 8 - QUANTUM MECHANICS LESSON

**Modern Timeline - GenVault Sublevel 3 Archive**
**November 19, 2019 - 2:47 PM**
**12 days until THRESHOLD deployment**
**47 hours remaining until Wells's detention deadline**

---

Sarah had documented thirty-nine hubs.

Fifteen hours typing casualty projections until the numbers stopped being people and started being just numbers: 47.3 million, 89.4 million, 103.7 million dead.

(Her hands were cramping. Again. She'd switched back to writing an hour ago when typing became impossible.)

The current file: **Hub 39: Istanbul, Turkey**.

Population density calculations. Pathogen delivery vectors. Storage location beneath the Hagia Sophia museum. Security protocols requiring seven biometric confirmations. Deployment timing synchronized to morning prayer when maximum population concentration occurred.

Projected casualties: 78.2 million.

Sarah wrote it down.

Moved to the next file.

Hub 40: Lagos, Nigeria.

(She'd seen this one before. Hour eight. Morrison showing her Africa's "optimization potential." Places where population density exceeded resource capacity and THRESHOLD would create "sustainable equilibrium.")

(Genocide. The word Morrison never used because "optimization" sounded like mathematics instead of murder.)

Lagos, Nigeria. 89.4 million dead.

Sarah's pen stopped moving.

Not because of the number—she'd seen worse. Hub 33: Delhi, India killed 127.3 million. Hub 41: Jakarta killed 94.1 million. The numbers had stopped shocking her around Hub 15 when her brain just... stopped processing them as human beings and started categorizing them as data points.

She stopped because her notebook was full.

Eight notebooks. Three hundred pages. Thirty-nine hubs documented in handwriting that got worse as exhaustion accumulated. She'd need a ninth notebook for the remaining eight hubs.

(Eight more cities. Eight more casualty projections. Eight more ways Morrison's mathematics turned billions of lives into targeting parameters.)

Sarah stood.

Walked to the supply cabinet.

(Her legs didn't work right. Fifteen hours sitting. Blood flow compromised. She stumbled, caught herself on the desk, waited for the pins-and-needles sensation to pass.)

The cabinet had notebooks. Pens. Office supplies Morrison had stocked because he knew she'd be documenting everything. Because this whole thing was his ethics test and Sarah gathering complete deployment data was the point.

She grabbed the ninth notebook.

Walked back to the desk.

(Didn't remember walking. Time distortion. Exhaustion making minutes blur together.)

Sat down.

Opened Hub 40 again.

Started writing.

---

The door opened.

Sarah didn't look up. Just kept writing. Hub 40 specifications. Delivery vectors. Casualty projections for West Africa exceeding ninety million when accounting for secondary cascade effects.

"You've been down here fifteen hours." Lagos's voice. Tired. Guilty. The man who'd given her the thumb drive and told her about the escape window and said **coffee will be waiting** like it was a promise instead of just coffee. "Morrison sent me to check on you again."

"Tell Morrison I'm almost done." Sarah pulled up Hub 41: Jakarta. "Eight more hubs. Maybe three more hours."

Lagos walked closer. Looked at the notebooks spread across the desk. Eight full notebooks. Casualty projections and hub specifications covering three hundred pages of handwritten documentation.

"You're memorizing everything," he said quietly. "Every hub. Every casualty projection. Every security protocol. You're building a complete intelligence package in your head."

"That's the point, isn't it?" "Morrison's testing whether defensive Protocol works. Can't prove prevention scales if I don't gather complete data first."

"Sarah—"

"Don't." Her hand stopped writing. "Don't tell me to rest. Don't tell me I'm pushing too hard. Don't tell me fifteen hours is unhealthy or I need sleep or I'm going to burn out before the escape window."

She looked up at Lagos.

This man who'd spent two years sabotaging THRESHOLD. Who'd lost Ava. Who was standing in the archive at 2 PM watching Sarah destroy herself trying to memorize genocide because Morrison needed proof that ethics could compete with mathematics when given every advantage.

"I have forty-seven hours," Sarah said. "Forty-seven hours before Wells transfers me to Site Seven and the intelligence gathering stops and Morrison's test becomes irrelevant. Forty-seven hours to document eight more hubs and memorize everything and build the intelligence package that either stops THRESHOLD or proves Morrison was right all along."

"You can't document all forty-seven hubs in forty-seven hours."

"Watch me."

Lagos was quiet for a long moment.

Then he pulled out a chair. Sat down. Set a fresh cup of coffee on Sarah's desk.

(GenVault coffee. Still tasted like shit. Sarah drank it anyway.)

"Let me help," Lagos said.

"Help how?"

He pulled out his tablet. Accessed files Sarah didn't have clearance for. Pulled up Hub 42: São Paulo, Brazil.

"I'll read specifications," Lagos said. "You write. We work together, we finish faster."

"Why would you help me?"

"Because Morrison's watching on surveillance right now thinking his ethics test is proceeding according to plan. Because Wells is three miles away preparing detention orders that override Morrison's timeline. Because in forty-seven hours you either escape with complete deployment data or get transferred to Site Seven and we lose our chance to stop THRESHOLD before deployment."

Lagos opened the São Paulo file. Started reading.

"Hub 42: São Paulo, Brazil. Storage location: Sublevel 3 of the Instituto Butantan. Security: Biometric plus retinal plus voice recognition. Pathogen delivery vectors calibrated for South American genetic markers concentrated in urban populations. Projected casualties: 52.1 million."

Sarah wrote.

Lagos kept reading.

"Deployment timing: December 1, 2019, 0400 local time. Synchronized with Atlanta hub to create simultaneous outbreak across Western Hemisphere. Secondary cascade effects: 73.8 million additional casualties across Latin America within six weeks as healthcare systems collapse."

Sarah's hand cramped.

She ignored it.

Kept writing.

Lagos moved to Hub 43.

"Singapore. Storage location: Sentosa Island biological research facility. Security classification: Level 5 containment. Projected casualties: 31.7 million across Southeast Asian corridor."

Sarah filled pages.

Lagos read specifications.

They worked in silence except for his voice reading genocide and her pen scratching casualty projections into the ninth notebook.

Three hours.

Eight hubs.

Hub 42 through Hub 47.

São Paulo. Singapore. Toronto. Cairo. Sydney. Manila. Johannesburg. Buenos Aires.

Cities Sarah had visited. Cities she'd never seen. Cities containing millions of people who'd wake up on December 1st and start dying because Morrison's mathematics said their genetics weren't optimized for the next crisis.

(467.3 million people total in the final eight hubs.)

(4.2 billion total across all forty-seven hubs.)

(Numbers that stopped meaning anything around Hub 15 and just became data points in Morrison's equation: population exceeds carrying capacity, therefore reduction is necessary, therefore four billion dead is optimization instead of genocide.)

Lagos read the last specification.

"Hub 47: Buenos Aires, Argentina. Projected casualties: 43.8 million. Deployment completes THRESHOLD global coverage. Total mortality across all forty-seven hubs: 4.237 billion people. Estimated timeline to new equilibrium: eighteen months."

Sarah wrote the last number.

Set down her pen.

Closed the ninth notebook.

Forty-seven hubs documented. Complete deployment specifications. Every casualty projection. Every storage location. Every security protocol Morrison had built over thirty years to kill four billion people and call it mathematics.

She'd memorized genocide.

Now she had forty-four hours to escape with it before Wells made Morrison's ethics test irrelevant.

---

Lagos looked at the nine notebooks spread across the desk.

At Sarah sitting in the metal chair, exhausted, hands cramping, eyes burning from fifteen hours of continuous documentation.

"You did it," he said quietly. "Complete intelligence package. Every hub. Every specification. If you get out with this data—if the FBI raids even half these locations before December 1st—THRESHOLD fails."

"If I get out."

"Forty-four hours until Wells's deadline. Fire alarm testing happens in thirty-six hours. Forty-three-second window during shift change when electronic locks disengage and you can reach the medical bay exit to parking garage B2."

"You said that before." Sarah drank more coffee. Cold again. Developing a pattern: drink coffee while it's hot, get distracted by documenting mass murder, drink coffee while it's cold and bitter and wrong. "What you didn't say is what happens when Morrison realizes I'm running. When Wells overrides the fire alarm. When security locks down the building before I reach the exit."

Sarah looked at the notebooks.

Nine notebooks containing 4.2 billion deaths.

"What's the probability I make it out?" she asked.

Lagos was quiet for a long moment.

Then: "You want mathematics or hope?"

"Mathematics."

"Ten percent. Maybe fifteen if Martin's extraction team is positioned perfectly. Forty-three-second window during fire alarm testing. Electronic locks disengage. Security protocols pause for safety override. But you still have to run from sublevel 3 to ground level medical bay. Still have to reach parking garage B2 before locks reengage. Still have to avoid seventeen guards and Morrison's security personnel and Wells's people who want you detained before you escape."

"Fifteen percent."

"Generous estimate. Ten percent you make it out. Ninety percent you get caught, detained, transferred to Site Seven, and THRESHOLD deploys on schedule."

Sarah did the math.

Ten percent survival probability.

Ninety percent failure.

One hundred percent certainty that doing nothing meant 4.2 billion deaths anyway.

"And if I get caught?" she asked.

"Site Seven. Permanent detention. Intelligence gets contained. Martin loses your signal. December 1st arrives and Morrison learns that ethics loses when tested against optimization."

"No pressure."

"I didn't say there was no pressure." Lagos smiled. Sad. True. "I said ten percent. That's better than zero."

He stood. Walked to the door. Stopped.

"Thirty-six hours. Fire alarm testing. Forty-three seconds. Medical bay exit to parking garage B2. You run, you don't stop, you don't look back. Fifteen percent if you're fast. Twenty if you're lucky."

"And you?"

"I'll be in Morrison's office during fire alarm testing. Keeping him distracted. Making sure he doesn't override the escape window."

"He'll know you helped me."

"He already knows." Lagos glanced at the surveillance camera in the corner. "He's been watching us document the final eight hubs. Not stopping it."

Ten percent probability that felt more like hope than mathematics.

"Why are you doing this?" she asked. "Really."

Lagos looked at the nine notebooks.

At 4.2 billion deaths documented in three hundred pages.

"Because I told Ava to stay quiet and she died anyway." His voice was flat. Final. "I'm not making that choice again."

He walked to the door.

Opened it.

Looked back one more time.

"Thirty-six hours, Sarah. Fire alarm testing. Forty-three seconds. Run like hell and don't stop. Ten percent probability is enough to choose when the alternative is four billion deaths."

Lagos left.

The door locked behind him.

Sarah sat alone in the archive.

Surrounded by nine notebooks containing genocide.

Forty-four hours before Wells's detention order.

Thirty-six hours before the escape window.

Ten percent probability she made it out.

(Ninety percent probability she got caught.)

(One hundred percent certainty that trying was better than not trying.)

She pulled out her phone.

Typed a message to the encrypted number Martin had given her:

**Fire alarm testing in 36 hours. 43-second window. Medical bay exit to parking garage B2. Be ready.**

Waited.

Martin's response came three minutes later:

**Extraction team positioned. Coordinates locked. We'll be waiting. Don't stop running.**

Sarah deleted the messages.

Set down the phone.

Looked at the surveillance camera in the corner.

She picked up the first notebook.

Started reviewing Hub 1: Atlanta.

Memorizing everything.

Because ten percent probability meant she needed to be perfect. Needed complete intelligence. Needed every hub specification and casualty projection and security protocol memorized so thoroughly that when she escaped—if she escaped—the FBI could raid all forty-seven locations before THRESHOLD deployed.

(If she got caught, the intelligence died with her.)

(If she escaped, 4.2 billion people lived.)

(Ten percent probability.)

(Forty-four hours until Wells made it zero.)

Sarah read.

Memorized.

Counted down.

---

Fourteen floors above, Morrison's phone buzzed with another message from Wells.

He didn't open it.

Just watched Sarah memorize genocide and counted down to the fire alarm he'd scheduled himself.

Forty-four hours.
