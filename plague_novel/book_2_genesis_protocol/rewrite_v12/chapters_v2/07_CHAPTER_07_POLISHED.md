# CHAPTER 7 - THE INSIDE GAME

**Modern Timeline - GenVault Sublevel 3 Archive**
**November 19, 2019 - 3:17 AM**
**12 days until THRESHOLD deployment**
**64 hours remaining in Morrison's deadline**

---

Sarah's eyes burned.

Eight hours in the archive. Eight hours reading casualty projections and hub specifications and deployment protocols. Eight hours memorizing genocide while pretending she was considering recruitment.

(Her coffee had gone cold four hours ago. She drank it anyway. GenVault coffee tasted like shit even when fresh.)

The current file on her screen: **THRESHOLD Hub Network - Complete Deployment Manifest**.

Forty-seven hubs. Forty-seven cities. Addresses. Storage protocols. Security specifications. Pathogen delivery vectors calibrated for population density and geographic distribution.

Sarah opened her eighth notebook. Started writing.

Not for herself—the trackers behind her ears were recording everything, broadcasting encrypted video to Martin three miles away. But writing helped her think. Helped her process the mathematics of mass murder Morrison kept presenting as optimization.

**Hub 1: Atlanta CDC Storage Facility**
- Sublevel 4, Biological Containment Wing C
- Security: Biometric + keycard + armed response
- Target population: Southeastern US corridor
- Projected casualties: 47.3 million
- Deployment date: December 1, 2019, 0600 EST

She kept writing.

Hub after hub. City after city. Millions and millions of people who'd wake up on December 1st and start dying because Morrison's mathematics said their genetics weren't optimized enough for the next crisis.

(Forty-seven million dead in Atlanta alone.)

(Just one hub.)

(Just one city.)

Sarah's hand cramped. She switched to typing. Faster. More efficient. Racing against the seventy-two hours Morrison had given her before—

(Before what?)

(Before he stopped pretending recruitment was possible and detained her permanently?)

(Before Wells overrode him and Sarah disappeared into whatever black site the Order used for people who refused to help with genocide?)

The door behind her opened.

Sarah didn't turn. Just kept typing. Hub specifications for Chicago. Projected casualties: 38.9 million. Storage location: Former nuclear bunker converted to biological containment. Security protocols. Deployment vectors.

"You've been down here eight hours." Dr. Lagos's voice. Tired. Guilty. The man who'd given her the thumb drive and told her about Ava and said **coffee will be waiting** like it meant something more than coffee. "Morrison sent me to check on you."

"Tell Morrison I'm fine." Sarah pulled up Hub 15: Tokyo. "Just trying to understand the scope before I make a decision about partnership."

Lagos walked closer. Stood behind her shoulder. Looking at the screen showing Tokyo hub specifications and casualty projections for Japan: 67.2 million dead.

"You're memorizing deployment data," he said quietly. "Not considering recruitment. You've photographed twelve hubs. Your trackers are broadcasting everything to teams outside. Morrison knows. He's been watching the whole time."

Sarah's hands stopped typing.

(Fuck.)

(How long had Lagos known? How long had Morrison been watching her gather intelligence while pretending she didn't know he knew she was gathering intelligence while pretending to consider—)

Her brain was spiraling. Too many layers. Too much deception wrapped in deception wrapped in Morrison's twisted ethics test.

"Then why hasn't he stopped me?" Sarah asked.

Lagos sat in the chair beside her. Exhausted. Done pretending. "Because Morrison wants you to prove him wrong. He gave you complete deployment data as a test. Seventy-two hours to demonstrate that defensive Protocol prevents collapse better than genocide. If you succeed—if you get out with evidence and stop THRESHOLD—that proves prevention works and Morrison can admit thirty years of work was wrong."

"And if I fail?"

"Then THRESHOLD deploys on schedule and Morrison knows he exhausted every alternative first." Lagos rubbed his face. Hands shaking slightly. "You're not gathering intelligence. You're Morrison's absolution. His final proof that ethics can't win against mathematics when the stakes get big enough."

Sarah looked at the screen.

At Tokyo. 67.2 million dead.

At Chicago. 38.9 million.

At Atlanta. 47.3 million.

At Hub 23: São Paulo. 52.1 million.

At Hub 31: Mumbai. 71.8 million.

Billions of lives reduced to targeting parameters and casualty projections and Morrison's desperate need to prove he wasn't a monster, just a man who'd made hard choices that happened to look like monstrousness when you stopped using euphemisms like "optimization" and started calling it what it was: genocide.

"How much time do I have?" Sarah asked.

"Sixty-four hours." Lagos pulled up a countdown on his tablet. Showed her. "Morrison told Wells you have until November 21st at 11:47 PM to decide about recruitment. After that, Wells gets authorization to detain you permanently. But Morrison also—"

He stopped.

Sarah waited.

"Morrison told me when you run—during shift change, when the fire alarm testing creates a forty-three-second window—don't stop you. Don't report it. Let you escape with the deployment data."

Sarah turned to look at Lagos.

This man who'd spent two years trying to sabotage THRESHOLD from inside. Who'd lost Ava. Who was sitting in an archive at 3 AM helping Sarah understand that Morrison wasn't just testing her—he was testing himself.

"He wants me to get out," Sarah said.

"He wants defensive Protocol to prove it works." Lagos's voice was flat. Dead. "If you escape with complete deployment data. If you expose THRESHOLD to authorities. If somehow you prevent deployment through pattern recognition and crisis response and cooperative behavior—then that proves the defensive Protocol scales. That proves Morrison wasted thirty years building unnecessary genocide. That proves he could have chosen prevention instead."

"And if I get caught escaping?"

"Then defensive Protocol failed and THRESHOLD deploys and Morrison knows he was right all along." Lagos stood. Walked to the door. Stopped. "Sixty-four hours, Sarah. Morrison gave you complete access. GPS trackers broadcasting your location to rescue teams. Escape route planned during fire alarm testing. He's giving ethics every advantage because he needs to know—really know—whether he's saving the world or just a man who lost his daughter and built sophisticated math to justify grief."

He left.

Sarah sat alone in the archive.

Surrounded by casualty projections. Deployment protocols. Forty-seven hubs containing pathogen vectors designed to kill 4.2 billion people in the name of optimization.

(Morrison watching her on surveillance.)

(Wells demanding detention.)

(Sixty-four hours before the window closed and Sarah had to choose: escape with the data or get detained trying.)

She pulled up the next file.

Hub 33: Lagos, Nigeria.

Projected casualties: 89.4 million.

Sarah kept typing.

---

Three miles away, Martin Reeves watched the video feed broadcasting from Sarah's trackers.

Both signals strong. Everything she was seeing, he was seeing.

He pulled up the intelligence package they'd been building from Sarah's broadcasts. Twelve hubs documented. Addresses, security specs, deployment vectors.

"Twelve hubs isn't enough." Maya's voice through the encrypted phone. Sharp. Clinical. "Morrison's network has forty-seven hubs. Twelve compromised means thirty-five still operational. THRESHOLD still deploys."

"Then we need her to get more before she runs."

"We're betting on Morrison's crisis of conscience lasting long enough for Sarah to gather complete intelligence?"

"We're betting," Martin said quietly, "that Morrison wants to be stopped more than he wants to be right."

"That's not a plan. That's psychology."

"That's all we have."

Maya was quiet on the line for a moment. When she spoke again, the clinical edge was gone.

"She's been in there four days, Martin. If Morrison changes his mind—if Wells overrides him—" She stopped. Started over. "We should have a limit. A point where we pull her out whether she's finished or not."

"She'd never agree to that."

"I'm not asking her. I'm asking you."

Martin watched Sarah's feed. Hub after hub. Methodical. Precise.

Sixty-four hours.

---

**GenVault Sublevel 3 Archive - 5:23 AM**

Hour ten. Twenty-three hubs documented. Her notebooks were full. Her brain was full. Casualty projections and deployment vectors and security protocols until the numbers stopped meaning anything and started just being numbers: 47.3 million, 67.2 million, 89.4 million dead dead dead.

She needed to stop.

(Couldn't stop.)

(Twenty-three hubs documented. Twenty-four more to go. If she stopped now—)

Her coffee cup was empty.

She stood. Walked to the small kitchenette in the corner of the archive. Poured another cup. GenVault coffee still tasted like shit but the caffeine was necessary and taste didn't matter when you were racing against genocide.

The door opened.

Sarah turned.

Dr. Catherine Wells stood in the doorway. Late fifties. Severe. Cold. The woman Morrison had overruled when Wells demanded detention.

"Dr. Chen." Wells stepped inside. Let the door close behind her. "I thought we should have a conversation. Director to target."

Sarah held her coffee cup.

(Careful. Steady. Don't spill. Don't show fear.)

"I'm not a target," Sarah said. "Morrison's considering me for partnership."

"Morrison's testing his conscience using you as a metric." Wells walked closer. Precise. Controlled. Not someone who'd joined the Order—someone who'd been raised in it. Whose certainty was inherited, not learned. "I've worked with Morrison for sixteen years. Watched him recruit twenty-three people. He's brilliant at identifying who'll compromise when shown big enough stakes. But you're different."

"How?"

"You're not recruitment. You're Morrison's crisis of faith." Wells stopped three feet away. Too close. Invading space. Making Sarah feel threatened without touching her. "He gave you complete deployment data. Seventy-two hours to prove prevention works better than optimization. GPS trackers broadcasting to extraction teams. Escape route planned during fire alarm testing. He's using you to test whether ethics wins when given every advantage."

Sarah's hands wanted to shake.

She gripped the coffee cup harder.

(Steady. Don't show weakness. Wells is looking for cracks.)

"Then why are you here?" Sarah asked.

"Because Morrison's test is irrelevant." Wells smiled. Cold. "In sixty-two hours, my authorization to detain you overrides Morrison's recruitment timeline. You'll be transferred to Site Seven. Permanently. No more archives. No more deployment data. No more testing Morrison's conscience."

"Morrison won't allow that."

"Morrison doesn't have a choice. The Order's leadership approved the detention—my family has carried this mission longer than Morrison's been alive." Her voice didn't waver. "He gets sixty-two more hours to pretend ethics matters, and then reality resumes and THRESHOLD deploys on schedule."

Wells walked to the monitor showing Sarah's research. Looked at Hub 23: São Paulo. 52.1 million casualties.

"You're documenting hubs," Wells said quietly. "Building an intelligence package. Thinking you'll escape during fire alarm testing and expose THRESHOLD before deployment. Morrison's letting you because he wants to believe prevention works. But here's what happens: In sixty-two hours, you get transferred. Your intelligence gets contained. Your extraction teams lose your signal. And December 1st arrives and 4.2 billion people die and Morrison learns that ethics loses when tested against optimization."

She turned to face Sarah.

"So enjoy your sixty-two hours, Dr. Chen. Morrison's giving you a fair chance to prove him wrong. I'm just here to remind you that the Order doesn't believe in fair chances when billions of lives depend on optimization."

Wells left.

The door locked behind her.

Sarah stood alone in the archive.

Coffee cup shaking in her hands now.

(Sixty-two hours. Not seventy-two. Wells had cut it.)

She set the coffee cup down before she dropped it.

Looked at her notebooks. Twenty-three hubs. Twenty-four more to document.

Sarah pulled up Hub 24: Rio de Janeiro.

Casualties: 41.2 million.

She started typing again.

Faster now.
