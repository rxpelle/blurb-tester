# CHAPTER 4 - THE SACRIFICE

**Modern Timeline - GenVault Headquarters, Boston**
**November 18, 2019 - 2:47 PM**
**13 days until THRESHOLD deployment**

---

Morrison's office looked like a museum exhibit titled "What Genocidal Architects Think Looks Professional."

Mahogany desk. Leather chairs. Floor-to-ceiling windows overlooking Boston Harbor that cost more than Sarah's entire graduate education. Bookshelves lined with medical journals and what looked like medieval manuscripts in climate-controlled cases.

And on the wall behind Morrison's desk: seventeen monitors showing collapse models in real time.

Climate curves trending down. Resource depletion trending up. Political fragmentation indexes that looked like someone's EKG during a heart attack.

Sarah stood in the doorway, trying not to look like someone who'd walked into a trap on purpose with two GPS trackers broadcasting her location to teams who'd burn this building down if she disappeared.

(She was looking like that.)

(Morrison knew she was looking like that.)

(This was fine.)

"Coffee?" Morrison gestured to a silver service on the side table. China cups. The kind that screamed "we have money and taste and nothing to hide."

"I'm fine," Sarah said.

Her voice came out steadier than she felt. Good. Professional competence was easier when your voice cooperated even if your stomach was doing complicated things involving nausea and terror and the coppery taste of panic.

Morrison smiled. Patient. Warm. The smile of someone who had all the time in the world and knew how this conversation would go.

"Please. Sit." He gestured to one of the leather chairs. "You've come a long way. Literally and metaphorically."

Sarah sat.

The chair was obscenely comfortable. The kind of comfort that felt like a trap. Like if she relaxed even slightly she'd sink into leather and never get out.

(Which was the point.)

Morrison poured himself coffee. Moved with careful precision. Steady hands. The hands of someone who could authorize murder and then drink coffee without spilling a drop.

Sarah's own hands wanted to shake.

She gripped the armrests. Leather squeaked under her fingers.

"You have questions," Morrison said. Not a question. Statement. He sat across from her, coffee cup balanced perfectly. "That's why you came. To see the complete picture. To verify everything independently before making decisions."

"I want to see the archive," Sarah said. "The original manuscripts. The genetic data. The deployment protocols. Everything you showed Jenson when you recruited him."

Morrison's smile widened.

Slightly.

Like she'd said what he'd expected her to say.

(She had.)

"Of course. Transparency is critical." He set down his coffee. Pulled up something on his tablet. "But first, I need you to understand the context. The *why* behind what we do. Because the data without context is just numbers. And numbers without understanding lead to panic instead of comprehension."

He turned the tablet toward her.

A graph. Simple. Devastating.

Two lines diverging like a fork in the road except instead of roads it was "humanity survives" versus "humanity extinct."

"This is the Bronze Age Collapse," Morrison said. "1200 BCE. Eastern Mediterranean civilization—Hittites, Mycenaeans, Egyptians, everyone—collapsed over fifty years. Ninety percent mortality. The survivors regressed to pre-literate conditions. Took four centuries to recover."

Sarah stared at the graph.

She knew this. Had studied it. Ancient history, archaeological evidence, the mysterious end of the Bronze Age that historians still argued about.

"Drought," she said. "Climate change. Systems collapse."

"Exactly." Morrison glanced at the medieval manuscripts in their climate-controlled cases. "The analysis of that collapse goes back almost as far as the collapse itself. We inherited the data." He pulled up another graph. "Now look at this."

Modern collapse projections.

Starting now. 2019. Trending down for thirty years until—

Nothing.

Just flatline.

Ninety percent of current population gone.

Sarah's stomach dropped.

"Climate crisis," Morrison continued. Clinical. Professional. Like he was presenting at a conference instead of showing her the end of civilization. "Resource depletion. Political fragmentation. Migration pressure. War. Famine. Pandemic. All the horsemen showing up at once because we crammed eight billion people onto a planet that can sustainably support two billion."

He swiped to another graph.

This one had two lines.

Red line: uncontrolled collapse. Seven point two billion dead over thirty years.

Blue line: managed intervention. Four billion dead over ten years.

"THRESHOLD," Morrison said quietly. "Four billion dead." He paused. Let the number sit. "But civilization survives. Infrastructure intact. Humanity diminished but *alive*."

Sarah couldn't look away from the graphs.

Seven point two billion dead.

Or four billion dead.

Her brain was trying to process the numbers. Failing. Crashing. Because how do you choose between two versions of apocalypse?

(You don't. You prevent both. That's what the defensive Protocol was designed to do—*prevent* collapse instead of managing extinction.)

"You're showing me trolley problems," Sarah said. Voice flat. "Kill one or let five die. Except scaled to billions."

"I'm showing you mathematics." Morrison leaned forward. "The Bronze Age collapse was chaotic. This one doesn't have to be."

"By killing four billion people."

"By *saving* four billion people." Morrison's voice carried absolute conviction. "Look at the data and tell me you see another option."

Sarah looked at the graphs.

Really looked.

The projections were... well, they were accurate. Climate models, resource curves, political fragmentation—she'd seen similar analyses at MIT, at GenVault, in every systems dynamics course she'd ever taken.

The collapse was coming.

That part was real.

The question was whether Morrison's "solution" prevented worse collapse or just made him feel better about mass murder.

(The second one.)

"The defensive Protocol," Sarah said carefully, "was designed to *prevent* collapse. Identify crisis patterns early. Distribute crisis-response genetics across populations. Build resilience *before* the collapse happens. Not—" She gestured at the blue line. "Not engineer survivors through controlled extinction."

Morrison smiled.

Not patient anymore. Hungry.

Like she'd just confirmed something he'd been waiting to hear.

"The defensive Protocol," he said slowly, "was designed three thousand years ago for populations of thousands. Bronze Age city-states. Maybe a few hundred thousand people across the entire Mediterranean." He pulled up more data. "It worked because the numbers were manageable. You could identify crisis-response individuals, position them strategically, build resilience through targeted intervention."

He swiped to modern population data.

Eight billion people.

Spread across every continent.

Interconnected through technology and trade and migration in ways ancient civilizations couldn't imagine.

"The defensive Protocol doesn't *scale*," Morrison said. "Eight billion people. The math doesn't work. The timeline doesn't work."

Sarah's throat was doing that closing thing again.

Because he was... he was right about that part.

The defensive Protocol was designed for small populations. City-states. Manageable numbers.

Not eight billion people spread across a globally interconnected civilization.

"So you gave up," Sarah said. "Decided prevention was too hard and optimization was easier."

"We adapted." Morrison's voice hardened. "Different tool for different context."

"Different tool that kills four billion people."

"Different tool that *saves* four billion people from dying in seven point two billion worth of chaos." Morrison stood. Walked to the windows. Looked out at Boston Harbor.

He turned back to her.

"If you had to choose between seven point two billion dead versus four billion dead—knowing those are the only options—which would you choose?"

Sarah's mouth tasted like copper.

Like she'd bitten her cheek again.

(When had she bitten her cheek? She couldn't remember. Stress response. Body doing stupid things.)

"I'd choose prevention," she said.

"That's not on the table anymore."

"Then I'd make it be on the table."

Morrison laughed.

Not mocking. Genuinely amused.

"I said the same thing when I joined the Order. Thirty years ago. Fresh PhD, brilliant systems analyst, thought I could fix everything." He sat back down. Suddenly looked older. Tired. "Spent fifteen years trying to make defensive Protocol work at modern scale. Built models. Ran simulations. Tried every variation I could imagine."

He pulled up data on his tablet. Showed her.

Simulation after simulation. Defensive Protocol implementations. All of them failing. Collapse happening anyway. Seven point two billion dead.

"It doesn't work," Morrison said quietly. "I *wanted* it to work. Others before me wanted it to work—spent lifetimes trying, generation after generation." His voice dropped. "But the mathematics don't care about what we want. The timeline doesn't care. The collapse is coming and prevention is no longer possible."

Sarah stared at the simulations.

They looked... professional. Detailed. The kind of work that took years of effort and genuine hope that maybe, maybe there was another way.

Morrison had tried.

And failed.

(Or convinced himself he'd failed because the alternative was admitting he was a murderer instead of a savior.)

"Emily," Sarah said quietly.

Morrison went still.

Very still.

The kind of stillness that meant she'd just hit something that hurt.

"My daughter," he said finally. "You read the files Maya stole."

"Pediatric leukemia. Seven years old. Died before you could engineer defensive genetics into populations that might have saved her."

"Twenty-seven years ago." Morrison's voice was flat. Empty. "Before THRESHOLD existed. Before I understood that random genetic failure—cancers, immune deficiencies, all the ways children die for no reason—could be optimized out of populations if we had the courage to do the math."

Sarah's stomach twisted.

"You built a genocide because your daughter died."

"I built a *system*," Morrison corrected. Voice sharp now. Defensive. "That prevents other people's daughters from dying the same way. That optimizes human genetics for resilience, crisis response, cooperative behavior. That gives humanity a future instead of watching everything collapse into chaos."

"By killing everyone who doesn't have the right genetic markers."

"By *saving* everyone who does." Morrison's hands gripped the armrests. Knuckles white. "Four billion people survive THRESHOLD. Four billion daughters get to grow up. Emily didn't. But if the defensive Protocol had been implemented earlier—if crisis-response genetics had been optimized across populations before she was born—maybe her immune system would have fought harder. Maybe she'd be thirty-four right now instead of dead at seven."

His voice cracked.

Slightly.

"So yes, Dr. Chen. I built this because Emily died. Because I couldn't accept that her death was random and meaningless. Because if I can save four billion other Emilys by making hard choices, then maybe her death meant something."

Sarah looked at him.

Really looked.

Saw a man who'd lost his daughter and built a genocide to make sense of it. Who'd convinced himself that mass murder was mathematics. That optimization justified extinction.

Who genuinely believed he was saving the world.

(Which made him more dangerous than any cartoon villain could ever be.)

"I'm sorry about Emily," Sarah said.

Meant it.

"But you're wrong about THRESHOLD. About the math. About optimization." She leaned forward. "The defensive Protocol wasn't about genetic optimization. It was about *distributing resilience*. Building systems that could absorb shocks. Creating networks of people who'd help each other survive instead of competing for resources."

"That's idealism."

"That's systems theory." Sarah pulled out her own phone. Showed him data—the genetic correlations she'd stolen from GenVault, cross-referenced with Maya's CDC research. "Look at the populations that survived historical collapses. Black Death survivors. Spanish flu survivors. They weren't all genetically optimal. They survived because they had *community*. Cooperation. Resource sharing. The defensive markers you're breeding for? They don't predict individual survival. They predict *social cohesion*."

Morrison studied the data.

Expression unreadable.

Sarah pressed on. "THRESHOLD won't save civilization by killing everyone without optimal markers. It'll destroy the social cohesion that lets civilization exist. You'll have four billion genetically optimal people who watched their families die and spent the rest of their lives traumatized. That's not a future. That's post-apocalyptic hellscape with better genetics."

Morrison was quiet for a long moment.

Then he smiled.

Slow. Satisfied.

Like she'd just passed a test she didn't know she was taking.

"That's why we need you," he said.

Sarah's stomach dropped.

"What?"

"Your analysis is correct. Social cohesion matters." Morrison leaned forward. "That's exactly why THRESHOLD needs someone like you. You could help us refine the deployment. Reduce casualties."

Sarah stared.

He was recruiting her.

Using her own arguments about why THRESHOLD was terrible to explain why she should help make it *less* terrible.

"I'm not helping you murder four billion people," Sarah said.

"You're helping me *save* four billion people." Morrison's voice was patient. Reasonable. "All I'm asking is that you help optimize the outcome."

"Hard choices like which four billion people get to live?"

"Hard choices like making sure the four billion who survive can rebuild civilization instead of just inheriting traumatized rubble." Morrison pulled up more data. "With your help, we could reduce projected casualties from four billion to three point eight billion. Maybe three point five. Every refinement saves millions of lives. Millions of Emilys who get to grow up."

Two hundred million lives.

The space between four billion dead and three point eight billion dead.

Sarah's brain tried to process that.

Failed.

"You're offering me a trolley lever," she said. "Kill slightly fewer people and call it mercy."

"I'm offering you agency." Morrison stood. Moved to the door. Held it open.

"Or you can refuse. Walk out. Watch it happen without you."

She gripped the armrests tighter.

Behind her ear, the tracker pulsed. Warm. Subtle. Martin watching. Maya ready to activate bioterrorism protocols if Sarah disappeared.

She wasn't alone.

She was bait.

Here to gather intelligence, not to join them.

(But what if Morrison was right about the math?)

(What if prevention was impossible and she was choosing between two types of apocalypse?)

(What if four billion dead was the better option and refusing to help meant seven point two billion died instead?)

Sarah stood.

Slowly.

"I want to see the deployment protocols," she said. "Hub locations. Pathogen vectors. Timeline. Everything. If you want me to consider helping optimize THRESHOLD, I need complete transparency."

Morrison's smile returned.

"Of course. Transparency is critical." He gestured down the hallway. "The archive is on sublevel three. Climate-controlled. Every manuscript, every genetic dataset, every deployment plan we've developed over thirty years. You'll have full access."

"Unsupervised?"

"Supervised by Dr. David Lagos. Our lead geneticist. He'll answer any technical questions." Morrison started walking. "But first, there's something else you need to see. The breeding program facilities."

Sarah's blood went cold.

"Breeding program."

"Defensive Protocol carriers like you are rare." Morrison's tone stayed pleasant. Professional. Like he was discussing crop yields instead of human breeding. "Natural reproduction is too slow. Too random. But with modern techniques—"

"You're breeding people." Sarah's voice came out flat. Dead.

"We're *optimizing* reproduction. Ensuring defensive Protocol markers propagate efficiently. Creating the next generation of crisis-response individuals who'll rebuild civilization after THRESHOLD." He paused. Looked back at her. "You have optimal markers, Dr. Chen. Participation would be voluntary, of course. But your contribution to the program could save—"

"No."

The word came out harder than Sarah intended.

Morrison stopped walking.

Turned.

"That's the line?" he asked quietly. "You'll consider helping optimize mass deployment that kills four billion people. But voluntary genetic contribution crosses the line?"

"It's not voluntary if the alternative is watching billions die."

"It's not coercion if you genuinely have a choice." Morrison's expression shifted. Something colder underneath the patience. "THRESHOLD deploys in thirteen days whether you help or not. The only question is whether you make it better or worse."

He moved closer.

Not threatening. Just. Intense.

"The breeding program is optional. You can refuse. But understand what you're refusing."

Sarah's hands were shaking again.

Badly.

"I need to think," she said.

"Of course. Take all the time you need." Morrison gestured back toward his office. "Seventy-two hours. That's when we finalize deployment protocols. Until then, you have full access to the archive. Ask questions. Verify data. See the complete picture."

"And after seventy-two hours?"

Morrison's smile was patient.

Sad.

"After seventy-two hours, you decide: help us optimize THRESHOLD and save millions of lives, or walk away knowing you chose ideology over pragmatism. Either way, the Protocol deploys on schedule."

He turned and walked down the hallway.

Left Sarah standing there.

Shaking.

Counting her heartbeats.

Ninety-three beats per minute.

Way too fast.

Behind her ear, the tracker pulsed.

Martin watching.

Maya waiting.

Both of them thinking she was gathering intelligence.

Both of them trusting her to stay strong.

To not fall for Morrison's mathematics.

Sarah touched the tracker.

Tapped twice—signal that she was okay.

(She wasn't okay.)

(Morrison had just shown her evidence that prevention was impossible and optimization was the only option and four billion dead was mercy.)

(And she was terrified he might be right.)

---

Three miles away, Martin watched Sarah's signal on his laptop.

Two taps.

She was okay.

"Signal received," he said into his encrypted phone. "She's transmitting."

Maya's voice: "How long has she been in there?"

Martin checked the timestamp. "Forty-seven minutes."

"Morrison works fast."

"He's had thirty years to perfect his pitch." Martin pulled up the building schematics again. Studied entry points. Extraction routes. "How long do we give her before we assume compromise?"

"Seventy-two hours. Like we planned." Maya paused. "Unless she signals distress."

"And if she doesn't signal but stops transmitting?"

"Then we activate bioterrorism protocols and burn GenVault to the ground."

Martin stared at the green dots on his screen.

Sarah Chen.

Inside enemy territory.

Gathering intelligence or getting recruited.

Hard to tell the difference from three miles away.

"Good luck, Sarah," he whispered.

On his laptop, both green dots pulsed.

Steady.

Alive.

For now.
