# CHAPTER 3 - THE TRAP

**Three Days After Marsh's Death**
**Martin's Safe House - Vermont**

---

Sarah had been staring at the same document for three hours.

Seventy-eight pages. Single-spaced. Everything Marsh had died for.

She'd titled it "The Aethelred Cipher" because historians loved obscure Anglo-Saxon references and maybe that would get it past the Order's monitoring algorithms for five extra minutes before they found it and buried it and buried her.

(Buried her.)

The cursor blinked at the end of page seventy-eight.

Waiting for her to write the conclusion.

Waiting for her to decide what happened next.

Sarah's coffee had gone cold. Again. She couldn't remember making coffee. Couldn't remember the last time she'd slept. Her apartment key was still in her jacket pocket. She'd felt it there this morning — the brass weight of it, the keyring with the little MIT Beaver charm she'd bought herself at the campus bookstore the day she defended her dissertation. A reward. The only celebration she'd allowed herself. She was carrying the key to an apartment she might never go back to, the way you carry a photograph of a place you used to live.

Three days ago she'd been a person. She'd had office hours on Tuesdays and Thursdays. She'd had a running argument with Professor Okafor about statistical methodology that they'd been conducting by email for two years, neither of them willing to concede, both of them enjoying it more than they'd admit. She'd had a favorite booth at the café on Massachusetts Avenue where she went every Saturday to read papers and eat an almond croissant and pretend she was the kind of person who had Saturday rituals instead of the kind of person who worked seven days a week and called it a life.

That person was gone. Not dead — just unreachable. The distance between Sarah-who-graded-midterms and Sarah-who-was-writing-the-only-evidence-of-planned-genocide was not a distance you crossed back.

Exhaustion sat behind her eyes like a physical weight. *Caffeine crash. Adrenaline withdrawal. The specific tired that came from watching someone's house burn with them inside and realizing the people hunting you had institutional reach and murder on their to-do lists.*

"The exposé is ready," Sarah said.

Martin looked up from the window. He'd been standing there for an hour. Watching the tree line. Rifle propped against the wall within arm's reach. On the windowsill beside him, a creased photograph he carried everywhere—a woman in hiking gear on a mountain trail, laughing at whoever held the camera. He never talked about it. Sarah had learned not to ask.

"And then what?" His voice was flat. Analytical. The tone of someone who'd seen this movie before and knew how it ended. "They deny it. Call it conspiracy theory. We've seen this before. Dr. Lisa Park had evidence three years ago. Published it. The Order buried her story in forty-eight hours."

He paused. Glanced at the photograph on the windowsill.

"Then buried her. Sixth researcher in twenty years. Everyone who gets close to this ends up dead."

Elena had gone quiet through the encrypted phone on the kitchen counter — coordinating with resistance contacts, the encrypted chatter Sarah had learned not to interrupt.

"Lisa didn't have Pattern Eye documentation," Elena said through the speaker. "She didn't have Otto Aris's historical proof. This is different."

"Is it?" Martin turned from the window. Looked at Sarah. "You have documentation. You have proof. What you don't have is hub locations. Deployment protocols. The tactical intelligence that would let us *stop* this instead of just exposing it."

Sarah knew he was right.

(She hated that he was right.)

"Morrison has that intelligence," Sarah said.

The words hung in the air.

Martin nodded. Once. Sharp. Like he'd been waiting for her to say it. "GenVault's Boston headquarters. Morrison runs everything from the third floor. We've known about the facility for six years. Never been able to get inside."

"They'd never let someone infiltrate," Elena said. "Security is—"

"They'd let me in."

Sarah's voice was steady. Calm. The decision crystallizing as she spoke.

"If I contacted Morrison. If I said I was reconsidering cooperation."

Martin's expression didn't change.

(That was worse than if he'd reacted. The stillness meant he'd thought of this. Done the math. Decided whether it was tactical suicide or regular suicide with extra steps.)

"You want to get *captured*." Elena's voice sharpened through the speaker.

"I want to get *inside*," Sarah corrected. "There's a difference."

"Is there?"

Sarah stood. Moved to the window beside Martin. Outside, November was settling into Vermont like it planned to stay. First snow dusting the dead grass. Trees stripped bare against gray sky. A landscape that looked like it had given up pretending warmth was coming back.

"THRESHOLD deployment is coming soon," Sarah continued. "The deployment files show an accelerated timeline. If we publish the exposé now, they'll just deploy early. We prevent nothing."

"But if you're inside Site Seven," Martin said slowly, "with access to their systems..."

"I gather real intelligence. Everything we need to stop THRESHOLD, not just write a convincing article that gets buried under conspiracy theories about lizard people."

"Sarah, once you're in there, we can't extract you." Elena's voice hardened through the phone. "Morrison isn't stupid. He'll know you're gathering intelligence."

"He'll suspect," Sarah agreed. "But he'll also think he can control me." She turned from the window. "Morrison believes humans are predictable. Pattern-able. Optimize the inputs, control the outputs. That's his weakness."

Martin was quiet for a long moment.

Then he opened his laptop. Pulled up surveillance photos of Site Seven—concrete and steel behind razor wire, armed guards at every entrance, the kind of facility that didn't appear on any map.

Sarah moved back to her laptop. Closed the exposé document. Opened a new file. Started typing notes—insertion strategy, intelligence priorities, extraction contingencies that sounded more confident than she felt.

"Marsh spent decades fighting the Order. She died for this evidence." Her voice cracked. Slightly. "I'm not wasting her sacrifice by publishing an exposé that gets buried in two days while THRESHOLD deploys on schedule."

"You'll need a tracking device," Martin said. "Something Morrison won't find."

"And you'll need an exit strategy," Elena added. "Even if you gather intelligence, you're useless if you die in there."

"I'll have an exit strategy," she said. "Once I have the intelligence. Once I know we can stop THRESHOLD."

Martin closed his laptop. "This is insane."

"This is tactical."

"Those aren't mutually exclusive."

"I know."

Sarah returned to her laptop. Opened a new message draft. "Elena, I need you to create a secure email account that looks like it was set up in panic. No sophisticated encryption. Just enough security to look like I'm trying to hide but don't know what I'm doing."

"Already on it." Keyboard sounds through the speaker.

Sarah's phone buzzed.

Maya.

**"Where are you? Need to talk. Urgent."**

Then, before Sarah could respond: **"I'm in Burlington. Drove up this morning. Tell you when I get there."**

Sarah showed the texts to Martin. Read them aloud for Elena.

Martin nodded. "She helped you find the pattern. She should be part of this."

"Sending her location," Elena said.

---

Twenty minutes later, headlights cut through the trees.

Sarah watched from the window as a CDC vehicle pulled into the driveway. Government plates. Official enough to get through checkpoints. Unmarked enough to not draw attention.

Maya Chen stepped out carrying a medical kit.

She looked exhausted. Dark circles under her eyes. Hair pulled back in a ponytail that had seen better days. Tired from too much coffee and not enough sleep and the weight of knowing millions of people were about to die and you were one of maybe five people who could stop it.

"You're planning something insane," Maya said as she entered. Not a question.

"How did you know?" Sarah asked.

"Because I've been monitoring GenVault's communications since the fire." Maya set down her kit. Heavy. The kind of weight that meant serious medical equipment. "Morrison sent encrypted messages to six facility coordinators this morning. Subject line: 'Chen recruitment protocols.' He's expecting you to make contact."

Sarah exchanged a glance with Martin.

"He's that confident?"

"He's that prepared." Maya pulled out her laptop. Opened files. "Which is why you're going to need more than Martin's tracker. Morrison's security will sweep for electronics. Standard GPS devices. Anything broadcasting on normal frequencies."

She opened her medical kit. Inside: two subcutaneous trackers, each the size of a grain of rice.

"CDC biocontainment protocol," she explained. "We use these to track field personnel in outbreak zones. Encrypted transmission — looks like environmental noise. They won't find them unless they're scanning for the exact frequency."

"Two trackers," Martin said. "Redundancy."

"One behind the ear. One on the hip." Maya held up both trackers. "Different frequencies. If Morrison finds one, you still have the other."

Martin went first. Local anesthetic behind Sarah's right ear. Cold spreading through her scalp. Then pressure — sharp, quick, gone. Foreign object sliding under skin. Her fingers gripped the table edge, not from pain but from the *knowledge* of what was happening.

Maya handled the hip. Steadier hands. The injection stung — sharp pain that radiated outward, then faded to a dull ache.

"Both active," Maya confirmed, checking her laptop. Two glowing dots on a map. "Clean signals."

Sarah touched both spots. Behind her ear. On her hip.

"Now," Elena said through the speaker. "How do you make contact?"

"I walk straight into GenVault headquarters in Boston. No meetings — meetings give Morrison time to control the environment. I call their recruitment contact, tell him I'm ready to see the complete picture. Voluntary. Demanding transparency."

"They'll imprison you," Elena warned.

Sarah touched the tracker behind her ear. "Then you know exactly where I am."

Wind outside. Trees creaking. The quiet of a decision that couldn't be unmade.

"When?" Martin asked.

"Tomorrow morning."

---

The next morning, Sarah stood outside GenVault's Boston headquarters.

Glass and steel tower. Forty-three stories. Executive entrance on Causeway Street gleaming with money and indifference. Security visible through the windows—guards with earpieces and suits that didn't quite hide the weapons underneath.

Sarah's hand trembled as she pulled out her phone.

She dialed Jenson's number. GenVault's head of recruitment — the voice on the other end of every offer Morrison ever extended.

The phone rang.

Once.

Twice.

Three times.

Sarah counted her heartbeats. Eighty-two beats per minute. Too fast. Calm people had heartbeats in the sixties. She was in the "about to have a panic attack" range.

(Breathe.)

"Jenson."

His voice. Warm. Professional. The practiced tone of GenVault's recruitment machine.

"Dr. Jenson, it's Sarah Chen."

A pause.

Then something shifted — too eager, too rehearsed. The voice of someone who'd been *expecting* this call. "Dr. Chen. I'm so glad you reached out. Are you somewhere safe?"

"I'm downstairs," Sarah said. "In your lobby. I want to see the complete archive. The original manuscripts. The genetic data. Everything Morrison showed you when he recruited you. I need to verify this independently before I make any decisions."

Silence on the line.

Sarah counted it. Three seconds. Four. Five.

She read the silence. *Surprised. Analyzing. Calculating whether this is genuine or a trap.*

(It was a trap. Just not the trap he thought it was.)

Then: "You're here? Right now?"

"Executive entrance. I'll wait five minutes." Sarah made her voice steady. Calm. The voice of someone who'd thought this through and wasn't panicking. "If someone doesn't come down to escort me up, I walk away and you never hear from me again."

She ended the call.

Stared at her phone.

Her reflection stared back from the glass tower. Twenty-eight years old. Exhausted. Terrified. About to walk into a building full of people who thought genocide was mathematics.

She thought about her apartment. The futon. The pothos plant. The midterms she'd never finish grading — thirty-two undergraduates who would get their papers back from a substitute who didn't know their names, who wouldn't write *good instinct here, but show your work* in the margins the way Sarah did, who wouldn't notice that Jamie Okonkwo in the third row was getting the concepts but freezing on exams and needed someone to tell him that freezing was normal and didn't mean he was stupid.

She thought about Maya's handshake in the library basement. Warm hands. *Partners.* The word that meant she wasn't alone.

She thought about the café on Massachusetts Avenue. The almond croissant. The Saturday ritual she'd never had. The life that was small and good and might have been enough if the world had been smaller and the pattern had stayed hidden.

The apartment key was still in her pocket. She left it there.

Sarah touched the tracker behind her ear. Tapped twice — signal for Martin and Maya that she was moving. That this was happening. That if she disappeared they should assume the worst and activate every contingency.

The lobby doors opened.

A security guard approached. Young. Professional. The kind of person who thought they worked for a legitimate pharmaceutical company and had no idea their job involved protecting genocidal architects.

"Dr. Chen? Dr. Morrison is waiting for you. Third floor."

(Third floor. Morrison. This was it.)

She walked through the doors.

Glass lobby. Marble floors. Corporate elegance that screamed *we have money and power and nothing to hide.*

The elevator was waiting.

Empty.

Just Sarah and a security guard and mirrors on three walls that showed her reflection from every angle—exhausted, terrified, walking into the trap with eyes open because it was the only way to get the intelligence they needed.

The guard pressed the button for the third floor.

Doors closed.

Sarah watched the number change.

(This is a terrible idea. You're going to die. At least Maya will avenge you.)

Two.

The tracker behind her ear felt heavy. Foreign. A reminder that she wasn't alone even though she was about to be alone in a building full of people who thought she was choosing to join them.

Sarah took a deep breath. In. Out. Oxygen was useful.

Three.

The elevator dinged.

Doors opened.

Dr. James Morrison stood in the hallway. Expensive suit. Patient smile. The smile of someone who'd been expecting her and had planned how this would go.

"Dr. Chen," he said. "Welcome to GenVault. I'm so glad you decided to see the complete picture."

"Lead the way," Sarah said.

Morrison gestured down the hallway. Sarah followed.

And somewhere in Vermont, Martin watched her signal move deeper into the building.

And somewhere in a CDC office, Maya prepared bioterrorism protocols just in case.

And somewhere in her chest, Sarah's heart counted beats that might be numbered.

But she was *inside*.
