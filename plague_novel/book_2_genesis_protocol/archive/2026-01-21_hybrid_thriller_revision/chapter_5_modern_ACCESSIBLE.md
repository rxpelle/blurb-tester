# CHAPTER 5 - THE RECORDING

## Modern Timeline - New Hampshire Safe House

### Morning After Escape

The address Yuki had given Sarah led to a farmhouse forty miles north of the Massachusetts border, deep enough into New Hampshire that the roads had stopped being numbered and started having names like "Old Mill Road" and "Founders Way."

Old colonial structure, weathered boards the color of ash, surrounded by forest so dense the nearest house wasn't visible even in winter with the leaves down. The kind of place where someone could disappear completely.

Or where someone could be made to disappear.

Sarah had arrived at dawn after four hours of driving, constantly checking her rearview mirror. Every set of headlights made her heart jump. Every vehicle that stayed behind her for more than two minutes made her change routes, double back, take side roads.

But the farmhouse had been empty when she arrived. Door unlocked. No signs of recent occupation. Just a note on the kitchen table in neat handwriting: *Supplies in the basement. Wait. Trust no one. -Original Network*

Now, twelve hours later, Sarah sat in that basement surrounded by everything she needed to complete her work.

Three laptops arranged on a makeshift desk of plywood and sawhorses, all disconnected from the internet until she was ready. External hard drives, neatly labeled. Encrypted cloud storage credentials written on slips of paper in careful block letters. A satellite internet connection that couldn't be traced to her physical location—or so the instructions claimed. And Marsh's complete archive, organized and digitized across multiple formats.

Everything she needed to finish the exposé that would either save the world or make her look completely insane.

Everything except certainty that she was making the right choice.

Sarah stared at the screens, at the evidence spread across digital folders and physical documents. Seventy years of genetic manipulation documented in corporate memos and research papers. Twelve generations of her own family bred like show dogs. The Genesis Protocol fragments suggesting something even worse coming—population thresholds and triggering events and controlled collapse.

Her phone rang. The new burner she'd bought with cash at a gas station outside Concord. Nobody should have this number.

Unknown number on the display.

Sarah's heart hammered. She answered.

"Dr. Chen." Catherine Wells's voice, smooth and controlled as ever. "I hope you've had time to consider our conversation at the facility."

Sarah closed her eyes, steadied her breathing. This was it. The performance that would determine everything.

"I have."

"And?" A note of genuine curiosity in Wells's voice.

Sarah looked at the evidence one more time. The family trees. The genetic profiles. The twelve generations of manipulation that had produced her.

"I want to meet," Sarah said. "In person. To discuss terms."

Silence on the line. Long enough that Sarah wondered if she'd miscalculated.

Then: "Terms?"

"If I'm going to join you, I need to understand the complete picture. Not fragments. Not carefully edited presentations designed to recruit me. Everything. The full Genesis Protocol. What you're actually building toward. What happens when you hit your threshold."

"That information is classified at the highest levels, Dr. Chen. Board members only—"

"I'm a product of thirty generations of genetic selection, right? You said so yourself. Optimal outcome, all target traits successfully concentrated." Sarah let bitterness creep into her voice—not hard, since it was genuine. "So treat me like it. Show me the complete picture. Let me make an informed decision instead of a blind one."

More silence. Sarah could almost hear Wells thinking, calculating, weighing risk against potential gain.

"There's a restaurant," Wells said finally. "Burlington, Vermont. The Farmhouse Tap & Grill. Public, neutral territory. I can be there in three hours."

Burlington. Ninety minutes from here. Public place.

"I'll be there," Sarah said.

"Come alone. No recording devices. No weapons. No insurance policies." Wells's voice hardened slightly. "Just a conversation between colleagues about a potential collaboration. Anything else and we're done. Understood?"

"Understood," Sarah lied.

She hung up.

Then immediately opened the most sophisticated of the three laptops and began setting up the recording system.

The equipment was elegant in its simplicity. A smartwatch—expensive, the kind a successful geneticist might wear—with modified internals that could record audio and video at broadcast quality, encrypt it in real-time, and stream it to a cloud server via cellular connection.

Even if they searched her and found it, even if they confiscated it and destroyed it, the conversation would already be uploaded. Already safe. Already impossible to suppress completely.

Sarah strapped the watch onto her wrist. Tested the connection. Green light. Streaming active.

She was about to record a confession from the head of a conspiracy to commit genocide.

Or she was about to get herself killed.

Probably both.

## Scene 2: The Restaurant

### Burlington, Vermont, Evening

Sarah arrived forty-five minutes early. Circled the block three times in the rental car she'd paid for with cash. Checked out the restaurant from multiple angles.

The Farmhouse Tap & Grill occupied a brick building on Main Street, renovated industrial look mixed with Vermont rustic. Large windows facing the street showed a busy dinner crowd. Multiple exits visible: front entrance, side door to the patio, back entrance likely through the kitchen.

Public enough that Wells wouldn't try anything overtly violent. Private enough that their conversation wouldn't be overheard.

Perfect.

Sarah parked two blocks away. The October evening was cold, breath misting in the air, leaves crunching underfoot. She wore clothes purchased at a thrift store—nothing GenVault had ever seen her in, nothing that could be tracked.

The recording device sat against her wrist, warm and slightly heavy. She resisted the urge to check it.

Inside, the restaurant smelled like beer and fried food and wood smoke from a fireplace in the corner. Sarah asked for a booth in the back corner—good view of the entrance, back to the wall, quick access to the side exit if needed.

She ordered water. Needed to stay sharp.

Waited.

Wells arrived exactly on time. Same expensive charcoal suit, different silk blouse, pearl earrings catching the light. Same perfect hair pulled back in a French twist. Same aura of absolute control and competence.

She spotted Sarah immediately. Smiled. Walked over like they were old friends meeting for dinner.

"Dr. Chen. Thank you for agreeing to meet." Wells slid into the booth with practiced grace. "I'm glad you reconsidered."

"Dr. Wells." Sarah kept her voice neutral.

A waiter appeared. Wells ordered wine without looking at the menu. "The Côte du Rhône, please. The 2019 if you have it." Then to Sarah: "Are you sure I can't tempt you?"

"Water's fine."

Wells smiled like she understood something Sarah didn't.

The waiter left. Wells settled back, studied Sarah with those calculating eyes.

"Let's skip the pleasantries," Wells said. "You want information. I want your cooperation. Let's see if we can find mutually beneficial terms."

"Start with the Genesis Protocol," Sarah said. "What is it actually trying to accomplish? Not the sanitized version. The truth."

Wells smiled like she'd been waiting for exactly this question.

"What do you know about cyclical civilizational collapse?"

"Broad strokes. Bronze Age collapse around 1200 BCE. Rome's fall in the fifth century. Theories about 600-800 year cycles, though mainstream historians dispute the pattern."

"Mainstream historians are wrong." Wells pulled out a tablet. She brought up a graph. "Major civilizations collapse like clockwork. And it's not random. It's systems failure. Too much complexity, too little resilience. Societies optimize for efficiency until a single shock brings everything down."

The graph showed collapse events going back four thousand years. Bronze Age collapse: 1200 BCE. Roman collapse: 400 CE. Medieval collapse: 1347 CE. Each separated by roughly 600-800 years.

"We're due," Sarah said, seeing the pattern extend to the present.

"Overdue, actually. Should have happened in the 1940s based on the cycle. Would have, except..." Wells zoomed in on the modern section, showed intervention points marked in red. "Except the network has been carefully managing the variables. Preventing the worst shocks. Extending the current cycle beyond its natural endpoint."

"How?"

"Targeted interventions throughout the twentieth century." Wells's voice was matter-of-fact, clinical. "World Wars One and Two? Controlled population reductions that prevented worse conflicts. The Green Revolution? Network-backed research that prevented mass starvation. The AIDS epidemic? We ensured treatment protocols were optimized, that the virus was contained before it reached truly catastrophic levels."

Sarah felt sick. The casual way Wells discussed engineering world wars.

"But we can't prevent the next collapse," Wells continued. "It's already started. Climate change accelerating beyond intervention capacity. Pandemic potential increasing yearly. Resource wars brewing. Financial systems ready to collapse. Political polarization approaching pre-war levels."

She scrolled through more data. Each graph showed approaching tipping points.

"All the markers are present, Dr. Chen. Within a decade, probably less, the current global system will fragment. Billions will die. Not speculation. Mathematical certainty based on nine hundred years of pattern observation."

"So the Genesis Protocol is a survival plan?"

"An optimization protocol." Wells pulled up genetic markers Sarah recognized. Bloodline charts tracking families across continents. "We can't stop the collapse. But we can control who survives it. Ensure that the rebuilt civilization is better than what came before. Stronger. More resilient. Better equipped to avoid the same mistakes."

"Better meaning what?"

"Higher average intelligence. Better disease resistance. Improved neuroplasticity for faster learning. Reduced genetic markers associated with violence and tribalism. Enhanced pattern recognition for complex problem solving." Wells scrolled through the markers. "We've been breeding these traits into target populations for seventy years. Before that, the network did it for nine hundred years using cruder methods."

"That's eugenics." Sarah's voice was flat. "That's genocide with scientific window dressing."

"That's evolution guided by intelligence instead of randomness." Wells met Sarah's eyes, unflinching. "Do you know how many people carry the protected genetic markers now? Globally?"

"I don't."

"Fourteen point three percent. We need fifteen percent for critical mass. Once we hit that threshold, we can proceed to the final phase."

Sarah's blood went cold. "The trigger?"

"A genetic targeting virus." Wells said it like discussing quarterly earnings. "Highly contagious airborne transmission. Fatal to anyone who doesn't carry specific protective markers—the markers we've been breeding into protected populations. Incubation period of seven days to ensure maximum global spread before symptoms appear. Mortality rate approximately ninety-five percent in unprotected populations. Nearly zero percent in protected populations."

The restaurant sounds faded away. The conversations. The clinking of silverware. The laughter. All distant and unreal.

"You're going to kill hundreds of millions of people."

"Phase One field validation targets approximately 400 million casualties," Wells said, her voice steady as if discussing quarterly projections. "It's a controlled deployment to gather empirical data—delivery mechanism performance, government response capabilities, genetic marker reliability across diverse populations. Phase Two implementation follows in five to seven years, using what we learn to optimize the final deployment."

She leaned forward slightly. "The alternative is chaotic collapse—ecosystems failing, supply chains breaking down, resource wars that consume everything. Uncontrolled, everyone dies over the next thirty to fifty years. We manage it scientifically. Phase One validates our models. Phase Two implements controlled population reduction bringing global numbers to sustainable levels. Quick. Clinical. Survivable. It prevents the slow catastrophic collapse that kills everyone."

Sarah couldn't breathe. The magnitude of what Wells was describing...

Wells pulled up a graph showing projected mortality curves. "THRESHOLD Phase One—what will become known publicly as COVID-19—deploys in approximately six weeks. Six hubs, simultaneous release. We project 400 to 410 million deaths over the following four years. It will teach us how populations respond to pandemic conditions, how governments prioritize economics over containment, how quickly emergency measures become normalized background noise."

"You're calling it COVID?" Sarah's voice was hollow.

"COronaVirus Infectious Disease, year 2019. Simple. Medical. Forgettable." Wells's expression remained clinical. "The public will believe it emerged naturally—a zoonotic spillover from wildlife markets. By the time anyone questions that narrative seriously, Phase One data collection will be complete and we'll be optimizing Phase Two protocols."

"You're insane," Sarah managed.

"We're pragmatic." Wells finished her wine, signaled for another. "The network has done this before, Dr. Chen. Bronze Age collapse? We guided the survivors, ensured knowledge preservation. Same with Rome's fall. Each time, the next civilization rose faster, stronger, more advanced, because we ensured the right people with the right traits survived."

"And you think I'll help you with this? With planned genocide?"

"You're already part of it." Wells pulled up Sarah's genetic profile, more detailed than anything she'd seen before. "Your bloodline has been optimized for pattern recognition and analytical thinking for twelve generations. Exactly the traits needed to manage complex systems during reconstruction. You don't have to die in the collapse. You can be part of what comes after."

Sarah felt her watch vibrating slightly against her wrist. So subtle she'd have missed it if she wasn't looking for it. The recording was transmitting. Everything Wells had said was being uploaded in real-time.

She just needed to keep Wells talking. Get everything on record.

"What about consent?" Sarah asked, working to keep her voice steady. "Phase One kills 400 million. Phase Two brings global population to 'sustainable levels.' How many people total are you planning to kill?"

"Phase Two projects 3.2 to 4.1 billion casualties," Wells said matter-of-factly. "Combined with Phase One: approximately four to four and a half billion total. Roughly half of current global population brought to sustainable levels over seven to ten years."

Sarah felt the room tilt. Not 400 million. Not even a billion. Four billion people.

"Did the dinosaurs consent to the asteroid?" Wells accepted her second glass of wine. "This isn't about fairness, Dr. Chen. It's about survival of the species. The current system is unsustainable. We can manage the transition or let it happen chaotically. Those are the only options."

"There's a third option. Stop it. Change the systems that are failing. Fix what's broken instead of burning it all down."

"We tried that." Wells's voice hardened slightly, first real emotion Sarah had heard. "For fifty years, the network tried to prevent collapse through conventional means. Funding climate research. Supporting sustainable development. Encouraging international cooperation. But the problems are too deeply rooted. Too much momentum toward catastrophe. The time for prevention passed decades ago. Now we're in damage control."

She leaned forward slightly. "I'm showing you this because we need people like you. Smart enough to understand the mathematics. Willing to make hard choices. Strong enough to survive the knowledge of what we're doing. Most people would break under this weight. But you won't. Your genetics won't let you."

"Why tell me all this? Why not just kill me like you killed Marsh?"

"Because killing you would be wasteful. You're valuable." Wells smiled. "And because you're recording this conversation. Your watch. Very clever. Good surveillance tech. I'm impressed."

Sarah's stomach dropped.

"I let you record me," Wells continued calmly. "Because I want you to have the evidence. I want you to release it. Tell the world exactly what we're planning."

"Why would you want that?"

"Because no one will believe you." Wells's smile widened. "A conspiracy to reduce population by four billion using genetic targeting viruses? Guided evolution over nine hundred years? Seven secret corporations coordinating the largest mass murder in history?" She laughed. "It sounds insane. You'll be dismissed as a conspiracy theorist. Again."

"The evidence—"

"Can be explained away. All of it." Wells ticked off points on her fingers. "Genetic markers? Natural variation, corporate research into hereditary disease. Population planning? Fertility research helping families conceive. Bloodline tracking? Genealogical studies. The virus? Theoretical pandemic preparedness work."

She finished her second wine. "By the time anyone takes you seriously enough to investigate properly, we'll be at fifteen percent. And then it won't matter. The trigger will have been released."

Sarah's hand was shaking. Wells was right.

The truth was too big. Too horrifying. Too movie-plot to be taken seriously until it was too late.

"I'm giving you a choice, Sarah." Wells stood, preparing to leave. Placed a hundred dollar bill on the table. "Release your recording. Try to warn the world. Spend the next six months being dismissed as crazy while we complete the protocol. Or join us. Help guide what comes after. Be part of the solution instead of dying with the problem."

"That's not a choice."

"It's the only choice anyone ever gets: Accept reality or be destroyed by it." Wells picked up her handbag. "You have forty-eight hours to decide. After that, we begin the final phase. With or without you. I hope you make the right decision. You're too valuable to waste."

She walked out.

Left Sarah sitting in the booth, shaking, the recording device still transmitting.

Sarah sat there for five more minutes. Making sure the recording had finished uploading. Making sure Wells was gone.

She had everything. Wells's full confession. The genetic targeting virus. The fifteen percent threshold. The plan to kill four billion people.

And Wells was right—nobody would believe it.

But Sarah was going to try anyway.

Because the alternative was giving up.

Sarah paid for her water, left the restaurant, and drove back to the farmhouse to upload evidence of the largest conspiracy in human history.

## Scene 3: The Upload

### Safe House Basement, Late Night

Sarah drove back with her mind racing through scenarios.

Wells wanted her to release the recording. Expected her to be dismissed as crazy. Was counting on Sarah's exposure attempt to fail.

Which meant Sarah needed to do more than just release it. She needed to make it undeniable. Overwhelming. Too distributed to suppress.

The moment she reached the basement, she began working with focused intensity.

First: The recording from the restaurant. She uploaded it to five different encrypted cloud services—different providers, different jurisdictions. Sent copies to twenty journalists she'd carefully researched—reputable ones with track records investigating corporate conspiracies, independent ones not embedded in network-controlled media.

Each package was customized. Background on Sarah's credentials. Context from Marsh's research. Specific evidence relevant to that journalist's beat. Instructions for verification.

Then: Marsh's complete archive. Everything about the medieval cipher, the bloodline tracking, the network's nine-hundred-year history. She uploaded it to Wikileaks, Internet Archive, academic research repositories, anywhere that couldn't be easily deleted.

Then: The GenVault data David had stolen. Personnel files showing network members embedded in governments, corporations, media. Financial records proving coordination. Research files about the genetic targeting virus.

She created packages. Customized for different audiences. Scientists got genetic data with verification protocols. Journalists got human interest stories. Historians got medieval manuscripts with translation keys. Activists got corporate conspiracy evidence with action items.

Everything. All of it. Released at once across multiple platforms using time-delayed uploads so even if they stopped her, even if they killed her, the information would keep releasing.

At 2 AM, Sarah's finger hovered over the final upload button.

Once she pressed it, there was no taking it back. Every intelligence agency, every corporation, every person with internet access would be able to see what she'd found.

GenVault would know immediately. Would come for her. Would probably kill her.

But the information would be out. Scattered across hundreds of servers. Impossible to completely suppress.

Sarah thought about Marsh, bleeding out while GenVault cleaned up. About Yuki, captured helping Sarah escape. About twelve generations of ancestors bred like livestock. About four billion people who would die if nobody stopped this genocide the network had designed.

She pressed upload.

And watched as the data began distributing across the internet like a virus.

Status bars filled. Upload complete. Upload complete. Upload complete.

Eighteen different platforms. Twenty-seven journalists. Five Wikileaks mirrors. Three academic journals. Twelve independent news sites. Four government whistleblower portals.

Done.

Sarah sat back, exhausted, hands shaking.

Her phone rang. The burner phone that nobody should have the number for.

She answered.

"Dr. Chen." Male voice, unfamiliar, digitally distorted. "You just made a very brave and very stupid decision."

"Who is this?"

"Someone who's been watching you. Someone who's been waiting for you to do exactly what you just did." The voice paused. "You've just declared war on the most powerful conspiracy in human history. They will come for you. Tonight. Within the hour."

"Let them come. The information is out. They can kill me but they can't stop it from spreading."

"You're not thinking clearly about second-order effects. You just told them exactly where you are."

Sarah froze. "What?"

"The uploads. They're being traced right now. GenVault has people inside every major tech company. Your satellite connection shows this farmhouse's location. You have maybe thirty minutes before an extraction team arrives."

"Shit."

"Run. Now. Destroy the equipment. Take only what you can carry. We'll contact you when it's safe."

"Who ARE you?"

"The people who remember what the network was supposed to be. Before it became what you're fighting." The voice softened. "You did well, Dr. Chen. Better than anyone has in decades. But you need to survive the next twenty-four hours. Go. Now. Run."

The line went dead.

Sarah didn't hesitate. Grabbed a hammer from the workshop. Smashed the laptops. Glass screens splintering, plastic cracking. Smashed the hard drives. Smashed the satellite modem. Destroyed anything that could contain data or tracking.

Kept only her burner phone and a flash drive with encrypted copies. Insurance. Backup.

Upstairs, she heard it. Vehicles approaching. Multiple engines. Moving fast.

They were here.

Sarah ran for the back door, grabbed the emergency bag she'd prepared. Out into the October night, into the forest, into darkness and cold and the unknown.

Behind her, doors crashed open. Shouts. Flashlights.

But she was already gone.

## Scene 4: The Pursuit

### New Hampshire Forest, Night

Sarah crashed through the forest like an animal fleeing predators, branches whipping her face, roots trying to trip her, her own breathing loud and ragged.

Behind her: "Basement clear!" "Kitchen clear!" "Subject fled on foot, heading northeast!"

Flashlight beams cutting through the trees like searchlights.

She'd left thirty seconds before the extraction team breached the front. Thirty seconds. The difference between escape and a black site interrogation room.

Sarah ran without direction. Just away. Away from the lights, the voices, the coordinated search pattern organizing behind her.

She'd never been good at outdoors survival. Her childhood was libraries and laboratories. Her adulthood was research facilities and university offices. She didn't know how to navigate by stars or build shelter or move silently through wilderness.

She just ran.

Five minutes. Ten. Fifteen.

Her lungs burned. Her legs screamed. She was running on pure adrenaline and terror.

Behind her, the pursuit continued. They had night vision goggles—she could tell from the way they moved. They had radios—she could hear static bursts. They had coordination—multiple teams spreading out to surround her.

She had panic and desperation and twelve generations of genetically optimized pattern recognition that let her see gaps in their search grid.

Sarah stumbled into a stream. Ice-cold water shocked through her shoes, soaked her jeans, numbed her feet. She kept running, splashing upstream, remembering something from a movie about dogs losing scent in water.

The voices behind her were getting quieter. More distant.

Was she losing them?

Sarah climbed out of the stream, kept moving. Her wet shoes squelched with every step. She was freezing, soaked, exhausted.

But alive.

She found a fallen tree—massive oak brought down by some storm, roots torn from earth, creating a hollow underneath. Sarah crawled into it. Pressed herself into dead leaves and mud and tried not to breathe too loud.

Flashlight beams swept past. Fifty feet away.

"Grid four clear. Moving to grid five."

"Copy. Grid six clear. No thermal signatures."

Thermal. They had infrared. The tree's bulk might be blocking her heat signature, but if they swept directly over her...

Sarah didn't breathe.

The flashlight beam swept closer. Forty feet. Thirty. Twenty.

Then paused.

"Hold position. Possible contact at bearing two-seven-zero."

Sarah's heart hammered.

Footsteps approaching.

Then a radio crackle. "All teams, priority call. Shift to grid nine. Possible vehicle departure on access road three."

"Copy. Moving to grid nine."

The footsteps stopped. Turned. Moved away.

The flashlight beams swept past in a different direction.

Then nothing. Silence.

Sarah waited. Ten minutes. Twenty. Half an hour.

Nothing.

Her burner phone vibrated. Text message from unknown number:

*North. Two miles. Road. Black sedan. Will wait 15 minutes. Come alone.*

Sarah looked at the message. Could be the mysterious voice. Could be GenVault pretending to help.

Could be her only chance.

She checked the compass app. Found north. Started walking.

Two miles felt like twenty. Every sound was a threat. Every shadow could be someone waiting.

But finally, she saw it. A road. Narrow, rural, barely maintained. And a black sedan parked on the shoulder, engine running but lights off.

Sarah approached slowly. Ready to run if this was wrong.

The passenger window rolled down.

"Get in, Dr. Chen. Quickly. We don't have much time."

The voice matched the one from the phone. Male, middle-aged, professionally calm.

Sarah got in.

The car pulled away immediately, no lights until they were half a mile down the road. Then headlights flicked on and they were just another vehicle on a rural highway at 3 AM.

The driver was in his fifties. Gray hair cut short, weathered face, professional manner. Dressed like he'd just left an office job—khakis, button-down shirt, fleece jacket.

"Who are you?" Sarah asked, still breathing hard, soaked and muddy and probably in shock.

"Someone who's been fighting GenVault longer than you've been alive." He glanced at her. "You can call me Martin. Not my real name, but it'll do. Are you hurt?"

"No. Just... everything hurts."

"That's the adrenaline crash. You'll feel worse in an hour." Martin handed her a bottle of water and a thermal blanket from the backseat. "Your upload worked. It's spreading across the internet faster than they can suppress it. Major news outlets are picking it up. You actually did it."

"Did what?"

"Broke the story they've been suppressing for seventy years. Exposed what hundreds of people tried and failed to expose." Martin smiled grimly. "It won't stop them—they're too far along. But it'll slow them down. Make the final phase harder to execute quietly. That's more than anyone else has managed."

"Wells said no one would believe me."

"Wells underestimated how much evidence you had. And how many people have been waiting for proof." Martin turned onto a highway, heading south. "The original network—the uncorrupted one—has been preserving evidence for decades. But we couldn't release it ourselves. We needed someone outside the conspiracy. Someone like you."

Sarah was too tired to process this. Too cold and wet and exhausted. "Where are we going?"

"Safe house. Real one this time. Not compromised. You'll rest, recover, and then we'll discuss next steps."

"Next steps?"

"You've exposed part of the conspiracy. Now we stop the rest of it." Martin looked at her. "The genetic targeting virus. Project THRESHOLD. It's real. And they're planning to release it in six months. Probably sooner now that you've forced them to accelerate."

"How do we stop them?"

"That's the question." Martin drove in silence. "The original Genesis Protocol—created during the Bronze Age Collapse, 1177 BCE—contains the answer. But it's been fragmented across seven keys. We have pieces. They have pieces. No one has the complete picture."

"The keys," Sarah said, remembering Marsh's research. "Seven bronze keys Nefertari created."

"Exactly. Thomas distributed fragments of the CORRUPTED protocol in 1347—the offensive breeding records. But the original DEFENSIVE protocol? The prevention methods? Those were deliberately hidden and suppressed for three millennia. Somewhere in the complete defensive protocol is information on how to stop what they've built."

Sarah felt exhaustion pulling at her. "If there's a way to stop them in the protocol, why would they build it in the first place?"

"Because they didn't build it to create what GenVault is doing. They built it to prevent exactly what GenVault is doing." Martin's voice was grim. "Nefertari created a system to encode pattern recognition—teach future generations to SEE collapse coming and intervene. But Amenhotep's faction corrupted it immediately. Turned prevention into engineering. Three thousand years of corruption. Now we need to recover what Nefertari actually created."

"How?"

"We find someone who can read the complete protocol. Someone with the genetic markers and training to access it. Someone who carries ancestral memory encoded in their DNA." Martin glanced at her. "Someone like you."

Sarah stared. "What?"

"Your bloodline, Dr. Chen. Twelve generations of genetic optimization. You don't just carry markers for pattern recognition. You carry markers for accessing genetic memory. Ancestral knowledge encoded in your DNA."

"That's not possible. Genetic memory is pseudoscience—"

"It's real. Proven in classified research. Suppressed because the network doesn't want people knowing it exists." Martin pulled into a rest stop, parked in a dark corner. "You're exhausted. Sleep. You're safe. I promise."

Sarah wanted answers. Wanted to understand.

But exhaustion won. Her eyes closed.

And she slept in the passenger seat while the internet exploded with her revelations, while GenVault scrambled to contain the damage, while the world began to learn about the conspiracy that had been guiding human evolution for nine hundred years.
