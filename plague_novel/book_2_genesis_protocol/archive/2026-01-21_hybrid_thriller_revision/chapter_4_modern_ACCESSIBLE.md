# CHAPTER 4 - THE FACILITY

## Modern Timeline - Day After Capture

### Unknown Location - Time Unknown

Sarah woke to white.

White ceiling tiles with barely visible seams. White walls with that particular eggshell texture that screamed institutional. White sheets on a bed too comfortable to be a prison but too sterile to be a hotel.

Her head felt thick, stuffed with cotton. The inside of her mouth tasted like copper and chemicals. Drugged. They'd drugged her during transport.

She sat up slowly, fighting the dizziness that made the room tilt sideways. Her vision blurred, doubled, then steadied. The nausea passed, leaving a dull ache behind her eyes.

Small room. Maybe ten by twelve feet. Hospital-grade mattress. Modern desk. Comfortable chair. A door with a handle but no visible lock on this side. A bathroom visible through an open doorway—sink, toilet, shower. Everything clean, modern, sterile.

No windows.

The absence hit like a physical blow. No natural light. No way to tell where she was or what time it was. No connection to the outside world.

Sarah's pulse jumped. She stumbled to the door, legs unsteady. Grabbed the handle, twisted.

Locked from the outside.

She was in a cell. A comfortable cell with good lighting and clean sheets, but a cell nonetheless.

How long have I been unconscious?

She checked herself with shaking hands. Still wearing the clothes from the cabin—jeans, hoodie, both wrinkled and stale. No watch. No phone. Her hands were clean, unmarked. No bruises on her wrists. They hadn't hurt her. Just transported her somewhere while unconscious and locked her in a comfortable room to wake up alone.

Sarah's mind raced through possibilities. What time was it? What day? Had midnight passed? Had her document released? Was it spreading across the internet, exposing GenVault and the conspiracy?

Or had they stopped it somehow?

And David—was David alive?

She went to the bathroom, turned on the tap. Water ran clear and cold. She splashed it on her face, gasping at the shock. Looked at herself in the mirror.

Same face. Same dark circles under her eyes, maybe deeper now. Same woman who'd spent four days writing an exposé to save the world.

But was the world even saveable?

Back in the main room, she examined every surface for cameras. Found them immediately—not hidden, installed openly in the upper corners. Professional surveillance cameras with tiny red lights. They wanted her to know she was being watched.

Fine. Let them watch.

Sarah sat at the desk. On its surface: fresh clothes folded neatly in her size, toiletries still in packaging, a tray with food covered by a metal dome. The food smelled good—eggs, toast, fruit. Her stomach growled despite everything.

And a folder.

Manila folder, unlabeled. Thick, maybe two inches of papers inside.

She shouldn't open it. Obviously bait. They wanted her off-balance and confused when they came to talk to her.

She looked anyway.

Inside: her complete family tree.

Twelve generations, printed on connected pages that unfolded like an accordion. Names, dates, locations, connections drawn in careful lines. Her great-great-great-grandmother born in Guangdong, 1847. Her great-great-grandfather coming to California, 1869. Each generation carefully documented, each marriage noted, each child's birth and death recorded with archival precision.

But that wasn't the disturbing part.

The disturbing part was the annotations.

Next to each name, in small precise handwriting—different hands across different generations, all sharing the same clinical detachment:

Chen Wei-Lin (1847-1893) - Pattern recognition: High. Spatial memory: Exceptional. Musical aptitude: Notable. Selected for breeding program. Matched with Liu Hong-Zhang (mathematical processing markers).

Chen Huang (1869-1941) - Analytical processing: Superior. Mathematical aptitude: 98th percentile. Visual-spatial integration: Excellent. Paired with Zhang Li-Mei (compatible genetic markers, eidetic memory present in bloodline).

Chen Robert (1923-1987) - All target cognitive traits present. Optimal pairing: Chen-Liu bloodline merger. Three offspring produced, two successful.

On and on, generation after generation. Every marriage carefully noted and apparently guided. Every child's traits evaluated like livestock at auction. Branches of the family tree that "failed to produce desired markers" marked with red lines—terminated from program.

Terminated.

What did that mean? Just removed from selective breeding consideration? Or something worse?

Sarah's hands shook as she followed the tree forward to her own generation.

Chen Michael (1965-2014) - Cognitive markers: All present. Spatial reasoning: 99th percentile. Pattern recognition: Exceptional. Eidetic memory: Confirmed. Paired with Williams Rebecca (European bloodline, analytical processing superior, mathematical markers present).

Her parents. Their marriage carefully planned, annotated, approved by people they'd never met.

And then:

Chen Sarah (1991-) - Pattern recognition: Exceptional. Spatial reasoning: 99th percentile. Eidetic memory markers: Present. Analytical processing: Superior. Mathematical aptitude: 98th percentile. Cognitive flexibility: High.

OPTIMAL OUTCOME. All target traits successfully concentrated after 12 generations of guided breeding. Recommend for advanced program placement.

She was going to be sick.

Sarah dropped the folder like it had burned her, stumbled to the bathroom, bent over the toilet. Nothing came up but bile—she hadn't eaten in who knew how long. Her stomach clenched, empty and aching.

She was a product. A designed outcome. Twelve generations of controlled breeding had produced her the way you'd breed dogs for specific traits.

Her mother hadn't chosen her father because she loved him. Or maybe she had loved him—maybe the love itself had been real—but they'd been put together deliberately. Same graduate program, same lab, given opportunities to meet and collaborate until attraction became inevitable.

Even Sarah's own choices—MIT instead of Stanford. Genetics instead of medicine. The specific PhD program focused on population genetics and evolutionary biology—had any of it been her choice? Or had she been subtly guided her entire life toward exactly this field, this knowledge, this moment?

Is anything about me real?

The question echoed in the white-tiled bathroom.

Are my thoughts my own? Or am I programmed like software, executing code written into my genes generations before I was born?

A knock at the door.

Sarah wiped her mouth with shaking hands, stood on legs that felt like water.

The door opened.

Dr. Catherine Wells entered, looking exactly as polished as she had at the Vermont cabin. Expensive charcoal suit, perfect hair pulled back in a French twist, calm expression that suggested she'd just come from a pleasant board meeting instead of overseeing a kidnapping.

"Good morning, Dr. Chen." Her voice was warm, almost friendly. "I see you've been reading your file."

## Scene 2: Wells's Offer

Sarah wanted to attack her. Wanted to scream, to throw the folder in her face, to do something violent to break through that calm exterior.

Instead she said, voice hoarse: "What time is it?"

Wells smiled like Sarah had asked a perfectly reasonable question. "Eleven AM. Thursday. You've been unconscious for approximately eighteen hours. The sedative was quite gentle—no side effects beyond mild grogginess."

Thursday. Past midnight. The document had released. Or should have.

"Where's David?"

"Your cousin is at home in Cambridge. Probably at work right now, actually—he's quite dedicated to his teaching schedule." Wells sat in the chair, gestured for Sarah to sit on the bed. Sarah remained standing, swaying slightly. "He's fine, Dr. Chen. We gave him a mild sedative, kept him unconscious during transport back to his apartment, and he woke up this morning thinking he'd had too much wine and fallen asleep on his couch. We're not monsters."

"You're eugenicists. You've been breeding humans like livestock for generations."

"We've been carefully guiding human evolution, yes." Wells crossed her legs, perfectly at ease. "But isn't that what we've always done, really? Farmers select the best crops for the next season. Breeders select the best animals to strengthen bloodlines. Every culture in history has encouraged marriages between 'good families.' Why should the modern era be different?"

"Because humans aren't crops. We're not animals to be bred for traits."

"Aren't we?" Wells pulled out a tablet from her briefcase. "Let me show you something, Dr. Chen. Something I think you'll appreciate as a scientist."

She brought up a graph. Global temperature over time, the upward curve accelerating sharply in the last fifty years. "Climate change. Accelerating beyond our ability to control it. Even with aggressive action, we're locked into at least 2.5 degrees of warming."

Another graph. "Antibiotic resistance. Pandemic potential increasing yearly. We're one mutation away from a disease we can't treat spreading globally."

Another. "Resource depletion. Water scarcity affecting three billion people by 2030. Topsoil degradation reducing crop yields. Peak phosphorus approaching."

Graph after graph, each one showing a world approaching multiple catastrophic tipping points at once. Sarah recognized the data—she'd seen versions of it in academic journals, had factored it into her own research on population genetics.

"We're heading toward collapse, Dr. Chen. Not in centuries. Not even in decades. In years." Wells set the tablet down. "Do you know what happens when civilizations collapse?"

"People die," Sarah said flatly.

"Ninety percent or more. That's the historical average." Wells's voice was calm, clinical, like discussing weather patterns. "Bronze Age collapse: estimated 90% population reduction across the Eastern Mediterranean. Rome's fall: 80-90% loss in affected regions. Mayan civilization: nearly total collapse."

She stood, walked slowly toward where Sarah stood by the wall.

"But some survive. The question that's faced every major collapse is: which ones? Left to chance, the survivors are random. Luck of location. Access to resources. Genetic resistance to whatever plague is spreading. Pure chaos."

Wells stopped a few feet away, maintaining careful distance. "But guided, Dr. Chen... guided, we can ensure the survivors are the best of us. The smartest, strongest, most adaptable. The ones who can rebuild civilization instead of descending into barbarism."

"That's genocide."

"That's pragmatism." Wells's eyes were steady, unflinching. "Your document released last night. At midnight, exactly as you planned."

Sarah's breath caught. "It did?"

"Yes. Would you like to see the results?" Wells offered the tablet.

Sarah took it with trembling hands. Searched for her exposé, the carefully sourced article that had taken four days of sleepless work.

Found it immediately. Dozens of articles, thousands of shares.

Her heart leaped—

Then plummeted as she read the headlines:

"Disturbed Scientist's Conspiracy Theory Goes Viral"

"GenVault Corporation Addresses Baseless Accusations of Genetic Manipulation"

"Former Employee's Mental Health Crisis Raises Questions About Corporate Oversight"

Her carefully sourced, meticulously documented exposé—complete with primary source documents from Marsh's archive—was being treated as the ravings of a paranoid conspiracy theorist.

Major outlets: dismissing it outright or treating it as entertainment, something to share for shock value before scrolling to the next story.

Independent journalists: some had tried to verify her claims, but within hours they'd posted retractions, apologies, statements that they'd been misled by fake documents.

Her evidence. Her proof. Everything Marsh had died for, everything David had been drugged for, everything she'd sacrificed—dismissed, discredited, buried under an avalanche of mockery and professional skepticism.

"How?" The word came out as a whisper.

"Seventy years of preparation, Dr. Chen." Wells took the tablet back gently. "We have board members at every major publication. Legal teams ready to bury anyone who publishes in court battles. Relationships with fact-checkers and verification services. Partnerships with social media platforms to flag 'misinformation.' And most importantly, we control enough of the media landscape that we can shape the story before it even begins to spread."

She pulled up another screen. "But let's say, hypothetically, that you'd succeeded. Let's say the public believed you. What then?"

"They'd stop you."

"How?" Wells tilted her head, genuinely curious. "Shut down seven major corporations employing millions of people worldwide? Arrest everyone involved—thousands of researchers, executives, board members? Undo generations of genetic guidance? Un-marry the couples we helped bring together? Delete the children who've already been born?"

She shook her head slowly. "The work is done, Dr. Chen. Has been for decades. The bloodlines are already positioned. The genetic markers are already distributed through target populations across six continents. Even if you exposed us completely, even if every word you wrote was believed by every person on earth, you couldn't stop what's coming."

"The collapse," Sarah said.

"The collapse." Wells confirmed. "Within six months, a series of cascading failures will begin. Climate disaster triggers crop failures. Crop failures trigger economic collapse. Economic collapse triggers pandemic. Pandemic triggers war. The dominoes are already falling. We didn't start this—we're just ensuring controlled outcomes."

Sarah's mind was reeling, trying to find something solid to hold onto. "You're going to let billions die."

"We're going to ensure millions survive. The right millions." Wells's voice was gentle now, almost kind. "Dr. Chen—Sarah. You're angry. I understand. You feel manipulated, violated, robbed of free will and agency. Those feelings are completely valid."

"But?"

"But consider what you are. Not just your genes—though those are extraordinary. Your mind. Your capacity for pattern recognition that let you see connections in Marsh's research that she herself had missed. Your drive to find truth even when it's dangerous. Your unwillingness to accept comfortable lies."

Wells smiled. "Those traits aren't weaknesses. They're gifts. Gifts from thirty generations of careful selection. You exist because we helped ensure the right genetic combinations would occur. Without us, you might never have been born at all."

"I exist because my ancestors were bred like horses."

"You exist because we valued intelligence over chance. Because we believed humanity could be better than random genetic drift." Wells leaned forward slightly. "Would you prefer to not exist? To not have your mind, your capabilities? To be someone else, someone... less?"

It was a trap question. Sarah knew it was a trap.

But she couldn't answer.

Because the truth was complicated. She loved her mind. Loved being able to see patterns, to understand complex systems, to think through problems that stumped others. Her intelligence had defined her since childhood. Skipping grades. Academic scholarships. PhD at twenty-five.

Who would she be without those traits?

Would she be anyone at all?

"I'm offering you a choice," Wells said. "Join us. Help complete the work. Use your gifts—the gifts we helped give you—to help select and position the next generation. When the collapse comes, and it is coming, you and your family will be protected. Safe zones, resources, medical care. You'll survive. You'll help rebuild."

"And if I refuse?"

"Then you're a disturbed former employee who stole corporate data and descended into paranoid delusions. We'll keep you here—comfortable, safe, but contained—until the collapse makes your resistance irrelevant." Wells walked to the door, paused with her hand on the handle. "You have twenty-four hours to decide, Dr. Chen. I suggest you use them wisely."

The door closed. Locks engaged with a soft electronic click.

Sarah stood alone in her comfortable cell, staring at the folder full of her designed existence.

## Scene 3: The Ally

Sarah didn't sleep.

Couldn't sleep.

She paced the small room, ten feet one direction, twelve the other, mind racing through everything Wells had said.

The collapse was coming. That much seemed objectively true—the data Wells had shown matched projections Sarah had seen in academic journals. Multiple systems approaching failure at the same time, feedback loops accelerating beyond human capacity to stop.

But the breeding program. The controlled evolution. The planned culling of "inferior" genetic lines.

That was monstrous.

Wasn't it?

At 2 PM—according to the clock on the tablet Wells had left behind, probably intentionally—Sarah sat at the desk and opened the folder again. Studied her family tree more carefully this time, forcing herself to read past the horror to the actual information.

Her great-grandmother had died at thirty-two. The file said childbirth complications. But the notation beside her name said: Failed to produce desired cognitive markers in offspring. Terminated from program. Subject deceased 1924.

Terminated.

Subject deceased.

Had she really died of complications? Or had the network... helped? An infection allowed to spread. Medical care delayed.

Her grandfather—her mother's father—had died in a car accident when Sarah was five. She remembered him vaguely. Kind eyes. Told stories about China, about crossing the ocean. The notation beside his name: Optimal breeding outcome achieved. Three children, all carrying target markers. No further genetic value. Removed from program. Subject deceased 1996.

Removed.

Subject deceased.

The car accident. Single vehicle, late at night, lost control on a curve he'd driven hundreds of times.

Had it been an accident?

Sarah felt sick again, but this time pushed through it. She needed to think. Needed to understand.

How many of her family members had died not from chance but from being deemed genetically redundant?

The tablet was still on the desk. Sarah picked it up, turned it on. Probably monitored. Probably tracked. But it had internet access.

She started searching.

Her exposé: everywhere. Trending on multiple platforms. But the commentary...

"This is what happens when smart people have mental breaks"

"Obviously fake documents, look at the 'medieval' handwriting lol"

"GenVault should sue her into oblivion"

Comments sections full of people calling her crazy, pointing out "obvious fakes," sharing memes mocking her claims. The narrative was set. She was a conspiracy theorist. Nothing she said would ever be believed.

She searched for David's social media.

Found his accounts. Recent posts from this morning—photos of coffee and a student paper that made him laugh. A joke about academic conferences.

Normal. Casual. Exactly like David.

Like nothing had happened.

Either he genuinely didn't remember—the sedative had erased the last twelve hours from his memory—or they'd done something worse.

Sarah didn't know which possibility was worse.

She searched for Evelyn Marsh's car accident. Found the police report. December 1987. Single vehicle accident on Route 2 outside Boston. Dr. Marsh survived with major injuries. Passenger Tanaka Keiko, visiting research fellow from Japan, died on scene.

Tanaka Keiko.

Sarah searched the name. Found an obituary. Age forty-three. Survived by husband and daughter. Researcher in population genetics. Multiple publications on genetic drift in isolated populations.

Why hadn't Marsh mentioned her? In all the documents, all the notes, she'd never once mentioned a research partner who died.

Unless it was too painful. Unless she blamed herself.

Unless mentioning the name would have put the daughter at risk.

Sarah went back to Marsh's research, stored in her encrypted email—which of course they had access to.

She opened the files. Read through Marsh's decades of work again, this time looking for patterns instead of content.

And found something she'd missed before.

Contradictions.

In the oldest manuscripts—the ones from the 14th and 15th centuries—the cipher marks followed one system. Consistent. Mathematical. Precise.

But in the later manuscripts—17th, 18th, 19th centuries—the system had changed. Subtle variations. Errors that wouldn't be noticeable unless you were looking for them specifically.

Like the knowledge had been corrupted over time.

Or deliberately altered.

Marsh had noted this in a document labeled WARNING in all caps:

The Order may have corrupted their own protocol. Evidence suggests original intent was DIFFERENT from current implementation. Need to find earlier sources—Bronze Age origins? Need complete Genesis Protocol. Current network interpretation may be fundamentally flawed.

Sarah sat back, mind racing.

What if the original defensive network—the one that started during the Bronze Age Collapse, three thousand years ago—had truly been about preventing collapse? About teaching humanity to recognize the patterns?

But over millennia, as the knowledge passed through generations, as the Order seized control and reinterpreted the markers, the purpose had shifted. From prevention to exploitation. From saving civilization to selecting it. From protecting humanity to culling it.

What if the modern corporations weren't continuing the original mission?

What if they'd corrupted it entirely?

A knock at the door. Different pattern than Wells's confident rap. Tentative. Three quick taps, pause, two more.

Sarah looked up.

The door opened slowly. Not Wells. A young woman in a lab coat—Asian, maybe thirty, nervous energy radiating from her like heat.

"Dr. Chen?" She whispered, glancing behind her. "I don't have much time. Five minutes, maybe less."

The woman slipped inside, closed the door most of the way but left it cracked slightly—listening for approaching footsteps.

"Who are you?" Sarah asked, standing slowly.

"Dr. Yuki Tanaka. I work in GenVault's genetics division. East Asian bloodline program." She spoke quickly, words tumbling over each other. "I've been watching you since they brought you in. Read your exposé when it posted. Started doing my own research into the archives."

"Why are you here?"

"Because I found something. Something they don't want anyone to see." Yuki pulled a flash drive from her pocket, held it up, then put it back. "I can't give this to you—they'll know I was here, they'll search you. But I needed to tell someone. You're the only person who might understand. The only person who tried to stop them."

Sarah's heart was racing. "Tell me what?"

"The collapse they're planning. The pandemic that'll kill billions. It's not theoretical. It's not a natural disaster they're preparing for." Yuki's voice shook. "They're going to cause it. Deliberately. They've already developed the bioweapon."

The room seemed to tilt.

"What?"

"Project THRESHOLD. I found the files buried in the secure server. It's a genetic targeting virus—highly contagious, kills anyone who doesn't carry specific protective markers. The markers they've been breeding into target populations for seventy years." Yuki was almost hyperventilating now. "They're waiting to hit 15% global coverage of the protected population. Then they release it. Kill everyone else. Reset civilization with only the 'optimal' genetic stock."

"That's genocide. Mass murder. They can't—"

"They can. They will. They're at 14.7% coverage now according to last week's briefing. Maybe six months until threshold." Yuki glanced at the door, checked her watch. "I have maybe two minutes before shift change ends and someone notices I'm not at my station. I'm telling you this because you tried to stop them. Because you're still trying, even though they captured you."

"What can I do? I'm locked in here. They control the media, they have David, they—"

"I can get you out."

Sarah stared. "What?"

"Tonight. Shift change at 2 AM. Minimal security—they rotate staff then. I have access codes for this wing." Yuki pulled out a piece of paper from her other pocket, pressed it into Sarah's hand. Handwritten address. "This is a safe house in New Hampshire. The original network."

"The original—what?"

"Not everyone in the network is corrupt, Dr. Chen. Some of us still remember what it was supposed to be. Preservation, not genocide. Knowledge, not control." Yuki glanced at the door again. "My grandmother worked with Dr. Marsh in the '80s. They tried to expose the corruption then. The car accident that killed her? My grandmother was in that car. Tanaka Keiko. She died. Marsh barely survived."

Yuki pulled a photograph from her pocket. Black and white, faded. Two women—young Evelyn Marsh, maybe forty, and an Asian woman of similar age, both standing in front of a university building, both smiling. Friends.

"That's her. Tanaka Keiko. My grandmother." Yuki's eyes were bright with unshed tears. "She died trying to expose what they'd become. I'm finishing what she started."

"If you help me escape, they'll know it was you."

"I know."

"They'll kill you. Or worse."

"I know that too." Yuki straightened, meeting Sarah's eyes. "But some things are worth the risk. Seventy years of genocide disguised as evolution. Billions about to die. If I can help stop that, even a little..." She shrugged. "My grandmother would have done it. So can I."

Sarah looked at the address. New Hampshire. Remote location. Could be real. Could be another trap.

But what choice did she have?

"How?" Sarah asked.

"Shift change at 2 AM. I'll come for you. I have access codes for this wing—I'm authorized, I work late all the time. We go through the research levels. Down to the parking garage. I'll give you keys to my car. You drive north." Yuki touched the paper. "That address. They'll help you. They have pieces of the original Protocol. The real one, before it was corrupted."

"Who are 'they'?"

"Network members who split off fifty years ago. Went underground. Preserved the old texts, the uncorrupted cipher marks from before the 17th century. They've been trying to piece together what the Genesis Protocol actually says." Yuki checked her watch. "One minute. I have to go."

"Yuki—"

"Don't thank me. Just stop them. Stop the killing." Yuki opened the door, checked the corridor. Clear. "2 AM. Be ready. Wear the dark clothes they left you. Move quietly."

She slipped out.

The door closed. Locks engaged.

Sarah stood alone, holding an address in New Hampshire and the possibility of hope.

Or another trap.

She looked at the family tree still spread across the desk. Twelve generations of controlled breeding. Hundreds of people whose lives had been guided, manipulated, terminated.

And somewhere, maybe, people who remembered what the network was supposed to be.

Sarah folded the address into her pocket and started preparing.

## Scene 4: The Escape

Sarah didn't sleep. Spent the hours until 2 AM preparing mentally.

She changed into the dark clothes they'd left her—black jeans, dark gray sweater. Comfortable running shoes that fit perfectly because of course they knew her size. They knew everything about her. Twelve generations of data.

No plan. No backup. Just trust that this young woman she'd never met would actually come back. Would actually help her escape.

At 1:58 AM, she heard footsteps in the corridor. Different rhythm than the guards who'd passed periodically. Quicker. Nervous.

The door opened.

Yuki, in lab coat over casual clothes, carrying a tablet like she belonged there. Dark circles under her eyes. "Now. We go now."

Sarah followed her into a clean white corridor. Fluorescent lights, too bright for the hour. Multiple doors, all closed and numbered.

"Walk like you belong here," Yuki muttered. "We're just researchers working late. This happens all the time. Act bored."

They passed a security checkpoint. Guard behind bulletproof glass, barely glanced up from his phone as Yuki scanned her badge. Door clicked open with an electronic buzz.

Into another corridor—this one with windows into laboratories. Sarah glimpsed genetics equipment behind the glass. Breeding tanks for cell cultures. Computer stations analyzing vast datasets. Charts on walls tracking bloodlines across continents.

"This is the bloodline tracking center," Yuki whispered. "East Asian program. We monitor forty thousand individuals across six countries."

Forty thousand people. Controlled like livestock. Guided toward genetic outcomes they'd never know about.

Another checkpoint. Yuki's badge worked again. The guard—different one, older—nodded to her. "Working late again, Dr. Tanaka?"

"Always." Yuki smiled, tired but natural. "Grant deadline."

"Good luck."

Through the checkpoint. Down a different corridor, this one leading to stairs.

"Service stairs," Yuki breathed. "Cameras but no guards. Keep your head down."

Down concrete stairs—industrial, harsh lighting, echoing footsteps. Sarah's heart hammered so loud she was sure someone would hear it. One floor, two, three.

Into the parking garage.

Concrete and shadows and the smell of oil and exhaust. Rows of cars under flickering fluorescent lights.

"My car is section C, spot 47. Gray Honda Civic." Yuki pressed keys into Sarah's hand. "Drive north. Don't stop for anything except gas and bathrooms. Follow the address. Don't call anyone, don't—"

Alarms.

Loud, harsh, echoing through the concrete space like the end of the world.

"Shit," Yuki breathed. "They know. How do they—" She shook her head. "Run! Now!"

Sarah ran.

Behind her, shouts. Footsteps. Multiple people converging from stairwells.

She found section C, scanning the numbers painted on concrete pillars. 43, 45, 47. Gray Honda, slightly dusty. Fumbled with the keys, fingers shaking so hard she could barely grip them.

Unlocked it. Got in. Started the engine.

In the rearview mirror, security personnel converging on Yuki. Four of them, professional and efficient. She didn't fight. Just stood there, hands raised in surrender.

Their eyes met across the parking garage.

Yuki nodded once. Go.

Sarah went.

Floored the accelerator, tires squealing on concrete. Security personnel diving out of the way, shouting into radios.

A barrier at the exit—yellow and white striped arm blocking the way. Sarah didn't slow. Crashed through it in a shower of splintering wood and plastic.

Out into darkness and cold air.

No idea where she was.

Industrial park. Buildings, warehouses, everything dark at this hour. She looked for street signs. Found one: Waltham. Massachusetts. Still in the Boston metro area.

Headlights behind her. Two vehicles, fast and professional, no sirens but moving with obvious purpose.

Sarah had never been good at driving. She'd failed her first driving test twice.

She drove like her life depended on it. Which it did.

Through the industrial park, weaving between buildings. Onto a main road. Signs: Route 3 North. New Hampshire.

The vehicles stayed with her. Professional drivers, coordinated pursuit. Not trying to run her off the road. Just following, waiting for her to make a mistake.

Sarah took a turn too fast. The car lurched, tires screaming. Nearly rolled. She corrected, overcorrected, regained control with hands white-knuckled on the wheel.

Another turn. Into a residential area—quieter, fewer lights, houses dark at 2:30 AM. She killed her headlights, drove by streetlamps and muscle memory and pure terror.

The pursuit vehicles overshot the turn. Gave her maybe thirty seconds.

Sarah pulled into a random driveway—house completely dark—cut the engine. Sat in absolute silence, heart hammering.

The pursuit vehicles circled back. Drove past slowly, scanning driveways.

Sarah didn't breathe.

One vehicle stopped. Right in front of the house where she was hiding.

She could see the driver scanning with a flashlight.

Thirty seconds. A minute.

The radio in their car crackled.

The vehicle moved on. Turned the corner. Disappeared.

She waited. Five minutes that felt like five hours. Ten. Fifteen.

Nothing.

At 3:30 AM, Sarah started the car again. Drove slowly, headlights on now, just another early morning commuter.

Found Route 3 North. Followed it away from Waltham, away from Boston, away from the facility and Wells and everything that had just happened.

At 4 AM, she pulled into a rest stop. Needed to think. Needed to understand what she was driving toward.

Behind her, GenVault was hunting her.

Ahead, New Hampshire and an address that might be salvation or another trap.

Sarah drove north through the predawn darkness, toward answers or annihilation.

Either way, she was done running.

Now she was hunting.

And somewhere behind her, Yuki Tanaka was being questioned for helping her escape.

Sarah gripped the wheel tighter and drove faster.

She had six months before they hit 15% coverage and released their genocide weapon.

Six months to find the truth.

Six months to stop them.

The sky began to lighten in the east as she crossed into New Hampshire.

Sarah Chen drove toward answers, carrying the weight of billions of lives she might not be able to save.

But she would try.

God help her, she would try.
