# CHAPTER 5.75: THE ALMOST KISS

**Site Seven - Cell Block C, Cell 17**
**December 3, 2025 - Day 25 of Captivity**
**3:42 AM**

---

Sarah woke screaming.

The nightmare clung to her—Marsh's house burning, the old woman's face at the window, flames eating through wood and flesh and forty years of research. Morrison's voice: *You were too late.* Wells's voice: *Twenty-three million people, Dr. Chen.* Bennett's children crying: *Why did you let Daddy die?*

She sat up gasping, covered in cold sweat, hands shaking.

The cell was dark. Four concrete walls. Observation window showing the empty corridor. Fluorescent lights dimmed for night cycle. Everything quiet except her ragged breathing and the hum of ventilation.

Just a nightmare.

Except it wasn't.

Marsh had burned. Lagos would deploy in—she'd lost count of the days. Forty-eight hours? Thirty-six? Bennett was still alive because she'd refused Wells's offer, but for how long? Morrison was still recruiting her. David was still bringing coffee and pretending he wasn't falling for someone he'd have to kill eventually.

Everything was real.

Everything was impossible.

Sarah buried her face in her hands, trying to slow her breathing. Trying not to think about the fact that she'd been captive for twenty-five days and Morrison was winning. Slowly. Methodically. Showing her the math. Showing her the collapse models. Showing her that refusing to choose was still a choice, and people died either way.

The observation window lit up.

Sarah looked up, saw David Rivera standing on the other side, wearing his night-shift uniform and an expression of concern that had become too familiar over the past three weeks.

He shouldn't be there. Night shift guards weren't supposed to check on prisoners unless alarms triggered. But David had been breaking protocol more and more often—extra coffee deliveries, extended conversations, that look he gave her when he thought she wasn't watching.

The magnetic lock disengaged.

Sarah's breath caught.

The cell door opened.

David stepped through, and Sarah saw immediately that something was different. He wasn't carrying coffee. Wasn't wearing his usual careful professional distance. He looked like someone who'd heard a scream and stopped thinking about consequences.

"Sarah." His voice was quiet. Rough. "I heard you from the corridor. What happened?"

"Nightmare." She wiped her face, trying to compose herself. "I'm fine."

"You're shaking."

"I said I'm fine."

David closed the cell door behind him—not locking it, Sarah noticed, just closing it for privacy. He stayed near the door, giving her space, hands loose at his sides like he was afraid touching her would break some invisible boundary they'd been carefully maintaining for weeks.

"Do you want me to leave?" he asked.

Sarah looked at him. Really looked at him.

David Rivera. Age thirty-four. FBI undercover assignment. Three years embedded with the network. Carlos's brother. The man who brought her coffee every day like a ritual. The man who'd told her about timeline theory while they shared silence in the facility kitchen's blind spot. The man who was supposed to extract her but kept finding reasons to stay close instead.

The man who was going to die in—how many days? According to the timeline she'd seen in Morrison's files, David died November 19. Today was December 3.

Sixteen days.

Sixteen days until David Rivera walked into a fire to prove Sarah's philosophy right, and she'd spend the rest of her life carrying his death along with all the others.

"No," Sarah whispered. "Don't leave."

David moved then. Not fast. Not presumptuous. Just carefully crossing the small cell to sit on the floor beside her cot—close enough to be present, far enough that they weren't touching.

He didn't ask what the nightmare was about.

Didn't offer empty comfort.

Just sat with her in the dark, two people who'd found something impossible in the worst possible place.

---

They were quiet for a long time.

Sarah's breathing gradually slowed. The nightmare's grip loosened. She became aware of David's presence beside her—the warmth of another human being, the steady rhythm of his breathing, the way he smelled like coffee and soap and something she couldn't name but had started to associate with safety.

"I dreamed about Marsh," Sarah said finally. "The night Morrison killed her. I keep seeing her face at the window. Keep wondering if she suffered. If she knew Morrison was coming. If she had time to be afraid."

David was quiet. Then: "Morrison said it was fast. House fire. She probably died of smoke inhalation before the flames reached her."

"Morrison lies."

"Not about that. He's proud of his efficiency." David's voice was bitter. "He told me once that unnecessary suffering was inefficient. That if you're going to kill someone, you do it cleanly. No torture. No prolonged fear. Just... elimination."

Sarah looked at him. "That doesn't make it better."

"I know. But it means Marsh probably didn't suffer long." He paused. "Doesn't mean Morrison isn't a monster. Just means he's an organized one."

Sarah almost laughed. It came out broken.

David shifted slightly, and his shoulder brushed hers. Neither of them moved away.

"You want to know the worst part?" Sarah asked. "I can see his logic. Morrison's. Wells's. The math is real. The collapse is coming. If I just—" She stopped. "If I just accepted their solution, I could save millions of people. Maybe billions. But accepting their solution means becoming them. Means deciding that some people are acceptable losses."

"Like Bennett."

Sarah flinched. "You know about that?"

"Morrison told me. Said Wells tested you. Said you chose principle over twenty-three million lives." David's voice was careful. Non-judgmental. "He thinks it proves you're not ready for the mathematics yet. That you still value individual loyalty over collective survival."

"And what do you think?"

David was quiet for a long moment.

Then: "I think you chose not to become complicit in genocide. I think refusing to play their game was the only way to keep your humanity intact." He turned to look at her. "I also think you're going to carry those twenty-three million deaths for the rest of your life, wondering if you made the right choice."

"Lagos deploys in two days," Sarah whispered. "I could have stopped it. I had the information. I just—"

"You just refused to prove Wells right." David's voice was firm. "Sarah, if you'd given up Bennett, Wells would have killed him and deployed Lagos anyway. You know that. The choice wasn't real. It was a test to see if you'd compromise your principles when the numbers got big enough."

"Twenty-three million people, David."

"Twenty-three million people Morrison and Wells are killing. Not you." He shifted to face her more directly. "You're not responsible for their genocide just because you refused to become an accessory to it."

Sarah closed her eyes. "Then why does it feel like I am?"

"Because you're a good person carrying impossible weight in an impossible situation." David's hand moved—not touching her, just resting on the cot between them. "Because Morrison is very good at making you feel responsible for outcomes you can't control. That's how he recruits people. Makes them believe the only moral choice is to help optimize the murder."

Sarah looked down at his hand. So close to hers. Close enough that moving an inch would bridge the gap.

She didn't move.

Neither did he.

"Tell me about Carlos," Sarah said quietly.

David's breath caught. "Why?"

"Because you've been carrying his death for three years. Because you're here trying to change timelines and save people the way you couldn't save him. Because—" Sarah's voice softened. "Because I need to understand why you're still here instead of extracting me like your orders said."

David was very still.

Then he started talking.

---

"Carlos was two years younger than me," David said. His voice was distant. Remembering. "Brilliant kid. PhD in epidemiology by twenty-five. Recruited by GenVault straight out of graduate school. Thought he was going to save the world—develop pandemic countermeasures, help prevent the next plague."

"He didn't know."

"Not at first. They hired him for legitimate work. Disease modeling. Containment protocols. Real science." David's hands clenched. "Then they started giving him THRESHOLD data. Told him it was theoretical. Academic exercise. He built the models Morrison wanted—calculated optimal deployment patterns, mortality rates, genetic targeting efficiency."

Sarah felt ice in her stomach. "He helped design it."

"He helped optimize it. Didn't know he was optimizing real genocide until—" David stopped. "Until he noticed the timeline. The models weren't theoretical. They were scheduled for deployment in eight months."

"What did he do?"

"What you did. Stole the files. Tried to expose them. Contacted FBI, CDC, WHO. Sent evidence to every authority he could think of." David's voice went flat. "Morrison found out in forty-eight hours. Carlos knew he was compromised. Knew they'd come for him. He called me. Told me everything. Made me promise to finish what he started."

"And then?"

"And then he died in a car accident three hours later." David's jaw tightened. "Single-vehicle collision. Went off a bridge. Burned. They said he lost control. Mechanical failure. No evidence of foul play."

"But you knew."

"I knew. Because Carlos was the best driver I've ever met. Because the brake lines were cut. Because Morrison's people are very good at making murder look like accidents." David turned to look at Sarah. "I joined FBI's organized crime division six months later. Spent a year building credentials. Volunteered for the GenVault undercover operation. Spent three years embedded with the network, watching them prepare for genocide, gathering evidence."

"To finish what Carlos started."

"To prove he was right. That the network was real. That THRESHOLD was real. That someone had to stop them." David's voice cracked slightly. "And then you showed up. This brilliant pattern recognition specialist who'd figured out in six months what took Carlos two years. Who stole Morrison's files and made it to Marsh and survived long enough to become a problem Morrison couldn't just eliminate."

Sarah's throat was tight. "So you were supposed to extract me."

"Supposed to get you out. Protect the witness. Let you testify about what you'd found so FBI could move on the network."

"But you didn't."

"But I didn't." David looked at her. "Because Morrison was recruiting you. Because I saw how he operates—shows you the math, shows you the collapse, makes you feel responsible for outcomes you can't control. And I thought—" He stopped.

"What?"

"I thought if you stayed inside long enough, you'd break. Like Carlos almost did. Like everyone breaks eventually when Morrison shows them seven billion deaths and asks which genocide they prefer." David's voice was raw. "So I stayed. Kept bringing coffee. Kept talking to you about timeline theory and defensive Protocols and Carlos's belief that individual choices mattered even when the math said they didn't."

"To keep me from breaking."

"To keep you human." David's hand moved closer to hers on the cot. Still not touching. "Because if you break, Morrison wins. If you compromise, he proves that even the defensive Protocol fails when the stakes get high enough. And Carlos's death means nothing."

Sarah looked at their hands. Inches apart. The space between them felt like an impossible distance and no distance at all.

"David." Her voice was barely a whisper. "What happens if I don't break?"

He was quiet for a long moment.

Then: "Then you prove Carlos was right. That individual morality matters. That some things are worth dying for even when the math says they shouldn't be."

"And what happens to you?"

David smiled. Sad. "I already know how this ends, Sarah. I saw the timeline projections. Morrison showed them to me when he was recruiting me last month—tried to convince me that joining the network was the only rational choice. Showed me probability trees. Deployment outcomes. Casualty estimates."

Sarah's breath caught. "You saw your own death."

"I saw a ninety-three percent probability that I die during the FBI raid on Site Seven. November 19, 2025. Fire suppression failure during the extraction operation." He looked at her. "I'm a data point in Morrison's models. A statistical casualty. My death is already calculated into the optimal timeline."

"That's not—" Sarah's voice broke. "That's not certain. You can change it. Choose differently. Get out before—"

"Before I die saving you?" David's smile was gentle. "Sarah, Carlos taught me that timelines aren't deterministic. That individual choices create branches. That the moment you decide to act differently, you split reality into new possibilities."

"Then choose differently."

"I am." He looked at her. "In every other timeline—the ones where I followed orders, where I extracted you on day three, where I played it safe—I'm sitting in an FBI office drinking coffee and wondering if you survived Morrison's recruitment. Wondering if I could have done more."

Sarah's hands were shaking. "And in this timeline?"

"In this timeline, I stayed. Brought you coffee. Told you about Carlos. Sat with you in the dark when you had nightmares about Marsh burning." David's hand finally moved—not grabbing hers, just letting his fingers rest against hers. Barely touching. "In this timeline, I'm choosing to prove that one person matters. Even when the math says they shouldn't."

Sarah looked down at their hands. The slight pressure of his fingers against hers felt like the only real thing in the world.

"David." Her voice was shaking. "You know how this ends."

"I know how Morrison's models say it ends."

"He's not wrong about the math. You die. Lagos deploys. The network wins."

"Maybe." David shifted closer. Close enough that Sarah could feel the warmth of him. Close enough that tilting her head would close the distance between them. "Or maybe us choosing differently—you refusing Wells's offer, me staying instead of extracting you, both of us deciding that individuals matter even when the numbers say they don't—maybe that creates the timeline branch where something changes."

Sarah's heart was hammering. "What if it doesn't?"

"Then I die proving your philosophy right. That loyalty matters. That saving Bennett instead of trading him for Lagos was the moral choice even if it wasn't the mathematical one. That—" He stopped. Looked at her. "That spending twenty-five days bringing you coffee was worth more than forty years of playing it safe."

They were very close now. Sarah could see the exhaustion in David's eyes. The weight he'd been carrying since Carlos died. The choice he'd already made—was making right now—to stay when every rational calculation said he should run.

She could kiss him.

Lean forward six inches. Close the impossible distance. Take the comfort he was offering and let herself feel something other than guilt and fear and the weight of twenty-three million deaths she couldn't prevent.

She could—

Sarah pulled back.

Not far. Just enough to break the moment. Enough to put space between what she wanted and what would happen if she let herself want it.

"We can't," she whispered.

David didn't move. Didn't chase the distance. Just sat with the rejection like he'd expected it.

"I know," he said quietly.

"If I—" Sarah's voice cracked. "If I let myself care about you, and then you die in sixteen days, I'll—"

"You'll carry my death along with all the others."

"I can't, David. I can't add you to the list of people I failed."

"You haven't failed me." His voice was gentle. "Sarah, just—being here. Talking to you. Bringing coffee. Watching you refuse to break even when Morrison shows you every reason why you should." He smiled. "That's not failure. That's the only thing keeping me human."

Sarah closed her eyes against tears that wouldn't come. "I chose Bennett over twenty-three million people. I'm choosing my principles over lives I could save. And now I'm—" She couldn't say it.

"You're choosing not to care about me because caring makes it harder when I die."

"Yes."

"Too late." David's voice was soft. "I already care about you. Have since the first time you looked at Morrison's collapse models and said the math didn't justify genocide. Since you chose to protect Bennett even knowing Lagos would deploy. Since you proved that someone could be brilliant enough to see the mathematics and still choose humanity over optimization."

Sarah opened her eyes. "That doesn't make this possible."

"I know." David stood. Slowly. Giving her space. "But it makes it real."

He walked to the cell door. Paused with his hand on the handle.

"For what it's worth," he said quietly, "in every other timeline, I'm wondering what would have happened if I'd stayed. If I'd sat with you in the dark. If I'd told you about Carlos and let you see that I understand why you carry impossible weight and refuse to put it down."

Sarah looked at him. This man who'd spent three years undercover trying to finish his brother's work. Who'd broken protocol to comfort her. Who knew he was going to die in sixteen days and chose to stay anyway.

"And in this timeline?" she asked.

David smiled. Sad. Honest.

"In this timeline, I know."

He opened the cell door. Stepped through. Closed it behind him with a soft click of the magnetic lock engaging.

But he didn't leave.

He stood on the other side of the observation window, looking at her through the reinforced glass.

Sarah stood. Crossed to the window. Put her hand against the glass.

David put his hand on the other side. Palm to palm with only glass between them.

They stood like that for a long time.

Two people in an impossible situation making impossible choices. Sarah choosing principles over mathematics. David choosing to stay when every rational calculation said he should run. Both of them knowing how it ended. Both of them choosing it anyway.

Finally, David lowered his hand.

"Try to sleep," he said. His voice was muffled by the glass. "I'll be outside if you need anything."

"David."

He stopped.

"Thank you," Sarah whispered. "For Carlos. For the coffee. For—" She couldn't finish.

"For staying," David said quietly. "I know."

He walked away.

Sarah stood at the observation window, watching him disappear down the corridor. Then she turned back to the small cell. Saw the cot where they'd almost—where she'd pulled back. Saw the space where their hands had touched. Saw the impossible distance between what she wanted and what the timeline said was coming.

Sixteen days.

In sixteen days, David Rivera would walk into fire to prove her philosophy right.

And Sarah would spend the rest of her life wondering if the moment they didn't kiss mattered as much as the ones they did.

If choosing not to care was its own kind of failure.

If love that never happened still counted as loss.

She lay down on the cot. Turned toward the wall. Closed her eyes.

Outside the cell, through the observation window, David Rivera stood watch in the corridor.

Two people separated by glass and timelines and impossible choices.

Both of them knowing how it ended.

Both of them choosing it anyway.

**END OF CHAPTER 5.75**
