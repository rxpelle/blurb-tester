# CHAPTER 7.5: MORRISON'S CRISIS OF FAITH

**Site Seven - Director's Office**
**December 8, 2025 - Day 30 of Sarah Chen's Captivity**
**2:17 AM**

---

Dr. James Morrison sat alone in his office three stories underground, watching surveillance footage of Sarah Chen sleeping in her cell.

She'd been captive for thirty days. Thirty days of recruitment attempts, philosophical debates, mathematical proofs, collapse projections. Thirty days of Morrison showing her every reason why THRESHOLD was necessary, why genocide was mercy, why 262 million deaths prevented 7.2 billion.

Thirty days, and she still refused.

On the monitor, Sarah shifted in her sleep. Dreaming, probably. Morrison had reviewed the night shift reports—she had nightmares most nights. Marsh burning. Lagos deploying. Bennett's children crying. David Rivera dying in sixteen days, according to the timeline projections Morrison had shown her.

She carried all of it. Every death. Every choice. Every mathematical certainty that proved her philosophy failed when numbers got large enough.

And still she refused.

Morrison closed the surveillance feed. Pulled up Wells's report from yesterday's assessment:

**SUBJECT: Dr. Sarah Chen - Recruitment Viability Assessment**
**CONCLUSION: Recruitment unlikely. Subject demonstrates pattern recognition capability and systems thinking consistent with defensive Protocol markers, but displays emotional resistance to utilitarian optimization. Recommend elimination within 72 hours to prevent interference with Phase I deployment.**

**DEPLOYMENT STATUS: T-minus 11 days, 4 hours**

Eleven days until THRESHOLD deployed globally. Eleven days until 262 million people died and Morrison saved the remaining 7.5 billion from slower, more chaotic extinction.

Eleven days until he became the greatest mass murderer in human history.

Or the greatest savior, depending on your philosophy.

Morrison had stopped being certain which one mattered somewhere around year twenty-three.

He opened his desk drawer. Pulled out the photograph he kept there but never looked at anymore.

Melissa. His wife. Twenty-seven years ago, standing in their garden in Berkeley, pregnant with the daughter they'd lose six months later to a hospital infection that shouldn't have happened. Smiling like the world made sense. Like the future was something to look forward to instead of a mathematical certainty grinding toward collapse.

She'd left him two years after Emily died. Said he'd stopped being human. Stopped feeling anything except the need to fix broken systems and prevent suffering at scale.

She wasn't wrong.

Morrison set down the photograph. Looked at his reflection in the darkened monitor.

Fifty-four years old. Three decades embedded in the network. Twenty-three years running THRESHOLD development. He'd sacrificed his marriage, his daughter's grave (he hadn't visited in eleven years), his sleep, his conscience, his humanity—all for the mathematics.

All to save people who'd never know they needed saving.

All to become the monster who prevented worse monsters.

His phone buzzed. Encrypted message from Catherine Wells:

**Chen refuses to cooperate. Authorize elimination protocol. We can't risk her interfering this close to deployment.**

Morrison stared at the message.

Sarah Chen. Twenty-eight years old. Brilliant pattern recognition specialist. Carrier of defensive Protocol markers going back twenty-eight generations. The exact kind of person the Genesis Protocol was designed to create—someone smart enough to see collapse coming and ethical enough to care about preventing it without resorting to genocide.

She was proof the defensive Protocol worked.

And Morrison was about to kill her because she proved it too well.

His hand hovered over the keyboard.

**AUTHORIZE ELIMINATION**

Three words. Sarah dead by morning. Problem solved. THRESHOLD deploys on schedule. 262 million casualties, 7.5 billion saved. The mathematics stayed intact.

He didn't type them.

Instead, he pulled up Sarah's file. Not the recruitment assessment. The original research—her published papers, her MIT dissertation, the pattern recognition work that had caught network attention two years ago.

*"Genetic Inheritance Markers and Population Resilience: Evidence for Directed Selection in Post-Crisis Populations"*

Her thesis had documented something the network had spent two thousand years trying to hide: that certain genetic markers appeared in post-collapse populations at rates far higher than random selection could explain. That someone had been engineering human resilience into bloodlines for millennia.

The defensive Protocol.

Sarah had found it. Proven it existed. Demonstrated that humanity had survived the Bronze Age Collapse, the Black Death, two world wars not through random chance but through deliberate genetic optimization spread across generations.

And her conclusion—the one that had made Morrison recruit her instead of eliminating her immediately—was devastating:

*"If historical collapse events show evidence of prepared genetic resilience, this suggests an alternative to Malthusian catastrophe models. Rather than population reduction, targeted genetic distribution may provide sufficient adaptive capacity for civilizations to survive crisis periods without collapse. Further research recommended."*

She'd found the answer Morrison had spent thirty years pretending didn't exist.

That maybe—just maybe—you didn't need to kill billions of people to prevent extinction.

That maybe the defensive Protocol actually worked.

Morrison closed the file.

Opened another one. The private research he'd buried fifteen years ago when the network promoted him to THRESHOLD director. The data he'd collected on defensive Protocol outcomes. The evidence he'd hidden because it contradicted everything THRESHOLD was built on.

**STUDY: Long-term Outcome Analysis of Defensive vs Offensive Protocol Implementation**

**Sample Size:** 47 historical crisis events over 3,200 years

**Defensive Protocol Events (n=23):**
- Bronze Age Collapse (1200-1150 BCE): 40% population reduction, 300-year recovery
- Black Death (1347-1353): 30% population reduction, 150-year recovery
- Spanish Flu (1918-1920): 3% population reduction, 20-year recovery
- Median outcome: Crisis survived, civilization maintained, recovery time decreasing

**Offensive Protocol Events (n=24):**
- Younger Dryas (10,800 BCE): 90% population reduction, 1000-year recovery
- Late Bronze Age interventions (multiple): 60% population reduction, 400-year recovery
- Median outcome: Crisis prevented, civilization collapsed anyway, recovery time increasing

**Statistical Analysis:**
Defensive Protocol shows improving efficacy over time (p<0.001). Offensive Protocol shows diminishing returns and increasing collateral damage (p<0.001).

**Conclusion:** Defensive Protocol optimization appears more effective at long-term species survival than offensive Protocol population reduction.

**Recommendation:** THRESHOLD deployment should be delayed pending—

Morrison had stopped writing the report fifteen years ago.

Stopped because the network didn't want to hear that maybe they were wrong.

Stopped because acknowledging the defensive Protocol worked meant admitting THRESHOLD wasn't necessary.

Stopped because if 262 million deaths weren't necessary, then Morrison had wasted thirty years preparing for genocide he didn't need to commit.

And what did that make him?

His office door chimed. Morrison closed the files quickly.

"Come in."

David Rivera stepped through. Night shift, checking on the director at 2 AM like that was normal. He'd been doing that more often lately—watching Morrison with the same careful assessment Morrison used on recruitment targets.

"You're up late," David said.

"Deployment preparations." Morrison gestured at the monitors. "Eleven days. A lot to coordinate."

"Right." David didn't move. "Wells wants authorization to eliminate Chen. You haven't responded."

Morrison looked at him. David Rivera. Thirty-four years old. FBI undercover. Morrison had known for eight months—had let him stay embedded because having a known variable was better than an unknown one. Had let him get close to Sarah because watching them together was useful data about defensive Protocol relationship patterns.

Had let David stay because some part of Morrison—the part that still remembered being human—wanted to see if Sarah's philosophy actually worked when tested against impossible choices.

"You think I should authorize it?" Morrison asked.

"I think Wells is right that Chen is dangerous." David's voice was careful. "She's smart enough to understand your mathematics and ethical enough to reject it anyway. That makes her the perfect voice to undermine THRESHOLD if she gets out."

"And if she's right? If the defensive Protocol actually prevents collapse without requiring genocide?"

David went very still. "Do you think she's right?"

Morrison looked at the closed files on his screen. The evidence he'd buried. The research that said maybe—just maybe—humanity could survive crisis without THRESHOLD.

"I think," Morrison said slowly, "that I've spent thirty years building THRESHOLD. That 147 research facilities across six continents are coordinating deployment. That 262 million people are going to die in eleven days because I convinced the network this was the only way to prevent extinction."

"That's not an answer."

"No." Morrison smiled. Bitter. "It's not."

He pulled up Sarah's surveillance feed again. Watched her sleep. This woman who carried twenty-eight generations of defensive Protocol markers. Who'd found proof the Genesis Protocol worked. Who refused to compromise her principles even when he showed her seven billion deaths and asked which genocide she preferred.

"What happens if I don't authorize elimination?" Morrison asked.

"Wells goes over your head. Network leadership eliminates Chen anyway. You lose credibility. Maybe lose directorship." David paused. "THRESHOLD deploys regardless."

"And if I authorize it? If Chen dies?"

"Then you prove Wells right. That defensive Protocol fails when tested. That individual ethics can't survive real-world mathematics. That THRESHOLD is the only rational choice." David's voice was very quiet. "And you spend the rest of your life wondering if you killed the one person who could have proven you wrong."

Morrison looked at him. "Why are you here, Rivera? Really?"

"Because you've been sitting in your office for six hours staring at Chen's files. Because Wells has been pushing for elimination and you keep delaying. Because—" David stopped. "Because I think you're not sure anymore. That maybe you're wondering if thirty years of work was in service of the wrong solution."

"And if I am?"

"Then you have eleven days to decide if you're committed to THRESHOLD or committed to finding out if Sarah's right."

Morrison was quiet for a long moment.

Then he typed:

**ELIMINATION AUTHORIZATION DENIED. Chen remains viable recruitment target. Continue assessment protocol.**

He sent it to Wells. Watched the message deliver. Felt the weight of what he'd just done.

Not a reversal. Not a conversion to Sarah's philosophy.

Just... a delay. One more test. One more chance to see if the defensive Protocol actually worked when pushed to breaking.

One more way to avoid admitting he might be wrong.

David watched him. "Wells won't accept that for long. You bought Chen maybe seventy-two hours. Then network leadership overrides you."

"I know."

"So what's the plan? Convert her in three days? Prove she'll compromise when the math gets big enough?"

Morrison looked at Sarah's surveillance feed. At this woman who carried impossible weight and refused to put it down. Who chose Bennett over twenty-three million people. Who pulled away from David Rivera even though Morrison could see in both their faces that they wanted to collapse the distance.

Who proved, every single day, that principles mattered even when mathematics said they shouldn't.

"No," Morrison said quietly. "I'm going to show her why I'm right. Why THRESHOLD is necessary. Why defensive Protocol thinking is beautiful philosophy but fails in practice." He closed the surveillance feed. "And if she can prove me wrong—if she can show me evidence that humanity survives collapse without genocide—then maybe I reconsider."

"You know she can't prove that in seventy-two hours."

"Then I proceed with THRESHOLD. And eliminate her. And spend the rest of my life knowing I killed 262 million people to prevent worse deaths that might not have happened." Morrison stood. "Either way, I'll know."

David studied him. "You want her to be right. Don't you? You want Sarah to prove that thirty years of work was wrong. That you didn't have to become this."

Morrison looked at Melissa's photograph on his desk. At the man he used to be before Emily died and the world stopped making sense and the only comfort was mathematics that promised suffering could be prevented if someone was willing to make hard choices.

"I want," Morrison said quietly, "to stop wondering if I'm saving the world or just justifying mass murder with sophisticated math."

He picked up the photograph. Put it back in the drawer. Closed it.

"But wanting doesn't change the mathematics. And in eleven days, THRESHOLD deploys regardless of what I want. So Chen has seventy-two hours to prove the impossible." Morrison walked to the door. "Or I prove Wells right. That defensive Protocol is beautiful philosophy that fails when tested against real-world collapse."

He left David standing in the office.

Walked down the corridor toward Sarah's cell.

Seventy-two hours. Three days to test whether thirty years of genocide preparation was necessary or just the sophisticated rationalization of a man who'd stopped being able to live with moral ambiguity.

Three days to see if Sarah Chen could save him from becoming the monster he'd spent thirty years preparing to be.

Or prove that monsters were necessary when the math got big enough.

Morrison stopped outside Sarah's cell. Looked through the observation window. Watched her sleep.

She'd refused Wells's offer. Chosen Bennett over twenty-three million people. Pulled away from David even though Morrison could see it was killing them both. Proven, over and over, that principles mattered even when mathematics said they shouldn't.

And in seventy-two hours, Morrison would either have to kill her or admit she was right.

He didn't know which outcome scared him more.

Morrison turned away from the cell.

Walked back toward his office where monitors displayed countdown timers and deployment protocols and 262 million deaths wrapped in mathematics that promised they were mercy.

**T-minus 11 days, 3 hours, 42 minutes**

The clock kept running.

The choice kept approaching.

And Morrison kept wondering if the man he'd been before Emily died—before the mathematics became the only comfort that made sense—would recognize the man he'd become.

Or just see a monster with sophisticated justifications.
