## Chapter 4 Addition: Emma Reveal

**INSERT AFTER:** "Because optimization is what saves humanity," Wells countered.

**NEW SECTION:**

Wells pulled something from his wallet. Set it on the table between them.

A photograph. Worn at the edges.

"What do you see?" Wells asked.

Sarah looked. A little girl. Maybe eight or nine. Dark curls. Gap-toothed smile. Holding a sign that said "THERE IS NO PLANET B."

"Environmental activism," Sarah said carefully. "Climate change protest. Early education." She looked closer. "Summer camp, based on the setting. Teaching children." Pause. "Your daughter?"

Wells said nothing. Just watched her.

Sarah turned the photograph over. On the back, in a child's handwriting: *Dad—I'm going to help save the world! Love, Emma*

The date: July 2019.

Sarah knew what happened in California, July 2019. She'd covered it as a journalist. Paradise. Camp Fire season. 85 dead.

She looked at Wells. At the way he was holding himself too still. Too controlled.

"*Is*?" Sarah asked quietly.

"Was."

One word. But the way Wells's voice cracked on it told Sarah everything else. The daughter who'd believed in prevention. The scientist who'd spent thirty years warning about exactly the catastrophe that killed her. The father who'd been proven right in the worst possible way.

"I'm sorry," Sarah said.

Wells took the photograph back. Carefully. Like it might shatter.

"Don't be sorry," he said. "Help me finish her work."

His voice cracked on the number.

"She died exactly the way my models predicted," Wells said. "Resource strain. Infrastructure failure. Population density exceeding sustainable thresholds. Climate-driven catastrophe overwhelming emergency response. Everything I'd warned about for three decades happened in six hours."

Sarah felt cold. "So you decided genocide was the answer."

"I decided *prevention* was impossible." Wells met her eyes. Grief there. Raw. Real. "For thirty years I tried to save humanity by convincing them to change behavior. Know what happened? They ignored every warning. Every prediction. Every model. And my daughter burned to death teaching children about climate adaptation."

He put the photograph back in his wallet. Carefully. Like it might shatter.

"The network found me six months after Emma died," Wells continued. "I was drinking myself to death. Couldn't go back to climatology—every model I ran just predicted more deaths. More fires. More collapse that people wouldn't prevent because prevention requires sacrifice and humans don't sacrifice until it's too late."

"So Morrison recruited you."

"Dr. Morrison showed me thirty years of data proving prevention doesn't work. Civilizations don't adapt before collapse. They adapt *after*. The only question is whether anyone survives to adapt." Wells's voice was quiet. Certain. "The network offers managed survival. Controlled reduction that leaves enough humans to rebuild. It's not what I wanted. It's what's possible when prevention fails."

Sarah stared at the table. At the cold coffee. At the space where Emma's photo had been.

"I'm sorry about Emma," she said quietly. "Truly sorry. But grief doesn't justify genocide."

"Doesn't it?" Wells leaned forward. "Sarah, you're a scientist. You understand predictive models. If I could have prevented Paradise—if I could have deployed targeted intervention that killed 85 people *before* the fire started, ensuring it never happened—would that be genocide? Or prevention?"

"Those aren't comparable—"

"THRESHOLD prevents worse collapse," Wells interrupted. "Six hundred million casualties in managed intervention. Or four billion in uncoordinated climate catastrophe over the next fifty years. That's the model. That's what Emma would have wanted."

"You don't know that."

"She wrote *I'm going to help save the world*." Wells's voice broke. "The network lets me finish her work. Save humanity. Make her death *mean* something instead of being one more preventable tragedy in a world that refuses to prevent tragedies."

Sarah understood then. Wells wasn't a monster. He was a grieving father who'd spent thirty years watching humanity ignore his warnings, who'd lost his daughter to exactly the catastrophe he'd predicted, and who'd been radicalized by Morrison into believing genocide was the only solution grief couldn't reach.

Sympathetic.

Still wrong.

But sympathetic.

"Emma was nine," Sarah said carefully. "She believed humans could change. That's what climate activism means—believing we can prevent collapse through adaptation. Not through killing the people we're supposed to save."

"Emma was nine and optimistic," Wells said. "I'm sixty-two and empirical. Thirty years of data says humans don't prevent collapse. They react to it. The network ensures someone survives to react."

He stood. Ready to leave. Ready to report this conversation to Morrison.

"I hope you choose cooperation, Sarah. Not for the network. For you." He paused. "Emma would have liked you. The way you challenge assumptions. The way you demand third options when everyone else accepts binary choices."

"What would Emma think about THRESHOLD?" Sarah asked quietly.

Wells flinched. Actually flinched.

"Emma's dead," he said. "She doesn't think anything. But if preventing 500 million more deaths like hers requires 600 million controlled casualties, then I'll make that trade. Because the alternative is everyone dies. And someone needs to remember she existed."

He walked out.

Sarah sat with her cold coffee, understanding Wells in a way that made stopping him harder.

Because he wasn't doing this for power. Or ideology. Or hate.

He was doing it for a nine-year-old girl who'd written *I'm going to help save the world* and died in a fire her father had predicted but couldn't prevent.

Sympathetic.

Tragic.

Still genocide.

And Sarah had to stop him anyway.

## Chapter 7 Addition: Wells Alone (NEW SCENE)

**INSERT AFTER existing Chapter 7 scenes, before THRESHOLD deployment:**

### Wells's Office - Site Seven - 2 Hours Before Deployment

**Day 68 - 2 AM**

Dr. Catherine Wells sat alone in her office, staring at the THRESHOLD deployment interface.

One button. Red. Clean. Efficient.

Forty-seven hubs. Six hundred million casualties. The culmination of eighteen years of planning, twelve years of development, five years of behavioral modeling.

Everything Morrison had worked for.

Everything Wells had convinced herself was necessary.

Her hand hovered over the button. Shaking.

On her desk: Emma's photograph. The one from Paradise. Gap-toothed smile. *THERE IS NO PLANET B*.

"I'm sorry," Wells whispered to the photograph. "I tried your way. Tried prevention. Tried convincing humanity to change. Thirty years of trying."

The deployment clock ticked down: **01:47:00**

Wells poured three fingers of whiskey into a glass that hadn't been washed in four days. Amber liquid. Expensive. The kind she'd bought to celebrate professional achievements back when achievements meant peer-reviewed papers instead of genocide.

She drank it. Felt the burn all the way down. Sharp at first, then spreading into warmth that had nothing to do with comfort. Just physics. Alcohol metabolizing. Numbing nerve endings that were screaming at her to stop stop stop you're about to kill 400 million people.

Three more fingers. Drank those too.

Her hand shook holding the glass. She set it down before the trembling got worse. The whiskey sloshed. Spilled over the rim. Dark drops on the deployment interface screen, running down toward the red button like accusations.

Emma's photograph lay on the desk beside the empty bottle. Wells picked it up. The edges were worn soft from touching. How many times in eleven years had she held this photo? How many nights had she traced Emma's gap-toothed smile with her thumb like maybe enough repetition would bring her back?

The paper felt fragile. Wrong temperature. Too warm from sitting under the desk lamp. Emma had been cold when they'd identified her body. Nine years old. Number 73. Burned beyond recognition except for the dental records and the environmental camp ID bracelet that had somehow survived.

Wells's hands shook worse.

Wells stood. Walked to the window. Site Seven was dark except for emergency lighting. Below, Morrison coordinated final deployment protocols. Bennett had defected—disappeared with Phase Two intelligence. Sarah Chen had spent seven weeks gathering hub locations.

But it didn't matter. Twenty-three hubs secured still left twenty-four operational. Still left 400 million casualties. Still left enough dead to prove the model worked.

Still left managed collapse instead of total extinction.

"Is this what you'd want?" Wells asked Emma's photo. "Would you understand? Would you see the math and agree that controlled intervention beats chaotic death?"

The photograph didn't answer.

Wells drank more whiskey. Her hands shook worse.

"Morrison says I'm weak," Wells said. "Says emotional attachment compromises operational effectiveness. Says I should have deployed THRESHOLD myself instead of delegating to him." She touched the deployment interface. The button glowed. Patient. Waiting. "But I can't. I've run the models. I know it's necessary. I know 400 million controlled casualties prevents 4 billion chaotic deaths. The math works."

She thought about Paradise. About 85 people burning. About Emma teaching nine-year-olds how to prevent the exact catastrophe that killed her.

"But you'd hate this," Wells whispered. "You'd hate that I'm using your death to justify more death. You'd tell me I'm betraying everything you believed. That prevention means stopping the fire, not managing who burns."

**01:52:00**

Wells picked up her phone. Dialed Morrison's extension.

"Wells." Morrison's voice was clinical. Impatient. "Deployment commences in two hours. Status?"

"Deploy without me," Wells said.

"Excuse me?"

"I can't push the button." Wells's voice cracked. "I've authorized deployment. I've verified protocols. I've confirmed hub status. But I can't... I can't be the one who activates it."

Silence on the line. Then: "Your daughter died in a climate catastrophe we could have prevented. THRESHOLD ensures fewer parents lose children to preventable collapse. I would think you'd want to finish her work."

"I *want* to prevent collapse," Wells said. "But Emma would want prevention through adaptation. Not through killing 400 million people."

"Emma's dead. The living require protection." Morrison's voice was ice. "I'll handle deployment activation. You're dismissed from operational command. Effective immediately."

The line went dead.

Wells sat in her office. Alone. Drunk. Staring at the deployment interface she couldn't bring herself to touch.

**01:47:00**

She picked up Emma's photograph. Held it.

"I'm still trying to save the world," Wells whispered. "Just... not your way. Because your way requires humanity to change before catastrophe forces them. And humanity doesn't change. You know that now. Paradise taught us both."

She kissed the photograph. Set it down. Poured more whiskey.

Below, Morrison would activate THRESHOLD. Would deploy at twenty-four hubs. Would kill 400 million people in seventy-two hours.

Wells had authorized it. Verified it. Made it possible.

But she couldn't push the button herself.

Because somewhere in the last eighteen years, she'd stopped doing this *for* Emma and started doing it *despite* Emma.

And that distinction was the difference between saving the world and destroying it.

She just didn't know which side she was on anymore.

**01:42:00**

The countdown continued.

Wells drank until the whiskey was gone.

And Emma's photograph smiled up at her. Nine years old. Forever optimistic.

Forever disappointed in what her father had become.

## Chapter 8 Addition: Lagos Deployment

**INSERT DURING Lagos deployment scene when Wells participates:**

Wells stood in Site Seven's command center, watching the Lagos hub activate on-screen.

Real-time satellite feeds showed Murtala Muhammed International Airport. Terminal chaos as the pathogen spread. Medical teams overwhelmed. Infrastructure failing exactly as the models predicted.

23 million casualties. Concentrated. Efficient. Managed.

Wells watched until she couldn't anymore.

Then she walked to the bathroom. Locked herself in a stall. And vomited until there was nothing left but dry heaves and guilt.

When she emerged—eyes red, hands shaking—Morrison was waiting.

"Operational stress?" he asked. Clinical. Detached.

"Operational reality," Wells corrected. "23 million people. Lagos. Emma's daughter could have been any of them."

"Emma's *daughter*?" Morrison frowned. "Emma had no—"

"If she'd lived." Wells's voice was barely a whisper. "If Paradise hadn't burned. If my models had been wrong. Emma would be twenty-two now. Might have had children. Might have been in Lagos with Doctors Without Borders because that's the kind of person she'd have become."

She met Morrison's eyes.

"And I just killed her," Wells said. "Killed the future she should have had. Killed everyone's Emma. All 23 million of them."

"You saved 200 million more in secondary cascade prevention," Morrison said. "The math works."

"The math always works," Wells said. "Until it's your daughter in the calculation."

She walked past him. Back to her office. Back to Emma's photograph and the empty whiskey bottle.

Behind her, Morrison watched Lagos continue to die.

Efficient. Managed. Exactly as planned.

But Wells's vomit was still in the bathroom trash can.

And Morrison realized, for the first time in eighteen years, that grief made terrible allies.

Because people consumed by guilt can't be trusted to finish what they start.

## Chapter 10 Addition: Arrest Scene

**INSERT DURING Wells's arrest (if she's captured in Chapter 10):**

FBI agents surrounded Wells in the São Paulo hub facility. Forty-eight hours after THRESHOLD deployment. The hub had been secured, but 47 million were already dead across six deployment sites.

"Dr. Catherine Wells," the lead agent said. "You're under arrest for crimes against humanity. Conspiracy to commit genocide. Bioterrorism. Approximately 262 million deaths attributed to THRESHOLD deployment."

Wells didn't resist. Just sat at the contamination monitoring station, staring at casualty statistics.

"262 million," she said quietly. "Down from 600 million projected. Because Sarah Chen and Dr. Bennett secured twenty-three hubs before deployment. Because the defensive protocol carriers actually *worked*."

"That's not a defense—" the agent started.

"It's not a defense," Wells interrupted. "It's irony. My daughter believed humans could prevent collapse through pattern recognition and adaptation. Exactly what the defensive protocol was designed to enable. And Sarah proved Emma was right."

She stood. Let the agents cuff her.

"Here's what you need to understand," Wells said. "THRESHOLD prevents 4 billion deaths from climate collapse over the next fifty years. That's the model. That's the math. 262 million dead now. 4 billion alive later."

"That's genocide—"

"That's triage," Wells corrected. "And if you don't implement population reduction—if you don't manage carrying capacity—the 4 billion die anyway. In fires like Paradise. In floods. In resource wars. In exactly the collapse I spent thirty years warning about and no one prevented."

The lead agent hesitated. "Are you saying..."

"I'm saying the network's methods are evil," Wells said. "But the diagnosis is correct. Humanity is approaching catastrophic systems failure. The only question is whether we reduce population through managed intervention or unmanaged collapse."

"There's always a third option—"

"My daughter believed that," Wells said softly. "She died still believing it. And I've spent eleven years since trying to prove her wrong. Because if there's a third option—if humanity CAN prevent collapse without genocide—then I killed 262 million people for nothing."

She looked at the casualty statistics one more time.

"Tell Sarah Chen something," Wells said. "Tell her Emma would have liked her. Tell her if there IS a third option—if defensive protocols can prevent collapse instead of engineering it—then make it work. Because if it works, my daughter was right. And 262 million people died for a genocide that wasn't necessary."

They led her away.

But the agent remembered those words.

And six months later, when Sarah established the Collapse Prevention Initiative, the agent sent her Wells's message.

Emma would have liked her.

Make it work.

Prove that 262 million died for nothing.

Which was either the most damning condemnation or the last shred of hope from a woman who'd chosen wrong and knew it.

## Chapter 7 CLIFFHANGER: Morrison Takes Control

**INSERT AT END OF CHAPTER 7 (after Wells alone scene):**

### Site Seven Command Center - 01:45:00 Before Deployment

The deployment interface was ready. All forty-seven hubs showed green status. THRESHOLD protocols loaded. 400 million casualties projected across seventy-two hours of optimized population reduction.

Everything Morrison had worked for eighteen years to achieve.

Dr. Wells stood at the activation console. Her hand hovered over the red button.

Shaking.

"Dr. Wells?" Morrison's voice came from behind her. Clinical. Impatient. "Deployment window commences in 105 minutes. Protocol requires command authorization."

Wells stared at the button. At Emma's photograph on the console. At the number 400,000,000 displayed in cold digital certainty.

She'd authorized this. Verified every protocol. Confirmed every hub. Spent eleven years making this moment possible.

Her hand wouldn't move.

"I can't," Wells whispered.

Morrison stepped forward. His hand was steady when he placed it over hers. Guided her trembling fingers away from the button. His touch was gentle. Almost kind.

"I'll handle deployment activation," Morrison said. "You're dismissed from operational command."

He pushed her aside. Not roughly. Just... efficiently. The way you'd move an obstacle.

Morrison's hand touched the button. Didn't hesitate. Didn't shake.

"Deploying in ninety minutes," he said. "All hubs. No delays."

His smile was the coldest thing Wells had ever seen.

"THRESHOLD is live."
