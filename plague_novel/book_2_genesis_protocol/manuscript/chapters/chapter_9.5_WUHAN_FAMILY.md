# CHAPTER 9.5 - ONE FAMILY

**Five Days After THRESHOLD Deployment**

## Scene 1: The Footage

### Hartford Safehouse - March 2020

Sarah sat in front of Martin's laptop, staring at news footage from Wuhan. Five days since three hubs deployed simultaneously. Five days since THRESHOLD became the pandemic the world thought was natural.

The numbers were already overwhelming: 47,000 confirmed cases. 2,100 deaths. Exponential growth across three continents.

But numbers didn't make you feel it.

Martin entered carrying coffee. Set it beside her without speaking. He'd learned over the past five days that Sarah needed to watch. Needed to see what her partial victory had cost.

"Three hundred ten million people are alive because you stopped the other hubs," he'd said yesterday.

Sarah hadn't responded. Because 287 million were dying. And she needed to see them.

The laptop showed BBC coverage: "Wuhan's Huanan Seafood Market remains the suspected origin point. Chinese authorities report hospitals at 340% capacity. WHO declares Public Health Emergency of International Concern."

Sarah clicked through channels. CNN. Al Jazeera. CCTV. All showing the same scenes: overwhelmed hospitals, hazmat-suited medics, families separated at checkpoints.

Then she found it.

A local Wuhan news crew had been documenting "typical families" before lockdown measures intensified. The segment was titled "Life Before the Outbreak: Stories from Huanan Market District."

The first family shown: Lin family. Father Lin Wei, 42. Mother Chen Jing, 39. Daughter Lin Mei, 8. Son Lin Jun, 11.

The footage was dated February 3, 2020—three days before deployment.

Sarah watched.

---

## Scene 2: February 3, 2020 - Before

The camera followed Lin Wei through Huanan Market. Early morning. He was buying groceries for the week—vegetables, chicken, rice. His daughter Mei held his hand, chattering about school.

"Baba, can we get dumplings for dinner?"

"If your mother says yes."

"Mama always says yes when you ask."

Lin Wei laughed. The camera caught it—a father's easy warmth with his child.

They stopped at a poultry stall. Lin Wei negotiated price with the vendor. Mei watched the chickens in their cages, fascinated.

The voiceover explained: "The Lin family has lived in Wuhan for three generations. Lin Wei works as an accountant. His wife Chen Jing is a middle school teacher. They have two children and live in a modest apartment near the market."

Cut to their apartment. Chen Jing was helping Jun with homework. Math problems. He was frustrated.

"I don't understand why we need to learn this."

"Because understanding patterns helps you see how the world works."

"But when will I use derivatives in real life?"

Chen Jing smiled. "Maybe never. But learning to think clearly—that you'll use every day."

The camera captured ordinary moments: Mei practicing piano. Jun playing video games. Lin Wei cooking dinner while Chen Jing graded papers. A family being a family.

The segment ended with them eating dinner together. Laughing. Teasing each other. Normal.

The reporter's final line: "For families like the Lins, life in Wuhan continues despite concerns about the novel respiratory illness emerging in the city. Most residents remain unconcerned, viewing it as similar to previous flu seasons."

Date stamp: February 3, 2020, 7:32 PM.

THRESHOLD would deploy in 62 hours.

Sarah's hands were shaking.

---

## Scene 3: February 8, 2020 - Day 3

The news crew returned five days later for a follow-up segment: "The Same Families, Five Days Later."

Chen Jing was sick.

The camera showed her in their apartment, wearing a mask, lying on the couch. Fever of 38.9°C. Dry cough. Fatigue.

"It started two days ago," Lin Wei explained to the camera. His voice was steady but his eyes showed worry. "We thought it was normal flu. But the fever won't break."

Mei sat across the room, watching her mother. "Is Mama going to be okay?"

"Of course." Lin Wei's voice was certain. "It's just flu. She'll be better in a few days."

But the camera caught Chen Jing's breathing. Slightly labored. She was trying to hide it from the children.

The reporter asked: "Have you considered going to the hospital?"

"Hospitals are overwhelmed," Lin Wei said. "Wait times are eight hours. We're managing at home for now. If it gets worse, we'll go."

Cut to Jun in his room, playing video games alone. The reporter interviewed him.

"Are you scared?"

"A little. But Baba says Mama will be fine."

"What if she's not?"

Jun looked at the camera. Eleven years old. Old enough to understand but young enough to still trust adults completely.

"She will be. She has to be."

---

## Scene 4: February 10, 2020 - Day 5

Both parents were sick now.

The news crew filmed from outside the apartment—quarantine protocols were intensifying. The camera showed Lin Wei through the window, wearing a mask, tending to Chen Jing. Both were bedridden. Fever. Cough. Breathing difficulties.

Mei and Jun were in a separate room, isolated from their parents. Mei was crying. Jun was trying to be brave.

A neighbor brought food, left it outside the door. No one entered.

The camera caught audio through an open window:

Lin Wei on the phone with emergency services: "Both of us. Fever above 39°C. Can't breathe properly. Please, we need ambulance."

Response (speaker phone): "Current wait time for ambulance dispatch is 14 hours. If condition becomes critical, call back and you'll be prioritized."

"It IS critical!"

"Sir, we have 3,000 critical cases right now. You're conscious and speaking. You're not in immediate danger of death. We'll get to you as soon as possible."

Lin Wei hung up. Put his head in his hands.

Through the window, the camera saw him straighten. Check on Chen Jing. Hold her hand. Then check on the children through their door.

"How are you feeling?"

"Scared," Mei said.

"I know. But we're going to be fine. The hospital will send ambulance soon."

"When?"

"Soon."

The reporter's voiceover: "For the Lin family and thousands like them, Wuhan's medical system has become overwhelmed. Hospitals are turning away patients. Ambulances are running 12-hour delays. Families are managing alone, hoping they'll recover before it gets worse."

---

## Scene 5: February 12, 2020 - Day 7

Ambulance came.

The news crew filmed from across the street. Two ambulances. Hazmat-suited medics entering the Lin apartment with stretchers.

They brought out Lin Wei first. Oxygen mask. Barely conscious.

Then Chen Jing. Oxygen mask. Not moving.

The children watched from the window. Mei was crying. Jun had his arm around her.

The camera zoomed in on their faces. Both terrified. Both trying to be brave.

A neighbor—wearing full PPE—was coordinating with the medics. Taking custody of the children until relatives could be contacted.

"Mama!" Mei screamed through the window.

Chen Jing didn't respond. Couldn't respond. The oxygen mask covered her face and she was barely conscious as they loaded her into the ambulance.

Lin Wei tried to turn his head toward the window. Tried to see his children one more time. The medics prevented him—moving too much strained his already compromised breathing.

The ambulances left.

Mei and Jun stood at the window, watching them go.

The reporter's voiceover was quiet: "Lin Wei and Chen Jing were admitted to Wuhan Central Hospital at 3:47 PM on February 12th. Their children, Mei and Jun, were placed in neighbor care pending family reunification."

---

## Scene 6: February 14, 2020 - Day 9

The news crew interviewed the neighbor who'd taken the children. She wore mask and gloves, maintaining distance even while caring for them.

"They ask about their parents every hour. I tell them they're getting treatment. That they'll be home soon."

"Is that true?"

The neighbor's eyes above her mask showed pain. "I don't know. Hospital stopped giving me updates. They're overwhelmed."

Cut to Mei and Jun in the neighbor's apartment. Eating quietly. Not speaking much. Mei was crying softly. Jun was staring at nothing.

The reporter tried to interview them through the door.

"How are you both doing?"

Jun answered: "We want our parents back."

"They're at the hospital getting treatment—"

"I know what that means." Jun's voice was flat. Older than eleven. "I watch the news. I know what the mortality rate is. I know—"

He stopped. Couldn't finish.

Mei looked at her brother. "Jun, Mama and Baba are going to be okay. You said—"

"I said what you needed to hear." Jun's voice broke. "I'm sorry, Mei. I don't know if they're okay."

Mei started crying harder.

The neighbor stepped in, ended the interview. Pulled the children away from the camera.

---

## Scene 7: February 16, 2020 - Day 11

The news crew received word that Chen Jing had died.

February 15th, 11:43 PM. Acute respiratory failure. She'd been on a ventilator for two days. It wasn't enough.

Lin Wei was still in ICU, critical condition. He didn't know his wife had died.

The crew filmed the social worker arriving at the neighbor's apartment to tell the children.

The camera stayed outside. Audio only.

"Mei. Jun. I need to tell you something about your mother."

Silence.

"She was very sick. The doctors tried everything they could. But sometimes, even with the best treatment, people don't—"

"She's dead." Jun's voice. Flat. Knowing.

"I'm so sorry."

Mei's scream could be heard through the door. Raw. A child's grief that didn't have words yet.

The camera caught motion through the window—Jun holding his sister. Both crying. The social worker trying to comfort them but maintaining distance because she couldn't risk exposure.

---

## Scene 8: February 18, 2020 - Day 13

Lin Wei died.

February 18th, 4:17 AM. Same cause. Acute respiratory failure. Ventilator wasn't enough.

The hospital reported he'd never regained full consciousness after admission. Never learned his wife had died. Never got to say goodbye to his children.

The news crew filmed the social worker's second visit to tell the children they were orphans.

This time there was no screaming. Just silence.

The reporter's voiceover: "Mei and Jun Lin are among an estimated 43,000 children in Wuhan who have lost one or both parents to the outbreak. Relatives are being contacted, but extended family members are often sick themselves or unable to care for additional children during quarantine."

The final shot showed Mei and Jun sitting together on the neighbor's couch. Not speaking. Not crying. Just sitting.

Two children who'd had parents and a normal life thirteen days ago.

Now orphans in a pandemic that was just beginning.

The segment ended.

---

## Scene 9: The Weight

Sarah sat frozen, staring at the black screen.

Martin stood nearby. He'd watched it too. When he spoke, his voice was quiet.

"That's what 287 million looks like. It's not a number. It's Mei and Jun Lin. Multiplied by 143 million families."

Sarah couldn't speak.

"But you also saved 310 million families from the same thing," Martin continued. "Dubai hub would have deployed. Istanbul hub would have deployed. Doha hub would have deployed. Three more hubs. Each one creating 100 million orphans like Mei and Jun."

"It doesn't feel like saving them," Sarah whispered.

"Because we can't see the people who aren't dying. We only see the ones who are." Martin moved closer. "But somewhere right now, in Dubai, there's a family eating dinner together. A father teaching his daughter math. A mother grading papers. A boy playing video games. And they're alive because you stopped that hub from deploying."

"I stopped three hubs. Three hubs are still killing people."

"Yes. And it's horrible. And you're going to carry Mei and Jun Lin for the rest of your life. But you're also going to carry the knowledge that you prevented 310 million other families from the same thing."

Sarah finally looked at him. "How do you live with that? Knowing you saved some but not all?"

"You don't live with it. You carry it. Forever." Martin's voice was steady. "And you use it to make sure Phase II never happens. You use it to make sure no network ever gets the chance to do this again. That's how you honor the 287 million who died. By making sure the 7 billion who survived never face this again."

Sarah looked back at the black screen. Thinking about Mei and Jun. About 143 million families like them.

About 155 million families in Dubai who were still whole because she'd stopped that deployment.

About David Rivera dying to make that possible. About Carlos before him. About Yuki executed for leaking intelligence. About Marsh dying in a basement.

About all of them choosing to pay the price so the Lin family would be only 287 million instead of 600 million.

"I need to find Wells," Sarah said. "I need to find the remnants. I need to make sure this never happens again."

"We will," Martin said. "Starting tomorrow, we hunt every splinter cell. Every fragment. Every piece of the network that survived. We finish what David and Carlos started."

Sarah nodded. Stood. Looked at the laptop one more time.

"I'm sorry, Mei. I'm sorry, Jun. I'm sorry I couldn't save your parents."

Then she walked out of the room to plan Phase II prevention.

Behind her, Martin closed the laptop.

The Lin family's story was over.

But 143 million families like them were still dying.

And 155 million families who would have died were alive.

That was what partial victory looked like.

Heartbreaking.

Necessary.

Real.
