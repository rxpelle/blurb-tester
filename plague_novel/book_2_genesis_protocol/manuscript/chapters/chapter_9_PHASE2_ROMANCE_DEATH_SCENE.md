### Sublevel Maintenance, 2:08 AM

David heard Sarah's footsteps echoing up the parking structure exit ramp. Running. Free. The sound of everything he'd spent seven months working toward.

Worth it.

He leaned against the boiler room wall, breathing hard. His hands shook—adrenaline from pulling the fire alarm, or maybe just the knowledge that Morrison would be here in less than sixty seconds, and this conversation would end the only way it could.

With David buying time Sarah didn't have.

His phone buzzed. Sarah's last message from two hours ago, still on his screen: *Coffee's waiting. Other side. Real coffee. The kind worth waiting in line for.*

David had replied: *Save me a place in line.*

Lie. Necessary lie. Tactical lie.

Because he'd known even then that there was no "other side" for him. Just this moment. This choice. This trade—his life for Sarah's freedom, for Phase Two intelligence reaching the FBI, for 450 million people who'd never know his name.

Fair trade.

Better trade than Carlos got.

Footsteps pounded down the corridor. Morrison. Exactly on schedule.

David tucked his phone in his pocket. Grabbed the heavy wrench from the maintenance cart. Not much of a weapon. But better than nothing.

Like the coffee. Better than nothing.

*Much better*, Sarah had signed.

His hands still remembered the shape of those words. The way her fingers moved. The way she'd looked at him when he brought that first terrible cup in Week 2—like it was a lifeline instead of burnt vending machine sludge.

The way she'd looked at him in Week 6 when he'd told her about timelines and she'd leaned close enough that he could have kissed her, could have closed that six-inch gap, could have had one perfect moment before—

Morrison burst through the door.

"Rivera." His voice was ice. Professional. Controlled. But his gun was already drawn. "Step away from the alarm. Hands visible."

David didn't move. Kept the wrench low, non-threatening. Buying seconds. "You know why I did this."

"Your brother." Morrison moved closer. Careful. Tactical. "Carlos's death was unfortunate. Necessary. But unfortunate. We would have preferred to bring him into partnership."

"You arranged a car accident because he found eight hubs." David's grip tightened on the wrench. "Just like you're about to arrange mine."

"Last chance. Stand down. Accept containment. We can still use your genetic material after you're dead, but voluntary cooperation is preferable."

David thought about Sarah. Running through sublevel 2 by now. Reaching the medical bay. Finding the exit. Cold air on her face. Freedom in her lungs.

He needed to buy more time.

"You want to know what Sarah stole?" David asked. His voice was conversational now. Almost friendly. Like they were discussing weather. "She didn't just get hub locations. She got Phase Two protocols. All forty-seven pages. Bennett transmitted everything to the FBI before you even realized he'd defected."

Morrison's expression cracked. Just slightly. A hairline fracture in that professional mask. "You're lying."

"Am I?" David's smile was genuine now. Terrible. The smile of a man who'd already won. "Check your servers. Check Morrison's tablet logs. She accessed everything during her final intelligence upload six hours ago."

It was true. Sarah had told him in their last meeting. Signed it via ASL where cameras couldn't see. *P-H-A-S-E T-W-O I-N-T-E-L-L-I-G-E-N-C-E. A-L-L O-F I-T.*

And then she'd signed something else. Something that wasn't tactical or professional or safe: *T-H-A-N-K Y-O-U F-O-R C-O-F-F-E-E*

Such a small thing. Stupid thing. Meaningless in the face of genocide and global deployment and 450 million deaths.

But it had meant everything.

Because underneath the tactical communication and the covert planning and the prison walls, they'd built something Morrison couldn't quantify. Couldn't optimize. Couldn't reduce to genetic compatibility scores.

Six weeks of terrible coffee and quantum mechanics and almost-kisses that never happened because the guard always interrupted at exactly the wrong moment.

Six weeks that should have been a lifetime.

"In every other timeline," David had told her, "we're drinking coffee somewhere. Not prison coffee. Real coffee."

She'd smiled. Sad and beautiful and so goddamn brave it hurt. "Sounds nice."

"It is. Maybe someday."

Someday wasn't coming.

David had known it even then.

Morrison's hand moved to his radio. "Security, verify—"

David rushed him.

It was stupid. Suicidal. Morrison had a gun and David had nothing but fury and seven months of grief over Carlos's death and six weeks of falling in love with a woman he'd never get to kiss properly.

But it bought seconds.

Morrison fired.

The bullet hit David's shoulder—not center mass, Morrison's aim disrupted by David's charge—and pain exploded through him like lightning made flesh. Hot. Immediate. Overwhelming.

But he crashed into Morrison anyway. They went down together. Morrison's head cracked against a pipe—David heard it, felt the impact vibrate through Morrison's body.

The gun skittered across the floor.

David didn't go for it. Went for Morrison's radio instead. Ripped it from his belt, threw it into the boiler. The radio disappeared into fire and steam with a sound like a dying animal.

Morrison punched him. Hard. Professional. David's head snapped back, copper blood flooding his mouth.

"You accomplished nothing," Morrison hissed, face inches from David's. "Chen is still in the facility. We'll find her. Bring her back. Your sacrifice is meaningless."

"No." David grabbed Morrison's collar with his good hand. His left arm hung useless now, blood streaming from the shoulder wound in pulses timed with his heartbeat. "It's not."

Because he could hear it. Distant. But clear.

A car engine. Racing up the parking structure exit ramp.

The sound of victory.

The sound of Sarah living.

David smiled through the pain. Blood on his teeth. Sweat in his eyes. The world going grey at the edges from blood loss. "She's gone. And she has everything. Phase Two protocols. Hub locations. Deployment schedules. Bennett got it all to the FBI. You're finished."

And more than that—Sarah was *free*. Would wake up tomorrow morning somewhere safe. Would drink real coffee (terrible coffee, probably, but real). Would see sunrise. Would *live*.

Would remember six weeks of quantum mechanics and ASL conversations and the moment David had almost kissed her and chickened out because he was an idiot who thought they had more time.

They didn't have more time.

But she had *time*. Had her whole life. Had the chance to stop Phase Two and save billions and maybe, eventually, in some impossible future, drink coffee with someone who deserved her.

Someone who could give her more than six weeks in a prison and a sacrifice she'd carry like grief.

Morrison lunged for the gun.

David tried to stop him. But the shoulder wound had weakened him. Blood loss making everything slow, distant. Morrison was faster.

The gun came up.

David thought about Sarah's hands. The way they moved when she signed. Precise. Beautiful. The only art Morrison's prison allowed.

He thought about her eyes—brilliant, furious, alive.

He thought about the coffee that would never get drunk. The kiss that would never happen. The timeline where they met in a coffee shop instead of a prison, where they had years instead of weeks, where "maybe someday" was a promise instead of a lie.

He thought about Carlos. About how his brother had spent eight months knowing this moment would come. How he'd faced it anyway because some things were worth dying for.

Sarah was worth dying for.

*"In every other timeline,"* David had told her, *"we're drinking coffee somewhere."*

This wasn't that timeline.

But it was the timeline where Sarah lived. Where 450 million people lived. Where Morrison's genocide failed.

Good enough.

Better than nothing.

*Much better.*

Morrison's finger tightened on the trigger. David saw it happening in slow motion—the muscle contracting, the mechanism engaging, the hammer drawing back.

He closed his eyes. Thought of Sarah running free. Thought of her hands signing *C-O-F-F-E-E W-A-I-T-I-N-G*.

His last thought wasn't fear. Wasn't regret.

It was: *In every other timeline, we're drinking coffee. But in this one, you're alive. Fair trade.*

Morrison fired.

The sound was wrong. Not the sharp crack David had expected. Deeper. Like thunder compressed into a single syllable. The kind of sound that rewrites reality.

David felt the impact before he heard it—chest, left side, just below the ribs where his body armor should have been but wasn't because Morrison had made him remove it during Week 3's "voluntary compliance assessment."

No pain at first. Just pressure. Like someone pushing a fist into his sternum. Hard. Insistent.

Then heat.

Not burning. Not fire. Just warmth spreading across his shirt like wine spilled at dinner. Dark. Wet. Growing larger with every heartbeat that pushed more blood out of the hole Morrison's bullet had made.

David looked down. Watched the stain spread. Fascinating, really—the way blood moved through fabric. Following the weave. Finding paths of least resistance. Physics even in dying.

His legs stopped working. He didn't fall so much as fold—knees buckling, torso collapsing, the maintenance room floor rising to meet him with concrete certainty.

The floor was cold. He felt it through his shirt where blood hadn't soaked yet. Temperature differential. His body heat bleeding out, the concrete absorbing it. Entropy. Everything moving toward equilibrium.

Morrison's footsteps. Coming closer. Checking. Professional. Making sure.

David tried to breathe. Tasted copper. Not from the air. From inside. Blood in his lungs. Filling spaces meant for oxygen. Drowning from the inside.

He thought about Sarah. Running through sublevel 2 by now. Reaching the medical bay. Finding the exit. Cold air on her face. Freedom in her lungs.

He'd bought her seventy-two seconds.

Enough.

Better than nothing.

*Much better.*

His vision darkened at the edges. Tunnel vision. The room compressing to a single point of light that was Morrison's face, expressionless, watching him die with the same clinical detachment he used to optimize genocide.

David's last thought wasn't fear.

Wasn't regret.

It was the memory of Sarah's hands moving through ASL signs. Precise. Beautiful. The only art Morrison's prison allowed.

*C-O-F-F-E-E W-A-I-T-I-N-G*, her fingers had spelled.

*In every other timeline*, David thought as the darkness closed in, *we're drinking coffee. But in this one, you're alive.*

The light went out.

Fair trade.

## NEW SCENE: Sarah Realizes (Add After Scene 4: Freedom)

### Scene 5: The Cost (NEW)

**Route 44, Martin's Extraction Point - 2:47 AM**

Sarah made it to the extraction point. Martin was there—two vehicles, Elena in the passenger seat of the lead car, armed resistance members securing the perimeter.

Alive. Free. Safe.

The intelligence would reach the FBI by dawn. Phase Two would be prevented. 450 million people would live.

They'd won.

Sarah collapsed against Martin's car. Breathing hard. Bare feet bleeding from the run. Prison clothes soaked with sweat.

"David?" Elena asked quietly.

Sarah shook her head.

Elena's expression fell. She didn't ask for details. Didn't need to.

The math was simple. David had pulled the fire alarm at 2:00 AM. Sarah had escaped at 2:17 AM. David should have been right behind her if the plan was working.

It was 2:47 AM.

No David.

Sarah stared at her hands. They were shaking. The same hands that had signed *C-O-F-F-E-E W-A-I-T-I-N-G* six hours ago. The same hands that had touched his for half a second in Week 1 and pretended it was accidental.

"He knew," Sarah said quietly. "When he brought me coffee yesterday. When he signed the plan. He knew he wasn't making it out."

"Sarah—"

"He looked at me." Her voice cracked. "The way you look at someone when you're trying to memorize them. Like he was taking a photograph for a future he wouldn't have."

And she'd known. On some level, she'd *known*. But she'd let herself believe the lie. Let herself plan for after. For coffee shops and real coffee and "someday."

"I never told him," Sarah whispered.

"Told him what?"

That in the six weeks she'd known him, David Rivera had become the highlight of her imprisonment. That she'd spent thirteen hours of every day counting down until their meetings. That the coffee was terrible but she'd treasured every drop because *he'd brought it for her*.

That when he'd said "In every other timeline, we're drinking coffee somewhere," her heart had shattered because she wanted that timeline so desperately she could taste it.

That she'd been falling in love with him since Week 3 and was too much of a coward to say it because they were prisoners and he was coerced and it wasn't real except it *was* real, it was the most real thing in that entire facility—

"That I—" Sarah couldn't finish. Couldn't say it to Elena when she'd never said it to David.

Six weeks. That's all they'd had.

Six weeks of coded messages and terrible coffee and a love story that happened in ASL because Morrison was watching and they never got their kiss and now they never would.

"We need to move," Martin said gently. "Morrison will send pursuit teams."

Sarah nodded. Climbed into the vehicle. Let Elena help her with the seatbelt because her hands were shaking too badly to manage it.

As they drove away from Site Seven, Sarah pulled out her phone—returned by Martin, fully charged. Opened her messages.

David's last text was still there: *Save me a place in line.*

Sarah typed a response she'd never send: *I did. I'm still saving it. I'll save it forever.*

Then she deleted it. Unsent. Because David was dead and there was no coffee shop waiting and in this timeline—this specific, terrible timeline—they got six weeks and a sacrifice and nothing more.

But 450 million people were alive because of those six weeks.

Fair trade, David had thought.

Sarah disagreed.

But she'd honor it anyway.
