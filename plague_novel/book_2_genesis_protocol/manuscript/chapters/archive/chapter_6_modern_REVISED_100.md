# CHAPTER 6 - THE STORM

## Modern Timeline - Safe House, Day After Upload

### Morning - The World Reacts

Sarah woke to the sound of her phone having a nervous breakdown.

Not literally—though the constant buzzing and chirping and vibrating made it sound like the device was trying to escape. She'd silenced all notifications before collapsing six hours ago, her body finally giving up after forty-eight hours of running and uploading.

But somehow the phone had turned itself back on. Or the sheer volume of incoming messages had overwhelmed the silence settings.

She grabbed it. The lock screen showed numbers that seemed impossible:

**234 missed calls**
**1,847 text messages**
**6,293 Twitter notifications**
**4,109 emails**

All in the six hours she'd been unconscious.

"You're trending," Martin said from the doorway. He looked like he hadn't slept at all—eyes bloodshot, hair disheveled, coffee mug in one hand and tablet in the other. "Number one globally on Twitter. Your name. 'Genesis Protocol.' 'GenVault Conspiracy.' All of it."

Sarah sat up, every muscle protesting. She'd slept in her clothes—jeans and sweater still smelling of forest and fear.

"Show me," she said, voice rough.

Martin handed her the tablet showing news aggregators pulling coverage from around the world:

**CNN Breaking News**: "GenVault Accused of 70-Year Genetic Manipulation Program"

**BBC World**: "Medieval Cipher Links to Modern Genetics Controversy"

**The New York Times**: "The Aethelred Cipher: Ancient Manuscript or Elaborate Hoax?"

**Al Jazeera**: "Is GenVault Preparing for Civilizational Collapse?"

The coverage was massive. Mainstream media. Serious journalists. Not relegated to fringe websites where uncomfortable truths went to die.

"They're believing it," Sarah said, almost afraid to hope.

"Some of it," Martin corrected. "The genetic manipulation part is documented well enough they can't dismiss it—your data is solid, the bloodline tracking is provable, the corporate connections are real. That part they're taking seriously."

He showed her a segment from a morning news show—two scientists debating with academic intensity.

"The genetic markers are real," the younger scientist was saying. "I've independently verified samples. These patterns shouldn't exist if breeding was random. The correlation is statistically impossible without coordination."

"But correlation isn't causation," the older scientist countered. "Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. This could be normal population genetics, founder effects, selection pressures we don't understand yet."

"Which Dr. Chen has provided," the younger scientist shot back. "Medieval manuscripts with cipher marks correlating to modern genetic data. Corporate records showing coordination. Meeting minutes discussing 'population optimization' and 'threshold percentages.' This isn't speculation—it's documentation."

Sarah watched with growing frustration. They were so close to believing. The evidence was right there. But the truth was too big, too impossible-sounding.

"What about the virus?" she asked. "Project THRESHOLD?"

Martin's expression darkened. He pulled up more coverage:

**Scientific American**: "Genetic Targeting Virus Claims Lack Supporting Evidence"

**Nature**: "Scientists Skeptical of Bioweapon Allegations"

**CDC Statement**: "No Evidence of Weaponized Pathogen in Leaked Materials"

"That's where we're losing them," Martin said heavily. "No one wants to believe a corporation would develop a genocidal bioweapon. It sounds too movie-villain. Too deliberately evil."

He pulled up GenVault's response—Catherine Wells at a podium, calm and professional.

"GenVault categorically denies developing any weaponized virus," Wells was saying. "The documents Dr. Chen leaked refer to theoretical pandemic preparedness research—modeling how a natural outbreak might spread. This is responsible corporate planning, the same scenario modeling governments conduct regularly."

She gestured to charts showing pandemic models, infection curves, containment strategies. "We live in an era of increasing pandemic risk. GenVault has been researching how genetic factors influence disease susceptibility. How we might identify at-risk populations and develop targeted interventions. This isn't eugenics—it's personalized medicine at population scale."

"Fuck," Sarah breathed. "She's good."

"She's had seventy years to prepare for exposure," Martin said grimly. "Multiple contingency plans. Legal teams on retainer. Crisis communication protocols practiced until they're reflexive."

Sarah scrolled through social media, watching public opinion form in real-time:

**#BelieveSarah** trending with 2.3 million tweets.
**#GenVaultHoax** with 1.8 million.

**@ScienceWriter87**: "The genetic data is real. I checked it myself. Those patterns don't lie. #BelieveSarah"

**@SkepticalDoc**: "Correlation isn't causation. Need more evidence before jumping to 700-year plot conclusions. #GenVaultHoax"

**@MotherOf3Kids**: "If this is true, my children might be genetically designed? Our marriage might have been arranged? I feel sick."

"We need more," Sarah said. "Better evidence. Something they can't spin as 'pandemic preparedness.' Something that proves THRESHOLD isn't theoretical but real, weaponized, ready to deploy."

"Like what?" Martin asked, though his expression suggested he already knew.

"Like the virus itself. Physical samples. Documentation showing it's weaponized with deployment plans. Evidence of the 15% threshold." Sarah stood, started pacing. "You said the original network has people inside GenVault. Can they get us proof?"

"Maybe. But security will be maximum after your exposure. They'll lock down hard." Martin pulled up building schematics. "We'd need someone they won't suspect. Someone who benefits from the program. Someone on the protected list."

Sarah stopped pacing. "You want me to infiltrate GenVault."

"It's the only way. And you specifically are the only person who can do it." Martin pulled up screens showing faces—dozens of them. "The original network. People fighting GenVault from inside for decades. You're the catalyst. Now we need you to get the final piece—the smoking gun."

"This is suicide," Sarah said flatly. "They'll be expecting exactly this. Maximum security. Every employee vetted. Every visitor screened."

"Which is why they won't expect you to succeed. What if you don't sneak in? What if you walk in the front door?" Martin pulled up Sarah's genetic profile. "Present yourself as someone who's reconsidered Wells's offer. Claim the media circus made you realize you'd rather be on the winning side."

Sarah stared. "You think they'll believe that?"

"Wells wants to believe you're recruitable. She's invested time trying to flip you. She believes you're smart enough to calculate odds and pragmatic enough to choose survival." Martin gestured to the profile. "You're a product of thirty generations of selection. If you tell her you've calculated the odds and decided to join the survivors, it fits her worldview perfectly."

"And if she sees through it?"

"Then you disappear into their research facility. Become a breeding specimen." Martin's voice was brutally honest. "But that risk exists whether you infiltrate or not. They want you either way. At least this way you get evidence first."

Sarah thought about six billion people who would die if THRESHOLD launched. About Marsh, dead trying to expose this. About Yuki, captured helping her escape.

Martin's phone buzzed. He glanced at the screen, and his expression shifted—surprise mixed with concern.

"What?" Sarah asked.

"Encrypted message. From inside GenVault." He read quickly, fingers swiping through what looked like a long text. "It's... Yuki Tanaka."

Sarah's breath caught. "She's alive?"

"Alive. In custody. Under interrogation." Martin continued reading. "But she's still got limited network access—they haven't realized she backdoored the secure messaging system before they arrested her." He looked up. "She's sending intelligence. THRESHOLD deployment locations."

"What does she say?"

Martin pulled up a map on his tablet, overlaying Yuki's data. Red dots appeared across major cities: "Twelve hubs. International airports. Beijing Capital, Heathrow, Dubai, JFK, LAX, Frankfurt, Singapore, Tokyo Narita, São Paulo, Mumbai, Sydney, Johannesburg. They're planning coordinated releases. Same day, same hour. Maximum global saturation before containment protocols can activate."

Sarah stared at the dots. Twelve simultaneous bioweapon releases across six continents. No way to stop all of them, even if authorities believed the threat.

"She included security protocols for each hub. Guard rotations. Access codes. Storage locations for the virus samples." Martin scrolled through the data. "This is... incredibly detailed. They're going to realize she sent this. When they do—"

"They'll kill her," Sarah finished.

"Probably." Martin's jaw tightened. "But she's doing it anyway. Says she'd rather die sabotaging them than live knowing she stayed silent."

Just like her grandmother. Just like Marsh.

Another person choosing to sacrifice everything to stop this.

"Can we use it?" Sarah asked. "The hub locations?"

"Maybe. If we can get the CDC or WHO to take it seriously. If we can provide enough corroborating evidence that they initiate inspections." Martin saved the data to multiple encrypted locations. "But this is gold. Real intelligence from inside their deployment planning. Yuki just gave us a roadmap to stopping THRESHOLD—if we can move fast enough."

Sarah felt the weight settle heavier. Yuki was risking execution to provide this intelligence. The least Sarah could do was make sure that sacrifice mattered.

"When?" she asked.

"Three days. Time to prepare, coordinate with the inside team, build your cover story. Then you walk into GenVault and either get the evidence or die trying."

"Not reassuring."

"It's honest. I'm done lying about the risks." Martin stood. "For now, rest more. Watch the coverage. Understand what you've created. The world is reacting. That reaction will determine whether we have a chance."

## Scene 2: Wells's Call

### Afternoon

By 2 PM, the narrative had shifted. GenVault wasn't denying the genetic research anymore—they were justifying it.

Wells gave a second press conference, more detailed, clearly prepared by crisis management teams.

"We're not denying GenVault has conducted controversial genetic research," Wells said, standing before graphs showing climate change, pandemic curves, resource depletion. "But we need to be clear about context."

She pulled up climate data. "We are living through multiple civilizational-level crises. Climate change. Pandemic potential. Resource depletion. These aren't theoretical future problems—they're current realities converging in our lifetimes."

More graphs. Sarah recognized legitimate climate science, pandemic modeling, economic analysis. All real data. All genuinely concerning.

"GenVault has been researching which genetic factors correlate with survival and recovery from major disruptions," Wells continued. "Not to create a master race—that's a grotesque mischaracterization. But to understand how we might help humanity survive what's coming. To identify populations who might need additional support."

It was compelling. Frighteningly so. Sarah watched the comment section shift, people being convinced by the reframing.

"She's winning," Sarah said. "Taking my evidence and spinning it as controversial-but-necessary. Making people debate ethics versus survival. And in that debate, ethics usually loses."

"Not if we prove THRESHOLD is real," Martin said. "You can't spin 'we're releasing a bioweapon that kills six billion people' into responsible planning."

Sarah's burner phone rang. Unknown number.

She answered cautiously. "Hello?"

"Dr. Chen." Catherine Wells's voice, smooth as expensive whiskey. "I've been watching the news. Quite a spectacle. The media loves a good conspiracy theory."

Sarah's hand tightened. "You let me create it. You wanted this public."

"I did. And now you're learning the lesson I wanted you to learn." Wells's voice carried satisfaction. "The truth is too big. Too complex. Too frightening. People can't process it without their worldview shattering. So they retreat into simpler narratives. Corporate corruption—believable. Genetic research gone too far—plausible. A few executives making questionable decisions—scandalous but manageable."

She paused. "But a 700-year conspiracy? Coordinated breeding across continents? Seven corporations in perfect coordination? Too big. Too implausible. Too movie-plot to fit into ordinary cognitive frameworks. So they reject it. Not because it's false, but because accepting it requires rebuilding their entire understanding of reality. That's too much cognitive effort."

"I'm not done," Sarah said, trying to keep her voice steady.

"I know. That's why I'm calling." Wells's tone shifted, became almost gentle. "Your forty-eight hours aren't up. The offer stands. Join us. Use your intelligence for something meaningful instead of futile resistance that's only damaging your reputation and mental health."

"I recorded our conversation. Released it. Everyone knows what you're planning."

"Everyone knows one interpretation of selected facts," Wells corrected. "Not the full picture. Not the mathematical certainty that collapse is coming whether we act or not." Her voice took on a persuasive quality. "Sarah, you're smart enough to see the pattern. Civilizations collapse every 600-800 years. Bronze Age. Roman. Medieval plague. We're overdue. Unlike our ancestors, we can choose who survives the reset. That's not evil. That's pragmatic mercy."

"It's genocide dressed as preservation."

"It's survival dressed as choice," Wells countered. "The collapse is coming whether we engineer it or not. Climate change, pandemic, resource wars—pick your catastrophe. We're ensuring humanity survives in some form instead of descending into barbarism. Ensuring the survivors can rebuild properly."

She let that hang. "I'm extending the deadline. One week. Think about what you want your legacy to be. Died fighting the inevitable? Or helped shape what comes after? Ensured your bloodline contributes to humanity's survival?"

"I'm not joining you."

"We'll see. You're a pattern recognition specialist. Eventually, you'll recognize the pattern: You can't stop us. Can't even significantly slow us down. But you can choose which side of history you're on." Wells paused. "One week. After that, we proceed with or without you. I hope you make the right choice. You're too valuable to waste."

The line went dead.

Sarah stared at the phone.

"What did she say?" Martin asked.

"She's confident. Too confident. She thinks the story is out but neutered." Sarah set down the phone. "She thinks I've shot my shot and missed."

"Have you?"

Sarah looked at the screens—the story slowly being buried under analysis and counter-narrative. "Maybe. But I'm not done. We need proof of THRESHOLD. Real, physical, irrefutable evidence."

She pulled up her laptop, started researching GenVault's facility using public records, building permits, employee posts—anything giving insight into the physical structure.

"Still planning to walk in there?" Martin asked.

"Not planning. Doing." Sarah's hands moved across the keyboard with focused intensity. "We have maybe a week before they hit 15% and feel secure enough to deploy. I need to be inside before then."

Martin sat down heavily. "This is insane."

"This whole situation is insane. I'm just matching the level of crazy required to stop it." Sarah scrolled through diagrams and security contracts. "Tell me about the original network. I need to know who I'm working with."

Martin pulled up files. "Fifteen people. Former GenVault employees—researchers who got uncomfortable, security personnel who saw too much. Plus historians tracking the network's evolution, descendants of medieval bloodlines who resisted recruitment."

He showed her faces. Sarah tried to memorize them.

"When do I make contact?" she asked.

"Tomorrow evening. Let them feel confident one more day. Let Wells feel like her counter-narrative is working. Then you call her. Say watching the media dismiss your evidence made you realize you'd rather be on the winning side." Martin met her eyes. "You'll need to sell it. Really convince her. She's smart—she'll be looking for deception."

Sarah nodded. Tomorrow she'd call Catherine Wells and pretend to join the conspiracy planning to kill six billion people.

Then she'd infiltrate their facility, steal proof of the genocidal bioweapon, and escape before they realized she'd betrayed them.

Simple.

Sarah almost laughed at the absurdity.

But she was done laughing.

Tomorrow, the real work began.
