> *"What most people don't know about the Black Death: it killed between 75 and 200 million people, but mortality rates varied wildly by region. London: 60% mortality. Milan: 15% mortality. Warsaw: 25% mortality. For centuries, historians assumed this was due to quarantine effectiveness or population density. They were wrong. It was genetics. The plague killed people with specific CCR5 gene variants and spared those without them. Someone knew this in 1347. And used it."*
>
> — Dr. Evelyn Marsh, "The Genesis Protocol: 3,000 Years of Engineered Selection" (unpublished manuscript, 2019)

# Chapter 2: The Revelation

**Modern Timeline - New Haven, Connecticut**
**November 2019**

---

## Scene 1: Arrival

### Greyhound Station, New Haven
### Wednesday, November 15, 2019 - 6:47 AM

Sarah stepped off the bus into gray dawn light that made everything look temporary, like a photograph fading in the sun.

The station smelled like diesel and burnt coffee. A handful of passengers shuffled toward exits with the blank expressions of people who'd been traveling all night. Sarah moved with them. Invisible.

Or so she hoped.

She'd spent four hours watching every car on I-91, tracking which stayed in view too long. Pattern recognition. Her brain doing what it was trained to do—finding threats the way it found genetic sequences.

First priority: stop looking like a fugitive.

The 24-hour diner two blocks over had harsh fluorescent lights and vinyl booths worn smooth by decades. Sarah ordered coffee, black. Found a corner booth with a clear view of both the door and the street.

The coffee tasted like burned dirt. She wrapped both hands around the chipped mug anyway, grateful for warmth.

Think. Plan. Prioritize.

1. Find Dr. Evelyn Marsh
2. Get answers about the cipher
3. Figure out what GenVault was actually doing
4. Survive

She caught her reflection in the window. Still screamed *scientist*. Practical slacks, sensible shoes. And—stupid—her GenVault lanyard was still around her neck.

Sarah yanked it off, shoved it deep in her pocket.

Across the street: Goodwill thrift store.

Twenty minutes later, she emerged in jeans that almost fit, a dark gray hoodie, cheap canvas sneakers. In the bathroom mirror, she'd cut her hair shorter with sewing scissors. Rough, uneven. Different.

Not Dr. Sarah Chen, GenVault geneticist.

Just Sarah. Nobody.

---

### Residential New Haven - 9:15 AM

The address led to a quiet neighborhood. Tree-lined streets, middle-class houses, basketball hoops over garage doors. Places where neighbors noticed strangers.

Sarah walked past 47 Elm Street twice, observing.

Two-story colonial. White clapboard, black shutters. Well-maintained but not showing off. A car in the driveway—old Honda Civic, primer-spotted. All curtains drawn despite it being mid-morning.

And across the street, parked three houses down: a dark sedan with tinted windows.

Sarah's pulse spiked.

The sedan had been there both times she'd passed. Engine off, but someone was inside—she could see the faint glow of a phone screen. Just sitting. Watching.

Watching Marsh's house.

*Morrison*, Sarah thought. He let me escape. Let me lead him straight to Marsh.

She kept walking. Circled the block.

Options:
1. Front door → get grabbed
2. Leave → abandon only lead
3. Find another way in

The neighborhood backed onto a small greenbelt. Sarah found a gap in the chain-link fence two streets over and slipped through.

Dead leaves crunched under her sneakers. She counted houses through bare branches. One. Two. Three. That should be Marsh's—yes, the white colonial, approached from the back where the sedan couldn't see.

Sarah crossed the backyard quickly, expecting shouts.

Nothing. Just November wind rattling branches.

She knocked on the back door. Soft.

Silence.

Knocked again. Louder.

"Go away or I'll call the police." Woman's voice. Sharp. Afraid.

Sarah pressed close to the door, keeping her voice low. "Dr. Marsh? My name is Sarah Chen. Dr. Otto Aris sent me. He said to tell you it's about the Aethelred manuscripts."

Long silence. Then: "Otto's dead. Has been for years."

"No, he's alive. Hiding. He helped me escape GenVault last night." Sarah's voice shook. "Please. They're hunting me. There's surveillance across the street. You're the only person who can help."

More silence. Someone on the other side, listening. Weighing. Deciding.

Finally, locks began to click. One. Two. Three. Four. A chain sliding free.

The door opened a crack.

A face peered out—woman, probably seventy, with wild gray hair and wire-rimmed glasses. Eyes sharp, bright, assessing.

"You're Chinese," the woman said.

"Chinese-American. Sarah Chen."

"Chen." The woman's eyes narrowed. "Your family from Guangdong province?"

Sarah blinked. "How did you—"

"Because the cipher tracked eleven families from Guangdong. Marked them since the 1300s for specific genetic markers." Dr. Marsh opened the door wider. "Get inside. Quickly."

Sarah stepped into a dim kitchen that smelled like old coffee and dust.

Marsh bolted the door immediately. All four locks, plus the chain. Then turned and studied Sarah with unsettling intensity.

Up close, Dr. Evelyn Marsh looked like she'd been living underground for forty years. Gaunt, hands stained with ink and maybe coffee. She wore layers—cardigan over sweater over thermal shirt. Hair more white than gray, secured with a pencil.

The house smelled like old books and paranoia.

"Dr. Aris really sent you?" Marsh asked.

"Last night. Maybe two AM. He gave me photocopied manuscripts. Told me about the cipher. Said you had the complete picture."

"The sedan." Marsh laughed—sharp, bitter. "GenVault?"

"I think so. They let me escape. I led them straight to you."

"Of course they did." Marsh walked deeper into the house. "They've been watching me for forty-two years. Waiting to see if I'd lead them to the documents I stole in 1982. Come. Don't touch anything. If I tell you to run, you run. No questions."

"Understood."

Sarah followed through the hallway lined with bookshelves. Medieval history next to population genetics. Ancient language grammars beside statistical books. Into a study that looked like an explosion in a research library.

Papers covered every surface. Manuscripts—photocopies and original vellum in special protective sleeves. A massive cork board dominated one wall, covered with photographs and documents connected by colored string. Maps with pins. Genetic charts. Population graphs. Mathematical equations in margins.

Obsession made physical.

"Welcome to forty years of my life," Marsh said. "Tea?"

"I... yes?"

Marsh disappeared into the kitchen. Sarah heard a kettle, a gas stove lighting.

She stood in the study, overwhelmed. The cork board alone—medieval manuscripts connected by red string to modern genetic data, migration maps, family trees going back centuries, news clippings.

And there, center of the web: a photograph of a medieval manuscript page. The marginal dots clearly visible. The same cipher pattern Sarah had found.

Marsh returned with two chipped mugs. The tea smelled like chamomile and something medicinal.

"Sit." Marsh gestured to a chair buried under papers.

Sarah carefully moved them—photocopies of 14th-century merchant ledgers—and sat.

Marsh remained standing, studying her. "Otto said you found the connection. The impossible one."

"Ninety-four percent accuracy," Sarah confirmed. "Protective genetic markers mapping perfectly onto 14th-century manuscript notations."

"And you realized that's impossible."

"Unless the cipher-makers had knowledge they couldn't possibly have possessed." Sarah set her tea down. "Dr. Marsh, what is the Aethelred Cipher?"

Marsh smiled—sharp, humorless. "You're asking the wrong question. The right question is: What were the cipher-makers really doing?"

"Then what were they doing?"

"Engineering civilization." Marsh pulled out a manuscript from a protective sleeve. Original vellum. "This is from 1338. Nine years before the Black Death reached Europe."

She laid it flat on the desk. Sarah leaned closer, studying the red and blue dots around Latin text discussing trade routes.

"The cipher-makers weren't predicting who would survive the plague," Marsh said. "They were *engineering* who survived. Positioning resilient bloodlines. Tracking inheritance patterns. Guiding who married whom across centuries."

She pulled out Sarah's genetic chart—when had she gotten that?

"The Chen bloodline. Twelve generations back to Guangdong." Marsh's finger traced highlighted sections. "Protective genes, pattern recognition traits, spatial memory—all marked in the medieval manuscripts three hundred years before your ancestors were born."

Sarah stared at the overlay. Medieval dots perfectly aligned with her family's genetic markers.

"They bred us," Sarah whispered. "Like livestock."

"For seven hundred years." Marsh's voice was clinical. "But here's what GenVault doesn't want you to know."

She pulled out another document. Ancient papyrus, protected in clear sleeves.

"The medieval cipher wasn't original. It was *corrupted*."

"Corrupted from what?"

Marsh's expression changed. "From something that was supposed to save humanity. Not engineer its collapse."

---

## Scene 2: The Truth

Marsh set down her tea carefully. "What I'm about to tell you, GenVault has spent seventy years suppressing. Before that, the network spent two thousand years keeping secret."

She pulled out the papyrus fragments.

"Bronze Age Egypt. 1200 BCE. When Mediterranean civilization collapsed—ninety percent gone in fifty years. Drought. Famine. War. The greatest catastrophe in recorded history."

Sarah leaned forward.

"In that chaos, Egyptian scholars created something revolutionary. They called it the Genesis Protocol. A system to encode knowledge about collapse—how to recognize the patterns, how to prevent it."

"Wait." Sarah held up a hand. "The Genesis Protocol was supposed to *prevent* collapse?"

"Yes." Marsh pulled out translation notes. "A woman named Nefertari designed it. The idea was to identify families who showed pattern recognition, systems thinking, crisis management traits. Preserve that knowledge through genetic memory. So the next time collapse threatened, humanity would recognize it and stop it."

"Genetic memory isn't—"

"Isn't possible according to our current understanding," Marsh interrupted. "But the Bronze Age scholars thought it was. And they built a system around it. Track bloodlines. Encode knowledge. Create a distributed intelligence that would survive catastrophe."

She wrote on a blank sheet:

**ORIGINAL PURPOSE - Defensive Protocol:**
- Recognize collapse patterns
- Preserve crisis response knowledge
- PREVENT future catastrophes

"But within a generation," Marsh continued, "the network split. Nefertari's defensive faction scattered. And a man named Amenhotep corrupted the system."

She wrote beside the first note:

**CORRUPTED PURPOSE - Offensive Protocol:**
- Accept collapse as inevitable
- Breed resilient bloodlines
- CONTROL who survives

"Two thousand years," Marsh said quietly. "Two thousand years of the network engineering humanity for survival through collapse—instead of preventing collapse entirely."

Sarah's mind raced. "The medieval cipher?"

"Offensive mode documents. By 1347, the network had completely suppressed defensive knowledge. They used the Black Death to engineer European genetics. Positioned resistant populations. Let everyone else die."

"And GenVault?"

"Modern continuation of the offensive network." Marsh pulled out corporate documents. "Seven corporations, all descended from medieval cipher families. Still using the system. Still engineering collapse and calling it optimization."

Sarah thought about the patterns she'd found. The impossible correlations. "What happened to the defensive knowledge?"

"Hidden." Marsh pulled out Sarah's genetic data again. "In bloodlines. Like yours. The Chen family descends from Maria of Toledo—who carried fourteen centuries of defensive markers. Pattern recognition. Systems thinking. The ability to see collapse coming and stop it."

"I'm carrying knowledge from the Bronze Age?"

"Encoded in your DNA. Not conscious memory—but predisposition. The reason you saw the cipher pattern when four other researchers missed it. The reason you're standing here instead of cooperating with Morrison."

Sarah felt cold. "Morrison."

"Dr. James Morrison. Network coordinator. Runs GenVault's security apparatus." Marsh's voice hardened. "He's been hunting defensive Protocol carriers for thirty years. Most he kills. A few he tries to convert—show them the 'complete picture,' make them think the offensive network is serving humanity."

"The sedan outside," Sarah said. "He knows I'm here."

"Oh yes." Marsh walked to the window, peered through the curtain. "He's letting this happen. Wants you to learn the truth from me. Thinks truth is a recruitment tool."

"Is it?"

"Depends which truth you believe." Marsh turned back. "The offensive network says collapse is inevitable—so engineer who survives. The defensive Protocol says collapse is preventable—so stop it entirely."

She pulled out more documents. Forty years of research.

"I've spent my life trying to prove the defensive knowledge works. That Nefertari was right—collapse patterns can be recognized and interrupted. That we don't have to accept mass death as optimization."

Marsh handed Sarah a laptop, a camera, manuscript photographs.

"This is everything. The complete history. The Bronze Age origins. The medieval corruption. GenVault's modern continuation. Forty years of documentation."

Sarah's hands trembled accepting the materials.

"You need to get this out," Marsh said. "Write it. Publish it. Show the world that the Genesis Protocol was never supposed to engineer genocide. That there's another way."

"Where do I go?"

"Somewhere remote. You need time to write this properly. To make it airtight." Marsh met her eyes. "I'm seventy-three. Dying—cancer, maybe six months. You're young. Brilliant. You carry defensive Protocol markers."

"Dr. Marsh—"

"Make this matter," Marsh said quietly. "Don't let them bury the truth again. Show the world collapse can be prevented. That humanity has another choice."

Sarah pressed the manuscripts against her chest. Forty years of one woman's lonely crusade.

"I won't let you down," Sarah promised.

"Then go. Before Morrison decides to move."

Sarah headed toward the back door, then stopped. "The iron key. From Dr. Aris."

"Ah." Marsh smiled slightly. "That key opens a safe deposit box in Geneva. Aris's backup documentation. If they kill me, if they destroy my research—you have redundancy. The box number is engraved on the key shaft."

Sarah looked at the key. Found the tiny numbers: 847-2156.

"Go," Marsh said. "Write the truth. Stop them."

Sarah slipped out the back door into cold November morning, manuscripts pressed against her chest.

Behind her, Marsh stood in the doorway, watching.

Neither of them knew it would be the last time.

## Scene 3: The Hunter Closes In

**Dark Sedan - Across From Marsh's House**
**9:47 AM**

Morrison watched Sarah Chen disappear into the greenbelt through high-powered binoculars.

"She has the documents," he said into his phone. "Marsh gave her everything. Laptop, manuscripts, forty years of research."

"Excellent." The voice on the other end was calm. Satisfied. "She now has the complete picture. Offensive and defensive Protocol history. She'll spend the next week trying to understand it."

"And then?"

"Then she'll realize she can't expose us without proof the defensive Protocol actually works. She'll try to infiltrate. Gather tactical intelligence. Make herself useful."

Morrison smiled. "Walking straight into recruitment."

"Exactly. Monitor her location. Let her think she's free. When she makes contact with resistance networks, we'll know everyone involved."

"And Marsh?"

Pause. "Dr. Marsh served her purpose. Chen has the documents. Marsh is redundant."

"Understood." Morrison ended the call.

He pulled up the surveillance tablet. Thermal imaging showed two heat signatures in Marsh's house—Marsh in the study, someone else in the kitchen. Security operative, standing by.

Morrison sent a text message: **Execute after Chen clears 5-mile radius.**

The response came immediately: **Confirmed. Electrical fire. Will look accidental.**

Morrison put away the tablet and started the sedan's engine.

Dr. Sarah Chen thought she was exposing a conspiracy.

What she was actually doing was gathering the evidence that would make her too valuable to kill.

And when she finally understood that—when she realized the offensive network had the resources, the reach, the two-thousand-year institutional knowledge to win any fight she started—she'd realize cooperation was her only option.

The defensive Protocol said collapse could be prevented.

The offensive network was about to prove it wrong.

Morrison pulled away from the curb, already planning the next seventy-two hours.

Let Chen run. Let her write her exposé. Let her believe she was fighting back.

The trap was already closing.

And Dr. Evelyn Marsh—the woman who'd spent forty years proving the defensive Protocol could work—was about to learn the same lesson every carrier learned eventually.

The network always won.
