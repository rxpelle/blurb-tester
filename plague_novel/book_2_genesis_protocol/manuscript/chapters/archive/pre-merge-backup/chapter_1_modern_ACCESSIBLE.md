# CHAPTER 1 - THE PATTERN

**Modern Timeline - Boston, Massachusetts**
**November 15, 2019 - 1:23 AM**

---

Sarah Chen ran.

Through the emergency stairwell, taking steps three at a time, her laptop bag slamming against her hip with every landing. Twelve floors. Her lungs burned. Behind her, the stairwell door crashed open—security, moving faster than she'd expected.

She should have left the evidence behind.

Too late now.

Eighth floor. The flash drive in her jacket pocket felt like it weighed fifty pounds. Seven centuries of genetic manipulation compressed into 64 gigabytes. Proof that the Black Death hadn't been natural. Proof that GenVault wasn't a pharmaceutical company. Proof that Sarah Chen had just become the most dangerous person in Boston.

"Dr. Chen!" Male voice, echoing down the stairwell. Professional. Calm. "Building security. We need you to stop."

Like hell.

Fifth floor. Her ankle turned on a landing and she grabbed the railing, momentum carrying her forward into a stumbling recovery. The laptop bag swung wide, banged into concrete. Please don't let the hard drive crack. Please let the backup files still be intact.

Behind her: footsteps accelerating. Radio crackle. "Subject is fleeing. Repeat, subject is fleeing toward ground level exits."

They weren't treating this like a researcher working late who'd set off a false alarm. They were treating this like a containment breach.

Because that's exactly what it was.

Third floor. Sarah's hand slipped on the metal railing—sweat, fear, the terrible clarity that came from knowing too much. Twenty minutes ago she'd been analyzing medieval manuscripts in a comfortable lab. Now she was running for her life from a company that had spent seventy years hiding evidence of engineered genocide.

Dr. Aris had tried to warn her. His voice on the phone, tight with fear: *"They're listening. You triggered a Level Three security flag. The others who found the pattern are dead."*

Then the call had cut off.

And Marcus Webb—VP of Research Operations, fifty-thousand-dollar watch, dead eyes—had appeared in her lab doorway with security guards and a smile that meant Sarah was never going home.

Ground floor.

She hit the exit bar, burst into the parking garage. Cold November air slapped her face. Her car was forty yards away—black Civic, unremarkable, parked in shadow because she'd worked late enough that the good spots were all taken.

Keys in hand. Sprinting.

The stairwell door exploded open behind her.

"Dr. Chen, stop! This is building security!"

She didn't stop. Reached the Civic, fumbled the key fob, heard the locks click open with a chirp that sounded deafening in the concrete garage.

Slid into the driver's seat. Laptop bag on passenger seat. Engine starting with a cough that felt like it took forever.

A security guard appeared at her window, hand raised. "Ma'am, turn off the vehicle—"

Sarah floored the accelerator.

The Civic shot backward, tires screaming. The guard jumped aside. Sarah cranked the wheel, shifted into drive, and accelerated toward the exit ramp.

In her rearview mirror: three more guards running from the stairwell. One speaking into a radio. One drawing—was that a gun?

Research facility security didn't carry guns.

Unless they weren't actually security.

Sarah took the exit ramp at forty, tires squealing on the spiral. The garage gate was already descending—someone had triggered lockdown. Twenty feet of clearance. Fifteen. Ten.

She ducked instinctively and gunned the engine.

The Civic shot through with inches to spare. The gate scraped across her roof with a metallic shriek that she felt in her teeth.

Then she was out, onto Causeway Street, merging into sparse 1 AM traffic like she hadn't just fled a billion-dollar pharmaceutical company with evidence of crimes that didn't have names yet.

Her hands shook on the wheel. The laptop bag had tipped over, spilling pens across the passenger seat. Her phone buzzed insistently—blocked number, over and over.

They wanted her to answer. Wanted to talk, negotiate, explain why she needed to come back and discuss this misunderstanding like reasonable professionals.

Sarah turned off her phone and threw it out the window.

Couldn't go home—they'd be waiting. Couldn't go to the police—GenVault had connections that went deeper than local law enforcement. Couldn't access her email or bank accounts or any of the digital infrastructure that normal people used to navigate modern life.

Dr. Aris had said: *"Run. Tonight. Don't go home. Don't access your email."*

Where had he been calling from? Where had he gone after the call cut off?

Sarah merged onto I-93 South, no destination in mind except *away*. Her fuel gauge read half-empty. Her bank card was at home in her purse. She had maybe forty dollars cash in her wallet and a flash drive containing evidence that could destroy one of the world's largest pharmaceutical companies.

If anyone believed her.

If she lived long enough to find someone who'd listen.

The city lights blurred past, reflecting off her windshield like stars falling. Sarah Chen—MIT PhD, respected genomics researcher, daughter of immigrants who'd believed absolutely in the promise of American science—was now a fugitive.

Because she'd found a pattern.

Because medieval monks had somehow known which populations would survive the Black Death, and that knowledge had been preserved, refined, weaponized across seven centuries.

Because GenVault wasn't trying to cure diseases.

They were trying to cause them.

Her rearview mirror showed nothing but normal traffic. No pursuit vehicles. No flashing lights. They'd lost her.

For now.

Sarah took the next exit, merged onto smaller roads, kept moving. Eventually she'd need to stop. Find somewhere to hide. Figure out what to do with the flash drive that held evidence of something so vast and terrible her mind still couldn't fully process it.

But first, she just needed to survive the next hour.

Then the hour after that.

Then maybe, if she was smart and lucky and the people chasing her made one single mistake, she'd survive long enough to expose what GenVault had been hiding.

The pattern that connected medieval manuscripts to modern genetics.

The cipher system that predicted plague survival seven hundred years before anyone understood DNA.

The network that had spent two thousand years engineering human evolution like it was livestock breeding.

Sarah Chen drove into the night with shaking hands and evidence that could save the world.

Or get her killed.

Probably both.

## Six Hours Earlier

**GenVault Corporation, Genomics Research Division**
**November 14, 2019 - 11:47 PM**

---

The lab had smelled like every lab Sarah had ever worked in: ethanol, plastic, and the faint electrical burn of equipment running past its prime. After fourteen hours straight, she'd stopped noticing. Her coffee had gone cold an hour ago, bitter film coating the surface.

Dr. Sarah Chen stared at the dual monitors. Left screen: a fourteenth-century manuscript page from the Bodleian Library's digital archive—marginal decorations, tiny colored dots surrounding Latin text about plague mortality. Right screen: her own genetic dataset, population variants in CCR5-Delta32, the mutation that protected against certain blood-borne diseases.

She'd been chasing a hunch for three weeks.

Now, at nearly midnight in an empty lab, the hunch had become certainty.

The dots weren't random.

Sarah overlaid the medieval pattern onto her genetic map, scaling the resolution. Her breath caught.

Perfect alignment.

The colored dots in the manuscript margins—dismissed by historians as decorative flourishes—mapped exactly onto modern genetic variation hotspots. Red dots matched populations with high-frequency protective genes. Blue dots matched populations decimated by plague. Yellow matched middle-ground zones where the disease had killed selectively, leaving genetic signatures visible seven hundred years later.

"That's impossible," she whispered to the empty lab.

She pulled up another manuscript page. Different monastery, different scribe, different century. Same marginal notation system.

She ran the overlay.

Same result.

Her hands trembled as she opened a third document—a merchant's ledger from Mainz, 1347. The "decorative" dots appeared again, alongside what looked like trade routes mapped in fading brown ink.

She aligned it with modern genetic drift patterns.

Ninety-four percent accuracy.

"They knew." Sarah pushed back from her desk, heart hammering. "Somehow they knew which populations would survive."

But how? Medieval monks didn't have PCR machines or gene sequencers. They didn't understand inheritance beyond "like begets like." They certainly couldn't predict which specific genetic variations would protect against *Yersinia pestis*—the bacteria that caused plague.

Unless.

Unless someone had taught them.

Sarah pulled up GenVault's restricted database—the one she technically shouldn't have access to, but Dr. Aris had given her his login credentials six months ago "for emergencies."

She typed the query she'd been avoiding for weeks:

**SEARCH: Population genetic outliers / CCR5-Delta32 / Non-European distribution / Pre-1348 markers**

The results loaded with a soft ping.

CCR5-Delta32 was supposed to be a European mutation, naturally selected by plague exposure over multiple centuries. But GenVault's proprietary dataset showed unexpected high-frequency populations in North Africa, Central Asia, the Levant.

The same regions marked with red dots in the medieval manuscripts.

*Before* the plague had even reached them.

Someone had marked survival populations *before the catastrophe occurred*.

Her phone buzzed. Dr. Otto Aris.

She answered. "Dr. Aris, I know it's late, but I found—"

"Sarah." His voice was wrong. Tight. Afraid. Wind noise and traffic sounds in the background—he was outside, moving. "Hang up this phone. Right now."

She froze. "What?"

"They're listening. They monitor every query to the restricted database in real-time." His voice dropped to barely a whisper. "I saw your access logs. The population variants that don't fit the evolutionary model. The medieval correlation analysis. You triggered a Level Three security flag."

"I was just researching—"

"I know what you were doing. I know what you found." A car door slammed. "You think you're the first?"

Sarah's office suddenly felt very small. The security camera in the corner—when had that little red light started blinking?

"Dr. Aris, what are you talking about?"

"Twenty years ago, I found the same pattern. The same impossible connection between medieval annotations and modern genetic data." His breathing was ragged. "I published a preliminary paper. GenVault acquired my lab six months later. Buried my research. Made me head of a department that doesn't do anything real."

"That doesn't make sense. Why would they—"

"Because it's not a discovery, Sarah. It's a *map*." His voice cracked. "The founders of GenVault, the private equity backers—they have the original manuscripts. The complete cipher system. They've been using it to select for specific genetic traits for seventy years."

This was insane. Paranoid. The kind of thing you heard from conspiracy theorists.

But Dr. Aris was the most rational person she'd ever worked with. Published in *Nature* seven times. The man who'd taught her that science meant following evidence wherever it led.

"That's..." She couldn't finish the sentence.

"I'm sorry," Aris said quietly. "I hoped you wouldn't look. But you're too curious. Too good. And now they know you've seen it."

The fluorescent lights seemed brighter. The lab felt exposed.

"What do I do?"

"Run. Tonight. Don't go home. Don't access your email. Don't—"

The call cut off mid-sentence.

Sarah stared at her phone. Then at her monitors, where seven centuries of genetic manipulation glowed in red and blue and yellow dots.

She had maybe ten minutes before building security checked the late-night lab access logs.

Her research. She couldn't leave it.

Sarah grabbed a flash drive from her drawer—hallway lights outside flickered on. Footsteps? Or just the cleaning crew?

She jammed the drive into the USB port, started copying files. The manuscript overlays. Her genetic analysis. The restricted database queries.

*Progress: 23%... 34%...*

The lab door opened.

Marcus Webb stood in the doorway. VP of Research Operations. Expensive charcoal suit at midnight, shirt still crisp. Two security guards behind him, radios crackling.

"Dr. Chen." His smile didn't reach his eyes. "Working late?"

"Just finishing some data analysis." Her hand closed around the flash drive, still transferring. *Progress: 67%.*

"On medieval manuscripts?" Marcus walked into the lab, hands in pockets. Casual. Threatening. "Interesting pivot from your assigned project."

"Following a hunch about population genetics. Probably nothing."

"Probably." He glanced at her monitors. Something flickered behind his eyes—recognition. "Still, we should discuss it. Conference room. Now."

Not a request.

*Progress: 81%.*

"Of course." Sarah's voice was steadier than she felt. "Let me just save my work—"

"The guards will secure your station. Security protocol for after-hours restricted database access." Marcus gestured to the door. "Please."

*Progress: 89%.*

If she refused, they'd know she understood exactly what was happening. If she went, they'd wipe her computer before the transfer finished and she'd have nothing.

*Progress: 94%.*

Sarah looked at Marcus Webb. At his patient, predatory smile.

*Progress: 97%.*

She yanked the flash drive from the USB port.

"Actually," Sarah said, "I need to use the restroom first."

She grabbed her shoulder bag, leaving her phone and access badge on the desk like Marcus had instructed. Walked past him toward the door.

The taller guard moved to escort her.

That's when Sarah ran.

## Scene 2: The Rendezvous

Sarah drove south on I-93, hands still shaking, checking her mirrors every few seconds. No pursuit. Either they'd lost her in the garage chaos, or they were tracking her and waiting for the right moment.

She took the next exit, doubled back on smaller roads, kept moving. Her gas gauge showed a quarter tank. She had maybe an hour before she'd need to stop.

Where was Aris? Had he escaped? Was he—

Her burner instinct kicked in. She circled back toward GenVault's neighborhood, keeping to side streets. If Aris was trying to find her, he'd expect her to—

Headlights swept across her windshield. A car screeched to a stop in front of her, blocking the narrow street. Diesel exhaust, acrid.

Sarah's foot hit the brake.

The passenger window rolled down. Dr. Otto Aris looked out, his normally composed face haggard. He'd aged ten years since this afternoon.

"Get in. Leave your car. Now."

Sarah didn't hesitate. She grabbed her laptop bag, left the Civic running with keys in the ignition, and threw herself into the passenger seat. The interior smelled like old coffee and stale cigarette smoke—Aris had quit five years ago, but the car remembered.

Aris accelerated before she'd closed the door.

---

## Scene 3: The Truth She Wasn't Ready For

They drove in silence for ten minutes. Aris checked his mirrors constantly, took random turns, doubled back twice. Classic surveillance detection. Sarah watched her side mirror, looking for headlights that followed too consistently.

Finally, he pulled into a 24-hour diner parking lot. Killed the engine. The car ticked as it cooled, metal contracting.

Through the diner windows, Sarah could see normal life continuing: a tired waitress refilling coffee, a drunk couple laughing at the counter, a trucker hunched over apple pie. People who had no idea that the world was not what they thought it was.

"Are we safe?" Sarah asked.

"For about twenty minutes." Aris looked at her. Up close, he looked exhausted. Defeated. "Did you finish the data transfer?"

"No. Maybe got eighty-nine percent."

He nodded slowly. "Doesn't matter. They'll purge it anyway. Probably scrubbing your entire directory right now." He pulled a worn leather satchel from the back seat. "The question is: are you ready for this?"

"Ready for what?"

"The truth. The real reason I gave you database access. The reason I've been leaving medieval manuscripts on your desk for six months, hoping you'd notice the pattern without me having to explicitly show you."

Sarah's head spun. "You... you wanted me to find this?"

"I needed someone to confirm what I'd found twenty years ago. Someone brilliant enough to see it. Someone innocent enough that GenVault wouldn't be watching." Aris's hands shook slightly. "I'm sorry. I used you. But I had to know if I was insane or if it was real."

He opened the satchel. Inside: photocopied manuscripts, genealogical charts, genetic data, population maps. Years of work.

"What is this?"

"Twenty years of research they think I abandoned." Aris spread papers on the dashboard. "The medieval cipher isn't just correlation, Sarah. It's causation."

"That's impossible. You can't cause genetic variation across populations without—" She stopped.

"Without deliberate intervention across multiple generations," Aris finished. "Exactly."

He pulled out a manuscript page—1338, nine years before the Black Death reached Europe. Red dots marked specific villages in northern Italy.

"Now look at modern genetic sampling from those exact locations."

Sarah leaned in. The data was undeniable. The villages marked in red had the highest frequency of CCR5-Delta32 in all of Europe.

*Before the plague arrived.*

"Someone was tracking which populations would survive," Sarah whispered. "Before the selective pressure even occurred."

"Not just tracking." Aris flipped to another document—a genealogical chart from medieval Mainz, annotated with symbols that looked like the marginal dots. "Guiding. Preserving. Optimizing."

"But that would require—"

"A conspiracy spanning centuries. Yes." Aris pulled out more documents. Each one connecting to the next like a web. "The medieval cipher-makers weren't predicting plague survival. They were *ensuring* it. Positioning resilient populations. Tracking bloodlines. Encoding knowledge that would preserve civilization through collapse."

A car pulled into the lot. Both of them tensed. Just a night-shift worker getting coffee.

"How is this possible?" Sarah's voice was barely a whisper.

"I don't know the mechanism. Maybe experimental methods we'd call genetic engineering. Maybe sophisticated statistical observation over dozens of generations. Maybe—" He hesitated. "Maybe they knew things we've forgotten. Maybe there was a previous technological civilization that collapsed, and they preserved the knowledge."

"That's insane."

"Is it?" Aris met her eyes. "Humans have been around for 200,000 years. We think civilization started 5,000 years ago. But what if that's just when we *recovered* from a previous collapse? What if the medieval cipher-makers were protecting the last remnants of much older knowledge?"

Sarah's hands shook. The diner's neon sign reflected in the car windows—red and blue, pulsing like the dots in the manuscripts.

"We have to expose them."

"With what evidence?" Aris gestured at the papers. "Photocopies? Correlations? GenVault's legal team would bury this before you finished the press release. And we'd both disappear."

"Then what do we do?"

Aris pulled out a piece of paper. An address, written in careful block letters:

**Dr. Evelyn Marsh**
**47 Elm Street, New Haven, CT**

"Who is she?"

"The only surviving member of the original translation team that decoded the Aethelred manuscripts in 1978." Aris's jaw tightened. "The rest of the team is dead. Car accidents. Cancer. One 'suicide' that had the wrong ligature marks. Marsh went underground in 1982. Changed her name. Stopped publishing."

"Why her?"

"Because she has what I don't: the source documents. The original cipher key that proves the medieval system wasn't just observation—it was engineering." He handed Sarah the address. "If anyone can prove what GenVault is doing, and how the medieval network did it first, it's her."

Sarah took the paper. It was warm from his hand. "Why don't you go?"

"Because they've been watching me for twenty years. Every email. Every phone call. Every person I meet." He pulled an envelope from the satchel—cash, maybe two thousand dollars in worn bills. "But you're new. Unknown. You might have a twelve-hour head start before they connect you to me."

"Before they what?"

Aris looked at her. His expression said everything he couldn't.

"They killed the others," Sarah said flatly. "The ones who found the pattern before me."

He didn't deny it.

Sarah took the envelope. The cash smelled like old paper and other people's hands.

"One more thing." Aris handed her a small object wrapped in cloth. "Don't open this until you're with Dr. Marsh. She'll know what to do with it."

The object was heavy. Metal, maybe. About the size of a deck of cards.

"What is it?"

"Insurance. Proof. A piece of the puzzle they thought was destroyed." Aris started the car. "No credit cards. No phone calls to anyone you know. Get to New Haven. Find Marsh. And Sarah?"

"Yes?"

"Don't trust anyone who offers to help. The network is everywhere. It's been everywhere for seven hundred years. You'll think you're paranoid. You're not. You're not paranoid enough."

He drove her to the Greyhound station. Harsh fluorescent lights, diesel smell, a drunk sleeping on a bench. The ticket clerk looked half-asleep behind bulletproof glass.

Sarah got out, clutching the envelope and the wrapped object.

"Dr. Aris?"

He looked at her through the window. His face was gray.

"What happened to them? The others who found the pattern?"

His expression answered before his words did. "Go, Sarah. And whatever you do—don't look back."

The car pulled away, taillights disappearing into Boston traffic.

Sarah stood in the fluorescent glare, holding everything she had left: two thousand dollars in cash, an address in New Haven, a mysterious object wrapped in cloth, and the terrible knowledge that somewhere in medieval manuscripts, someone had figured out how to read and rewrite the future of the human race.

And GenVault had been using that knowledge for seventy years.

The next bus to New Haven left in forty minutes.

Sarah bought a ticket with cash, giving the sleepy clerk a fake name. The thermal paper was still warm.

## Scene 4: The Long Road

### Greyhound Bus to New Haven
### Wednesday, November 15, 2019 - 3:15 AM

The bus smelled like diesel fuel, stale chips, and someone's too-sweet perfume. Sarah sat in the back, watching Boston disappear through the smeared window. Her reflection stared back—pale, frightened, still wearing her lab coat.

She shrugged it off, balled it up, shoved it under the seat. One less thing identifying her.

The bus was mostly empty. An old woman slept across the aisle. Two rows up, a college kid with headphones, head bobbing. Nobody paying attention to the geneticist on the run.

Sarah pulled out the photocopied manuscripts Aris had given her. Studied the dots by the overhead light.

Red. Blue. Yellow. Geometric patterns that looked decorative but encoded something else.

Something that predicted genetic variation with ninety-four percent accuracy.

Seven hundred years ago.

The past wasn't supposed to know more than the present. But what if it did?

What if the Black Death hadn't just killed a third of Europe—what if it had killed everyone who understood what was really happening? Everyone except the cipher-makers, who'd hidden their knowledge in plain sight, disguised as marginal decorations in religious manuscripts?

And what if that knowledge had survived?

What if it was still being used?

Sarah unwrapped the object Aris had given her.

It was a key. Old. Iron. Covered in the same geometric symbols as the manuscript dots.

She stared at it. Heavy in her palm. Cold metal warming to her body temperature.

*Don't open this until you're with Dr. Marsh.*

But what was it a key to?

The college kid got up, walked past toward the bathroom. Sarah tensed, watching. He went in. Came out. Sat back down.

She was being paranoid.

Or not paranoid enough.

Her mind kept returning to Aris's final warning: *Don't trust anyone who offers to help.*

Sarah looked at the kid with headphones. She needed help. But could she risk it?

She stood, walked up to him. Tapped his shoulder.

He pulled out one earbud, annoyed.

"Sorry," Sarah said. "My phone died. Emergency. Could I borrow yours for one quick call?"

The kid hesitated.

Sarah pulled out a twenty from the envelope. "Please. Five minutes."

Money changed hands. The phone was cracked, sticky.

Sarah walked to the back of the bus. Dialed her cousin David's number from memory.

Three rings. Four. Then a groggy, annoyed voice: "Yeah? Who calls at three in the morning?"

"David. It's Sarah. Don't say my name."

Pause. Then, carefully: "Okay..."

"I need you to do something for me. Something illegal."

"Jesus. What—"

"GenVault Corporation. My user directory on their research servers. There's a flash drive on my desk with data they're going to delete. I need you to pull the files from their backup system before the purge cycle runs."

Silence. The bus hit a pothole. Then: "You're serious."

"Life or death serious. People are after me."

"What people?"

"I can't explain. Can you do it? You work for the DOD—you have tools, right? Ways to access private servers?"

"Sarah, what you're describing is corporate espionage. I could lose my clearance. Go to federal prison. What did you find?"

"Evidence that GenVault has been manipulating the human genome for seventy years. Using knowledge from medieval manuscripts that shouldn't exist."

The words sounded insane out loud.

David was quiet for a long moment. She could hear him typing—he was already at a computer, probably couldn't sleep anyway. "This is insane."

"I know."

"You could get arrested. I could get arrested—"

"I know. I wouldn't ask if it wasn't..." Her voice cracked. "Remember when you said you owed me for getting you into MIT? For convincing the admissions committee to overlook your record?"

"That was different—"

"They've been engineering people, David. Selecting for traits. Guiding evolution." Her whisper was barely audible over the bus engine. "Please."

Another pause. More typing. "What exactly am I looking for?"

Relief flooded through her. She gave him the details—server addresses, folder structures, file names. The flash drive location. Everything.

"How do I reach you?" David asked.

"You don't. I'll contact you in forty-eight hours. Different number."

"Where do I send the files?"

"I'll figure something out. Dead drop. I'll let you know."

"Sarah—"

"Thank you. Tell Aunt Lin I love her."

"Don't do that. Don't talk like you're—"

She hung up. Walked back to the college kid, handed him his phone. Wiped her hand on her jeans.

"Thanks."

The kid nodded, already back in his music.

Sarah returned to her seat. The iron key felt heavy in her pocket. Outside, the highway stretched endlessly, broken white lines disappearing under the bus.

In forty-eight hours, she'd either have proof of a centuries-long conspiracy to manipulate human genetics.

Or she'd be a paranoid ex-scientist with a stolen key and delusions.

The bus rolled on. Sarah closed her eyes.

Tried not to think about Dr. Aris's warning.

Tried not to think about what happened to the others who'd found the pattern.

Tried not to think about the fact that somewhere in the past, someone had figured out how to encode the future in colored dots and geometric symbols.

And that seven hundred years later, someone was still using that knowledge.

Still selecting. Still optimizing. Still guiding human evolution toward some unknown end.

The road to New Haven stretched ahead in the darkness.

Sarah hoped Dr. Evelyn Marsh had answers.

Because the questions were multiplying faster than she could process them.

And the dawn was still three hours away.
