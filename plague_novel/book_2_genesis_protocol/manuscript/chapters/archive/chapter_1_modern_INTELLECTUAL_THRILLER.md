# CHAPTER 1 - THE PATTERN

**Modern Timeline - Boston, Massachusetts**
**November 2019**

---

## Scene 1: The Discovery

### GenVault Corporation, Genomics Research Division
### Tuesday, November 14, 2019 - 11:47 PM

The lab was cold. Sarah had stopped noticing hours ago.

Dr. Sarah Chen rubbed her eyes and stared at the dual monitors. Left screen: a fourteenth-century manuscript page from the Bodleian Library's digital archive—marginal decorations, colored dots surrounding Latin text about plague mortality. Right screen: her own genomic dataset, population variants in CCR5-Delta32.

She'd been chasing a hunch for three weeks.

The dots weren't random.

Sarah overlaid the medieval pattern onto her genomic map, scaling the resolution.

Perfect alignment.

The colored dots in the manuscript margins—dismissed by historians as decorative flourishes—mapped exactly onto modern genetic variation hotspots. Red dots corresponded to populations with high-frequency protective alleles. Blue dots to populations decimated by plague. Yellow to intermediary zones where the disease had killed selectively.

"That's impossible," she whispered.

She pulled up another manuscript page. Different monastery, different scribe, different century. Same marginal notation system.

She ran the overlay.

Same result.

A third document—a merchant's ledger from Mainz, 1347.

Ninety-four percent accuracy.

"They knew." Sarah pushed back from her desk. "Somehow they knew which populations would survive."

But how? Medieval monks didn't have PCR machines or gene sequencers. They couldn't predict which specific genetic variations would protect against *Yersinia pestis*.

Unless.

Unless someone had taught them.

Sarah pulled up GenVault's restricted database—the one she technically shouldn't access, but Dr. Aris had given her his login credentials six months ago. Her mentor trusted her. Or wanted her to find something he couldn't openly show her.

She typed the query she'd been avoiding:

**SEARCH: Population genetic outliers / CCR5-Delta32 / Non-European distribution / Pre-1348 markers**

The results loaded.

And stopped making sense.

CCR5-Delta32 was supposed to be a European mutation, selected for by plague exposure over multiple centuries. Standard evolutionary response to massive selective pressure. But GenVault's proprietary dataset showed unexpected high-frequency populations in North Africa, Central Asia, the Levant.

The same regions marked with red dots in the medieval manuscripts.

*Before* the plague had even reached them.

Someone had marked survival populations *before the catastrophe occurred*.

Her phone buzzed. Dr. Otto Aris. Her mentor.

She answered. "Dr. Aris, I know it's late, but I found—"

"Sarah." His voice was wrong. Tight. Afraid. "Hang up this phone. Right now."

She froze. "What?"

"They're listening. Every query to the restricted database triggers real-time monitoring." Wind noise in the background—he was outside, moving. "I saw your access logs. The population variants. The medieval correlation analysis. You tripped a Level Three security flag."

"I was just researching—"

"I know what you were doing. I know what you found." A car door slammed. "You think you're the first?"

The security camera in the corner—when had that little red light started blinking?

"Dr. Aris, what are you talking about?"

"Twenty years ago, I found the same pattern. The same impossible correlation between medieval annotations and modern genetic data." His breathing was ragged. "I published a preliminary paper. GenVault acquired my lab six months later. Buried my research. Made me head of a department that doesn't do anything real."

"That doesn't make sense. Why would they—"

"Because it's not a discovery, Sarah. It's a *map*." His voice cracked. "The founders of GenVault, the private equity backers—they have the original manuscripts. The complete cipher system. They've been using it to select for specific genetic traits for seventy years."

This was insane. Paranoid conspiracy theory.

But Dr. Aris was the most rational person she'd ever worked with. Published in *Nature* seven times. The man who'd taught her that science meant following evidence wherever it led.

"That's..." She couldn't finish.

"I'm sorry," Aris said quietly. "I hoped you wouldn't look. But you're too curious. Too good. And now they know you've seen it."

"What do I do?"

"Run. Tonight. Don't go home. Don't access your email. Don't—"

The call cut off.

Sarah stared at her phone. Then at her monitors, where seven centuries of genetic manipulation glowed in red and blue and yellow dots.

She had maybe ten minutes before building security checked the logs.

Her research. She couldn't leave it.

Sarah grabbed a flash drive, jammed it into the USB port, started copying files. The manuscript overlays. Her genomic analysis. The restricted database queries.

*Progress: 23%... 34%...*

The lab door opened.

Marcus Webb stood in the doorway. VP of Research Operations. Expensive suit at midnight. Two security guards behind him.

"Dr. Chen." His smile didn't reach his eyes. "Working late?"

"Just finishing some data analysis." Her hand closed around the flash drive. *Progress: 67%.*

"On medieval manuscripts?" Marcus walked into the lab. "Interesting pivot from your assigned project."

"Following a hunch about population genetics. Probably nothing."

"Probably." He glanced at her monitors. His expression didn't change, but something flickered behind his eyes. "Still, we should discuss it. Conference room. Now."

*Progress: 81%.*

"Of course." Sarah's voice was steadier than she felt. "Let me just save my work—"

"The guards will secure your station. Security protocol for after-hours restricted database access." Marcus gestured to the door. "Please."

*Progress: 89%.*

Sarah's mind raced through possibilities. If she refused, they'd know she understood. If she went, they'd wipe her computer.

No proof. No evidence. No way to prove that seven centuries ago, someone had figured out how to read—and rewrite—the future of the human race.

"Actually," Sarah said, "I need to use the restroom first."

She grabbed her shoulder bag, leaving the flash drive hidden under papers. Walked past Marcus toward the door.

"Dr. Chen."

She stopped.

"Your phone and access badge. Company property."

Her stomach dropped. But she pulled out her phone, set it on the desk beside her badge.

Marcus nodded to the guard. "Escort her."

---

## Scene 2: The Bathroom Gambit

The women's restroom was down one floor. The guard—young, bored—waited outside.

Sarah went inside. Checked the stalls—empty. No windows. No fire exit.

She was trapped.

No phone. No research. No proof. And in about five minutes, Marcus Webb would realize she'd figured out what GenVault really was.

Sarah looked at the bathroom vent. Large. Old building, old ventilation system. The grate was held by four Phillips-head screws.

She didn't have a screwdriver.

But she had the underwire from her bra.

Sarah locked the stall door, removed her bra under her shirt, worked the underwire free. Bent it, creating a makeshift screwdriver tip.

Climbed onto the sink. Started working the screws.

The guard knocked. "You okay in there?"

"Yeah, just—stomach issue. Bad pad thai at lunch."

Silence. Then: "Take your time."

Third screw. Fourth screw. The grate came free.

Sarah pulled herself up into the vent—tighter than movies made it look. She army-crawled forward, her bag scraping metal.

Behind her, muffled: "Dr. Chen? You've been in there a while."

She crawled faster. The vent branched. She went right.

Voices behind her, echoing: "—the vent, she's in the fucking vent—"

Sarah's hands found another grate. She kicked it. Once. Twice. Third kick and it fell.

She dropped through. Her ankle twisted on landing. Pain shot up her calf but she kept moving.

Different bathroom. Second floor, west wing. Sarah ran.

Emergency exit stairs. Down four flights. Behind her, the stairwell door banged open.

"Freeze! GenVault security!"

She didn't freeze.

Ground floor. Emergency exit. The alarm screamed.

Sarah hit the push bar.

Cold November air. She ran toward the street.

Headlights swept across her. A car screeched to a stop.

Dr. Otto Aris looked out. "Get in. Now."

Sarah threw herself into the passenger seat.

Aris accelerated before she'd closed the door.

---

## Scene 3: The Truth She Wasn't Ready For

They drove in silence for ten minutes. Aris checked his mirrors, took random turns, doubled back twice.

Finally, he pulled into a 24-hour diner parking lot. Killed the engine.

"Are we safe?" Sarah asked.

"For about twenty minutes." Aris looked at her. Up close, he looked exhausted. "Did you finish the data transfer?"

"No. Maybe eighty-nine percent."

He nodded slowly. "Doesn't matter. They'll purge it anyway." He pulled a worn leather satchel from the back seat. "The question is: are you ready for this?"

"Ready for what?"

"The truth. The real reason I gave you database access. The reason I've been leaving medieval manuscripts on your desk for six months."

Sarah's head spun. "You... you *wanted* me to find this?"

"I needed someone to confirm what I'd found twenty years ago. Someone brilliant enough to see it. Someone innocent enough that GenVault wouldn't be watching." His hands shook. "I'm sorry. I used you. But I had to know if I was insane or if it was real."

He opened the satchel. Inside: photocopied manuscripts, genealogical charts, genetic data, population maps.

"What is this?"

"Twenty years of research they think I abandoned." Aris spread papers on the dashboard. "The medieval cipher isn't just correlation, Sarah. It's causation."

"That's impossible. You can't cause genetic variation across populations without—" She stopped.

"Without deliberate intervention across multiple generations," Aris finished. "Exactly."

He pulled out a manuscript page—1338, nine years before the Black Death reached Europe. Red dots marked specific villages in northern Italy.

"Now look at modern genetic sampling from those exact locations."

Sarah leaned in. The villages marked in red had the highest frequency of CCR5-Delta32 in all of Europe.

*Before the plague arrived.*

"Someone was tracking which populations would survive," Sarah whispered. "Before the selective pressure even occurred."

"Not just tracking." Aris flipped to another document—a genealogical chart from medieval Mainz. "Guiding. Preserving. Optimizing."

"But that would require—"

"A conspiracy spanning centuries. Yes." He pulled out more documents. "The medieval cipher-makers weren't predicting plague survival. They were *ensuring* it. Positioning resilient populations. Tracking bloodlines. Encoding knowledge that would preserve civilization through collapse."

A car pulled into the lot. Both tensed. Just a night-shift worker.

"How is this possible?" Sarah's voice was barely a whisper.

"I don't know the mechanism. Maybe experimental methods we'd call genetic engineering. Maybe sophisticated statistical observation over dozens of generations." He hesitated. "Maybe they knew things we've forgotten."

"That's insane."

"Is it?" Aris met her eyes. "We think civilization started 5,000 years ago. But what if that's just when we *recovered* from a previous collapse? What if the medieval cipher-makers were protecting the last remnants of much older knowledge?"

"We have to expose them."

"With what evidence?" Aris gestured at the papers. "Photocopies? Correlations? GenVault's legal team would bury this before you finished the press release. We'd both disappear."

"Then what do we do?"

Aris pulled out a piece of paper. An address:

**Dr. Evelyn Marsh**
**47 Elm Street, New Haven, CT**

"Who is she?"

"The only surviving member of the original translation team that decoded the Aethelred manuscripts in 1978." Aris's jaw tightened. "The rest are dead. Car accidents. Cancer. One 'suicide.' Marsh went underground in 1982."

"Why her?"

"Because she has what I don't: the source documents. The original cipher key that proves the medieval system wasn't just observation—it was engineering." He handed Sarah the address. "If anyone can prove what GenVault is doing, and how the medieval network did it first, it's her."

Sarah took the paper. "Why don't you go?"

"Because they've been watching me for twenty years." He pulled an envelope from the satchel—cash, maybe two thousand dollars. "But you're new. Unknown. You might have a twelve-hour head start."

"Before they what?"

Aris looked at her. His expression said everything.

"They killed the others," Sarah said flatly. "The ones who found the pattern before me."

He didn't deny it.

Sarah took the envelope.

"One more thing." Aris handed her a small object wrapped in cloth. "Don't open this until you're with Dr. Marsh. She'll know what to do with it."

The object was heavy. Metal.

"What is it?"

"Insurance. Proof. A piece of the puzzle they thought was destroyed." Aris started the car. "No credit cards. No phone calls. Get to New Haven. Find Marsh. And Sarah?"

"Yes?"

"Don't trust anyone who offers to help. The network is everywhere. It's been everywhere for seven hundred years."

He drove her to the Greyhound station.

Sarah got out, clutching the envelope and the wrapped object.

"Dr. Aris?"

He looked at her.

"What happened to them? The others who found the pattern?"

His expression answered before his words did. "Go, Sarah. And whatever you do—don't look back."

The car pulled away.

Sarah stood in the fluorescent glare, holding everything she had left: two thousand dollars in cash, an address in New Haven, a mysterious object, and the terrible knowledge that somewhere in medieval manuscripts, someone had figured out how to read and rewrite the future of the human race.

And GenVault had been using that knowledge for seventy years.

The next bus to New Haven left in forty minutes.

Sarah bought a ticket with cash, giving the clerk a fake name.

---

## Scene 4: The Long Road

### Greyhound Bus to New Haven
### Wednesday, November 15, 2019 - 3:15 AM

The bus smelled like diesel and stale chips. Sarah sat in the back, watching Boston disappear. Her reflection stared back—pale, frightened, still wearing her lab coat.

She shrugged it off, balled it up, shoved it under the seat.

The bus was mostly empty. An old woman slept across the aisle. Two rows up, a college kid with headphones. Nobody paying attention to the geneticist on the run.

Sarah pulled out the photocopied manuscripts Aris had given her. Studied the dots.

Red. Blue. Yellow. Geometric patterns that looked decorative but encoded something else.

Something that predicted genetic variation with ninety-four percent accuracy.

Seven hundred years ago.

The past wasn't supposed to know more than the present. But what if it did?

What if the Black Death hadn't just killed a third of Europe—what if it had killed everyone who understood what was really happening? Everyone except the cipher-makers, who'd hidden their knowledge in plain sight?

And what if that knowledge had survived?

What if it was still being used?

Sarah unwrapped the object Aris had given her.

It was a key. Old. Iron. Covered in the same geometric symbols as the manuscript dots.

She stared at it. Heavy. Cold metal warming to her body temperature.

*Don't open this until you're with Dr. Marsh.*

But what was it a key to?

The college kid got up, walked past toward the bathroom. Sarah tensed, watching. He went in. Came out. Sat back down.

Paranoia. Or not paranoid enough.

Her mind kept returning to Aris's final warning: *Don't trust anyone who offers to help.*

Sarah stood, walked up to the kid. Tapped his shoulder.

He pulled out one earbud, annoyed.

"Sorry," Sarah said. "My phone died. Emergency. Could I borrow yours for one quick call?"

The kid hesitated.

Sarah pulled out a twenty. "Please. Five minutes."

Money changed hands.

Sarah walked to the back. Dialed her cousin David's number from memory.

Three rings. Four. Then: "Yeah? Who calls at three in the morning?"

"David. It's Sarah. Don't say my name."

Pause. Then, carefully: "Okay..."

"I need you to do something for me. Something illegal."

"Jesus. What—"

"GenVault Corporation. My user directory on their research servers. There's a flash drive on my desk with data they're going to delete. I need you to pull the files from their backup system before the purge cycle runs."

Silence. Then: "You're serious."

"Life or death serious. People are after me."

"What people?"

"I can't explain. Can you do it? You work for the DOD—you have tools, right?"

"Sarah, what you're describing is corporate espionage. I could lose my clearance. Go to federal prison. What did you find?"

"Evidence that GenVault has been manipulating the human genome for seventy years. Using knowledge from medieval manuscripts that shouldn't exist."

The words sounded insane out loud.

David was quiet. She could hear him typing. "This is insane."

"I know."

"You could get arrested. I could get arrested—"

"I know. I wouldn't ask if it wasn't..." Her voice cracked. "Remember when you said you owed me for getting you into MIT?"

"That was different—"

"They've been engineering people, David. Selecting for traits. Guiding evolution." Her whisper was barely audible. "Please."

Another pause. More typing. "What exactly am I looking for?"

Relief flooded through her. She gave him the details—server addresses, folder structures, file names.

"How do I reach you?" David asked.

"You don't. I'll contact you in forty-eight hours."

"Where do I send the files?"

"I'll figure something out. Dead drop."

"Sarah—"

"Thank you. Tell Aunt Lin I love her."

"Don't do that. Don't talk like you're—"

She hung up. Walked back, handed the kid his phone.

"Thanks."

The kid nodded, already back in his music.

Sarah returned to her seat. The iron key felt heavy in her pocket.

In forty-eight hours, she'd either have proof of a centuries-long conspiracy to manipulate human genetics.

Or she'd be a paranoid ex-scientist with a stolen key and delusions.

The bus rolled on. Sarah closed her eyes.

Tried not to think about Dr. Aris's warning.

Tried not to think about what happened to the others who'd found the pattern.

Tried not to think about the fact that somewhere in the past, someone had figured out how to encode the future in colored dots and geometric symbols.

And that seven hundred years later, someone was still using that knowledge.

Still selecting. Still optimizing. Still guiding human evolution toward some unknown end.

The road to New Haven stretched ahead.

Sarah hoped Dr. Evelyn Marsh had answers.

Because the questions were multiplying faster than she could process them.

And the dawn was still three hours away.
