# CHAPTER 2 - THE DEFENSIVE KEY

**Modern Timeline - New Haven, Connecticut**
**November 2019**

---

## Scene 1: Arrival

### Greyhound Station, New Haven
### Wednesday, November 15, 2019 - 6:47 AM

Sarah stepped off the bus into gray dawn light.

The station smelled like diesel and burnt coffee. She moved with the handful of passengers shuffling toward exits, invisible in the crowd.

She'd spent four hours watching every car on I-91, cataloging which stayed in view too long. Pattern recognition. Her brain doing what it was trained to do.

First priority: stop looking like a fugitive.

The 24-hour diner two blocks over had harsh lights and vinyl booths. Sarah ordered coffee, found a corner booth with clear views of door and street.

The coffee tasted burned. She wrapped both hands around the mug anyway.

Think. Plan. Prioritize.

1. Find Dr. Evelyn Marsh
2. Get answers about the cipher
3. Figure out what GenVault was actually doing
4. Survive

She caught her reflection in the window. Still screamed *scientist*. And—stupid—her GenVault lanyard was still around her neck.

Sarah yanked it off, shoved it in her pocket.

Across the street: Goodwill thrift store.

Twenty minutes later, she emerged in jeans, dark hoodie, canvas sneakers. She'd cut her hair shorter with sewing scissors in the bathroom mirror. Rough, uneven. Different.

Not Dr. Sarah Chen, GenVault geneticist.

Just Sarah. Nobody.

---

### Residential New Haven - 9:15 AM

The address led to a quiet neighborhood. Tree-lined streets, middle-class houses, basketball hoops over garage doors.

Sarah walked past 47 Elm Street twice, observing.

Two-story colonial. White clapboard. Well-maintained. Old Honda Civic in the driveway. All curtains drawn despite mid-morning.

And across the street, parked three houses down: a dark sedan with tinted windows.

Sarah's pulse spiked.

The sedan had been there both times. Engine off, but someone was inside—she could see the faint glow of a phone screen. Watching.

Watching Marsh's house.

Sarah kept walking. Circled the block.

Options:
1. Front door → get grabbed
2. Leave → abandon only lead
3. Find another way in

The neighborhood backed onto a small greenbelt. Sarah found a gap in the chain-link fence and slipped through.

She counted houses through bare branches. That should be Marsh's—yes, the white colonial, approached from the back where the sedan couldn't see.

Sarah crossed the backyard quickly.

She knocked on the back door. Soft.

Silence.

Knocked again. Louder.

"Go away or I'll call the police." Woman's voice. Sharp. Afraid.

Sarah pressed close to the door. "Dr. Marsh? My name is Sarah Chen. Dr. Otto Aris sent me. He said to tell you it's about the Aethelred manuscripts."

Long silence. Then: "Otto's dead. Has been for years."

"No, he's alive. Hiding. He helped me escape GenVault last night—picked me up after I crawled through the ventilation system." Sarah's voice shook. "Please. They're hunting me. You're the only person who can help."

More silence. Someone on the other side, listening. Weighing.

Finally, locks began to click. One. Two. Three. Four. A chain sliding free.

The door opened a crack.

A face peered out—woman, probably seventy, with wild gray hair and wire-rimmed glasses. Eyes sharp, assessing.

"You're Chinese," the woman said.

"Chinese-American. Sarah Chen."

"Chen." The woman's eyes narrowed. "Your family from Guangdong province?"

Sarah blinked. "How did you—"

"Because the cipher tracked eleven families from Guangdong. Marked them since the 1300s for specific genetic markers." Dr. Marsh opened the door wider. "Get inside. Quickly."

Sarah stepped into a dim kitchen.

Marsh bolted the door immediately. All four locks, plus the chain. Then turned and studied Sarah with unsettling intensity.

Up close, Dr. Evelyn Marsh looked like she'd been living underground for forty years. Gaunt, hands stained with ink. She wore layers—cardigan over sweater over thermal shirt. Hair more white than gray, secured with a pencil.

"Dr. Aris really sent you?" Marsh asked.

"Last night. Maybe two AM. He gave me photocopied manuscripts. Told me about the cipher. Said you had the complete picture."

"The vents." Marsh laughed—sharp, bitter. "Creative. In my day we bribed the security guards." She walked deeper into the house. "How is Otto? Still paranoid?"

"Terrified. Said they've been watching him for twenty years."

"They have been. They watch everyone who figures it out. The ones they don't kill, they watch." Marsh gestured down a hallway lined with bookshelves. "Come. Don't touch anything. If I tell you to run, you run."

"Understood."

Sarah followed through the hallway. Medieval history next to population genetics. Ancient language grammars beside statistical monographs. Into a study that looked like an explosion in a research library.

Papers covered every surface. Manuscripts—photocopies and original vellum in archival sleeves. A massive cork board dominated one wall, covered with photographs and documents connected by colored string. Maps with pins. Genetic charts. Population graphs.

Obsession made physical.

"Welcome to forty years of my life," Marsh said. "Tea?"

"I... yes?"

Marsh disappeared into the kitchen. Sarah heard a kettle, a gas stove.

She stood in the study, overwhelmed. The cork board—medieval manuscripts connected by red string to modern genetic data, migration maps, genealogies going back centuries, news clippings.

And there, center of the web: a photograph of a medieval manuscript page. The marginal dots clearly visible. The same cipher pattern Sarah had found.

Marsh returned with two chipped mugs.

"Sit." Marsh gestured to a chair buried under papers.

Sarah carefully moved them—photocopies of 14th-century merchant ledgers—and sat.

Marsh remained standing, studying her. "Otto said you found the correlation. The impossible one."

"Ninety-four percent accuracy," Sarah confirmed. "CCR5-Delta32 population distribution mapping perfectly onto 14th-century manuscript notations."

"And you realized that's impossible."

"Unless the cipher-makers had knowledge they couldn't possibly have possessed." Sarah set her tea down. "Dr. Marsh, what is the Aethelred Cipher?"

Marsh smiled—sharp, humorless. "Wrong question. The right question is: What were the cipher-makers really doing?"

"Then what were they doing?"

"Engineering civilization." Marsh pulled out a manuscript from a protective sleeve. Original vellum. "This is from 1338. Nine years before the Black Death reached Europe."

She laid it flat on the desk. Sarah leaned closer, studying the red and blue dots around Latin text discussing trade routes.

"Now look at THIS." Marsh pulled out a modern genetic chart—family trees with genetic markers highlighted.

Sarah's breath caught. "That's... that's my family name."

"The Chen bloodline. Twelve generations back to Guangdong." Marsh's finger traced the highlighted sections. "CCR5-Delta32, pattern recognition traits, spatial memory enhancement—"

"How did you get this?" Sarah's voice rose. "This is private data—"

"I stole it. 1982." Marsh overlaid the medieval manuscript on the modern chart. "When I realized what the project really was. Watch."

She aligned the documents.

The red dots matched perfectly with the genetic markers in Sarah's family tree.

Sarah stepped back from the desk. "That's not possible."

"Exactly." Marsh tapped the overlay. "Completely impossible for 14th-century monks to know about genetics."

"Then how—"

"Time." Marsh pulled out another chart showing dates spiraling backward through centuries. "The cipher network started around 400 CE. After Rome fell. Sixteen centuries of patient observation."

She pulled out genealogies—handwritten family trees connected by colored lines. "Arranged marriages. Directed migration. Protected certain families. Eliminated competitors."

"Proto-eugenics." Sarah felt sick. "They let the plague kill people. Selectively."

"Worse." Marsh spread trade route maps across the desk. "Look at where the first cases appeared."

Sarah studied the maps. "Trading posts..."

"That they controlled." Marsh's finger traced routes. "The timing is too perfect to be coincidence."

Sarah stood abruptly, needing to move. Started pacing. "GenVault found this? The complete cipher?"

"Not just GenVault." Marsh pulled out a printed list, handed it to Sarah.

Sarah recognized names: GenVault, BioMerge International, Helix Dynamics. "Seven corporations?"

"All descended from the original seven cipher families. Continuing the work for seventy years."

"How specifically?"

Marsh pulled out corporate documents. "Fertility treatments selecting embryos. Genetic screening tracking bloodlines. Targeted vaccine distribution—"

"That does what?" Sarah interrupted.

Marsh hesitated. "Ensures certain populations survive while others don't."

Sarah gripped the desk edge. "To what end?"

"That's the question I've spent forty years trying to answer." Marsh looked at Sarah directly. "But based on acceleration over the past five years, I think another collapse is coming soon."

"How soon?"

"Six months. Maybe less." Marsh pulled out news clippings—disease outbreaks, climate disasters, political instabilities. "The pattern is accelerating. The cipher network has predicted every major collapse for two thousand years. And survived each one by managing who lives."

"We have to stop them."

"With what evidence?" Marsh's laugh was bitter. "I tried in 1982. Car accident three weeks later."

"But I have data from GenVault. My cousin David is hacking their backups—"

"Data they can claim is fabricated." Marsh shook her head. "We need the SOURCE. The original documents that prove not just what they did but what they're corrupting."

"Corrupting?"

Marsh's expression shifted. She pulled out two sets of documents, laid them side by side.

"Left side: Medieval manuscripts from the 14th century." Marsh tapped the vellum. "Thomas of Eltville distributed these. Breeding records. Catastrophe predictions."

Sarah recognized the pattern—the dots she'd found.

"Right side:" Marsh pulled out much older documents. Papyrus fragments in archival sleeves. "Bronze Age. Egyptian hieroglyphics, cuneiform."

"How old?" Sarah leaned closer.

"1200-1177 BCE. The Bronze Age Collapse."

Sarah's head snapped up. "Three thousand years ago?"

"Exactly." Marsh pointed to the ancient documents. "And these describe something entirely different."

"Different how?"

"They call it the 'Genesis Protocol.'" Marsh pulled out translation notes. "But it's not a breeding program. It's a defensive system."

Sarah's pulse quickened. "Defensive against what?"

"Against collapse itself. Against loss of knowledge when empires fall." Marsh spread more fragments carefully. "The Bronze Age Collapse was catastrophic—ninety percent population decline in fifty years. Writing disappeared for four hundred years."

She pulled out a specific papyrus fragment. "And in the chaos, scholars realized they were watching human knowledge go extinct."

Sarah stared at the ancient text. "So they created the Protocol to preserve it?"

"Yes. But not by writing it down." Marsh's finger traced hieroglyphics. "Libraries burn. They needed something more resilient."

"What could be more resilient than—"

"Genetic memory." Marsh pointed to margin notations. "Fragment from an Egyptian medical text, 1180 BCE. The scribe's name was Nefertari."

Sarah squinted at the translation notes.

"She describes a discovery," Marsh said quietly. "Knowledge in blood."

"That's impossible." Sarah stepped back. "Experience can't be—"

"Epigenetic inheritance." Marsh pulled out modern research papers. "Transgenerational trauma. Inherited fear responses. We're only now rediscovering what they understood."

Sarah's mind raced. "So they encoded knowledge in genetic memory?"

"More than that." Marsh pulled out detailed notes. "They created a dual-mode system. Two applications."

She wrote on a blank sheet:

**DEFENSIVE MODE - Nefertari's design:**
- Recognize collapse PATTERNS
- Encode systemic failure understanding
- PREVENT future collapses

**OFFENSIVE MODE - Amenhotep's design:**
- Accept collapse as inevitable
- Breed resilient bloodlines
- Control who SURVIVES

"Two factions?" Sarah studied the notes. "From the beginning?"

"Within a decade of the Protocol's creation." Marsh tapped the defensive side. "Seven keys—bronze markers—unlocking different layers. Defensive keys teach prevention."

"And offensive keys?"

"Survival through control." Marsh's voice went hard. "Both encoded in the same system."

Sarah stared at the notes. "The network split immediately."

"Nefertari's defensive faction scattered." Marsh pulled out a genealogy chart. "Hiding the true keys. Encoding prevention methods in bloodlines they called 'living libraries.'"

She pointed to a name circled in red: **Maria of Toledo, 1347**.

"Who carried fourteen centuries of defensive knowledge," Marsh continued. "In her genetic memory."

"Maria?" Sarah's voice was barely a whisper.

"Your ancestor." Marsh pulled out another chart—Sarah's family tree stretching back through Chinese history. "Twenty-eight generations removed."

Sarah's hands started shaking. "The Chen bloodline carries—"

"Defensive Protocol markers." Marsh pointed to highlighted traits. "Pattern recognition. Systems thinking. Not accident. Three thousand years of preservation."

Sarah stared at names stretching back. "Wait. Maria was Spanish. Jewish."

"Yes."

"So how does—" Sarah gestured at the genealogy. "How does that become Chinese?"

"Deliberate migration." Marsh traced the lineage. "After the Black Death, Maria had three children who survived. The defensive network knew the Order was hunting them."

"So they scattered the bloodline," Sarah said slowly.

"Exactly." Marsh pointed to three branches. "Maria's eldest stayed in Spain—wiped out during the Inquisition. Her daughter married French nobility—markers diluted within three generations."

"And the third?"

"Her youngest son. Rafael." Marsh traced a line moving east. "The network moved him. Venice, 1360s. Constantinople, 1380s."

Sarah's eyes widened. "The Silk Road."

"By 1420, his grandson was in Samarkand. By 1450, the family reached Guangzhou." Marsh pulled out migration maps. "Jews were particularly effective carriers—diaspora communities, multilingual, connected."

Sarah studied the maps. "Rafael's descendants married into Chinese families?"

"His great-great-granddaughter married a Chinese merchant, 1465." Marsh pointed to the merger point. "Their children were culturally Chinese, raised in Guangzhou. Within three generations, the bloodline was fully integrated. By the time your ancestors emigrated to America, they were Chinese, carrying centuries of that identity."

"But the genetic markers persisted," Sarah whispered.

"The defensive Protocol, waiting." Marsh's gaze was intense. "But the network doesn't see culture, Sarah. Only genetics. To them, you're not Chinese-American. You're Gen 138, carrier of markers they've tracked for twenty-eight generations. That reduction—treating humans as bloodlines—is exactly what the defensive network was created to prevent."

"The Order lost the trail," Sarah said quietly.

"Until GenVault's databases became comprehensive enough to track markers across ethnic boundaries." Marsh nodded. "That's why they flagged you. Your DNA lit up their algorithms."

Sarah's hands were shaking. "Thomas of Eltville—he found breeding records. Offensive documents."

"Exactly!" Marsh pulled out Thomas's manuscripts. "By 1347, offensive mode was dominant. Amenhotep's network—the Order—had spent fifteen centuries building power."

"So Thomas exposed their corruption," Sarah said.

"He distributed breeding records. Broke their monopoly." Marsh's voice went sharp. "But he never knew about defensive mode."

Sarah looked up. "The Order had suppressed it?"

"Completely. Thomas found their corruption—the breeding program." Marsh tapped the medieval manuscripts. "But he never learned what he was really fighting. Never discovered the original defensive system."

"Created to prevent collapse," Sarah breathed.

"While the Order was engineering it." Marsh spread corporate documents. "GenVault is the modern continuation. Seventy years of catastrophe preparation."

"THRESHOLD." Sarah gripped the desk. "When fifteen percent of global population carries their markers."

"They think they're fulfilling the Protocol's purpose." Marsh's voice went bitter. "But they're inverting it. The original Protocol was created to prevent collapse."

"And the defensive knowledge still exists?" Sarah asked.

"Hidden in bloodlines. Encoded in genetic memory." Marsh pulled out notation in medieval Latin, showed Sarah the translation:

*Seven keys unlock the Genesis truth. Three open corruption's path. Four open prevention's door. Choose wisely which locks you turn.*

"Three keys unlock offensive knowledge," Marsh explained. "Breeding, eugenics, catastrophe engineering. The Order has been using those for centuries."

Sarah studied the notation. "And the other four?"

"Defensive knowledge. Systems dynamics. Pattern recognition. Intervention methods." Marsh leaned forward. "The actual purpose of the Protocol."

"Thomas distributed the corruption," Sarah said slowly. "But never found the defense."

"That's what we need." Marsh's gaze burned. "Not just to expose GenVault's evil, but to show the world there's another way. That collapse isn't inevitable."

"That's why they're terrified of me." Sarah looked at her family tree. "I might find the defensive keys."

"Yes. Because if defensive mode becomes public..." Marsh smiled grimly. "The Order loses everything. Three thousand years of power built on claiming collapse is inevitable. Defensive mode proves they're lying."

"Where are the four defensive keys?"

Marsh pulled out her notes—decades of research condensed into handwritten pages. "I've found references to two."

She wrote on a fresh sheet:

**Defensive Key 1: 'The Living Key'**
- 'The flesh made cipher, memory in blood'
- Not an object—a PERSON
- Last documented: Maria of Toledo, 1347

"A person?" Sarah leaned over the notes. "Not a physical object?"

"A bloodline." Marsh pointed to Sarah's genetic chart. "Genetic markers suggest descendants exist."

**Defensive Key 2: 'The Pattern Eye'**
- 'The eye that sees what others cannot'
- Last mentioned: Prague, 1355

"Bronze marker?" Sarah asked. "Or something else?"

"Unknown. Possibly knowledge in eidetic memory carriers." Marsh shrugged. "The trail goes cold after Prague."

"And the other two keys?"

"No reliable references." Marsh's expression darkened. "Deliberately obscured or destroyed. May be encoded in Nefertari's original hieroglyphics, but I haven't cracked it."

Sarah looked at her genetic charts spread across the desk. Pattern recognition. Systems thinking. Eidetic memory traits.

"You think I'm the Living Key."

"I think you carry it." Marsh's gaze burned. "Twenty-eight generations from Maria. The defensive Protocol isn't just about you—it's in you."

"Waiting for what?"

"For someone to understand what they're seeing." Marsh tapped Sarah's pattern recognition markers. "When you look at collapse patterns."

"How do I activate it?" Sarah's voice rose. "If it's in my DNA, how do I—"

"That's what the other keys unlock." Marsh started pulling books from shelves. "Methods for accessing genetic memory. Pattern recognition techniques. Ways to see systemic failure before it cascades."

She handed Sarah a stack of documents. "I've spent forty years trying to decode fragments. But you have the actual genetic architecture."

"This is insane," Sarah whispered.

"True, though." Marsh's voice softened. "Otto sent you because I have forty years of research. But I'm seventy-three. Dying—cancer, maybe six months."

Sarah looked up sharply.

"You're young. Brilliant." Marsh gestured at the genetic charts. "You carry the defensive Protocol in your blood. First person in six centuries who might actually use it."

"To stop THRESHOLD?"

"To do what Nefertari intended three thousand years ago." Marsh's voice went fierce. "Recognize the collapse pattern. Identify intervention points. Prevent catastrophe instead of engineering who survives it."

Sarah looked at the documents. At her family tree. At three thousand years settling onto her shoulders.

Then—from the front of the house—a crash. Glass breaking. Wood splintering.

Both women froze.

Marsh's face went pale. "Basement. Now."

"What—"

"GO!"

---

## Scene 2: The Attack

Sarah ran.

Marsh grabbed manuscripts from the desk—originals, irreplaceable—and shoved them into Sarah's arms. "Basement door, behind the bookshelf. Tunnel. Built it in the '80s after the car accident. Leads to neighbor's property."

Footsteps upstairs. Multiple sets. Professional.

"What about you?" Sarah whispered.

"I'm seventy-three. I can't run. You can." Marsh pushed her toward a bookshelf that swung aside on hidden hinges. "Make this matter, Sarah. Don't let them bury the truth again."

"Dr. Marsh, I can't—"

"GO!" Marsh's voice was sharp. Final. "They want you alive. Me, they'll just kill. So go before they realize there's two of us."

Sarah went.

Down narrow stairs into a basement: climate-controlled, meticulously organized, full of stolen evidence. Manuscripts in archival sleeves. Original 14th-century vellum. Corporate files. Genetic databases printed on paper. Government documents stamped CLASSIFIED.

Forty years of obsession. One woman's desperate crusade.

Above, voices filtered through floorboards:

"—check the basement—"

"—Dr. Marsh, we just want to talk—"

Then Marsh's voice, loud and defiant: "You're forty years too late! The information is already out!"

A lie. Buying Sarah time.

Sarah found the tunnel entrance—behind a false panel. She grabbed what she could: the manuscripts, a laptop, a camera with hundreds of photographs.

Behind her, voices grew louder. Angrier.

"—WHERE IS SHE—"

"—Chen was seen approaching—"

"—find the basement—"

Then a sound that might have been a gunshot.

Sarah froze, tears burning. *Make this matter.*

She crawled into the tunnel, pulling the panel closed. Complete darkness. Damp earth smell. Rough concrete scraping elbows. Manuscripts pressed against her chest.

Behind her, muffled through walls: shouting. Then definitely a gunshot.

Sarah crawled faster. The tunnel sloped up, turned, opened into another basement.

Someone else's house. Empty. Quiet.

Sarah climbed out, closed the tunnel carefully. Found stairs. Emerged into a kitchen—normal smells in a normal house.

She grabbed a windbreaker from a hook and walked out the front door like she belonged.

Outside, sirens were wailing. Police or ambulance converging on Marsh's address. Sarah forced herself to walk, not run.

Two blocks away, she ducked into an alley between a dry cleaner and pizza place. Let herself shake. Let the tears come.

Marsh was dead. Or captured. Either way, gone.

Sarah looked at the manuscripts clutched in her arms. The laptop. The camera. This was all that remained of forty years.

She found a quiet corner behind a dumpster and sat on cold pavement. Opened the laptop. Battery at 43%. No password—Marsh had prepared for this.

The desktop was organized with obsessive precision. Folders: BLOODLINES. CORPORATIONS. CIPHER KEYS. GENESIS PROTOCOL. EVIDENCE. CONTACTS.

Sarah opened Genesis Protocol with trembling hands.

Scans of manuscript pages. Medieval Latin. Cipher dots. And extensive notes—Marsh's decades of translation and analysis.

Sarah read:

*Genesis Protocol - Master Summary:*

*1. ORIGINAL INTENT (1177 BCE - Defensive Mode):*
   - Collapse is cyclical but preventable
   - Pattern recognition allows early intervention
   - Encode prevention methods in genetic memory
   - Goal: Break the cycle

*2. CORRUPTION (Offensive Mode):*
   - Reframe collapse as inevitable
   - Use genetic tracking for breeding
   - Engineer who survives instead of prevent
   - Goal: Profit from cycle by positioning chosen bloodlines

*3. CRITICAL DISCOVERY:*
   - Thomas's distribution partially activated defensive networks
   - His eleven cities aligned with ancient defensive nodes
   - Knowledge scattered may have prepared ground for defensive revival
   - Sarah's emergence may be culmination of 670-year preparation

*4. CURRENT STATUS (2019):*
   - Offensive mode: 12.8% global population carries engineered markers
   - Approaching 15% trigger threshold
   - Defensive mode: Dormant, but carriers exist

Sarah's hands shook so hard she nearly dropped the laptop.

12.8% global coverage. Moving toward 15%. When they hit that threshold, they would trigger collapse. Kill billions.

She pulled out Marsh's camera and spent the next hour photographing everything. Every document. Every note. Every piece of evidence.

She needed help. She needed David.

Sarah pulled out the burner phone Aris had given her—cheap prepaid, untraceable. Dialed David's number from memory.

Four rings. "Hello?"

"David, it's me. Don't say my name."

"Jesus. Are you okay?"

"Physically, yes." Sarah's voice cracked. "What are they saying on the news?"

"GenVault announced an investigation into stolen research. Corporate espionage. They're getting federal law enforcement involved. FBI."

Sarah closed her eyes. "Did you get into their backup servers?"

"I'm working on it. Their security is sophisticated. I need maybe six more hours."

"You have four hours maximum."

"I'm going as fast as I can—"

"I know." Sarah looked at the laptop. "David, I'm going to send you something. Historical documents proving GenVault isn't just a corporation. They're continuing a seventy-year program that goes back seven centuries. Back three thousand years."

Silence. Then: "That sounds completely insane."

"I know. But it's true. Dr. Marsh spent forty years documenting it. And now she's dead."

"Fuck." More silence. "Send me everything. When I pull your GenVault data, I'll cross-reference. Pattern connecting medieval manuscripts to modern databases—that's harder to dismiss."

"Thank you. Thank you for believing me."

"Thank you for trusting me." Keyboard sounds. "Where are you going?"

"Somewhere remote. I need to write this properly. Document everything. Then we release it all at once."

"That's going to take days."

"I know."

"They'll be hunting you."

"I know that too."

David was quiet. "Aunt Lin always said you were the most stubborn person in the family."

Sarah almost smiled. "Tell her I love her. And David?"

"Yeah?"

"If something happens to me—if they catch me before I finish—"

"I'll make sure the story gets out anyway. Promise."

"Thank you."

She hung up.

The morning sun was burning through clouds now. Somewhere, people were living ordinary lives. Completely unaware that the world was built on lies.

Sarah gathered everything, stood on shaky legs, started walking.

She had work to do.

A story to write.

A conspiracy to expose.

Three thousand years of corruption to unravel.

And maybe—just maybe—a defensive protocol to activate before it was too late.

The truth was waiting.

All she had to do was survive long enough to tell it.
