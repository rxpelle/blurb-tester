# CHAPTER 1 - THE PATTERN (Modern Timeline - Present Day, Boston)

## Scene 1: Sarah's Discovery

### GenVault Corporation, Genomics Research Division - 11:47 PM

The lab smelled like every lab Sarah had ever worked in: ethanol, plastic, and the faint electrical burn of overworked equipment. After fourteen hours straight, she'd stopped noticing. Her coffee had gone cold an hour ago, bitter film on top.

Sarah Chen rubbed her eyes and stared at the dual monitors again. The left screen showed a 14th-century manuscript page from the Bodleian Library's digital archive—marginal decorations, tiny colored dots around Latin text about plague mortality. The right screen displayed her own genomic dataset: population variants in CCR5-Delta32, the mutation that conferred resistance to certain pathogens.

She'd been chasing a hunch for three weeks. Now, at nearly midnight in an empty lab, the hunch had become something else entirely.

The dots weren't random.

She overlaid the medieval pattern onto her genomic map, adjusting for scale. Her breath caught. The air conditioning kicked on with a mechanical hum that made her jump.

Perfect alignment.

The colored dots in the manuscript margins—dismissed by historians as decorative flourishes—mapped exactly onto modern genetic variation hotspots. Red dots corresponded to high-frequency protective alleles. Blue dots to populations that had been decimated. Yellow dots to intermediary zones where the plague had killed selectively.

"That's impossible," she whispered to the empty lab. Her voice sounded too loud in the silence.

She pulled up another manuscript page. Different monastery, different century, same marginal notation system. The fluorescent lights overhead flickered once, twice. Sarah barely noticed. She ran the overlay.

Same result.

Her hands trembled as she opened a third document—this one from a private collection, a merchant's ledger from Mainz, 1347. The "decorative" dots appeared again, now alongside what looked like trade routes.

She mapped it.

The dots predicted genetic drift patterns with ninety-four percent accuracy.

"They knew." Sarah pushed back from her desk, heart hammering. Her chair squeaked against the linoleum. "Somehow they knew which populations would survive."

But how? Medieval monks didn't have PCR machines or gene sequencers. They didn't understand inheritance beyond "like begets like." They certainly couldn't predict which specific genetic variations would protect against Yersinia pestis.

Unless.

Sarah pulled up GenVault's restricted database—the one she technically shouldn't have access to, but Dr. Aris had given her his credentials "for emergencies." She typed in the query she'd been avoiding for weeks:

**SEARCH: Population genetic outliers / CCR5-Delta32 / Non-European distribution**

The results loaded with a soft ping.

And stopped making sense.

CCR5-Delta32 was supposed to be a European mutation, selected for by plague exposure. But GenVault's proprietary dataset showed unexpected high-frequency populations in North Africa, Central Asia, and...

Sarah's blood went cold.

The same regions marked with red dots in the medieval manuscripts.

Before the plague had even reached them.

Her phone buzzed against the desk, vibrating loudly in the quiet. Dr. Otto Aris. Her mentor, the only person at GenVault who still seemed to care about science over stock prices.

She answered. "Dr. Aris, I know it's late, but I found—"

"Sarah." His voice was wrong. Tight. Afraid. "Don't answer this call."

She froze. "What?"

"They're listening. They're always listening." Background noise—he was outside, wind across the phone. Traffic sounds. "I saw your query logs. You accessed the restricted genome data. The population variants that don't fit the model. They flagged it."

"I was just researching—"

"I know what you were doing. I know what you found." His voice dropped to barely a whisper. "The medieval cipher. The mortality predictions. You think you're the first?"

Sarah's office suddenly felt very small. Very exposed. The security camera in the corner—had it always had that little red light?

"Dr. Aris, what are you talking about?"

"Twenty years ago, I found the same pattern. The same impossible correlation between medieval annotations and modern genetic data." A car door slammed in the background. "I published a preliminary paper. GenVault acquired my lab six months later. Buried my research. Made me head of a department that doesn't do anything real."

"That doesn't make sense. Why would they—"

"Because it's not a discovery, Sarah. It's a map." His breathing was ragged now, like he was running. "They've been using it. The founders of GenVault, the private equity backers—they have the original manuscripts. The complete cipher. They've been selecting for specific genetic traits for decades."

Sarah's mind raced. The air conditioning was too loud now, a mechanical drone that made it hard to think. "That's insane. You can't... genetic selection takes generations. You'd need—"

"Seventy years. That's how long GenVault's parent companies have existed under different names." His voice cracked. "And now they know you've seen it."

Sarah stood, legs unsteady. Her knee hit the desk. Coffee sloshed in the forgotten mug. "What do I do?"

"Run. Tonight. Don't go home. Don't access your email. Don't—"

The call cut off.

Sarah stared at her phone. Then at her monitors. The medieval dots, glowing accusingly in the darkened lab.

She had maybe ten minutes before building security checked the late-night lab access logs. Maybe less if Dr. Aris was right about active monitoring.

Her research. She couldn't leave it.

Sarah grabbed a flash drive from her drawer—the motion-activated lights in the hallway outside flickered on. Footsteps? Or just the cleaning crew?

She jammed the drive into the USB port, started copying files. The manuscript overlays. Her genomic analysis. The restricted database queries. Everything that proved—

Progress bar: 23%... 34%...

The lab door opened.

Sarah spun. Her heart stopped.

Marcus Webb stood in the doorway. VP of Research Operations. Expensive suit at midnight, crisp despite the hour. Two security guards behind him, their radios crackling with static.

"Dr. Chen." His smile didn't reach his eyes. His cologne was overpowering in the sterile lab—something expensive and synthetic. "Working late?"

"Just finishing up some data analysis." Her hand closed around the flash drive, still transferring. Progress bar: 67%. The computer fan whirred louder, processing.

"On medieval manuscripts?" Marcus walked into the lab, hands in pockets. Casual. Threatening. His shoes clicked on the floor—leather soles, each step deliberate. "Interesting pivot from your assigned project. The influenza vaccine study, wasn't it?"

"Following a hunch. Probably nothing."

"Probably." He glanced at her monitors. His expression didn't change, but something flickered. "Still, we should chat about it. Conference room. Now."

Not a request.

Progress bar: 81%.

"Of course." Sarah's voice was steadier than she felt. The metal flash drive was hot against her palm from the data transfer. "Let me just save my work—"

"The guards will handle your station. Security protocol for after-hours access." Marcus gestured to the door. "Please."

Progress bar: 89%.

Sarah's mind raced. If she refused, they'd know she was hiding something. If she went, they'd delete everything before the transfer finished.

She looked at Marcus Webb. At his patient, predatory smile.

Then at her monitors, where seven centuries of genetic manipulation glowed in red and blue and yellow dots.

"Actually," Sarah said, "I need to use the restroom first."

She grabbed her bag—it smelled like old leather and the lavender sachet her mother had given her—leaving the flash drive hidden under a stack of papers. Walked past Marcus toward the door.

"Dr. Chen."

She stopped. Her pulse hammered in her ears.

"Your phone. Company property. You'll need to leave it here."

Her stomach dropped. But she pulled out her phone, set it on the desk. The screen was still warm.

Marcus nodded to a guard. "Escort her."

## Scene 2: The Bathroom Gambit

The restroom was down one floor. Sarah walked ahead of the guard—young, bored, probably didn't even know why he was babysitting a geneticist at midnight. His keys jangled with each step.

The stairwell smelled like industrial cleaner and concrete dust.

"Wait here," Sarah said at the bathroom door.

The guard shrugged, leaned against the wall. Pulled out his phone.

Sarah went inside. Checked the stalls—empty. No windows. No fire exit. Just industrial tile and the sharp smell of antiseptic that made her eyes water.

She was trapped.

No phone. No research. No proof. And in about five minutes, Marcus Webb would realize she'd figured out exactly what GenVault really was.

Sarah leaned against the sink, breathing hard. The metal was cold under her hands.

*Think. What would someone who wasn't about to disappear do?*

She looked at herself in the mirror. Thirty-two years old. MIT PhD. Published researcher. Scientist. Dark circles under her eyes from too many late nights. Coffee stain on her shirt collar.

And apparently, the latest person to stumble onto a conspiracy that had been running since before she was born.

Her reflection looked terrified.

Then Sarah noticed something. The bathroom vent. Large. Old building, old ventilation system. The grate was held by just four screws.

She didn't have a screwdriver.

But she had a lab badge. Rigid plastic. Thin edge.

Sarah climbed onto the sink—her shoes scraped loudly against porcelain. She started working the screws. The first one came loose after thirty seconds of prying, metal squealing softly. The second stuck, then gave way with a pop that seemed too loud.

The guard knocked. "You okay in there?"

"Yeah, just—stomach trouble. The Thai food from lunch." Her voice shook. She steadied it. "The pad thai. Never again."

Silence. Then: "Take your time."

Third screw. Fourth screw. Each turn of the badge sent vibrations up her wrist.

The grate came free with a metallic clatter.

Sarah pulled herself up into the vent. It was tighter than movies made it look—she had to army-crawl, her bag scraping metal with each movement, the sound echoing in the enclosed space. Dust and cobwebs. The smell of old metal and whatever was rotting in the ductwork. But she fit.

Behind her, she heard the bathroom door open.

"Dr. Chen?"

She crawled faster, palms sliding on decades of accumulated grime. The vent branched—she went right, toward what she hoped was the building's east side. Away from the lab. Away from Marcus Webb.

Voices behind her, echoing and distorted by the ductwork: "—the vent, she's in the fucking vent—"

Sarah's hands found another grate. She kicked it. Once. The impact jarred up her leg. Twice. It fell away, clattering onto tile below.

She dropped through. Her ankle twisted on landing—pain shot up her calf but she kept moving.

Different bathroom. Different floor. Sarah ran.

The emergency exit stairs. Down four flights. Her lungs burned. Her ankle throbbed with each step. The metal railing was slick under her sweating palm. Her mind raced. She had no phone, no research, no proof.

But she was alive.

She hit the ground floor, burst through the exit into the parking lot. Cold November air hit her face like a slap. Frost on the asphalt. Her breath came out in visible clouds. She ran toward the street, her injured ankle screaming.

A car screeched to a stop in front of her. Headlights blinding. Diesel exhaust in her throat.

Sarah stumbled back, heart hammering—

The passenger window rolled down. Dr. Otto Aris looked out, his normally composed face haggard. He'd aged ten years since this afternoon.

"Get in."

Sarah didn't hesitate. She threw herself into the passenger seat. The interior smelled like old coffee and cigarette smoke—Aris had quit five years ago, but the smell had never left his car.

Aris accelerated before she'd even closed the door.

## Scene 3: Aris's Confession (REVISED)

They drove in silence for ten minutes. Aris checked the mirrors obsessively, took random turns, doubled back twice. The city lights blurred past. Sarah watched her side mirror, looking for headlights that followed too consistently.

Finally, he pulled into a 24-hour diner parking lot. Killed the engine. The car ticked as it cooled.

Through the diner windows, Sarah could see a tired waitress refilling coffee. Normal people living normal lives. A drunk couple laughing at the counter.

"Are we safe?" Sarah asked.

"For about twenty minutes. Maybe less." Aris looked at her. Up close, he looked exhausted. "Did you copy the data?"

"Started to. Didn't finish."

He nodded slowly. "Doesn't matter. They'll purge it anyway. Probably already have."

"Then what's the point?" Sarah's voice cracked. "If they control everything, if they've been doing this for seventy years—"

"The point is that you're alive. And awake." Aris pulled a worn leather satchel from the back seat. It smelled like old books and dust. "And now you need to decide what kind of scientist you are."

"What does that mean?"

"The kind who looks at impossible data and walks away? Or the kind who asks why it's impossible?" He opened the satchel. Inside were photocopies—old, faded manuscripts. The same marginal dot patterns Sarah had found.

"I've been collecting these for twenty years," Aris said quietly. "Every manuscript I could find with the cipher markings."

He pulled out a map—creased, annotated in multiple colors of ink. Europe, North Africa, Asia, marked with colored pins. Some pins had dates next to them. Some had question marks.

"The medieval cipher-makers weren't predicting plague survival," Aris continued. "They were recording it. In real-time. With precision that shouldn't have been possible."

Sarah stared at the map. A siren wailed past on the street—ambulance or police, she couldn't tell. Both of them tensed until it faded.

"How?" Sarah asked.

"That's the question." Aris smiled bitterly. He pulled out another document—a photocopy of what looked like a genealogical chart, but the annotations were mathematical. "My theory? They had tools for tracking inheritance and variation that we're only rediscovering now."

He spread more papers on the dashboard. Manuscript pages. Genetic charts. Population data. Each one connected to the next with Aris's cramped handwriting.

"Look." He pointed to a manuscript from 1338—nine years before the plague reached Europe. "Red dots marking specific villages in Italy. Now look at modern genetic sampling from those same locations."

Sarah leaned in. The data was undeniable.

"The populations marked with red dots in 1338 have the highest frequency of CCR5-Delta32 in all of Europe," she whispered. "Before the plague even started."

"Exactly." Aris flipped to another page. "And it's not just plague resistance. I've found markers for metabolic efficiency, immune response, even cognitive patterns. They were tracking everything."

"But how—"

"I don't know." Aris's voice was raw. A car pulled into the lot—both of them watched it until a teenage couple got out, headed into the diner. "Maybe experimental methods we'd call genetic engineering. Maybe just incredibly sophisticated statistical observation over generations. Maybe..." He hesitated. "Maybe they knew things we've forgotten."

Sarah's hands shook. "We have to expose them."

"With what evidence?" Aris gestured at the papers. "Photocopies of medieval manuscripts? Correlation doesn't prove causation—you know that. GenVault's lawyers would bury this in a week."

"Then what do we do?"

Aris pulled out a piece of paper. An address in New Haven, written in careful block letters. "Dr. Evelyn Marsh. She was part of the original team that translated the Aethelred manuscripts in the '70s."

"What happened to the team?"

"Dead. Car accidents. Cancer. One suicide." Aris's jaw tightened. "Marsh is the only one left. She went underground in 1982. Changed her name. Stopped publishing. Lives like a hermit."

"Why?"

"Because she has the source documents. The original cipher key." Aris handed Sarah the address. The paper was warm from his hand. "If anyone can prove what GenVault is doing—and how the medieval cipher-makers did it first—it's her."

Sarah took the paper. "Why don't you go?"

"Because they've been watching me for twenty years, waiting for me to slip." He pulled an envelope from the satchel. Cash. Sarah could smell it—old bills, passed through countless hands. Maybe two thousand dollars. "But you're new. Unknown. You have maybe a twelve-hour head start."

"Before what?"

Aris didn't answer. Instead, he started the car. "No credit cards. No phone calls to anyone you know. Get to New Haven, find Marsh."

He drove her to the bus station. The building was harsh with fluorescent light, smelling of diesel and stale cigarettes. A drunk slept on a bench. The ticket clerk looked half-asleep.

Sarah got out, clutching the envelope and the address.

"Dr. Aris?"

He looked at her through the window. His face was gray in the parking lot lights.

"What happened to the other people who found the pattern? Before me?"

His expression answered before his words did. "Go, Sarah. And don't look back."

The car pulled away, taillights disappearing into Boston traffic.

Sarah stood in the fluorescent glare of the bus station, holding everything she had left: two thousand dollars, an address in New Haven, and the terrible knowledge that somewhere in medieval manuscripts, seven centuries ago, someone had figured out how to read—and rewrite—the future of the human race.

And now, so had she.

The next bus to New Haven left in forty minutes.

Sarah bought a ticket with cash. The paper smelled like ink and thermal printing.

## Scene 4: The Road to New Haven (REVISED)

### Greyhound Bus - 3:15 AM

The bus smelled like diesel fuel, stale potato chips, and someone's too-sweet perfume. Sarah sat in the back, watching Boston disappear through the smeared window. Her reflection stared back—pale, frightened, wearing the same lab coat she'd forgotten to take off.

She shrugged it off, balled it up, shoved it under the seat. One less thing identifying her. The vinyl seat was sticky against her back.

The bus was mostly empty. An old woman slept across the aisle, snoring softly. Two rows up, a college kid with headphones, head bobbing to music Sarah couldn't hear. Nobody paying attention.

Sarah pulled out the photocopied manuscripts Aris had given her. The paper was rough under her fingers. Studied the dots again by the overhead light.

Red. Blue. Yellow. Arranged in geometric patterns that looked decorative but encoded something else. Something that predicted genetic variation with impossible accuracy.

The past wasn't supposed to know more than the present.

But what if it did?

What if the Black Death hadn't just killed a third of Europe—what if it had killed everyone who understood what was really happening? Everyone except the cipher-makers. The ones who'd hidden their knowledge in plain sight, disguised as marginal decorations in religious manuscripts.

And what if GenVault had found that knowledge?

Sarah's hands tightened on the papers, crinkling them.

The college kid got up, walked past her toward the bathroom. Sarah tensed, watching. He smelled like cheap beer and cigarettes.

He went in. Came out. Sat back down.

She was being paranoid.

Or not paranoid enough.

Her mind kept returning to Dr. Aris's last question: *What happened to the other people who found the pattern?*

Sarah looked at the college kid with headphones. She needed help.

She walked up to the kid, tapped his shoulder. He pulled out one earbud, annoyed. Up close he smelled even worse.

"Sorry," Sarah said. "My phone died. Could I borrow yours for one quick call? It's an emergency."

The kid hesitated.

Sarah pulled out a twenty-dollar bill from Aris's envelope. "Please. Just five minutes."

The kid took the money, handed over his phone. The screen was cracked, sticky with something.

Sarah walked to the back of the bus. Dialed David's number from memory, trying not to think about what the sticky substance might be.

Three rings. Four. Then a groggy voice: "Hello? Who's calling at three in the fucking morning?"

"David, it's Sarah. Don't say my name."

Pause. "...okay?"

"I need you to hack into GenVault's servers. There's a flash drive on my desk—they're going to delete everything on it. I need you to pull the files from their backup system before the purge cycle runs."

"Sarah, what—"

"Please. People are after me. This is life or death." Her voice cracked. "I left research that proves they've been manipulating the human genome for seventy years."

Silence. The bus hit a pothole, jostling her. Then: "Where are you?"

"Can't say. Can you do it?"

"...maybe. Whose server?"

"GenVault Corporation. Genomics division. My user directory." She gave him the details—server addresses she'd memorized, folder structures, file names. The flash drive location.

David was quiet for a long moment. She could hear him typing—he was already at a computer. "This is insane."

"I know."

"You could get arrested. I could get arrested. I could lose my clearance—"

"I know." Sarah swallowed. "I wouldn't ask if it wasn't important. Remember when you said you owed me for getting you into MIT?"

"That was different—"

"They've been engineering people, David. For seventy years." Her voice was barely a whisper. "Please."

Another pause. More typing. "Jesus, Sarah. This could burn my clearance."

"I know. I'm sorry."

"I'll try. No promises. How do I reach you?"

"You don't. I'll contact you in forty-eight hours. Different number."

"Where do I send the files if I get them?"

"There's a... I can't say. I'll figure something out. Dead drop. Something."

"This is insane," David said again.

"I know. Thank you. Tell Aunt Lin I love her."

"Don't do that. Don't talk like—"

She hung up. Walked back to the college kid, handed him his phone. Wiped her hand on her jeans.

"Thanks. Appreciate it."

The kid nodded, already back in his music.

Sarah returned to her seat. Stared at the photocopied manuscripts. Outside, the highway stretched endlessly, broken white lines disappearing under the bus.

In forty-eight hours, she'd either have proof of a seventy-year conspiracy to manipulate human genetics.

Or she'd be a paranoid ex-scientist on the run from her own delusions.

The bus rolled on through the darkness. The heating system kicked on, blasting stale warm air.

Sarah closed her eyes and tried not to think about Dr. Aris's final warning.

Or about the fact that somewhere behind her, in Boston, people who had been playing genetic chess for three generations were realizing she'd just stolen one of their pieces.

The road to New Haven stretched ahead.

Sarah hoped Dr. Evelyn Marsh had answers.

Because the questions were multiplying faster than she could think.
