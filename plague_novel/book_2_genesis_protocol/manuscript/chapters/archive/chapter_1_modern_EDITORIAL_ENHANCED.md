# CHAPTER 1 - THE PATTERN

**Modern Timeline - Boston, Massachusetts**
**November 2019**

---

## Scene 1: The Discovery

### GenVault Corporation, Genomics Research Division
### Tuesday, November 14, 2019 - 11:47 PM

The lab smelled like every lab Sarah had ever worked in: ethanol, plastic, and the faint electrical burn of equipment running past its prime. After fourteen hours straight, she'd stopped noticing. Her coffee had gone cold an hour ago, bitter film coating the surface like a failed experiment.

Dr. Sarah Chen rubbed her eyes and stared at the dual monitors. Left screen: a fourteenth-century manuscript page from the Bodleian Library's digital archive—marginal decorations, tiny colored dots surrounding Latin text about plague mortality. Right screen: her own genomic dataset, population variants in CCR5-Delta32, the mutation that conferred resistance to certain blood-borne pathogens.

She'd been chasing a hunch for three weeks.

Now, at nearly midnight in an empty lab, the hunch had crystallized into certainty.

The dots weren't random.

Sarah overlaid the medieval pattern onto her genomic map, scaling the resolution. Her breath caught. The air conditioning kicked on with a mechanical hum that made her jump—the only sound in the building's after-hours silence.

Perfect alignment.

The colored dots in the manuscript margins—dismissed by historians as decorative flourishes—mapped exactly onto modern genetic variation hotspots. Red dots corresponded to populations with high-frequency protective alleles. Blue dots to populations decimated by plague. Yellow to intermediary zones where the disease had killed selectively, leaving genetic signatures visible seven hundred years later.

"That's impossible," she whispered to the empty lab.

She pulled up another manuscript page. Different monastery, different scribe, different century. Same marginal notation system. The fluorescent lights flickered overhead, casting brief shadows across her workspace. Sarah barely noticed.

She ran the overlay.

Same result.

Her hands trembled as she opened a third document—a merchant's ledger from Mainz, 1347. The "decorative" dots appeared again, alongside what looked like trade routes mapped in fading brown ink.

She aligned it with modern genetic drift patterns.

Ninety-four percent accuracy.

"They knew." Sarah pushed back from her desk, the chair wheels squeaking on linoleum. Her heart hammered against her ribs. "Somehow they knew which populations would survive."

But how? Medieval monks didn't have PCR machines or gene sequencers. They didn't understand inheritance beyond "like begets like." They certainly couldn't predict which specific genetic variations would protect against *Yersinia pestis*.

Unless.

Unless someone had taught them.

Sarah pulled up GenVault's restricted database—the one she technically shouldn't have access to, but Dr. Aris had given her his login credentials six months ago "for emergencies." Her mentor trusted her. Or wanted her to find something he couldn't openly show her. The distinction felt important now.

She typed the query she'd been avoiding for weeks:

**SEARCH: Population genetic outliers / CCR5-Delta32 / Non-European distribution / Pre-1348 markers**

The results loaded with a soft ping that seemed deafening in the empty lab.

And stopped making sense.

CCR5-Delta32 was supposed to be a European mutation, selected for by plague exposure over multiple centuries. Standard evolutionary response to massive selective pressure. But GenVault's proprietary dataset—collected from tissue samples across five continents, millions of individuals—showed unexpected high-frequency populations in North Africa, Central Asia, the Levant, and...

Sarah's blood went cold.

The same regions marked with red dots in the medieval manuscripts.

*Before* the plague had even reached them.

Someone had marked survival populations *before the catastrophe occurred*.

Her phone buzzed against the desk, vibrating against the hard surface. Dr. Otto Aris. Her mentor, the only person at GenVault who still seemed to care about science over stock prices.

She answered. "Dr. Aris, I know it's late, but I found—"

"Sarah." His voice was wrong. Tight. Afraid. Wind noise and traffic sounds in the background—he was outside, moving fast. "Hang up this phone. Right now."

She froze. "What?"

"They're listening. Every query to the restricted database triggers real-time monitoring." His voice dropped to barely a whisper. "I saw your access logs. The population variants. The medieval correlation analysis. You tripped a Level Three security flag."

"I was just researching—"

"I know what you were doing. I know what you found." A car door slammed in the background. "You think you're the first?"

Sarah's office suddenly felt very small. The security camera in the corner—when had that little red light started blinking?

"Dr. Aris, what are you talking about?"

"Twenty years ago, I found the same pattern. The same impossible correlation between medieval annotations and modern genetic data." His breathing was ragged, words coming too fast. "I published a preliminary paper. GenVault acquired my lab six months later. Buried my research. Made me head of a department that doesn't do anything real. Gave me a corner office and a salary that guarantees my silence."

"That doesn't make sense. Why would they—"

"Because it's not a discovery, Sarah. It's a *map*." His voice cracked. "The founders of GenVault, the private equity backers—they have the original manuscripts. The complete cipher system. They've been using it to select for specific genetic traits for seventy years."

Sarah's mind raced. This was insane. Paranoid conspiracy theory. The kind of thing you heard from people who thought vaccines caused autism and the moon landing was staged.

But Dr. Aris was the most rational person she'd ever worked with. Published in *Nature* seven times. Peer review committee chair. The man who'd taught her that science meant following evidence wherever it led, even when you didn't like the destination.

"That's..." She couldn't finish the sentence. The air conditioning droned louder, mechanical and indifferent.

"I'm sorry," Aris said quietly. "I hoped you wouldn't look. Hoped you'd stay focused on the influenza project and never wonder why GenVault funds medieval manuscript digitization projects through shell companies. But you're too curious. Too good. And now they know you've seen it."

The fluorescent lights seemed brighter. The lab felt exposed, like a fishbowl lit from all sides.

"What do I do?"

"Run. Tonight. Don't go home. Don't access your email. Don't—"

The call cut off mid-sentence.

Sarah stared at her phone. Then at her monitors, where seven centuries of genetic manipulation glowed in red and blue and yellow dots.

She had maybe ten minutes before building security checked the late-night lab access logs. Maybe less if Dr. Aris was right about active monitoring.

Her research. She couldn't leave it.

Sarah grabbed a flash drive from her drawer—the hallway lights outside flickered on. Footsteps? Or just the cleaning crew making their rounds?

She jammed the drive into the USB port, started copying files. The manuscript overlays. Her genomic analysis. The restricted database queries. Everything that proved—

*Progress: 23%... 34%...*

The lab door opened.

Sarah spun.

Marcus Webb stood in the doorway. VP of Research Operations. Expensive charcoal suit at midnight, shirt still crisp despite the hour. Two security guards behind him, radios crackling static like mechanical insects.

"Dr. Chen." His smile didn't reach his eyes. "Working late?"

"Just finishing some data analysis." Her hand closed around the flash drive, still transferring. *Progress: 67%.* The computer fan whirred louder, processing thousands of files.

"On medieval manuscripts?" Marcus walked into the lab, hands in pockets. Casual. Threatening. His leather shoes clicked on linoleum with each deliberate step. "Interesting pivot from your assigned project. The H5N1 vaccine study, wasn't it?"

"Following a hunch about population genetics. Probably nothing."

"Probably." He glanced at her monitors. His expression didn't change, but something flickered behind his eyes—recognition, calculation. "Still, we should discuss it. Conference room. Now."

Not a request.

*Progress: 81%.*

"Of course." Sarah's voice was steadier than she felt. The metal flash drive was hot against her palm. "Let me just save my work—"

"The guards will secure your station. Security protocol for after-hours restricted database access." Marcus gestured to the door. The fluorescent lights reflected off his expensive watch—Rolex, probably fifty thousand dollars. "Please."

*Progress: 89%.*

Sarah's mind raced through possibilities, discarding each one. If she refused, they'd know she understood exactly what was happening. If she went, they'd wipe her computer before the transfer finished and she'd have nothing.

No proof.

No evidence.

No way to prove that seven centuries ago, someone had figured out how to read—and rewrite—the future of the human race.

She looked at Marcus Webb. At his patient, predatory smile.

Then at her monitors, where impossible knowledge glowed in colored dots.

"Actually," Sarah said, "I need to use the restroom first."

She grabbed her shoulder bag—worn leather, the lavender sachet her mother had tucked inside making it smell like home and safety—leaving the flash drive hidden under a stack of papers. Walked past Marcus toward the door, pulse hammering a warning rhythm in her throat.

"Dr. Chen."

She stopped. Pulse hammering.

"Your phone and access badge. Company property. You'll need to leave them here."

Her stomach dropped. But she pulled out her phone, set it on the desk beside her badge. Both were warm from her body heat, still alive with her digital life.

Marcus nodded to the taller guard. "Escort her."

---

## Scene 2: The Bathroom Gambit

The women's restroom was down one floor. Sarah walked ahead of the guard—young, bored, probably working his way through community college and had no idea he was babysitting a geneticist who'd just stumbled onto a seventy-year conspiracy.

The stairwell smelled like industrial cleaner and concrete dust, familiar and institutional.

"Wait here," Sarah said at the bathroom door.

The guard shrugged, leaned against the wall. Pulled out his phone, thumbs moving across the screen in the universal language of boredom.

Sarah went inside. Checked the stalls—empty. No windows. No fire exit. Just industrial tile, harsh fluorescent lights, and the sharp antiseptic smell that made her eyes water.

She was trapped.

No phone. No research. No proof. And in about five minutes, Marcus Webb would realize she'd figured out exactly what GenVault really was.

Sarah leaned against the sink. The porcelain was cold. Her reflection stared back, pale and frightened. Thirty-two years old. MIT PhD in three years. Published in *Cell* at twenty-eight. The brilliant Dr. Sarah Chen, who everyone said would revolutionize genomic medicine.

And now?

Now she was about to disappear.

*Think. What would someone who wasn't about to get vanished do?*

Then she saw it. The bathroom vent. Large. Old building, old ventilation system—probably hadn't been updated since the 1970s. The grate was held by just four Phillips-head screws.

She didn't have a screwdriver.

But she had a lab badge.

No—wait. She'd left that on her desk. Think. What did she have?

Her bag. She dumped it on the counter. Wallet. Keys. Lip balm. A pen. Receipts. A tampon. The lavender sachet. Nothing useful.

Except—

The underwire from her bra.

Sarah locked the stall door, removed her bra under her shirt, worked the underwire free from the fabric. Thin, flexible metal. She bent it, creating a makeshift screwdriver tip.

Climbed onto the sink—her shoes scraped porcelain loudly. Started working the screws.

The first one came loose after thirty seconds of prying, metal squealing softly. The second stuck, corrosion making it stubborn, then gave way with a pop that seemed deafening.

The guard knocked. "You okay in there?"

"Yeah, just—" Think. Woman troubles. Something he won't question. "—stomach issue. Bad pad thai at lunch."

Silence. Then: "Take your time."

Third screw. Fourth screw. Each turn sent vibrations up her wrist. The grate came free.

Sarah pulled herself up into the vent. It was tighter than movies made it look—she had to army-crawl, her bag scraping metal with each movement, the sound amplified by the enclosed space. Dust and cobwebs. Darkness. The smell of old metal and something long-dead rotting in the ductwork.

But she fit.

Behind her, muffled through the vent: "Dr. Chen? You've been in there a while. Dr. Chen?"

She crawled faster, palms sliding on decades of accumulated grime. The vent branched. She went right, toward what she hoped was the building's east side. Away from the lab. Away from Marcus Webb and whatever conference room interrogation he had planned.

Voices behind her, echoing and distorted: "—the vent, she's in the fucking vent, get someone up to the second floor—"

Sarah's hands found another grate. She kicked it. Once—the impact jarred up her leg. Twice—it held. Third kick and it fell away, clattering onto tile.

She dropped through. Her ankle twisted on landing. Pain shot up her calf but she kept moving, adrenaline overriding the injury.

Different bathroom. Second floor, west wing. Sarah ran.

Emergency exit stairs. Down four flights, her injured ankle throbbing with each step. The metal railing was slick under her sweating palm. Behind her, the stairwell door banged open.

"Freeze! GenVault security!"

She didn't freeze.

Ground floor. Emergency exit. The alarm would trigger but she didn't care anymore.

Sarah hit the push bar.

The alarm screamed into the night.

Cold November air slapped her face. Frost on the asphalt. Her breath came out in clouds. She ran toward the street, her twisted ankle screaming with each step.

Headlights swept across her. A car screeched to a stop. Diesel exhaust, acrid in her throat.

Sarah stumbled back—

The passenger window rolled down. Dr. Otto Aris looked out, his normally composed face haggard. He'd aged ten years since this afternoon.

"Get in. Now."

Sarah didn't hesitate. She threw herself into the passenger seat. The interior smelled like old coffee and stale cigarette smoke—Aris had quit five years ago, but the car remembered.

Aris accelerated before she'd closed the door.

---

## Scene 3: The Truth She Wasn't Ready For

They drove in silence for ten minutes. Aris checked his mirrors obsessively, took random turns, doubled back twice. Classic surveillance detection. Sarah watched her side mirror, looking for headlights that followed too consistently.

Finally, he pulled into a 24-hour diner parking lot. Killed the engine. The car ticked as it cooled, metal contracting in the November night.

Through the diner windows, Sarah could see normal life continuing: a tired waitress refilling coffee, a drunk couple laughing at the counter, a trucker hunched over apple pie. People who had no idea that the world was not what they thought it was.

"Are we safe?" Sarah asked.

"For about twenty minutes." Aris looked at her. Up close, he looked exhausted. Defeated. "Did you finish the data transfer?"

"No. Maybe got eighty-nine percent."

He nodded slowly. "Doesn't matter. They'll purge it anyway. Probably scrubbing your entire directory right now." He pulled a worn leather satchel from the back seat. "The question is: are you ready for this?"

"Ready for what?"

"The truth. The real reason I gave you database access. The reason I've been leaving medieval manuscripts on your desk for six months, hoping you'd notice the pattern without me having to explicitly show you."

Sarah's head spun. "You... you wanted me to find this?"

"I needed someone to confirm what I'd found twenty years ago. Someone brilliant enough to see it. Someone innocent enough that GenVault wouldn't be watching." Aris's hands shook slightly. "I'm sorry. I used you. But I had to know if I was insane or if it was real."

He opened the satchel. Inside: photocopied manuscripts, genealogical charts, genetic data, population maps. Years of work.

"What is this?"

"Twenty years of research they think I abandoned." Aris spread papers on the dashboard. "The medieval cipher isn't just correlation, Sarah. It's causation."

"That's impossible. You can't cause genetic variation across populations without—" She stopped.

"Without deliberate intervention across multiple generations," Aris finished. "Exactly."

He pulled out a manuscript page—1338, nine years before the Black Death reached Europe. Red dots marked specific villages in northern Italy.

"Now look at modern genetic sampling from those exact locations."

Sarah leaned in. The data was undeniable. The villages marked in red had the highest frequency of CCR5-Delta32 in all of Europe.

*Before the plague arrived.*

"Someone was tracking which populations would survive," Sarah whispered. "Before the selective pressure even occurred."

"Not just tracking." Aris flipped to another document—a genealogical chart from medieval Mainz, annotated with symbols that looked like the marginal dots. "Guiding. Preserving. Optimizing."

"But that would require—"

"A conspiracy spanning centuries. Yes." Aris pulled out more documents. Each one connecting to the next like a web. "The medieval cipher-makers weren't predicting plague survival. They were *ensuring* it. Positioning resilient populations. Tracking bloodlines. Encoding knowledge that would preserve civilization through collapse."

A car pulled into the lot. Both of them tensed. Just a night-shift worker getting coffee.

"How is this possible?" Sarah's voice was barely a whisper.

"I don't know the mechanism. Maybe experimental methods we'd call genetic engineering. Maybe sophisticated statistical observation over dozens of generations." He hesitated. "Maybe they knew things we've forgotten. Maybe there was a previous technological civilization that collapsed, and they preserved the knowledge."

"That's insane."

"Is it?" Aris met her eyes. "Humans have been around for 200,000 years. We think civilization started 5,000 years ago. But what if that's just when we *recovered* from a previous collapse? What if the medieval cipher-makers were protecting the last remnants of much older knowledge?"

Sarah's hands shook. The diner's neon sign reflected in the car windows—red and blue, pulsing like the dots in the manuscripts.

"We have to expose them."

"With what evidence?" Aris gestured at the papers. "Photocopies? Correlations? GenVault's legal team would bury this before you finished the press release. And we'd both disappear."

"Then what do we do?"

Aris pulled out a piece of paper. An address, written in careful block letters:

**Dr. Evelyn Marsh**
**47 Elm Street, New Haven, CT**

"Who is she?"

"The only surviving member of the original translation team that decoded the Aethelred manuscripts in 1978." Aris's jaw tightened. "The rest of the team is dead. Car accidents. Cancer. One 'suicide' that had the wrong ligature marks. Marsh went underground in 1982. Changed her name. Stopped publishing."

"Why her?"

"Because she has what I don't: the source documents. The original cipher key that proves the medieval system wasn't just observation—it was engineering." He handed Sarah the address. "If anyone can prove what GenVault is doing, and how the medieval network did it first, it's her."

Sarah took the paper. It was warm from his hand. "Why don't you go?"

"Because they've been watching me for twenty years. Every email. Every phone call. Every person I meet." He pulled an envelope from the satchel—cash, maybe two thousand dollars in worn bills. "But you're new. Unknown. You might have a twelve-hour head start before they connect you to me."

"Before they what?"

Aris looked at her. His expression said everything he couldn't.

"They killed the others," Sarah said flatly. "The ones who found the pattern before me."

He didn't deny it.

Sarah took the envelope. The cash smelled like old paper and other people's hands.

"One more thing." Aris handed her a small object wrapped in cloth. "Don't open this until you're with Dr. Marsh. She'll know what to do with it."

The object was heavy. Metal, maybe. About the size of a deck of cards.

"What is it?"

"Insurance. Proof. A piece of the puzzle they thought was destroyed." Aris started the car. "No credit cards. No phone calls to anyone you know. Get to New Haven. Find Marsh. And Sarah?"

"Yes?"

"Don't trust anyone who offers to help. The network is everywhere. It's been everywhere for seven hundred years. You'll think you're paranoid. You're not. You're not paranoid enough."

He drove her to the Greyhound station. Harsh fluorescent lights, diesel smell, a drunk sleeping on a bench. The ticket clerk looked half-asleep behind bulletproof glass.

Sarah got out, clutching the envelope and the wrapped object.

"Dr. Aris?"

He looked at her through the window. His face was gray.

"What happened to them? The others who found the pattern?"

His expression answered before his words did. "Go, Sarah. And whatever you do—don't look back."

The car pulled away, taillights disappearing into Boston traffic.

Sarah stood in the fluorescent glare, holding everything she had left: two thousand dollars in cash, an address in New Haven, a mysterious object wrapped in cloth, and the terrible knowledge that somewhere in medieval manuscripts, someone had figured out how to read and rewrite the future of the human race.

And GenVault had been using that knowledge for seventy years.

The next bus to New Haven left in forty minutes.

Sarah bought a ticket with cash, giving the sleepy clerk a fake name. The thermal paper was still warm.

## Scene 4: The Long Road

### Greyhound Bus to New Haven
### Wednesday, November 15, 2019 - 3:15 AM

The bus smelled like diesel fuel, stale chips, and someone's too-sweet perfume masking something less pleasant underneath. Sarah sat in the back, watching Boston disappear through the smeared window. Her reflection stared back—pale, frightened, still wearing her lab coat.

She shrugged it off, balled it up, shoved it under the seat. One less thing identifying her.

The bus was mostly empty. An old woman slept across the aisle. Two rows up, a college kid with headphones, head bobbing. Nobody paying attention to the geneticist on the run.

Sarah pulled out the photocopied manuscripts Aris had given her. Studied the dots by the overhead light.

Red. Blue. Yellow. Geometric patterns that looked decorative but encoded something else.

Something that predicted genetic variation with ninety-four percent accuracy.

Seven hundred years ago.

The past wasn't supposed to know more than the present. But what if it did?

What if the Black Death hadn't just killed a third of Europe—what if it had killed everyone who understood what was really happening? Everyone except the cipher-makers, who'd hidden their knowledge in plain sight, disguised as marginal decorations in religious manuscripts?

And what if that knowledge had survived?

What if it was still being used?

Sarah unwrapped the object Aris had given her.

It was a key. Old. Iron. Covered in the same geometric symbols as the manuscript dots.

She stared at it. Heavy in her palm. Cold metal warming to her body temperature.

*Don't open this until you're with Dr. Marsh.*

But what was it a key to?

The college kid got up, walked past toward the bathroom. Sarah tensed, watching. He went in. Came out. Sat back down.

She was being paranoid.

Or not paranoid enough.

Her mind kept returning to Aris's final warning: *Don't trust anyone who offers to help.*

Sarah looked at the kid with headphones. She needed help. But could she risk it?

She stood, walked up to him. Tapped his shoulder.

He pulled out one earbud, annoyed.

"Sorry," Sarah said. "My phone died. Emergency. Could I borrow yours for one quick call?"

The kid hesitated.

Sarah pulled out a twenty from the envelope. "Please. Five minutes."

Money changed hands. The phone was cracked, sticky.

Sarah walked to the back of the bus. Dialed her cousin David's number from memory.

Three rings. Four. Then a groggy, annoyed voice: "Yeah? Who calls at three in the morning?"

"David. It's Sarah. Don't say my name."

Pause. Then, carefully: "Okay..."

"I need you to do something for me. Something illegal."

"Jesus. What—"

"GenVault Corporation. My user directory on their research servers. There's a flash drive on my desk with data they're going to delete. I need you to pull the files from their backup system before the purge cycle runs."

Silence. The bus hit a pothole. Then: "You're serious."

"Life or death serious. People are after me."

"What people?"

"I can't explain. Can you do it? You work for the DOD—you have tools, right? Ways to access private servers?"

"Sarah, what you're describing is corporate espionage. I could lose my clearance. Go to federal prison. What did you find?"

"Evidence that GenVault has been manipulating the human genome for seventy years. Using knowledge from medieval manuscripts that shouldn't exist."

The words sounded insane out loud.

David was quiet for a long moment. She could hear him typing—he was already at a computer, probably couldn't sleep anyway. "This is insane."

"I know."

"You could get arrested. I could get arrested—"

"I know. I wouldn't ask if it wasn't..." Her voice cracked. "Remember when you said you owed me for getting you into MIT? For convincing the admissions committee to overlook your record?"

"That was different—"

"They've been engineering people, David. Selecting for traits. Guiding evolution." Her whisper was barely audible over the bus engine. "Please."

Another pause. More typing. "What exactly am I looking for?"

Relief flooded through her. She gave him the details—server addresses, folder structures, file names. The flash drive location. Everything.

"How do I reach you?" David asked.

"You don't. I'll contact you in forty-eight hours. Different number."

"Where do I send the files?"

"I'll figure something out. Dead drop. I'll let you know."

"Sarah—"

"Thank you. Tell Aunt Lin I love her."

"Don't do that. Don't talk like you're—"

She hung up. Walked back to the college kid, handed him his phone. Wiped her hand on her jeans.

"Thanks."

The kid nodded, already back in his music.

Sarah returned to her seat. The iron key felt heavy in her pocket. Outside, the highway stretched endlessly, broken white lines disappearing under the bus.

In forty-eight hours, she'd either have proof of a centuries-long conspiracy to manipulate human genetics.

Or she'd be a paranoid ex-scientist with a stolen key and delusions.

The bus rolled on. Sarah closed her eyes.

Tried not to think about Dr. Aris's warning.

Tried not to think about what happened to the others who'd found the pattern.

Tried not to think about the fact that somewhere in the past, someone had figured out how to encode the future in colored dots and geometric symbols.

And that seven hundred years later, someone was still using that knowledge.

Still selecting. Still optimizing. Still guiding human evolution toward some unknown end.

The road to New Haven stretched ahead in the darkness.

Sarah hoped Dr. Evelyn Marsh had answers.

Because the questions were multiplying faster than she could process them.

And the dawn was still three hours away.
