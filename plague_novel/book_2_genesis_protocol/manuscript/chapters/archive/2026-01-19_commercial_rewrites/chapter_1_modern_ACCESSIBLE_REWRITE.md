# CHAPTER 1 - THE PATTERN

**Modern Timeline - Boston, Massachusetts**
**November 15, 2019 - 1:23 AM**

---

Sarah Chen ran.

Through the emergency stairwell, taking steps three at a time, her laptop bag slamming against her hip with every landing. Twelve floors. Her lungs burned. Behind her, the stairwell door crashed open—security, moving faster than she'd expected.

She should have left the evidence behind.

Too late now.

Eighth floor. The flash drive in her jacket pocket felt like it weighed fifty pounds. Seven centuries of genetic manipulation compressed into 64 gigabytes. Proof that the Black Death hadn't been natural. Proof that GenVault wasn't a pharmaceutical company. Proof that Sarah Chen had just become the most dangerous person in Boston.

"Dr. Chen!" Male voice, echoing down the stairwell. Professional. Calm. "Building security. We need you to stop."

Like hell.

Fifth floor. Her ankle turned on a landing and she grabbed the railing, momentum carrying her forward into a stumbling recovery. The laptop bag swung wide, banged into concrete. Please don't let the hard drive crack. Please let the backup files still be intact.

Behind her: footsteps accelerating. Radio crackle. "Subject is fleeing. Repeat, subject is fleeing toward ground level exits."

They weren't treating this like a researcher working late who'd set off a false alarm. They were treating this like a containment breach.

Because that's exactly what it was.

Third floor. Sarah's hand slipped on the metal railing—sweat, fear, the terrible clarity that came from knowing too much. Twenty minutes ago she'd been analyzing medieval manuscripts in a comfortable lab. Now she was running for her life from a company that had spent seventy years hiding evidence of engineered genocide.

Dr. Aris had tried to warn her. His voice on the phone, tight with fear: *"They're listening. You triggered a Level Three security flag. The others who found the pattern are dead."*

Then the call had cut off.

And Marcus Webb—VP of Research Operations, fifty-thousand-dollar watch, dead eyes—had appeared in her lab doorway with security guards and a smile that meant Sarah was never going home.

Ground floor.

She hit the exit bar, burst into the parking garage. Cold November air slapped her face. Her car was forty yards away—black Civic, unremarkable, parked in shadow because she'd worked late enough that the good spots were all taken.

Keys in hand. Sprinting.

The stairwell door exploded open behind her.

"Dr. Chen, stop! This is building security!"

She didn't stop. Reached the Civic, fumbled the key fob, heard the locks click open with a chirp that sounded deafening in the concrete garage.

Slid into the driver's seat. Laptop bag on passenger seat. Engine starting with a cough that felt like it took forever.

A security guard appeared at her window, hand raised. "Ma'am, turn off the vehicle—"

Sarah floored the accelerator.

The Civic shot backward, tires screaming. The guard jumped aside. Sarah cranked the wheel, shifted into drive, and accelerated toward the exit ramp.

In her rearview mirror: three more guards running from the stairwell. One speaking into a radio. One drawing—was that a gun?

Research facility security didn't carry guns.

Unless they weren't actually security.

Sarah took the exit ramp at forty, tires squealing on the spiral. The garage gate was already descending—someone had triggered lockdown. Twenty feet of clearance. Fifteen. Ten.

She ducked instinctively and gunned the engine.

The Civic shot through with inches to spare. The gate scraped across her roof with a metallic shriek that she felt in her teeth.

Then she was out, onto Causeway Street, merging into sparse 1 AM traffic like she hadn't just fled a billion-dollar pharmaceutical company with evidence of crimes that didn't have names yet.

Her hands shook on the wheel. The laptop bag had tipped over, spilling pens across the passenger seat. Her phone buzzed insistently—blocked number, over and over.

They wanted her to answer. Wanted to talk, negotiate, explain why she needed to come back and discuss this misunderstanding like reasonable professionals.

Sarah turned off her phone and threw it out the window.

Couldn't go home—they'd be waiting. Couldn't go to the police—GenVault had connections that went deeper than local law enforcement. Couldn't access her email or bank accounts or any of the digital infrastructure that normal people used to navigate modern life.

Dr. Aris had said: *"Run. Tonight. Don't go home. Don't access your email."*

Where had he been calling from? Where had he gone after the call cut off?

Sarah merged onto I-93 South, no destination in mind except *away*. Her fuel gauge read half-empty. Her bank card was at home in her purse. She had maybe forty dollars cash in her wallet and a flash drive containing evidence that could destroy one of the world's largest pharmaceutical companies.

If anyone believed her.

If she lived long enough to find someone who'd listen.

The city lights blurred past, reflecting off her windshield like stars falling. Sarah Chen—MIT PhD, respected genomics researcher, daughter of immigrants who'd believed absolutely in the promise of American science—was now a fugitive.

Because she'd found a pattern.

Because medieval monks had somehow known which populations would survive the Black Death, and that knowledge had been preserved, refined, weaponized across seven centuries.

Because GenVault wasn't trying to cure diseases.

They were trying to cause them.

Her rearview mirror showed nothing but normal traffic. No pursuit vehicles. No flashing lights. They'd lost her.

For now.

Sarah took the next exit, merged onto smaller roads, kept moving. Eventually she'd need to stop. Find somewhere to hide. Figure out what to do with the flash drive that held evidence of something so vast and terrible her mind still couldn't fully process it.

But first, she just needed to survive the next hour.

Then the hour after that.

Then maybe, if she was smart and lucky and the people chasing her made one single mistake, she'd survive long enough to expose what GenVault had been hiding.

The pattern that connected medieval manuscripts to modern genetics.

The cipher system that predicted plague survival seven hundred years before anyone understood DNA.

The network that had spent two thousand years engineering human evolution like it was livestock breeding.

Sarah Chen drove into the night with shaking hands and evidence that could save the world.

Or get her killed.

Probably both.

## Six Hours Earlier

**GenVault Corporation, Genomics Research Division**
**November 14, 2019 - 11:47 PM**

---

The lab had smelled like every lab Sarah had ever worked in: ethanol, plastic, and the faint electrical burn of equipment running past its prime. After fourteen hours straight, she'd stopped noticing. Her coffee had gone cold an hour ago, bitter film coating the surface.

Dr. Sarah Chen stared at the dual monitors. Left screen: a fourteenth-century manuscript page from the Bodleian Library's digital archive—marginal decorations, tiny colored dots surrounding Latin text about plague mortality. Right screen: her own genetic dataset, population variants in CCR5-Delta32, the mutation that protected against certain blood-borne diseases.

She'd been chasing a hunch for three weeks.

Now, at nearly midnight in an empty lab, the hunch had become certainty.

The dots weren't random.

Sarah overlaid the medieval pattern onto her genetic map, scaling the resolution. Her breath caught.

Perfect alignment.

The colored dots in the manuscript margins—dismissed by historians as decorative flourishes—mapped exactly onto modern genetic variation hotspots. Red dots matched populations with high-frequency protective genes. Blue dots matched populations decimated by plague. Yellow matched middle-ground zones where the disease had killed selectively, leaving genetic signatures visible seven hundred years later.

"That's impossible," she whispered to the empty lab.

She pulled up another manuscript page. Different monastery, different scribe, different century. Same marginal notation system.

She ran the overlay.

Same result.

Her hands trembled as she opened a third document—a merchant's ledger from Mainz, 1347. The "decorative" dots appeared again, alongside what looked like trade routes mapped in fading brown ink.

She aligned it with modern genetic drift patterns.

Ninety-four percent accuracy.

"They knew." Sarah pushed back from her desk, heart hammering. "Somehow they knew which populations would survive."

But how? Medieval monks didn't have PCR machines or gene sequencers. They didn't understand inheritance beyond "like begets like." They certainly couldn't predict which specific genetic variations would protect against *Yersinia pestis*—the bacteria that caused plague.

Unless.

Unless someone had taught them.

Sarah pulled up GenVault's restricted database—the one she technically shouldn't have access to, but Dr. Aris had given her his login credentials six months ago "for emergencies."

She typed the query she'd been avoiding for weeks:

**SEARCH: Population genetic outliers / CCR5-Delta32 / Non-European distribution / Pre-1348 markers**

The results loaded with a soft ping.

CCR5-Delta32 was supposed to be a European mutation, naturally selected by plague exposure over multiple centuries. But GenVault's proprietary dataset showed unexpected high-frequency populations in North Africa, Central Asia, the Levant.

The same regions marked with red dots in the medieval manuscripts.

*Before* the plague had even reached them.

Someone had marked survival populations *before the catastrophe occurred*.

Her phone buzzed. Dr. Otto Aris.

She answered. "Dr. Aris, I know it's late, but I found—"

"Sarah." His voice was wrong. Tight. Afraid. Wind noise and traffic sounds in the background—he was outside, moving. "Hang up this phone. Right now."

She froze. "What?"

"They're listening. They monitor every query to the restricted database in real-time." His voice dropped to barely a whisper. "I saw your access logs. The population variants that don't fit the evolutionary model. The medieval correlation analysis. You triggered a Level Three security flag."

"I was just researching—"

"I know what you were doing. I know what you found." A car door slammed. "You think you're the first?"

Sarah's office suddenly felt very small. The security camera in the corner—when had that little red light started blinking?

"Dr. Aris, what are you talking about?"

"Twenty years ago, I found the same pattern. The same impossible connection between medieval annotations and modern genetic data." His breathing was ragged. "I published a preliminary paper. GenVault acquired my lab six months later. Buried my research. Made me head of a department that doesn't do anything real."

"That doesn't make sense. Why would they—"

"Because it's not a discovery, Sarah. It's a *map*." His voice cracked. "The founders of GenVault, the private equity backers—they have the original manuscripts. The complete cipher system. They've been using it to select for specific genetic traits for seventy years."

This was insane. Paranoid. The kind of thing you heard from conspiracy theorists.

But Dr. Aris was the most rational person she'd ever worked with. Published in *Nature* seven times. The man who'd taught her that science meant following evidence wherever it led.

"That's..." She couldn't finish the sentence.

"I'm sorry," Aris said quietly. "I hoped you wouldn't look. But you're too curious. Too good. And now they know you've seen it."

The fluorescent lights seemed brighter. The lab felt exposed.

"What do I do?"

"Run. Tonight. Don't go home. Don't access your email. Don't—"

The call cut off mid-sentence.

Sarah stared at her phone. Then at her monitors, where seven centuries of genetic manipulation glowed in red and blue and yellow dots.

She had maybe ten minutes before building security checked the late-night lab access logs.

Her research. She couldn't leave it.

Sarah grabbed a flash drive from her drawer—hallway lights outside flickered on. Footsteps? Or just the cleaning crew?

She jammed the drive into the USB port, started copying files. The manuscript overlays. Her genetic analysis. The restricted database queries.

*Progress: 23%... 34%...*

The lab door opened.

Marcus Webb stood in the doorway. VP of Research Operations. Expensive charcoal suit at midnight, shirt still crisp. Two security guards behind him, radios crackling.

"Dr. Chen." His smile didn't reach his eyes. "Working late?"

"Just finishing some data analysis." Her hand closed around the flash drive, still transferring. *Progress: 67%.*

"On medieval manuscripts?" Marcus walked into the lab, hands in pockets. Casual. Threatening. "Interesting pivot from your assigned project."

"Following a hunch about population genetics. Probably nothing."

"Probably." He glanced at her monitors. Something flickered behind his eyes—recognition. "Still, we should discuss it. Conference room. Now."

Not a request.

*Progress: 81%.*

"Of course." Sarah's voice was steadier than she felt. "Let me just save my work—"

"The guards will secure your station. Security protocol for after-hours restricted database access." Marcus gestured to the door. "Please."

*Progress: 89%.*

If she refused, they'd know she understood exactly what was happening. If she went, they'd wipe her computer before the transfer finished and she'd have nothing.

*Progress: 94%.*

Sarah looked at Marcus Webb. At his patient, predatory smile.

*Progress: 97%.*

She yanked the flash drive from the USB port.

"Actually," Sarah said, "I need to use the restroom first."

She grabbed her shoulder bag, leaving her phone and access badge on the desk like Marcus had instructed. Walked past him toward the door.

The taller guard moved to escort her.

That's when Sarah ran.
