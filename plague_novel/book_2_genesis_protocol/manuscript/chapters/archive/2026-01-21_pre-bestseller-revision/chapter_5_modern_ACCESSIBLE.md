# CHAPTER 5 - THE INSIDE GAME

**96 Hours Until THRESHOLD Deployment**

## Modern Timeline - Site Seven Containment Facility, Northwestern Massachusetts

### Day 3 of Imprisonment

Sarah had been imprisoned for seventy-two hours. Three days of Morrison's clinical lectures about breeding programs and genetic optimization. Three days of comfortable captivity in a cell that felt more like an executive apartment than a prison.

Three days of planning her counterattack.

Because Sarah hadn't given up. She'd just gotten quieter about her resistance.

The facility operated on predictable rhythms. Morrison visited twice daily—morning sessions focused on philosophical justification, evening sessions on practical cooperation timelines. Nurses checked vitals every four hours. Guards rotated shifts at 6 AM, 2 PM, and 10 PM. Meals arrived at 7 AM, noon, and 6 PM, delivered by a woman in a janitorial uniform who never spoke.

Except yesterday, the janitor had spoken.

"Your water glass," she'd said quietly, setting down the lunch tray. "Check under it."

Sarah had waited until she was alone. Lifted the glass. Found a slip of paper with neat handwriting:

*Yuki Tanaka. Facility systems janitor. Grandmother resisted network in 1970s. I can help. When ready, leave fork on floor near door. I'll know.*

Sarah had memorized the message. Burned it in the bathroom sink. Flushed the ashes.

And this morning, she'd left a fork on the floor.

---

## Scene 2: The Network Inside

### Lunch, Day 3

Yuki Tanaka arrived with the noon meal tray. Same janitorial uniform. Same practiced invisibility that made guards look past her.

But this time, she lingered. "You dropped this yesterday," Yuki said loudly enough for the camera microphones, holding up the fork Sarah had deliberately left. Then, quieter: "Bathroom. Two minutes. I'll create a distraction."

Sarah nodded. Took the tray. Waited.

Ninety seconds later, the cell door buzzed. Morrison's voice over the intercom: "Dr. Chen, there's been a minor plumbing issue in the facility. You'll need to use the bathroom now before we shut off water to your wing."

The camera in the bathroom had a blind spot near the sink—Sarah had mapped it during her first day. She stood in that spot, waiting.

The ventilation grate popped open. Yuki's face appeared, upside-down. "Three minutes maximum before they wonder why I'm taking so long."

"How did you—"

"I maintain the surveillance system. I know every blind spot in this facility." Yuki's voice was matter-of-fact. "My grandmother, Akiko Tanaka, worked for GenVault in the 1970s. She discovered evidence of the breeding program. They disappeared her. Officially, she died in a car accident. Unofficially, she's buried somewhere in these woods."

"I'm sorry."

"Don't be sorry. Help me finish what she started." Yuki pulled a small device from her pocket. "This is a data transmitter. Disguised as a feminine hygiene product. Hide it in the bathroom cabinet. When Morrison leaves his tablet unattended—and he will, he always does after his evening lectures—you'll have approximately ninety seconds to photograph hub deployment data. The device will transmit images via cellular network to a secure server."

Sarah took the device. It looked exactly like its disguise—incredibly clever engineering. "Who receives the transmissions?"

"External resistance. People who've been fighting the network for decades. They'll use the intelligence to secure hubs before deployment." Yuki glanced at her watch. "One more minute. Listen carefully: Dr. James Bennett visits you tomorrow. He's your genetic counselor—evaluates psychological readiness for breeding program. He's a true believer. Network recruited him twelve years ago straight from Stanford. He genuinely thinks this serves humanity."

"Then why are you telling me about him?"

"Because true believers are the easiest to turn. They have principles. If you can prove the network lied about its foundational purpose, Bennett will reconsider everything." Yuki pulled a flash drive from her pocket. "This contains Otto Aris's historical research. The ORIGINAL Genesis Protocol from 1177 BCE. Proof that it was designed to PREVENT collapse, not engineer it. Show Bennett. Plant the seed of doubt."

Sarah took the flash drive. "How do I access it?"

"Morrison's tablet. USB-C port on the side. You'll have time when he forgets it." Yuki started closing the grate. "Thirty seconds. I need to go. Remember: When you photograph hub data, you're saving millions of lives. Every hub secured is millions who survive. Don't let Morrison's lectures make you forget that."

The grate clicked shut.

Sarah stood in the bathroom blind spot, holding a data transmitter and a flash drive containing evidence that could turn a true believer.

For the first time since her capture, she felt something other than despair.

She felt like she was fighting back.

---

## Scene 3: The True Believer

### Day 4, Afternoon

Dr. James Bennett arrived at 2 PM the next day. Late thirties, academic bearing, wire-rimmed glasses, dressed in a cardigan that made him look like he'd walked out of a university faculty lounge.

"Dr. Chen." He extended his hand with genuine warmth. "I'm Dr. Bennett. I'll be serving as your genetic counselor during your transition into the partnership program."

Sarah shook his hand. Studied him. Bennett wasn't performing—his kindness seemed real. Which made him either a sociopath or a true believer who genuinely thought the network was saving humanity.

"Genetic counselor," Sarah said. "That's a polite term for 'convince her to cooperate with forced breeding.'"

"I prefer to think of it as helping you understand why this program serves humanity's long-term survival." Bennett sat down, pulled out a tablet. "I know this is difficult. The network's methods can seem extreme from outside the context. But once you understand the mathematics—"

"I've heard the mathematics. Morrison covered them thoroughly." Sarah kept her voice neutral. "Cyclical collapse. 600-800 year cycles. We're overdue. The network prevents worse outcomes. Save billions through controlled population reduction."

"Exactly." Bennett looked pleased. "You're a scientist. You understand the empirical basis—"

"I understand that's what the network CLAIMS. I'm less convinced that's what the Genesis Protocol was originally designed to accomplish."

Bennett frowned. "What do you mean?"

Sarah took the risk. "The Genesis Protocol. Where did it come from? Who created it?"

"The network. Obviously. Nine hundred years of accumulated knowledge—"

"Twelve hundred BCE," Sarah interrupted. "Nefertari. Bronze Age Collapse. Egypt. That's when the ORIGINAL protocol was created. Thomas distributed a version in 1347, but he wasn't the originator. He was just the medieval distributor."

Bennett's expression shifted—uncertainty creeping into his certainty. "I... that's not in the official history. The network's founding documentation says—"

"The network's documentation is incomplete. Deliberately." Sarah pulled up her tablet—the one Morrison had given her for "educational purposes"—and loaded the first page of Otto Aris's research from the flash drive she'd accessed late last night using the USB-C port. "Dr. Bennett, have you ever read primary source documents about the Genesis Protocol's creation? Not network summaries. Original texts."

"Those are classified. Only board-level access—"

"Read this." Sarah turned the tablet so Bennett could see. "This is from archaeological research by Dr. Otto Aris. Egyptology specialist. He's spent forty years reconstructing the ORIGINAL Genesis Protocol from Bronze Age fragments."

Bennett read. His expression changed as he progressed through the text.

"This says..." Bennett looked up, disturbed. "This says the protocol was created to PREVENT civilizational collapse. To teach future generations pattern recognition so they could see collapse coming and intervene. Not to engineer collapse."

"Keep reading."

Bennett scrolled through more pages. "Nefertari created seven bronze keys containing encoding patterns. But Amenhotep—her chief priest—immediately corrupted the system. Turned prevention into engineering. Turned defensive knowledge into offensive manipulation."

"Three thousand years of corruption," Sarah said quietly. "What you believe is the network's purpose—engineering controlled collapse—that's the CORRUPTED version. The original purpose was preventing collapse by optimizing humanity's ability to adapt. Not by killing billions."

Bennett sat back. He looked shaken. "The network... they told us we were serving humanity. Preventing worse outcomes. Ensuring survivors could rebuild—"

"They lied to you. The same way they've lied to hundreds of researchers over centuries." Sarah kept her voice gentle. "You're not evil, Dr. Bennett. You're a true believer who was given false information. But now you know. The question is: What are you going to do about it?"

Bennett stared at the tablet. "I need to verify this. Independently. If what you're saying is true... if the network has been operating on corrupted principles for three millennia..."

"Then everything you believed about serving humanity was manipulation." Sarah met his eyes. "I'm not asking you to trust me. I'm asking you to investigate. Read primary sources. Check Aris's citations. Verify the archaeological evidence. Do what any good scientist would do—test the hypothesis."

Bennett stood abruptly. "I... need to think."

"Take the flash drive." Sarah ejected it, held it out. "Research independently. Form your own conclusions. If I'm wrong, you can report me to Morrison and continue with the breeding program counseling. But if I'm right..."

Bennett took the flash drive. His hand was shaking.

"If I'm right," Sarah continued, "then you're working for a conspiracy that corrupted a defensive protocol into genocidal engineering. And you need to decide whether you're willing to continue serving that corruption."

Bennett left without another word.

Sarah sat back, pulse racing. She'd just planted doubt in a true believer's mind. Either Bennett would report her immediately, or he'd investigate.

And if he investigated, he'd find the truth.

## Scene 4: The Weekly Meeting

### Day 5, Morning

Morrison entered with professional satisfaction. "Dr. Chen, your first compatibility meeting is scheduled for this afternoon. David Rivera has agreed to participate in the partnership evaluation program."

Sarah's breath caught. David Rivera. Carlos Rivera's brother. The man who'd helped coordinate Atlanta hub security before his brother's death.

"You're pairing me with someone whose brother you killed?"

"Mr. Rivera's brother died in a vehicular accident," Morrison said with clinical precision. "Unrelated to network activities. Mr. Rivera has been informed that his genetic markers indicate strong compatibility with your profile. He's chosen to honor his brother's legacy by participating in our optimization program."

Sarah didn't believe that for a second. David Rivera hadn't "chosen" anything. He'd been coerced, manipulated, or trapped the same way she had been.

But she also realized: If David was Carlos's brother, he might be looking for revenge. Might be looking for allies.

Might be exactly the asset she needed.

---

## Scene 5: The Ally

### Day 5, Afternoon

David Rivera entered her cell at 2 PM. Late twenties, Latino, athletic build, wearing civilian clothes—jeans, flannel shirt, hiking boots. His expression was carefully neutral, but his eyes were sharp, assessing.

"Dr. Chen." He extended his hand.

Sarah shook it. Their eyes met. She saw calculation there. Intelligence. And anger, carefully controlled.

"Mr. Rivera." Sarah gestured to the chair. "Morrison tells me we have compatible genetic markers."

"That's what they told me." David sat down. He glanced at the camera in the corner, then back to Sarah. His hands moved subtly—American Sign Language. *C-A-M-E-R-A-S W-A-T-C-H*

Sarah kept her expression neutral. Signed back: *I K-N-O-W*

David spoke aloud: "I understand you're a geneticist. This must be fascinating from a scientific perspective—the breeding optimization program."

His hands signed: *M-Y B-R-O-T-H-E-R D-I-E-D S-T-O-P-P-I-N-G T-H-E-M*

Sarah responded verbally: "Fascinating and deeply troubling. The ethics are complicated."

Her hands: *I-M S-O-R-R-Y. H-E W-A-S B-R-A-V-E*

"Ethics always are," David said. "But sometimes you have to make difficult choices for the greater good."

His hands: *T-H-E-Y K-I-L-L-E-D H-I-M. I W-A-N-T R-E-V-E-N-G-E*

"The greater good," Sarah said carefully. "That's what Morrison keeps telling me. That serving the network serves humanity."

Her hands: *I W-A-N-T T-O S-T-O-P T-H-E-M*

David nodded. "Morrison is very persuasive about the mathematics. The cyclical collapse theory. The need for controlled intervention."

His hands: *I-M W-I-T-H Y-O-U. W-H-A-T-S T-H-E P-L-A-N*

Sarah felt hope surge. She had an ally. Inside the facility. Someone with Carlos Rivera's intelligence-gathering skills and David's personal motivation.

They spent the next hour having two conversations simultaneously. Verbally, they discussed genetic compatibility, breeding program protocols, and Morrison's philosophical justifications. Anyone listening would hear a scientist and a recruit having an awkward but cooperative first meeting.

Via ASL, they coordinated.

David had been documenting facility security for months. Guard rotations. Access codes. Emergency exits. Everything they'd need for an escape plan.

Sarah had Yuki's backdoor system and the data transmitter. She could photograph Morrison's deployment files and transmit hub intelligence to external resistance.

Together, they could do more than just document. They could act.

By the end of the meeting, they had a plan.

David would continue his "cooperation," attending weekly compatibility meetings to maintain his access. Sarah would continue appearing to consider Morrison's offers, buying time. Yuki would continue providing technical support and intelligence.

And when the opportunity came—when THRESHOLD deployment created enough chaos that security protocols failed—they would escape.

"I look forward to our next meeting," David said aloud as Morrison arrived to escort him out.

His hands signed: *O-N-E W-E-E-K. S-T-A-Y S-T-R-O-N-G*

Sarah signed back: *F-O-R Y-O-U-R B-R-O-T-H-E-R*

David's expression didn't change, but his eyes showed grief and determination.

He left with Morrison.

Sarah sat alone in her cell, no longer feeling imprisoned.

She had allies. She had a plan. She had a data transmitter that would save millions of lives with every hub location she photographed.

Morrison thought he'd captured and broken her.

He'd actually given her exactly what she needed to fight back from inside his facility.

## Scene 6: The First Intelligence Drop

### Day 6, Evening

Morrison arrived for his evening lecture at 7 PM. He talked for forty-five minutes about genetic partnership protocols, breeding timelines, and the importance of willing participation in optimization programs.

Sarah appeared to listen. Asked thoughtful questions. Showed signs of the "psychological adjustment" Morrison expected.

And when Morrison gestured enthusiastically to emphasize a point about multi-generational genetic improvement, he set his tablet down on the side table.

Morrison talked for another eight minutes. Sarah waited, timing it perfectly.

"Dr. Morrison," she said, "could I see the genetic compatibility analysis you mentioned? The one showing optimal partner traits?"

"Of course." Morrison reached for his tablet—then paused. "Actually, I have a hard copy in my office that shows the visualization more clearly. Give me two minutes."

He left.

Forgot his tablet.

Sarah had ninety seconds according to Yuki's estimate.

She grabbed the tablet. Morrison's administrative account was still logged in. She navigated to the THRESHOLD deployment dashboard.

**Hubs Secured: 0 of 47**
**Deployment Timeline: 96 hours**

Sarah scrolled frantically. Hub locations. Detailed addresses. Storage specifications.

She pulled out her phone—they'd let her keep it, thinking she had no way to communicate externally. She photographed screen after screen.

Atlanta hub (THRESH-01): Hartsfield-Jackson Airport, Cargo Facility 7, Section D
Chicago hub (THRESH-04): O'Hare International, South Cargo Complex
Los Angeles hub (THRESH-08): LAX, Terminal B Air Filtration System
Seattle hub (THRESH-11): Sea-Tac Airport, International Terminal Maintenance Level 3
Tokyo hub (THRESH-15): Haneda Airport, Cargo Terminal 2
Seoul hub (THRESH-19): Incheon International, Air Circulation Main Control
Paris hub (THRESH-23): Charles de Gaulle, Terminal 2E Environmental Systems
London hub (THRESH-27): Heathrow, Terminal 5 HVAC Control Room

Eight hubs. Complete addresses. Storage locations. Access protocols.

Sarah heard footsteps in the hallway.

She quickly navigated back to the compatibility analysis screen. Set the tablet down. Returned to her chair.

Morrison entered with the hard copy analysis. "Here we are. This shows the correlation between your markers and optimal partner profiles—"

Sarah listened. Nodded. Asked more questions.

And in her pocket, her phone vibrated three times.

Yuki's transmitter confirming upload: *Data received. Transmitting to external resistance.*

Sarah had just given the FBI exact locations for eight THRESHOLD hubs.

Eight hubs that could be secured before deployment.

Eight hubs that wouldn't kill millions of people.

Morrison continued his lecture, completely unaware that his prisoner had just stolen critical intelligence from under his nose.

Sarah smiled and asked about breeding program timelines, playing the cooperative captive perfectly.

While eight cities worth of lives were being saved by the information now racing through encrypted channels to agents who would secure those hubs before THRESHOLD could deploy.

Morrison thought he controlled her.

He had no idea she was dismantling his genocide one hub at a time, right from inside his facility.

## Scene 7: Bennett Returns

### Day 7, Morning

Dr. James Bennett returned at 10 AM. He looked like he hadn't slept. His cardigan was rumpled. His eyes were red.

"Dr. Chen," he said without preamble. "I've spent the last three days verifying Otto Aris's research. I accessed restricted archaeological archives. Cross-referenced primary sources. Examined the original Genesis Protocol fragments from Bronze Age Egypt."

Sarah waited.

"You were right." Bennett sat down heavily. "The network lied to me. For twelve years, I believed I was serving humanity. Preventing worse outcomes. Ensuring optimal genetics survived civilizational collapse." His voice cracked slightly. "I was serving a corruption. A three-thousand-year distortion of prevention protocols into genocidal engineering."

"I'm sorry," Sarah said quietly.

"Don't be sorry. Help me stop them." Bennett looked at her with newfound determination. "I have board-level access to network systems. I know deployment protocols. I know hub locations—all forty-seven of them, not just the initial six-hub Phase One field validation. I know Phase Two timelines."

Sarah's pulse quickened. "You'd help us?"

"I'd help humanity." Bennett pulled out a flash drive. "This contains everything I can access without triggering security alerts. Hub locations. Personnel files. Genetic targeting specifications. Deployment coordination protocols. Everything you'd need to prevent THRESHOLD from deploying."

Sarah took the flash drive. It felt impossibly light for something containing intelligence that could save billions of lives.

"Why are you doing this?" she asked.

"Because I'm a scientist." Bennett smiled grimly. "I follow evidence. The evidence says the network corrupted a prevention protocol into a genocide engine. I won't serve that. I'd rather die trying to stop it than live knowing I helped."

"They'll kill you. When they realize you helped—"

"Probably." Bennett stood. "But I'll die knowing I served humanity instead of serving corruption. That's worth something."

He walked to the door. Paused. Looked back.

"For what it's worth, Dr. Chen—you were right. About the cyclical collapse, about the need for intervention, about teaching humanity to see patterns and adapt. That's what the ORIGINAL protocol was supposed to accomplish. Not through genocide. Through education. Pattern recognition. Collective wisdom."

"We can still do that," Sarah said. "After we stop THRESHOLD."

"I hope so." Bennett knocked on the door. The guard opened it. "I'll see you next week for our follow-up counseling session."

But his eyes said: *You won't see me again. I'm disappearing. Taking what I know and running. Use the intelligence. Stop them.*

Bennett left.

Sarah held the flash drive containing intelligence on all forty-seven THRESHOLD hubs.

She now had the complete blueprint for preventing Phase One deployment.

And Morrison still thought she was a cooperative captive considering his breeding program offers.
