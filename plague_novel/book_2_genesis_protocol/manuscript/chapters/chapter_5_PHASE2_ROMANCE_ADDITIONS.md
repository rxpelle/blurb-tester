Sarah's first thought was that Morrison had excellent taste in genetic compatibility. David Rivera was—objectively, scientifically speaking—an attractive man. Not that it mattered. She was a prisoner. He was coerced. This was Morrison's breeding program, not a dating app.

**AFTER: "She had allies. She had a plan..."**

**INSERT (Internal Conflict):**

She had allies. She had a plan. She had a data transmitter that would save millions of lives with every hub location she photographed.

And she had David Rivera's eyes burned into her memory—the way he'd looked at her during that final sign, like she mattered beyond tactical value.

Sarah pushed the thought away. This was a prison. He was coerced. She was imprisoned. Whatever that moment had been, it was adrenaline and shared danger and the human need for connection in isolation.

Nothing more.

Couldn't be more.

*Shouldn't* be more.

But when she closed her eyes to sleep that night, she didn't think about hub locations or deployment protocols.

She thought about the half-second his hand had stayed against hers. The warmth of it. The promise of *one week*.

And told herself it was just tactical anticipation.

It was a convincing lie.

Until it wasn't.

## NEW SCENE: Week 2 Meeting (Add After Scene 7)

### Scene 8: Coffee Theory (NEW)

**Day 14, Afternoon**

David arrived for their second compatibility meeting carrying two paper cups from the facility cafeteria. Steam rose from the tops. The smell of coffee cut through the sterile prison air like a memory of freedom.

"Thought you might want actual coffee," he said aloud for the cameras. "Better than the vending machine version they provide."

He set one cup in front of Sarah. Their hands brushed during the handoff. Brief. Electric.

David sat across from her. Signed with his free hand: *M-O-R-R-I-S-O-N T-H-I-N-K-S W-E-R-E B-O-N-D-I-N-G*

Sarah sipped the coffee. It was terrible—burnt, over-extracted, probably sitting on a burner for three hours. But it was *real coffee* from the outside world.

She signed back: *A-R-E W-E?*

David's expression flickered. Almost a smile. Almost something more dangerous.

His hands: *T-A-C-T-I-C-A-L-L-Y U-S-E-F-U-L I-F T-H-E-Y T-H-I-N-K W-E C-O-M-P-L-Y*

Her hands: *A-N-D A-C-T-U-A-L-L-Y?*

He held her gaze. Didn't sign for five full seconds.

Then: *D-A-N-G-E-R-O-U-S*

Sarah's pulse jumped. "This coffee is terrible," she said aloud.

"Yeah." David's voice was carefully neutral. "But it's better than nothing."

*B-E-T-T-E-R T-H-A-N N-O-T-H-I-N-G*, she signed.

*M-U-C-H B-E-T-T-E-R*, he agreed.

They spent the next hour planning Bennett's defection and reviewing facility schematics David had memorized. Professional. Tactical. Focused.

But the coffee cups sat between them. Cooling. A small rebellion against Morrison's control.

And when the meeting ended, David signed: *C-O-F-F-E-E W-A-I-T-I-N-G N-E-X-T W-E-E-K*

Sarah understood the layers. The promise. The code that meant *I'm coming back. You're not alone. We're in this together.*

And underneath all of it, unspoken: *I see you. Not as prisoner. Not as genetic material. As you.*

She signed back: *I-L-L B-E H-E-R-E*

*I K-N-O-W*, David signed. Then, barely visible—a movement so small the cameras would miss it—he added: *T-H-A-T-S W-H-Y I-M C-O-M-I-N-G B-A-C-K*

## Week 4 Enhancement (Add to Existing Scene)

**INSERT AFTER existing tactical coordination:**

By week four, the coffee had become ritual. David brought it every meeting. Sometimes good. Sometimes terrible. Always *real*.

Morrison thought it showed compliance—David treating imprisonment like courtship, Sarah accepting comfort.

What Morrison didn't see was the code in coffee temperature.

Hot = Bennett made progress, hub intel transmitted.
Warm = Security change, adjust protocols.
Cold = Danger. Abort communication.

The coffee was scalding.

Sarah took a sip. Smiled for the cameras. Her tongue burned.

David signed: *B-E-N-N-E-T-T G-A-V-E F-B-I C-O-M-P-L-E-T-E P-H-A-S-E T-W-O D-A-T-A*

Her hands shook signing back: *H-O-W M-A-N-Y H-U-B-S S-E-C-U-R-E-D?*

*T-W-E-N-T-Y T-H-R-E-E*, David signed. *B-E-C-A-U-S-E O-F Y-O-U*

"This coffee is perfect," Sarah said aloud.

"Thought you'd like it." David's smile looked genuine. Almost was genuine. "Next week, I'll bring something better."

*N-E-X-T W-E-E-K*, Sarah signed. The most important words. The promise that kept her functional between meetings.

*A-L-W-A-Y-S*, David confirmed.

But his eyes said something different. Something that made Sarah's carefully maintained professional distance fracture just slightly.

They said: *I'm not just coming back for the mission.*

And Sarah found herself thinking: *Good. Because I'm not just waiting for tactical updates.*

## Week 6 - The Almost Moment (NEW SCENE)

**Day 42, Evening**

Morrison had changed protocols. Weekly meetings were now biweekly "to accelerate compatibility bonding." Which meant Sarah saw David twice as often. Which meant twice the intelligence coordination.

Which meant twice the danger of Morrison noticing that "compatibility bonding" had stopped being an act somewhere around week three.

David entered her cell at 7 PM. The guard locked them in—new protocol, "privacy for partnership development"—and departed.

Two hours. Alone. Unsupervised except for cameras.

Morrison really was an idiot.

"Evening," David said. Set down the coffee—hot, which meant good news. Then pulled out his tablet. "I brought the genetic compatibility analysis Morrison requested. Thought we could review it together."

Translation: I have new facility schematics. Let's coordinate escape routes.

Sarah moved closer to see the tablet. Their shoulders touched. Neither moved away.

For the cameras, it looked like intimacy. For the mission, it was practical—both needed to see the screen.

For Sarah, it was the highlight of her entire week, which was pathetic and dangerous and completely undeniable.

They spent ninety minutes reviewing "compatibility data" (escape routes) and discussing "partnership timelines" (deployment countermeasures). Professional. Focused.

Until the tablet battery died.

Sudden darkness on the screen. Both of them leaning close. David's hand on the tablet edge. Sarah's hand next to his.

The shift from tactical to personal happened in the silence.

Both of them leaning close. David's shoulder touching hers. Neither moved away.

"My brother used to say something about infinite timelines," David said quietly.

"Multiverse interpretation," Sarah said. "Quantum mechanics. Every choice creates a branch."

"Yeah. But Carlos said..." David paused. "He said in most timelines, we make different choices. But in the ones where we make the *right* choices—"

"We end up where we need to be," Sarah finished. Understanding. "Free will and destiny. Same thing."

David looked at her. They were six inches apart. Close enough that she could count his eyelashes. Could feel the heat of him. Could lean forward and—

"What timeline is this?" Sarah asked. Barely a whisper.

David's voice was rough. "The right one."

"How do you know?"

"Because you're in it."

She felt her pulse hammering. Heat rising to her face. Every nerve aware of the six inches between them.

The door lock clicked.

They separated instantly. Professional distance. Sarah grabbed the tablet, made a show of reviewing the dark screen. David leaned back, casually checking his watch.

The guard opened the door. "Time's up. Mr. Rivera, Dr. Morrison is waiting."

David stood. Signed quickly: *T-W-O W-E-E-K-S*

Sarah signed back: *S-T-A-Y S-A-F-E*

He paused at the door. Looked back. "In every other timeline," he said aloud, conversationally, "we're drinking coffee somewhere. Not prison coffee. Real coffee. The kind you have to wait in line for because it's actually good."

Sarah felt her throat tighten. "Sounds nice."

"It is." David smiled. Sad. Real. "Maybe someday."

He left.

Sarah sat in her cell, staring at the dead tablet, thinking about infinite timelines.

In most of them, she probably died in Marsh's burning house. Or stayed hidden in Vermont. Or published an exposé that got ignored.

But in this timeline—this specific, impossible timeline—she was imprisoned in a facility planning genocide, working with a man who brought her terrible coffee and talked about quantum mechanics and looked at her like she was the reason the universe had bothered to exist.

Pathetic.

Dangerous.

And the most alive she'd felt in seven weeks.
