And learned to weaponize them."*
>
> — Dr. Otto Aris, Genetic Anthropology Review, 2018

# CHAPTER 3 - THE SACRIFICE

**Modern Timeline - Boston, Massachusetts**
**November 15, 2019 - 2:47 PM**

---

## The Decision

Sarah sat in a Starbucks three blocks from GenVault headquarters, staring at her laptop.

The burner phone Maya had given her sat next to her coffee. No calls yet. No messages. Just the weight of knowing that in 70 hours, THRESHOLD would deploy.

Unless she stopped it.

She'd spent the morning compiling everything she knew:

**EVIDENCE:**
- 89% of GenVault's restricted database (incomplete)
- Otto Aris's 1912 defensive Protocol translation (historical, not actionable)
- Photocopied medieval manuscripts (correlation, not causation)
- Maya's CDC data (suspicious patterns, not proof)

**GAPS:**
- Actual THRESHOLD deployment mechanism
- Hub locations and activation schedules
- Pathogen vector and transmission method
- Network infrastructure and command structure
- Who else is involved beyond Morrison and GenVault

Publishing what she had now would get her labeled a conspiracy theorist. GenVault would deny everything. Morrison would accelerate the timeline.

262 million people would die.

And she'd spend the rest of her life knowing she could have stopped it if she'd been braver.

Sarah pulled up Morrison's last message—the one he'd sent after she fled Marsh's burning house:

*"72 hours, Dr. Chen. When you're ready to see the complete picture, call this number. We're not your enemy. We're trying to save humanity."*

A phone number. An invitation.

A trap.

But also: an opportunity.

---

## The Call

Sarah used the Starbucks wifi to research GenVault's personnel structure. Found what she needed in three minutes:

**Dr. Preston Wells** - VP of Strategic Recruitment
Specialized in converting "high-value targets" who initially resisted network philosophy.

Success rate: 73%.

Sarah had been recruited by Wells two years ago. He'd sold her on GenVault's mission: "Using cutting-edge genetics to optimize human health outcomes."

She'd believed him.

Now she understood "optimize" meant "engineer extinction events."

But Wells didn't know that she knew. As far as GenVault was concerned, Sarah had fled in panic, was hiding, trying to understand fragments of data she'd stolen.

Desperate. Overwhelmed. Seeking answers.

Exactly the psychological state that made people vulnerable to recruitment.

Sarah pulled out the burner phone. Dialed Wells's direct line—she still had it memorized from two years of working there.

Three rings.

"Dr. Chen." Wells's voice was warm. Relieved. Like he'd been waiting for this call. "I'm so glad you reached out. Are you somewhere safe?"

"I don't know what 'safe' means anymore."

"I understand. The last few days must have been terrifying." Paper shuffling in the background. "Where are you right now?"

"Boston. Near the office." Sarah let her voice shake—not hard, given what she was about to do. "Dr. Wells, I need to understand what's happening. The data I took—the medieval patterns, the population markers—I can't make sense of it alone."

"Of course you can't. You have fragments without context." Wells's tone shifted—sympathetic, almost fatherly. "Sarah, what you've found is real. The Genesis Protocol is real. But it's not what you think it is."

"Then what is it?"

"Something that's been protecting humanity for three thousand years. Guiding us through catastrophic bottlenecks. Ensuring our survival as a species." A pause. "Dr. Morrison would very much like to speak with you. Show you the complete picture. Help you understand why this work matters."

Sarah closed her eyes. This was it—the moment she either walked into the trap or ran.

"I want to see the original manuscripts," she said carefully. "The ones the medieval cipher came from. The Bronze Age documents. I need to verify this independently."

"Absolutely. We have the Aethelred collection here—complete originals, not photocopies. Plus genetic data going back forty generations. Everything you need to verify the correlations." Wells sounded genuinely excited. "When can you come in?"

"Now. This afternoon." Sarah's hands were shaking. "But I'm scared, Dr. Wells. Dr. Marsh died twelve hours after I visited her. Dr. Aris is missing. Everyone who finds this pattern ends up dead."

"Sarah." Wells's voice dropped, became intimate. "Dr. Morrison knows you've been researching the network. He's the one who authorized letting you access restricted databases six months ago. He *wanted* you to find the pattern. Because you're exactly the kind of person the Protocol has been selecting for over a hundred generations."

"What do you mean?"

"Your family carries defensive markers. Pattern recognition. Systems thinking. Crisis response traits that let you see collapse coming." Wells paused. "You think Morrison is your enemy. He's not. He's been cultivating you. Waiting for you to discover the truth on your own. Because people who find it themselves—who verify it independently—make better network members than people who just obey orders."

Sarah felt cold. "You're saying this was all planned."

"The database access was planned. Your recruitment was planned. Aris giving you the manuscripts—we knew he'd kept copies, we let him keep them. Because eventually, someone brilliant enough would find the pattern." Wells's tone carried absolute conviction. "That person was you, Sarah. You were always supposed to find this."

"And Marsh?"

Silence on the line. Then, carefully: "Dr. Marsh died of smoke inhalation during an accidental electrical fire. Tragic. But unrelated to you or your research."

Lie. Sarah heard it in the slight pause, the too-careful phrasing.

They'd killed Marsh and were pretending it was coincidence.

"Come in," Wells continued. "Meet with Morrison. See the complete archive. Verify everything independently. Then make an informed decision about whether you want to help us or fight us."

"And if I choose to fight?"

"Then you walk away. We don't force cooperation, Sarah. The network has lasted three thousand years because we recruit people who *choose* to join after understanding what's at stake." Wells sounded genuinely honest. "All we ask is that you see the complete picture before deciding."

Sarah took a breath. "Okay. I'll come in. This afternoon. But I want full access—archives, genetic databases, deployment models. Everything."

"Of course. I'll have Morrison clear you for Level Five access. Can you be here by 4 PM?"

Two hours. Enough time to meet Maya, get wired up, brief CDC.

"Yes."

"Excellent. Use the executive entrance on Causeway Street. Security will escort you to Morrison's office." Wells paused. "Sarah? Thank you for reaching out. You won't regret this."

The line went dead.

Sarah sat in the Starbucks, coffee gone cold, staring at her phone.

She'd just volunteered to walk into GenVault headquarters.

Into Morrison's office.

Into the heart of a network that had spent seventy years engineering genocide.

Time to call Maya.

---

## The Preparation

They met in a parking garage three blocks from GenVault. Maya pulled up in a CDC vehicle, backed into a corner spot away from cameras.

"You're insane," Maya said by way of greeting.

"Probably." Sarah slid into the passenger seat. "Did you bring it?"

Maya pulled out a small medical kit. Inside: a GPS tracker the size of a grain of rice, a syringe, and antiseptic wipes.

"Subcutaneous implant. Goes under the skin on your hip—they won't find it during a normal search." Maya loaded the tracker into the syringe. "Encrypted transmission to CDC servers. I'll know where you are at all times."

"Can they detect the signal?"

"Not unless they're actively scanning for GPS frequencies in a Faraday cage. And even then, it'll look like environmental noise." Maya gestured for Sarah to lift her shirt. "This is going to hurt."

Sarah exposed her left hip. "Do it."

Maya swabbed the area with antiseptic, positioned the syringe, and injected the tracker in one smooth motion.

Sarah hissed through her teeth. Pain flared—sharp, then fading to dull ache.

"Done." Maya pressed gauze over the injection site. "Don't touch it for six hours. The tracker will embed in subcutaneous tissue and start transmitting in twenty minutes."

Sarah pulled her shirt down. "What's the range?"

"Unlimited. It piggybacks on cellular towers. As long as you're not in a lead-lined room, I'll know exactly where you are."

"And if I disappear?"

"I activate CDC bioterrorism protocols. Lock down GenVault. Raid the facility. Arrest Morrison and Wells on suspicion of planning a pandemic event." Maya met her eyes. "But that's last resort. If they know CDC is onto them, they'll either destroy evidence or accelerate THRESHOLD deployment."

"Which is why I need to get the intel first."

"Hub locations. Deployment schedules. Pathogen specifications." Maya pulled up a checklist on her laptop. "Anything that lets us stop them clean—shut down hubs simultaneously, contain the pathogen before release."

"Seventy hours."

"Assuming Morrison didn't lie about the timeline." Maya studied her. "You ready for this?"

Sarah thought about Marsh. About burning alive in a staged electrical fire. About forty years of fighting alone, dying alone, being dismissed as an accident.

"I'm ready."

Maya handed her a second burner phone. "Call me every twelve hours. If I don't hear from you for twenty-four, I assume you're compromised and activate response protocols."

"Understood."

"And Sarah?" Maya's expression was fierce. "If they hurt you—if Morrison tries anything—I will burn that building to the ground. Network or no network."

Sarah felt something warm in her chest. Alliance. Partnership. Someone who'd fight back if she failed.

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me yet. Just come back alive with intelligence we can use."

Sarah got out of the car. Checked the time: 3:47 PM.

Thirteen minutes until her meeting with Morrison.

Seventy hours until THRESHOLD deployed.

And she was about to walk into the trap voluntarily.

Sarah crossed the street, heading toward GenVault's executive entrance.

Behind her, Maya watched from the parking garage, tracking the GPS signal that blinked steadily on her laptop screen.

One green dot. Moving toward the building.

Into danger.

Into the network that had engineered humanity for three thousand years.

Maya whispered a prayer she didn't really believe in and started recording.

Whatever happened next, CDC would have evidence.

---

## The Executive Entrance

Security at the Causeway Street entrance was expecting her.

"Dr. Chen?" The guard—young, professional, earpiece visible—gestured toward a private elevator. "Dr. Morrison is waiting. Twelfth floor."

Sarah stepped into the elevator. Brushed steel walls. Classical music playing softly. The doors closed with a hydraulic hiss.

The tracker under her skin felt warm. Active. Transmitting.

Maya was watching.

The elevator rose smoothly. Sarah watched floor numbers tick past—3, 4, 5—and prepared herself for what came next.

She was Dr. Sarah Chen. MIT PhD. Pattern recognition specialist. Defensive Protocol carrier.

And for the next seventy hours, she was going to convince Dr. James Morrison that he could recruit her.

While she gathered intelligence that would destroy him.

The elevator chimed. Twelfth floor.

Doors opened.

Morrison stood in the hallway. Expensive suit, patient smile, hands clasped behind his back.

"Dr. Chen," he said warmly. "Welcome home."

Sarah stepped out of the elevator.

The trap closed.
