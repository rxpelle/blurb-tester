# CHAPTER 3: THE WATER CODEX

**Timeline:** March 1121 - September 1125 CE
**Location:** Jerusalem, Templar Quarter, Temple Mount excavations
**Guilhem's Age:** 26-30 years old

---

The stone spoke in ratios.

Guilhem traced his fingers along the diagram wall in the chamber beneath the Temple Mount, now lit by proper oil lamps instead of torches. Three months since Pattern Eye custody. Six months since the synthesis agreement with Zoe and the defensive network. And still the engineering knowledge revealed new layers every time he touched the ancient limestone.

"This section," he said to Eschiva bat Solomon, who sat cross-legged on the chamber floor with parchment spread across her lap. "The pressure calculations. They're not using atmospheric pressure at sea level as the baseline."

Eschiva looked up. She was forty-three, a Jewish engineer from a family that had maintained water systems in Jerusalem for six generations. Defensive network, like her father and grandfather before her. Her gray-streaked black hair was covered by a simple headscarf, her hands stained with ink from weeks of documentation work.

"What baseline?" she asked.

Guilhem pressed both palms against the stone. Blood-knowledge surged—not his ancestors' memories, but the imprints left by whoever had carved these diagrams nine hundred years ago. Solomon's engineers. Phoenician consultants. Egyptian hydraulic specialists brought north during the temple's construction.

"They're using Jerusalem's elevation," he said slowly, translating sensation into words. "Seven hundred fifty meters above sea level. The pressure differential calculations only work at this specific altitude."

Eschiva's eyes widened. She pulled a fresh sheet of parchment, began sketching rapidly. "That's why the Templar test cistern worked perfectly here but the one they tried building in Acre kept failing. Acre is at sea level. The pressure ratios are different."

"We need altitude correction formulas," Guilhem said. He moved to a different wall section, one he'd been avoiding because the diagrams were more complex. "I think... yes. Here."

He touched a series of geometric patterns carved in blue lapis lazuli inlaid against white limestone. The contrast wasn't just aesthetic—it was mathematical notation. Blue for water-related calculations. White for structural elements. Red sandstone for heat transfer. Black basalt for filtration.

A color-coded engineering language.

"Eschiva," he said quietly. "We're not looking at a single document. This is a library. An entire architectural tradition encoded in stone that would survive manuscript burning, temple destruction, civilizational collapse."

"Genesis Protocol," Eschiva murmured. She'd been briefed on network history after agreeing to help with translation work. "Your great-great-grandmother discovered this in medical knowledge. You're finding it in engineering."

"The same principle," Guilhem agreed. "Preserve what matters most for human survival. Medical knowledge prevents plague. Engineering knowledge prevents drought, siege, infrastructure collapse."

He traced the blue lapis patterns, letting blood-memory translate the mathematics:

*Altitude correction factor: For every 100 cubits above sea level, reduce chamber depth by 1 cubit to maintain equivalent pressure differential.*

*Temperature variance: Summer expansion requires 5% volume buffer in sealed chambers.*

*Seismic adjustment: In earthquake-prone regions, use interlocking stone rather than mortar for cistern walls—flexibility prevents catastrophic cracking.*

Knowledge that had kept Jerusalem's population supplied with water for nine hundred years, through sieges and droughts and earthquakes.

"I need to document this properly," Guilhem said. He pulled his own parchment closer—not loose sheets like Eschiva used, but a bound codex he'd commissioned from a monastery scriptorium. Forty pages so far, each one filled with engineering diagrams, mathematical formulas, practical instructions.

The Water Codex, Brother Arnaud had started calling it. A fitting name.

Guilhem began sketching, translating the stone diagrams into portable manuscript form. But as he drew, he incorporated something new—dot notation, the encoding method he'd learned from studying Alienor's final letter and the cascade teaching materials from Ciarán's time two centuries earlier.

Visible layer: Clear engineering diagrams anyone could use.

Hidden layer: Encoded in the spacing between diagram elements, advanced mathematical principles that only trained carriers could extract.

Distribution and protection simultaneously.

"You're doing it again," Eschiva observed. "That spacing pattern between your pressure valve diagrams. What are you encoding?"

Guilhem smiled. Eschiva wasn't a carrier—her family's defensive network involvement came from philosophical alignment, not blood-knowledge—but she had a mathematician's eye for hidden patterns.

"The theoretical maximum," he said. "These basic diagrams show how to build cisterns that work. The encoded layer explains *why* they work—underlying fluid dynamics principles that could be applied to other engineering challenges."

"Cascade teaching in written form," Eschiva said. "Basic knowledge distributed freely. Advanced knowledge requires mentorship to decode. Your great-great-grandmother would approve."

---

**April 1121 CE**

The Templar test cistern in Jerusalem's northern quarter had been operational for seven months. Success by any measure—it stored 40,000 gallons, filled from winter rains, supplied a neighborhood of 200 families through the dry summer. Guilhem visited weekly to check for structural issues.

Today he found Brother Arnaud waiting by the cistern entrance.

"The defensive network is pleased," Arnaud said without preamble. He was sixty now, his beard fully white, but still sharp-minded. Still teaching, still coordinating. "Zoe reports that Byzantine engineers in Constantinople have begun implementing your altitude correction formulas. Three new cisterns built this spring in the hills outside the city."

"That was fast," Guilhem said. "I only documented those calculations two months ago."

"Cascade teaching operates at transmission speed now that we have your codex." Arnaud gestured to the cistern. "You've created something remarkable, Brother Guilhem. Portable knowledge. Any stonemason with basic mathematics can use the visible layer. Trained engineers can extract the advanced layer. And the underlying principles can be taught orally to the next generation."

"How many copies exist?" Guilhem asked.

"The defensive network has produced seventeen so far. Constantinople, Alexandria, Damascus, Córdoba, and thirteen distributed across Europe. Each copy made by hand, carefully verified against your original. We're calling them Water Codex manuscripts."

Seventeen copies. In two months.

Guilhem felt the weight of what he'd created. Knowledge diffusion on a scale Alienor would have recognized—her medical cascade teaching had spread across three continents in her lifetime. But this was faster. Manuscripts could travel distances that oral teaching couldn't match.

"And the Templar Order?" he asked carefully.

Arnaud's expression darkened. "Hugh de Payens is pleased with the practical results. The cistern works. Templar properties across Outremer are implementing your designs. But..."

"But?"

"There's a debate within the Order. Grand Master Hugh sees this as sacred work—preserving ancient knowledge, serving God by serving humanity. But some of the younger knights, the ones being recruited from noble families in France and Italy, they see commercial opportunity."

Guilhem had feared this. "Infrastructure monopoly."

"Precisely. If Templars control the most advanced water engineering in Christendom, they become indispensable to every city, every castle, every monastery. Not just military power. Economic power."

"That violates the synthesis agreement," Guilhem said flatly. "Controlled distribution, not monopolization. Both networks agreed."

"Hugh holds to the agreement," Arnaud assured him. "But he's sixty-three, Brother Guilhem. How much longer can he maintain that position against younger knights who see wealth and influence within reach?"

A familiar pattern. Offensive network thinking: control equals power equals security.

And a familiar counter-argument forming in Guilhem's mind: distribution equals resilience equals actual security.

"Then we accelerate distribution," Guilhem said. "If the knowledge is so widely spread that monopolization becomes impossible, the debate becomes moot."

Arnaud smiled. "Alienor's grandson. You have her tactical mind."

"Great-great-grandson," Guilhem corrected automatically. Then paused. "Wait. You knew her personally?"

"I was twelve when she died," Arnaud said. "My father was one of her students—fourth-generation cascade teaching of medical knowledge. When she learned I had aptitude for engineering and architecture, she added me to her network of what she called 'infrastructure carriers.' Those of us who preserve practical survival knowledge rather than medical or theological."

"How many infrastructure carriers exist?"

"Active? Perhaps two hundred across the Mediterranean and Europe. Your work is reaching all of them, Brother Guilhem. The Water Codex will be taught to their children, and their children's children. Three more generations, and cistern engineering at this level of sophistication will be so commonplace that no one will remember it was once secret knowledge hidden under the Temple Mount."

That was the goal. That was synthesis. Neither network winning exclusively, but knowledge persisting regardless of political power shifts.

"I need to expand the codex," Guilhem said. "Right now it's mostly cisterns and basic aqueducts. But the chamber diagrams include pressure valve mechanics, filtration systems, even thermal exchange principles I don't fully understand yet. There's enough engineering knowledge here to fill ten codices."

"Then write ten codices," Arnaud said simply. "The defensive network will copy and distribute. You focus on translation."

---

**August 1122 CE**

Eschiva arrived at the excavation chamber carrying a small wooden box, her expression troubled.

"Problem?" Guilhem asked. He was working on a particularly complex diagram—something involving water pressure being used to operate mechanical gates. The blood-knowledge translation kept slipping away before he could fully grasp it.

"Visitor," Eschiva said. "At your workshop. Bernard of Clairvaux."

Guilhem looked up sharply. Bernard of Clairvaux—the Cistercian reformer who had spent the last decade rebuilding monastic discipline across France. Brilliant theologian. Powerful preacher. And, according to Brother Arnaud's network briefings, a key offensive network coordinator in Western Europe.

"What does he want?" Guilhem asked.

"To see your work. He's been corresponding with Hugh de Payens about the Templar Order's mission and legal status. Apparently your engineering discoveries came up in their letters."

Of course they had. Hugh would want theological legitimacy for the Templar excavations. And Bernard could provide that—at a price.

Guilhem carefully closed his current codex—Water Codex Volume II, focused on aqueduct design—and secured it in the leather satchel he used for transporting manuscripts. Pattern Eye went in a separate inner pocket. Never carry all critical items together.

"Let's not keep him waiting," Guilhem said.

---

Bernard of Clairvaux was forty-two years old, thin to the point of asceticism, with intense eyes that seemed to evaluate everything simultaneously—spiritual worth, political utility, strategic advantage. He stood in Guilhem's workshop examining the test models: small-scale cisterns carved from limestone blocks, miniature aqueduct sections demonstrating pressure differentials.

"Brother Guilhem," Bernard said, his French-accented Latin precise. "Grand Master Hugh speaks highly of your talents. Both engineering and theological."

"I'm a stonemason who reads ancient diagrams," Guilhem replied carefully. "The theology is secondary."

"Nothing is secondary to theology," Bernard corrected, but his tone was pleasant. "Still, I confess fascination with your work. May I see the codex Grand Master Hugh mentioned?"

Guilhem hesitated. Then pulled Water Codex Volume I from his satchel—the oldest copy, most complete, with both visible and encoded layers.

Bernard accepted it reverently, began turning pages. His eyes moved quickly, reading Latin annotations, studying diagrams. Guilhem watched for signs of recognition—could Bernard see the dot notation encoding? Most readers couldn't without specific training.

"Remarkable," Bernard murmured. "You've translated ancient Hebrew engineering into practical Christian application. This is holy work, Brother Guilhem. Recovering Solomon's wisdom from beneath his temple."

"It's universal wisdom," Guilhem said. "The engineers who designed these systems included Phoenicians, Egyptians, possibly Greeks. The knowledge transcends religious boundaries."

A slight tightening around Bernard's eyes. Not anger—calculation.

"Universal wisdom," Bernard repeated. "How many copies of this codex exist?"

"I haven't counted," Guilhem lied smoothly. Twenty-three copies as of last week, according to Brother Arnaud's network reports. "The defensive network handles distribution."

"Defensive network." Bernard closed the codex, handed it back. "Brother Hugh mentioned you have connections to what he called 'non-Templar scholars.' I assume this is what he meant?"

"Scholars committed to knowledge preservation," Guilhem said. "Christians, Jews, Muslims. Anyone willing to learn and teach."

"Admirable," Bernard said. But his tone suggested otherwise. "Tell me, Brother Guilhem—do you believe this knowledge should be freely available to Muslims? Even those currently at war with Christian kingdoms?"

The trap was obvious. Answer yes, be labeled a traitor. Answer no, violate synthesis principles.

"I believe knowledge of how to store clean water should be available to anyone who needs it," Guilhem said carefully. "Whether they're Christian, Muslim, or pagan. Drought and disease don't respect religious boundaries."

"A humanitarian position," Bernard acknowledged. "But perhaps naïve. Consider: you teach a Muslim engineer how to build superior cisterns. That engineer improves water supply to Damascus or Cairo. Those cities become more resistant to siege. Christian military campaigns become more difficult. More Christians die in prolonged conflicts."

Guilhem met Bernard's gaze steadily. "Consider the alternative: I restrict this knowledge to Christians only. The next drought kills thousands of Muslims. Their survivors blame Christians for hoarding survival knowledge. Resentment intensifies. Religious war becomes even more bitter and prolonged. More people die—Christian and Muslim both."

"You assume shared knowledge creates peace," Bernard said. "I've found the opposite is often true. Shared knowledge creates competition. Better to maintain advantage through exclusive access."

Offensive network philosophy, stated plainly.

"That's not the lesson from my great-great-grandmother's work," Guilhem replied. "She distributed medical knowledge across religious boundaries during the worst of the Plague of Justinian's aftershocks. According to network records, survival rates improved for everyone. Including Christians."

"Alienor de Périgord," Bernard said, and Guilhem went very still. Bernard knew. Of course he knew. "Yes, I'm familiar with her cascade teaching methods. Impressive in their time. But medical knowledge and engineering knowledge are different categories, Brother Guilhem. Medicine heals individuals. Infrastructure shapes civilizations. The strategic implications are incomparable."

"The moral implications are identical," Guilhem countered. "Preserve what helps humans survive. Distribute what prevents civilizational collapse."

Bernard was silent for a long moment, studying Guilhem with those calculating eyes.

"Grand Master Hugh told me you were principled," Bernard finally said. "He didn't mention you were dangerously idealistic. Very well, Brother Guilhem. Continue your translation work. Document your codex. I won't interfere."

The relief Guilhem felt lasted exactly two seconds.

"However," Bernard continued, "I will be writing a treatise on the Templar Order's sacred mission. 'De Laude Novae Militiae'—In Praise of the New Knighthood. I intend to argue for papal recognition and legal protection for your Order. The engineering discoveries will feature prominently as evidence of divine favor."

"That's... appreciated," Guilhem said carefully.

"In return," Bernard said, "I expect the Templar Order to maintain certain standards. Christian moral leadership. Strategic cooperation with Church authorities. And prudent control over strategically significant knowledge."

There it was. The price for Bernard's support: offensive network alignment.

"I translate what the stone remembers," Guilhem said. "What happens to that knowledge afterward is above my authority to determine."

"For now," Bernard agreed. "But authority structures can change. Grand Master Hugh won't live forever. When new leadership emerges, they would benefit from theological guidance regarding how to steward such valuable discoveries."

Threat and offer combined. Bernard would help legitimize the Templar Order—but expected future cooperation in controlling knowledge distribution.

After Bernard departed, Eschiva emerged from where she'd been listening in the workshop's back room.

"That man is dangerous," she said flatly.

"He's brilliant," Guilhem corrected. "Which is why he's dangerous. He sees exactly what this engineering knowledge represents—not just cisterns, but civilizational advantage. And he's already planning how to leverage it for Church power."

"What will you do?"

Guilhem looked at the Water Codex in his hands. Twenty-three copies already distributed. More being made every week. The knowledge was already too dispersed for Bernard or anyone else to fully control.

But Bernard wasn't trying to stop distribution now. He was positioning for influence over future distribution. Playing a longer game.

"I accelerate," Guilhem said. "Volume II focuses on aqueducts. Volume III will cover filtration systems. Volume IV, pressure valves. Volume V, thermal exchange. I'll translate everything in that chamber and distribute it so widely that by the time Bernard's ecclesiastical influence peaks, the knowledge will be embedded in practical engineering across three continents."

"Cascade teaching at manuscript speed," Eschiva said.

"Exactly. Alienor proved it works for medical knowledge. I'll prove it works for engineering. Within one generation, this knowledge will be so commonplace that monopolization becomes impossible."

"And if the offensive network retaliates?"

Guilhem touched the inner pocket where Pattern Eye rested. Forty-two generations of carriers who had faced exactly this question: distribute knowledge and risk persecution, or hoard knowledge and guarantee eventual loss.

The blood remembered.

The answer was always the same.

"Then we survive the retaliation," Guilhem said quietly. "The way we always have. By being too distributed to eliminate, too resilient to suppress, too necessary to destroy."

---

**January 1124 CE**

The test cistern in Acre finally worked.

Guilhem stood beside it, watching clear water flow from the collection chamber through three filtration stages into the final storage basin. Altitude corrections applied. Pressure differentials adjusted for sea level. Structural modifications for Acre's sandy soil rather than Jerusalem's bedrock.

Twelve failed prototypes over two years. But this one worked.

The Templar knight supervising the project—Brother Raymond of Toulouse—was ecstatic. "Forty thousand gallons storage capacity, just like the Jerusalem model. We can supply the entire Templar compound plus lease excess capacity to the city."

"Lease?" Guilhem asked.

"Of course. Acre's current water supply is inadequate. Municipal authorities will pay for guaranteed access to superior cistern storage. Steady income for the Order."

Commercial thinking. Not wrong, necessarily—Templars needed funding to maintain their military operations. But Raymond's focus was entirely on revenue, not knowledge distribution.

"I assume you'll be documenting the construction techniques?" Guilhem asked carefully. "So future engineers can replicate this without needing direct Templar involvement?"

Raymond looked at him strangely. "Why would we do that? If we teach everyone our methods, we lose competitive advantage. Better to maintain expertise internally, hire our services out. That's sustainable income."

Monopolization logic. Exactly what Bernard of Clairvaux had recommended.

"The synthesis agreement—" Guilhem began.

"Was negotiated by Grand Master Hugh," Raymond interrupted. "Who is seventy years old and increasingly focused on theological matters rather than practical administration. Younger knights like myself handle actual operational decisions now."

Generational shift. Hugh de Payens still held the title of Grand Master, but real power was dispersing to regional commanders. And those commanders were thinking like businessmen, not knowledge preservers.

"I need the construction documentation," Guilhem said firmly. "For the codex. That was part of the original excavation agreement—all engineering knowledge gets translated and distributed."

"Take it up with the Grand Master," Raymond said dismissively. "I'm not handing over proprietary techniques to anyone who asks."

Guilhem left Acre frustrated. The defensive network's distributed approach was working—Water Codex manuscripts were being implemented successfully across the Mediterranean. But the offensive network was adapting, learning to leverage Templar institutional structure to restrict the knowledge Guilhem was trying to preserve.

He needed to document faster. Distribute wider. Make monopolization impossible before the Templar Order's internal politics shifted completely toward Bernard's philosophy.

---

**June 1125 CE**

Water Codex Volume IV was complete.

Guilhem sat in the excavation chamber beneath the Temple Mount, surrounded by five completed manuscript volumes:

- **Volume I**: Basic cistern design and construction
- **Volume II**: Aqueduct engineering and pressure differentials
- **Volume III**: Multi-stage filtration systems
- **Volume IV**: Pressure valves and flow control mechanisms
- **Volume V** (in progress): Thermal exchange and seasonal variation management

Each volume contained two layers: visible engineering diagrams anyone could use, and encoded mathematical principles accessible only to trained carriers. Cascade teaching in written form.

Brother Arnaud had reported forty-seven complete copies now distributed across defensive network nodes. Constantinople, Alexandria, Damascus, Córdoba, Toledo, Paris, Cologne, and dozens of smaller cities. Jewish engineers, Muslim scholars, Christian monks—anyone willing to learn and teach further.

But the offensive network wasn't sitting idle.

Bernard of Clairvaux's treatise "In Praise of the New Knighthood" had circulated widely across Western Europe, generating enormous support for the Templar Order. Donations poured in. Noble families sent younger sons to join. The Order was expanding rapidly—and with expansion came pressure to demonstrate value.

Economic value.

Infrastructure monopoly looked increasingly attractive to Templar leadership.

"Brother Guilhem."

He looked up. Hugh de Payens stood at the chamber entrance, moving carefully. The Grand Master was seventy-six now, visibly aging. But his mind remained sharp.

"Grand Master," Guilhem greeted him respectfully.

"I wanted to see the chamber one more time," Hugh said, descending the carved stone steps slowly. "Before I'm too old to manage the climb."

Guilhem helped him to a seat on one of the stone benches they'd installed for study work. Hugh looked around the chamber with its color-coded diagrams, oil lamps casting warm light on ancient engineering knowledge.

"You've done well," Hugh said quietly. "Better than I expected when I recruited a twenty-four-year-old stonemason to help dig tunnels. You've translated knowledge that could change the world."

"It's already changing the world," Guilhem replied. "Forty-seven manuscripts distributed. Dozens of successful cistern installations. More planned every month."

"Yes," Hugh said. "That's what I wanted to discuss. The younger knights—Brother Raymond, Brother Godfrey, others—they're arguing that we should restrict distribution. Maintain Templar competitive advantage."

"That violates our agreement," Guilhem said flatly. "The synthesis we negotiated with the defensive network."

"I know. I've held firm on that point. But Brother Guilhem, I won't be Grand Master forever. My health is failing. Within two years, perhaps less, the Order will elect new leadership. And I cannot guarantee they will honor the synthesis agreement."

The generational transfer problem. Leadership changes, institutional memory fades, principles erode under competitive pressure.

"What do you recommend?" Guilhem asked.

"Finish your translation work as quickly as possible," Hugh said. "Document everything that chamber contains. Distribute it so widely that by the time I'm gone, restriction becomes impossible. Make the knowledge so commonplace that monopolization would be like trying to monopolize knowledge of how to make bread."

"That's my plan," Guilhem confirmed. "Volume V should be complete by autumn. Volume VI will cover advanced topics—structural engineering, earthquake resistance, integrated urban water systems. I estimate two more years to fully translate all the chamber diagrams."

"Two years," Hugh repeated. "I hope I live that long. But even if I don't, Brother Guilhem—continue this work. It's more important than Templar politics. This is what the excavation was truly for. Not finding gold or religious relics. Finding practical knowledge that helps humans survive."

"That's Genesis Protocol philosophy," Guilhem said. "Preserve what matters most."

"Your great-great-grandmother understood that," Hugh said. "I wish I'd met her. From what you've told me, she and I would have agreed on many things." He paused. "The offensive network offered me authority and influence if I'd monopolize your discoveries. I refused. Not because I'm opposed to authority or influence—but because some things are more valuable when distributed than when hoarded."

"Why?" Guilhem asked. "Why did you refuse? Most men in your position would have accepted."

Hugh smiled. "Because I'm seventy-six years old, Brother Guilhem. I've lived through wars, plagues, famines, political upheavals. I've seen kingdoms rise and fall. And the one constant I've observed is this: civilizations that hoard knowledge eventually collapse. Civilizations that distribute knowledge adapt and survive."

Defensive network philosophy from a man who had spent decades in offensive network circles.

"You're a synthesis thinker," Guilhem said.

"I'm a pragmatist," Hugh corrected. "Offensive network methods work for short-term advantage. Defensive network methods work for long-term survival. Wise leaders use both as circumstances require. That's what I've tried to teach the Templar Order. But wisdom doesn't always transfer across generations."

He stood slowly, Guilhem helping him rise.

"Complete your work," Hugh said. "Distribute it as widely as possible. And when I'm gone—when younger knights try to restrict what you've discovered—remember that you serve something larger than the Templar Order. You serve the pattern. Forty-two generations of knowledge preservation. That's your true authority."

After Hugh departed, Guilhem returned to his work. Volume V awaited. Then Volume VI. Then whatever else the stone remembered.

The blood remembered too.

And Guilhem de Carcassonne, thirty years old, carrier of Generation Fifty-Eight, translated on.

---

**September 1125 CE**

Eschiva arrived at the workshop looking pale.

"What happened?" Guilhem asked immediately.

"Bernard of Clairvaux returned to Jerusalem," she said. "He's meeting with Templar leadership. Brother Arnaud's network sources say he's pressuring them to formalize restrictions on water engineering distribution."

"Hugh won't agree to that," Guilhem said.

"Hugh is dying," Eschiva replied bluntly. "He has months at most. Bernard is positioning for influence over whoever becomes the next Grand Master. And he's arguing that your work represents strategic Christian advantage that should be protected from Muslim competition."

Religious framing. The same argument Bernard had used during his first visit three years ago, now deployed with institutional pressure behind it.

"How many manuscripts have we distributed?" Guilhem asked.

Eschiva consulted her notes. "Defensive network reports fifty-three complete copies of Volumes I-IV across the Mediterranean. Volume V is being copied now—twenty preliminary manuscripts in circulation. Total distribution: seventy-three codex copies containing the full visible engineering knowledge plus encoded advanced principles."

Seventy-three copies. Across Christian, Muslim, and Jewish scholarly networks. In multiple languages—Latin, Greek, Arabic, Hebrew.

Could Bernard suppress that? Even with Templar institutional support?

"We need insurance," Guilhem said. He pulled out parchment, began sketching a new diagram. Not engineering this time—geographic.

"What are you doing?" Eschiva asked.

"Mapping the defensive network nodes that hold Water Codex manuscripts. Constantinople, Alexandria, Damascus, Toledo, Córdoba... I want documented proof that this knowledge is already too distributed to control. When Bernard makes his move, I'll present evidence that restriction is futile."

"And if he argues the Templars should recover all copies?"

"Then I'll argue that attempting recovery would violate agreements with Byzantine, Fatimid, and Abbasid scholars who received manuscripts in good faith. Bernard wants Christian advantage, but he won't risk diplomatic incidents with three major powers over engineering knowledge."

Eschiva nodded slowly. "Strategic distribution. Make suppression more costly than acceptance."

"Alienor's method," Guilhem confirmed. "According to network histories, she faced exactly this challenge with medical knowledge during plague outbreaks. Authorities wanted to restrict treatment to nobility. She distributed so widely that restriction became politically impossible."

"History repeating," Eschiva murmured. "Different context, same pattern."

The blood remembered.

The pattern continued.

And when Bernard of Clairvaux arrived at Guilhem's workshop four days later, Guilhem was ready.

---

Bernard examined the geographic distribution map without expression.

"Seventy-three manuscripts," he said finally. "Across how many political jurisdictions?"

"Seventeen," Guilhem replied. "Byzantine Empire, Fatimid Caliphate, Abbasid Caliphate, Kingdom of Jerusalem, County of Tripoli, Principality of Antioch, and eleven European kingdoms. Each manuscript given with formal agreements regarding peaceful use and educational distribution."

"Formal agreements," Bernard repeated. "With Muslim caliphates."

"With scholarly institutions that transcend political boundaries," Guilhem corrected. "The same way medical knowledge transcends boundaries during plague outbreaks."

"You're comparing engineering knowledge to medicine?"

"I'm comparing survival knowledge to survival knowledge. Clean water prevents disease as surely as medical treatment cures it."

Bernard set down the map. "Brother Guilhem, I'll be direct. The Templar Order is on the verge of receiving papal recognition and formal legal protections. This would make the Order answerable only to the Pope—not to local bishops, not to kings, not to anyone else. Unprecedented autonomy for a military organization."

"I'm aware," Guilhem said carefully.

"That recognition depends partly on demonstrating that the Templars serve Christian interests exclusively. Not universal human interests. Not interfaith cooperation. Christian strategic advantage."

"And you want me to restrict distribution of engineering knowledge to Christian recipients only."

"I want you to acknowledge reality," Bernard said. "You serve the Templar Order. The Order is Christian. Your work should benefit Christians first, others second—if at all."

"I serve the pattern," Guilhem replied. "Forty-two generations of knowledge preservation that existed long before the Templar Order and will exist long after. The Templars are a temporary institution. The pattern is permanent."

Bernard's expression hardened. "That's dangerously close to heresy, Brother Guilhem. Claiming allegiance to something above Church authority."

"I claim allegiance to preserving what helps humans survive civilizational collapse. The Church claims the same goal. We should be allies, not adversaries."

"We would be allies if you accepted appropriate boundaries on knowledge distribution. As it stands, you're undermining Christian strategic position by teaching our enemies how to build superior infrastructure."

"There are no enemies in drought," Guilhem said quietly. "Only humans who need water to survive."

Silence.

Then Bernard stood. "I see I won't convince you. Very well. Continue your work. But understand: when the next Grand Master is elected, when Templar policy shifts toward strategic prudence, when your distribution activities become untenable—don't say you weren't warned."

After Bernard left, Guilhem sat motionless for a long time.

The offensive network was preparing to move against him. Not immediately—Hugh de Payens still lived, still held authority. But soon. Within months, perhaps.

He had two choices: stop distribution now and preserve his position within the Templar Order, or accelerate distribution and accept eventual expulsion.

Guilhem touched the inner pocket where Pattern Eye rested.

Forty-two generations of carriers who had faced exactly this choice.

The blood remembered what they had chosen.

Every single time.

He pulled out fresh parchment and began Volume VI.
