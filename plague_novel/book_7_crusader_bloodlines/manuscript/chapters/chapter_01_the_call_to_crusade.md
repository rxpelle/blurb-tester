# CHAPTER 1: THE CALL TO CRUSADE

## Clermont, France - November 27, 1095 CE

The cold bit through Alienor's woolen cloak as she stood in the crowd outside the Cathedral of Notre-Dame-du-Port. Ten thousand people packed the field—more than Clermont had ever seen—nobles in fur-lined mantles beside peasants in threadbare tunics, knights with mud-spattered surcoats standing among merchants who'd closed their shops to witness history.

She'd ridden three days from her family's estate in Apulia to reach this place. Three days of hard travel through the dying autumn, driven by something she couldn't name. A restlessness. A sense that her life's purpose waited here in this cold French field, if she could only grasp it.

"Can you see?" her maid Jehanne whispered, standing on tiptoe beside her.

"Barely." Alienor was tall for a woman—nearly five and a half feet—but even she struggled to see over the mass of bodies. She'd positioned herself among a group of merchants' wives, respectable enough to avoid harassment, anonymous enough to escape her father's men if they'd followed.

They shouldn't have come at all. Women didn't attend church councils. But when Pope Urban II had announced he would deliver an address of unprecedented importance, something in Alienor's blood had sung with recognition. She had to hear it. Had to be here.

Even if she couldn't explain why.

A trumpet sounded. The crowd stilled like a held breath.

The cathedral doors opened. Bishops processed out in crimson and gold, followed by abbots in black, monks in brown. And finally, Pope Urban II himself—a thin man in his sixties, white-haired beneath his papal tiara, moving with the careful dignity of age and power combined.

They'd built a platform in the field for him. As he climbed it, assisted by two cardinals, Alienor felt the crowd lean forward like wheat bending before wind.

Urban raised his hands. Ten thousand people fell silent.

"Brethren," his voice carried across the field, trained by decades of preaching, pitched to reach the farthest listeners. "I come to you in this hour of dire need, speaking not with my voice alone, but with the voice of Christ our Lord, and of His Church which bleeds."

Alienor felt Jehanne shiver beside her. The Pope's words had weight, each syllable chosen for maximum impact.

"From the confines of Jerusalem and the city of Constantinople, a horrible tale has come to our ears," Urban continued, his voice rising. "A race utterly alienated from God has invaded the lands of the Christians, has reduced the people with sword, rapine, and flame. They have destroyed the altars polluted by their foul practices. The kingdom of the Greeks is now dismembered by them and deprived of territory so vast that it could not be traversed in a march of two months."

Around Alienor, the crowd began to murmur. She saw faces hardening, heard the first whispers of outrage.

But something felt wrong.

She'd studied medicine in Salerno, at one of the few schools that admitted women. Her teachers—Christian, Jewish, and Muslim both—had taught her to observe carefully, to question, to see what lay beneath surface symptoms. And watching Pope Urban, seeing the practiced gestures, the perfectly timed pauses, she recognized the signs of performance.

This wasn't spontaneous grief. This was theater.

"On whom, therefore, rests the labor of avenging these wrongs and of recovering this territory, if not upon you?" Urban's voice cracked with emotion—or the appearance of it. "You are the race upon which God has bestowed glory in arms, greatness of spirit, physical energy, and the courage to humble the heads of those who resist you."

The crowd was swaying now, caught in his rhythm. Alienor saw a knight near her weeping openly, hand on his sword hilt.

"Let the deeds of your ancestors move you and incite your minds to manly achievements!" Urban's voice reached its crescendo. "The Holy Sepulchre of the Lord our Savior is possessed by unclean nations! Will you permit the infidels to contaminate the holy places with their filth?"

"No!" The roar came from thousands of throats simultaneously. Men began to shout, to weep, to call out vows.

"Let those who have been fighting against their brothers now fight against the infidels! Let those who have been robbers now become soldiers of Christ! Let those who have been hirelings for a few pieces of silver now attain an eternal reward!"

Alienor watched in fascination and growing unease as the crowd transformed before her eyes. Individual people becoming a single organism, a great beast roused to righteous fury. She'd seen mobs before—what city dweller hadn't?—but this was different. This was controlled. Directed.

Weaponized.

"Undertake this journey for the remission of your sins," Urban proclaimed, his voice now gentle, seductive. "Assured of the imperishable glory of the kingdom of heaven. Let none of your possessions detain you, nor solicitude for your family affairs. For this land which you inhabit is too narrow for your large population. It scarcely furnishes food enough for its cultivators. Hence it is that you murder and devour one another."

The crowd had fallen silent again, hanging on his words.

"Enter upon the road to the Holy Sepulchre. Wrest that land from the wicked race, and subject it to yourselves." Urban's final words rang like a bell across the field. "That land which, as Scripture says, flows with milk and honey."

A pause. The entire world seemed to hold its breath.

Then, from the front ranks, a bishop's voice: "Deus vult!"

God wills it.

The crowd erupted. "DEUS VULT! DEUS VULT! DEUS VULT!"

The chant hammered through Alienor's chest like a second heartbeat. Around her, men were tearing strips from their cloaks to fashion crosses, pinning them to their shoulders. Women wept. Children stared in awe.

And Alienor felt something stir in her blood.

Not the righteous fury consuming the crowd around her. Something older. Deeper. A recognition that whispered through her veins like memory, though she'd never experienced anything like this moment before.

*You've been waiting for this,* the blood-knowledge whispered. *Forty-one generations waiting. The pattern continues. And you are needed.*

She gasped, stumbling back against Jehanne.

"My lady?" Her maid caught her elbow, worried. "Are you unwell? The crowd—"

"I'm fine." Alienor steadied herself, but her hands were shaking. What was that? For a moment, she'd felt... she'd *known* things. Seen things. As if viewing this moment through eyes that weren't her own.

Through the crowd, she glimpsed a man watching her.

Not watching the Pope. Not caught in the fervor. Watching her specifically, with dark eyes that seemed to see through skin to the blood beneath.

He was dressed as a monk—Byzantine, she thought, noting the different cut of his habit. Older, perhaps forty-five, with a scholar's stoop and a warrior's watchful stillness. And when their eyes met, he nodded slowly, as if confirming something he'd suspected.

Then the crowd surged between them, and he was gone.

Alienor's heart hammered. That look. That recognition. As if he'd been searching for her and had finally found what he sought.

"My lady, we should go." Jehanne tugged at her sleeve. "Before the crowd becomes unruly. Your father's men—"

"Wait." Alienor pushed forward instead, drawn by an instinct she didn't understand. Through the mass of bodies, following the direction the Byzantine monk had gone.

But he'd vanished into the chaos of ten thousand people being born again as crusaders.

Around her, men were already organizing. Knights shouting for their retainers. Nobles pledging their estates. Common soldiers swearing oaths. The logistics of holy war assembling themselves from raw religious fervor like order condensing from chaos.

And among them, Alienor noticed other watchers.

A bishop in expensive furs, standing apart from the crowd's emotion, his eyes calculating as he surveyed the gathering armies. Two noblemen speaking in low, urgent tones, one showing the other what looked like a list written on parchment. A knight whose surcoat bore no device, who moved through the crowd marking certain men—young, strong, from good families—with small chalk marks on their backs that they didn't notice.

Organizing. Selecting. As if the spontaneous religious fervor was actually something planned, managed, directed toward specific ends.

The memory-that-wasn't-memory stirred again in her blood. She'd seen this before. Not personally, but through ancestral eyes. Seen other gatherings where religious passion was harvested like wheat, directed toward purposes the passionate never suspected.

*Forty-one generations,* the blood-voice whispered again. *Forty-one generations since the schism. Forty-one generations since the protocol split. And here, in this field, both networks gather to harvest what they've planted.*

"My lady, please." Jehanne's voice was frightened now. "We must leave before—"

A hand caught Alienor's arm.

She spun, reaching for the small knife she kept in her sleeve—then stopped.

The Byzantine monk stood before her. Up close, she could see gray in his dark beard, intelligence in his weathered face, and something else. A weight of knowledge, as if he carried histories that would crush lesser men.

"Alienor de Hauteville," he said in perfect Latin, though his accent was Greek. "Born 1070, Apulia. Daughter of Robert and Clementia. Trained in medicine at Salerno. Generation Fifty-Five."

Ice flooded her veins. "How do you know—"

"Your blood knows me, even if you do not yet know yourself." His grip on her arm was gentle but firm. "I am Brother Petros of Constantinople. And I have been searching for you for three years."

"I don't understand." But her heart hammered, because some part of her *did* understand. The blood-memory that had been whispering all morning now sang in recognition.

Petros glanced around the surging crowd. "Not here. Too many eyes." His gaze flicked toward the noble with the chalk marks, who was now watching them. "Some are offensive. Some defensive. You need to know which is which before you choose your army."

"I haven't chosen to join any army." Though even as she said it, Alienor knew it was a lie. Something in her had already decided. Had decided before she'd left Apulia, before she'd heard Urban's speech. Had decided, perhaps, forty-one generations ago.

"Yes, you have." Petros's voice was sad and certain. "You just don't know why yet." He pressed something into her palm—a small bronze medallion, warm from his body heat, marked with symbols she didn't recognize but which her blood knew, oh yes, her blood *remembered*—

"Find me in Raymond de Toulouse's camp when the armies gather in the spring," Petros said. "Come with your medical supplies. You'll be needed as a healer, which gives you reason to join that won't raise questions. And Alienor?" His dark eyes held hers. "Trust no one who says they serve God. Not even me. Especially not me. Trust only what your blood remembers, and even that carefully. The protocol can be defensive or offensive. The difference is what you choose to do with what you carry."

Then he released her and melted back into the crowd before she could respond.

Alienor stood frozen, the bronze medallion burning against her palm like a coal. Around her, the future army of Christ shouted and wept and swore oaths. Jehanne clutched her arm, asking questions she couldn't hear over the roaring in her ears.

Because her blood was remembering now, fragmentary and overwhelming. She saw:

A Egyptian scribe with dark skin, writing on papyrus, hiding knowledge as empires burned—

A Jewish woman fleeing Jerusalem as Roman soldiers approached, carrying a bronze key wrapped in linen—

A Byzantine scholar copying manuscripts by candlelight while barbarians hammered at the gates—

Her own grandmother, dying, pressing a ring into young Alienor's hand and whispering, "When the call comes, you'll know. The blood remembers. The blood always remembers."

Alienor gasped, the visions releasing her as suddenly as they'd struck.

She opened her palm. The medallion Petros had given her bore three symbols in a triangle: a cross, a star, and a crescent.

Christian. Jewish. Muslim.

The three faiths that would meet on crusade. The three networks that Petros claimed opposed each other—offensive and defensive, though she didn't yet understand what those terms meant.

She only knew, with the certainty that came from blood and bone and generations of encoded memory, that her life had just changed forever.

"My lady?" Jehanne's voice was very small. "What did he give you?"

Alienor closed her fist around the medallion. Around her, the armies of Christ were being born. Knights swearing to take Jerusalem. Nobles pledging fortunes. Common soldiers seeking redemption through righteous violence.

And hidden among them, two networks playing a longer game. Using holy war as cover for something else. Something her blood knew but her mind had yet to grasp.

She thought of Urban's calculated performance. The watchers in the crowd. The nobles with their lists. The strange recognition in Petros's eyes when he'd called her "Generation Fifty-Five" as if it meant something specific, something important.

As if she'd been bred for this moment.

The thought should have terrified her. Instead, it clarified everything.

"Come," Alienor told Jehanne, turning away from the cathedral, away from the roaring crowd. "We're going home."

"Home?" Relief flooded Jehanne's face. "Then you're not joining them? You're not going on crusade?"

Alienor looked back one last time at the field full of future crusaders. Saw the Byzantine monk watching her from a distance. Saw the noble with the chalk marks making notes on his parchment. Saw Pope Urban being escorted back into the cathedral, his work complete.

Saw the pattern, though she didn't yet understand its shape.

"I'm going home," she confirmed. "To gather my medical supplies. To prepare."

"Prepare for what, my lady?"

Alienor opened her hand, looked at the medallion with its three intertwined symbols. Cross. Star. Crescent.

"To save as many as I can," she said quietly. "From whichever network tries to kill them."

She didn't fully understand what that meant. Not yet. But her blood remembered. And when the call came in spring—when Raymond de Toulouse's army gathered, when the great march toward Jerusalem began—she would be ready.

Ready to heal. Ready to witness. Ready to choose, when the moment came, between the offensive network that saw crusade as opportunity and the defensive network that saw it as catastrophe to be mitigated.

Ready, perhaps, to determine which network would control the aftermath of holy war.

Though she didn't know it yet, standing in that cold French field with a bronze medallion burning in her palm, Alienor de Hauteville had just become the most important piece in a game that had been playing for forty-one generations.

A game that wouldn't end for another forty-one more.

The blood remembered.

And the pattern continued.

## Hauteville Estate, Apulia, Southern Italy - December 1095 CE

The journey home took five days.

Five days of hard riding through winter-touched mountains, across rain-swollen rivers, past villages where rumors of the crusade had already spread like fever. Everywhere they stopped, Alienor heard the whispers: *An army for God. Jerusalem to be liberated. The Holy Lance to be recovered. Redemption for any who take the cross.*

The fever was spreading faster than any plague she'd studied.

By the time they reached her father's estate in the hills above the coast, Alienor had counted fourteen different groups of men heading north to join the gathering armies. Knights, yes, but also farmers abandoning their fields, merchants closing their shops, even a group of monks who'd left their monastery to become soldiers of Christ.

The madness was universal. And orchestrated, her instincts insisted. Too coordinated to be spontaneous, too uniform in message to be truly decentralized.

*Both networks gather to harvest what they've planted,* her blood-memory had whispered in Clermont. Now, watching the human tide flowing north, she began to understand what that meant.

"My lady." The guard at her father's gate snapped to attention, clearly relieved. "Lord Robert has been asking for you daily. He's—" The man hesitated. "He's not pleased you left without permission."

"I'm sure he isn't." Alienor dismounted, handing her reins to a stable boy. "Where is he?"

"The great hall, my lady. With—" Again the hesitation. "—with your intended."

Alienor's stomach dropped. "My intended?"

"Count Tancred, my lady. He arrived two days ago. Your father says you're to be married before Lent."

Of course. She'd known this was coming—her father had been negotiating marriage contracts for months. But she'd hoped for more time. Hoped the crusade fever would distract him from domestic arrangements.

Instead, it seemed to have accelerated them.

Jehanne caught her hand as they crossed the courtyard. "My lady, if you don't wish to marry Count Tancred—"

"It doesn't matter what I wish." Alienor kept her voice flat. "I'm unmarried at twenty-five, which makes me dangerously close to unmarriageable. My father needs alliances. Tancred is Norman nobility with lands and connections. The match makes political sense."

"But the crusade—"

"Will be full of married couples." Though as Alienor said it, she realized that might actually work in her favor. Married women could join crusade with their husbands. It would give her legitimacy, protection, access to the noble contingents where decisions would be made.

It would also bind her to a man she barely knew, chain her to his ambitions and loyalties. If Tancred was part of whichever network opposed Brother Petros...

She needed to know more. Needed to understand what she was walking into before she committed to anything.

The great hall smelled of roasted meat and wood smoke. Her father sat at the high table with his guest—a young man, perhaps twenty-three, broad-shouldered and handsome in the Norman fashion, with close-cropped dark hair and the easy confidence of one who'd never doubted his place in the world.

Count Tancred of Hauteville. Distant cousin, though the family connection was thin enough to permit marriage. Rising star among the Norman knights of Southern Italy. And from the cross sewn prominently on his surcoat, already committed to crusade.

"Alienor." Her father rose, his weathered face stern. "You return at last."

"Father." She curtsied precisely, giving him no excuse to escalate. "I apologize for my absence. I had business in Clermont."

"Business." Robert's voice was tight with controlled anger. "You left without permission, without escort, to attend a church council that was no place for a woman. Do you have any idea how that reflects on this family? On the marriage negotiations that were nearly complete when you vanished?"

"I heard Pope Urban speak." Alienor kept her eyes downcast, playing the dutiful daughter while her mind raced. "I wanted to understand what was being asked of Christian knights. Since my intended—" she glanced at Tancred, "—has already taken the cross, it seemed important to know what cause he serves."

Tancred smiled, clearly pleased. "Your daughter has a crusader's spirit, my lord. That's rare in these soft times."

"My daughter has an independent spirit that needs tempering." Robert's voice carried warning. "Alienor, this is Count Tancred of Hauteville, who has done you the honor of accepting my proposal of marriage. You will be wed in six weeks, and you will comport yourself as befits a future countess. No more running off to church councils. No more playing physician to peasants. No more—"

"I wish to join the crusade."

Silence crashed over the hall.

Her father stared. Tancred's smile widened. The servants hovering near the walls tried very hard to become invisible.

"You what?" Robert's voice was dangerously quiet.

Alienor lifted her chin, meeting his eyes directly for the first time. "Pope Urban called for all Christians to liberate Jerusalem. He offered remission of sins to any who take the cross. I'm a trained physician. The army will need healers. I should go."

"Absolutely not."

"Father—"

"Women do not go on crusade." Robert's hand slammed the table. "Women stay home and manage estates while their men fight. Women pray for victory. Women do *not* march with armies into heathen lands where they'll be captured, raped, and sold into slavery."

"Some women are going." Alienor had heard the rumors on the road. "Wives accompanying their husbands. Nuns serving as nurses. If I'm married to Count Tancred, and he's joining the crusade—"

"Which is exactly why the marriage should happen *after* I return from Jerusalem." Tancred spoke for the first time, his voice smooth and reasonable. "I wouldn't want to leave a new bride so soon after our wedding, Lady Alienor. And your father is right—crusade is no place for a woman, even one with medical training."

Alienor studied him. Handsome, yes. Well-spoken, certainly. But his eyes were flat when he smiled, and something about the way he'd phrased that—*even one with medical training*—suggested he viewed her skills as a quirk to be tolerated rather than an asset to be valued.

And the way he'd looked at her when she'd entered. Not with desire or even curiosity, but with assessment. As if checking her against a list of required qualities, confirming she met specifications.

The same way the nobleman in Clermont had marked men with chalk.

Her blood went cold.

"You're going on crusade," she said slowly. "With Raymond de Toulouse's army?"

Tancred's smile didn't waver. "I am. Raymond is organizing the Provençal forces. I've pledged my household knights and a significant treasury contribution. Why do you ask?"

Because Brother Petros had told her to join Raymond's camp. Because her blood was singing warning. Because Tancred's eyes held the same calculating emptiness she'd seen in the watchers at Clermont.

"No reason." Alienor forced herself to smile. "I simply wondered which army my intended would join. It's natural to want to pray for the correct force, surely."

"Of course." Tancred rose, moving around the table with the grace of a trained warrior. He stopped an arm's length away, close enough to be intimate without being improper. "Lady Alienor, I understand your desire to serve God's cause. It's admirable. But crusade is brutal. Thousands will die—not just from battle, but from disease, starvation, exhaustion. The roads will be littered with bodies. That's no place for a woman of gentle birth."

"I'm a physician." Alienor held her ground. "I've treated battle wounds, delivered breech births, cut infected limbs. I'm not delicate, Count Tancred."

"Even so." His hand lifted as if to touch her cheek, then dropped when she didn't lean into the gesture. "I would prefer my wife safe in Apulia, managing my estates while I fight. Can you not do me that service?"

It was beautifully phrased. Framing her obedience as service to him rather than submission to male authority. Giving her a role—estate management—that carried real responsibility and power.

Any reasonable woman would accept.

"If we marry before you leave," Alienor said carefully, "I'll be your wife and obligated to obey you. If we marry after you return, I'm still my father's daughter and can make my own choices in the interim."

Tancred's smile finally cracked. For just a moment, she saw something cold beneath the charm. "You want to delay our marriage?"

"I want to understand what I'm being asked to support." Alienor kept her voice level. "You're pledging my potential dowry to finance crusade. You're planning to be gone for years—it took the last Roman forces four months just to reach Constantinople, and that's barely halfway to Jerusalem. You're asking me to manage your estates, your lands, your people, while you pursue holy war. I think it's reasonable to want time to consider such a commitment."

"She has a point," Robert said grudgingly. "The campaign could take two years or more. If Alienor marries you now and you die in Palestine, she's widowed before properly being a wife. Better to wait until you return victorious."

Alienor could have kissed her father. He'd reached the conclusion she'd wanted for entirely practical, male-focused reasons that Tancred couldn't easily argue against.

But Tancred wasn't done. "If I might suggest a compromise?" He looked between Alienor and her father. "What if Lady Alienor accompanies the army as far as Constantinople? She could serve as a healer during the march—God knows we'll need skilled physicians. The journey to the Byzantine capital is relatively safe, with established roads and Christian territories. Once we reach Constantinople, she could return home with a Byzantine escort while the army continues to Jerusalem."

It was clever. Gave Alienor what she'd asked for while maintaining Tancred's control. Made him appear reasonable, flexible, modern in his thinking.

And put her exactly where Brother Petros had told her she needed to be: in Raymond de Toulouse's camp.

Why?

What did Tancred gain by having her in his army? Unless—

Unless he'd marked her somehow. The same way the nobleman at Clermont had marked men. Unless she was on a list, a name that had been flagged, someone he'd been told to watch or recruit or evaluate.

*Generation Fifty-Five,* Petros had called her. As if it meant something specific.

As if she'd been bred for something specific.

"I accept your compromise," Alienor said before her father could object. "I'll accompany the army to Constantinople as a healer. When we reach the Byzantine capital, I'll return home. We can be married after you return victorious from Jerusalem."

Tancred's smile returned, warm and pleased. "Excellent. I'll ensure you have proper protection during the march. My personal guard will keep you safe."

*Or keep me watched,* Alienor thought. But she smiled and nodded like a dutiful betrothed, while her mind raced through implications.

Two networks. Offensive and defensive. Brother Petros wanting her in Raymond's camp. Tancred wanting her in Raymond's camp. Which meant either they served the same network—unlikely, given Petros's warning to trust no one—or they both wanted her close for different reasons.

Wanted her. Not just any healer. Her specifically.

Because of her bloodline. Because of what her blood carried. Forty-one generations of encoded memory, whatever that meant.

Because she was Generation Fifty-Five.

That night, alone in her chamber, Alienor examined the bronze medallion Petros had given her.

Three symbols in a triangle: cross, star, crescent. Christian, Jewish, Muslim. The three faiths that would clash in Jerusalem.

But the medallion showed them united. Interconnected. As if whatever secret the three networks guarded transcended religious division.

She traced the symbols with her finger, and her blood sang in recognition. Images flickered behind her eyes:

*A dark-skinned woman in Egyptian linen, hiding scrolls as soldiers approached—*

*A man in Roman armor, fleeing Jerusalem with bronze keys wrapped in his cloak—*

*A Byzantine scholar, copying manuscripts by candlelight while barbarians burned the city—*

The same visions from Clermont, but clearer now. More detailed. As if her blood was teaching her, preparing her, showing her the pattern that repeated across centuries.

Networks. Always networks. One trying to preserve knowledge, the other trying to control it. The defensive and the offensive, locked in eternal struggle, using religion and war and politics as camouflage for their true purposes.

And now crusade. Holy war. A hundred thousand people marching toward Jerusalem, driven by faith and greed and papal manipulation.

The perfect cover for both networks to operate at unprecedented scale.

*You've been bred for this,* her blood whispered. *Forty-one generations. Each one chosen, matched, positioned. Your grandmother knew. Your mother suspected. And now you know.*

*Know what?* Alienor asked silently.

The blood showed her:

She saw herself standing in a burning city, smoke rising from buildings, bodies in the streets. Saw herself holding a key—bronze, shaped like a hand—while men fought around her for possession of it. Saw herself choosing, in that moment of fire and blood, which network would control the aftermath of crusade.

Saw herself determining whether the next collapse would be prevented or engineered.

Whatever that meant.

Alienor closed her fist around the medallion. Around her, the estate slept. Outside, winter settled over Southern Italy. To the north, armies gathered. Nobles pledged treasuries. Knights sharpened swords. Common soldiers took the cross and dreamed of redemption.

And two networks prepared to harvest what they'd planted.

In six months, when spring came and the great march began, Alienor would join them. Would ride with Tancred and Raymond de Toulouse and all the rest, marching toward Jerusalem and whatever destiny waited there.

Would meet Brother Petros and learn what Generation Fifty-Five meant.

Would discover which network she truly served.

Would find the Bronze Hand key that her blood remembered, though she'd never seen it.

Would choose, when the moment came, whether to prevent collapse or engineer it.

Though she didn't know—couldn't know, not yet—that her choice would echo for two hundred and fifty years. That a man named Thomas would carry forward what she began. That the pattern she joined in 1095 would continue until it finally broke or humanity broke with it.

She knew only that her blood remembered.

And that memory was both burden and gift, both destiny and choice.

In the morning, she would begin preparing. Medical supplies. Travel gear. Everything a healer would need for a two-year journey to Constantinople and back.

Everything a spy would need to infiltrate both networks.

Everything a woman bred for forty-one generations would need to fulfill a purpose she didn't yet understand.

The blood remembered.

The pattern continued.

And Alienor de Hauteville, Generation Fifty-Five, took her first conscious steps toward Jerusalem.
