# CHAPTER 4: THE ANATOLIAN CRUCIBLE

## Asia Minor, Ten Miles from Nicaea - May 1097 CE

The heat was killing them faster than the Turks.

Alienor moved through the hospital tent in the pre-dawn darkness, checking patients who'd collapsed during yesterday's march. Seventeen men down with heatstroke. Eight more with dysentery so severe they'd need at least three days to recover. Two dead in the night—one from infected wounds that had turned septic, the other from simple exhaustion and dehydration.

They'd been marching through Asia Minor for three weeks since leaving Constantinople. Three weeks of brutal sun, inadequate water, and Turkish skirmishers harassing the column's flanks. The crusader army had already lost four hundred people—most to disease and heat, not battle.

And the siege of Nicaea hadn't even begun yet.

"How many can march today?" Marguerite appeared beside her, dark circles under her eyes. The nun had been up all night treating dysentery cases.

"Maybe six of the seventeen heatstroke cases. None of the dysentery patients. The two who died, obviously not." Alienor wiped sweat from her forehead, though dawn hadn't yet broken. The heat here was relentless, even at night. "We need to slow the march. Let the sick recover. Establish better water discipline."

"Count Raymond won't slow." Marguerite's voice was flat with exhaustion and frustration. "We're racing the other crusader armies to Nicaea. Godfrey of Bouillon's forces are two days ahead of us. Bohemond's army is a week behind. Whoever reaches Nicaea first gets first claim on whatever we capture."

"So we'll arrive with an army half-dead from heat and disease."

"Better than arriving second."

It was the offensive network's logic—accept losses to maintain strategic advantage. Speed over survival. Mission over individuals.

And Alienor could see the pragmatic sense in it, which disturbed her more than she wanted to admit.

Because in the three weeks since Constantinople, since accessing the Copper Heart and seeing forty-one generations of network history, she'd been watching both networks operate openly. Watching how they made decisions. How they allocated resources. How they chose who lived and who died when choices had to be made.

The defensive network—Theophania, Petros, Marguerite, and others she'd identified—tried to save everyone. They snuck extra water rations to the weakest soldiers. They treated all patients regardless of military value. They argued constantly with commanders about slowing the march, improving hygiene, preventing preventable deaths.

They saved lives individually while losing strategically.

The offensive network—Tancred and others she'd identified among Raymond's senior knights—triaged ruthlessly. They ensured the best soldiers got the best care, the most water, the most rest. They accepted that weaker soldiers would die and focused resources on keeping the army's core strength intact. They sacrificed individuals without hesitation if it served the mission.

They lost lives individually while winning strategically.

And Alienor, trapped between them, treating patients from both approaches, couldn't decide which horrified her more.

"Lady Alienor." A soldier appeared at the tent entrance, his surcoat marked with Tancred's device. "Count Tancred requests your presence. There's been an incident."

Alienor exchanged glances with Marguerite. "Incident" was never good.

"What kind of incident?"

"Three soldiers collapsed during night watch. One died. Count Tancred wants your assessment of whether it was natural causes or..." The soldier hesitated. "...deliberate."

Poison. Or poisoned water. Or sabotage by one network targeting the other's people.

"I'll come." Alienor grabbed her medical kit. To Marguerite: "Can you manage here?"

"Go. But Alienor?" The nun's voice dropped to a whisper. "Be careful. If this is network conflict becoming open, you don't want to be caught in the crossfire."

Too late for that, Alienor thought. She'd been in the crossfire since Clermont.

---

## Command Tent - Dawn

Tancred stood over three bodies laid out on rough canvas. Two soldiers and one knight, all showing similar symptoms—vomiting, seizures, blue lips suggesting respiratory failure.

"Poison," he said without preamble as Alienor entered. "The question is whether it was intended for them specifically or whether the water supply was contaminated to hit the entire camp."

Alienor knelt beside the bodies, examining them with practiced efficiency. Similar symptoms. Rapid onset—the soldier had said they'd collapsed during night watch, which meant the poison had acted within hours. Blue lips and fingernails suggested respiratory paralysis.

"Hemlock," she said after a moment. "Or something similar. Plant-based toxin that causes progressive paralysis, respiratory failure, death within hours. Whoever did this knew what they were doing."

"Could it have gotten into the water accidentally?"

"No. Hemlock requires preparation—crushing the seeds, steeping them. This was deliberate."

"Could it have been targeting these three specifically, or was it meant for broader casualties?"

The right question. Alienor stood, thinking through logistics. "Where did they get their water?"

"Same source as everyone in the northern camp section. About three hundred people."

"And only three collapsed?"

"Only three we know of. I've got men checking with other tent groups now."

If it was water contamination meant to cause mass casualties, more people should be affected. If only three died from a contaminated source that three hundred people used, either the poison was poorly distributed or these three were specifically targeted.

"Do you know these men?" Alienor asked. "Any connection between them?"

"All three were part of the hospital guard contingent. They provided security for the medical tents." Tancred's expression was grim. "Which means someone either targeted the hospital security specifically, or..."

"Or poisoned them to create vulnerability before hitting the medical supplies." Alienor's blood went cold. "We need to check the medicine stores. If someone's sabotaging the hospital, they might have contaminated our remedies."

They ran.

The medical supply tent was in chaos when they arrived. Marguerite stood in the center, holding a broken seal on a water jar, her face pale.

"Someone's been here," she said without preamble. "Multiple supply seals broken. I was checking our freshwater stores for treatment and found this." She held up the jar. "This should contain clean water for wound washing. It smells of almonds."

Cyanide. Or a related compound.

"Don't let anyone use it." Alienor began a frantic inventory. "Check every jar, every vial, every package. If the seals are broken, don't trust the contents."

They worked with desperate speed as dawn broke over the camp. Found six jars of water contaminated with what smelled like cyanide. Three packages of wound herbs mixed with something that might be arsenic. Two vials of pain tincture that had been diluted with a substance Alienor couldn't identify but which made her blood memory scream warnings.

By the time they'd finished, a crowd had gathered—patients, guards, other healers, and both Tancred and Count Raymond himself.

"Who did this?" Raymond's voice was cold with controlled fury. "Who had access to the medical supplies?"

"Everyone." Marguerite gestured at the tent. "We don't lock supplies because patients need access during emergencies. Any soldier could walk in here at night. Any merchant traveling with the army. Any—"

"Any network operative," Tancred finished quietly.

Silence crashed over the tent.

"You're suggesting," Raymond said carefully, "that there are factions within my army actively trying to kill each other?"

"I'm suggesting," Tancred replied, "that not everyone in this crusade shares the same goals. Some want to reach Jerusalem to liberate the Holy Sepulchre. Others see this march as opportunity to test disease resistance, arrange strategic breeding, position resources for the next collapse. And when those goals conflict—when preserving everyone's life conflicts with engineering optimal survival—sometimes sabotage happens."

He was being remarkably direct about the networks. Either he'd decided that openness was the best strategy, or the situation was dire enough that pretense was too expensive.

Raymond studied him for a long moment. "You're talking about the physician networks. The ones Emperor Alexios warned me about."

"Yes."

"And you believe they're operating in my army?"

"I know they're operating in your army. The question is whether this sabotage was one network attacking the other, or one network attacking our medical capacity to create pressure for different resource allocation decisions."

"Explain."

Tancred glanced at Alienor. She could refuse to answer, could claim ignorance, could try to maintain the fiction that she was just a physician treating patients.

But three soldiers were dead. Her supply stores were contaminated. And both networks had just demonstrated they were willing to kill to advance their agendas.

"There are two primary networks operating within the crusade," Alienor said, choosing her words carefully. "One prioritizes preserving as many lives as possible through superior medical care, even if that slows the march or consumes resources. The other prioritizes maintaining military effectiveness even if that means accepting higher casualties among less strategically valuable soldiers."

"Prevention versus selection," Raymond said. He didn't sound surprised. "Defensive versus offensive network philosophy. Yes, I'm aware. Emperor Alexios explained the situation. What I need to know is: which network is sabotaging my army's medical supplies?"

Alienor looked at the contaminated jars, thought through the logic.

If the defensive network wanted to save lives, sabotaging medical supplies made no sense. Unless they were trying to force a slowdown by making it impossible to treat casualties, thereby forcing Raymond to stop marching until supplies could be replaced.

If the offensive network wanted to maintain strategic advantage, sabotaging supplies also made no sense. Unless they were trying to create a crisis that would justify more ruthless triage—make resources so scarce that commanders would have to accept their preferred allocation strategies.

Or—a third possibility—this wasn't about network philosophy at all. This was about controlling her.

Three guards who protected the medical tent, dead. Supplies contaminated. The hospital vulnerable. And Alienor forced to make decisions about how to respond, decisions that would reveal which network she was leaning toward.

"This wasn't about the supplies," she said slowly. "This was a test. Whoever did this wanted to see how I'd respond. Whether I'd prioritize replacing everything, ensuring absolute safety even if it means delaying medical care. Or whether I'd accept some risk, use what's clearly safe and discard what's contaminated, and keep treating patients with reduced supplies."

"A test," Raymond repeated. "Someone is testing my army's chief physician."

"Someone is testing Generation Fifty-Five." Tancred's voice was quiet. "To see which network she'll choose."

Raymond looked between them. "I'm too old and too busy to care about physician politics. Lady Alienor, can you continue providing medical care with remaining supplies?"

"With reduced capacity, yes."

"Then do so. Count Tancred, find whoever poisoned my guards and contaminated my supplies. I don't care which network they represent—they answer to me first, and they'll hang for murder." Raymond's voice hardened. "We reach Nicaea in three days. I need my army functional, which means I need my physicians alive and my soldiers healthy enough to fight. Network politics can wait until after we've taken the city."

He left, radiating controlled anger.

Tancred waited until Raymond was out of earshot, then turned to Alienor. "You realize you can't stay neutral now."

"I never could."

"But you could have delayed choosing. Now both networks know you're aware of them, aware of being tested, and aware that you're important enough to test. That eliminates the pretense of ignorance." He studied her face. "So what will you do?"

Alienor looked at the contaminated supplies. At Marguerite's worried expression. At the hospital tent full of patients who needed care regardless of network politics.

"I'll do what I always do," she said. "Treat the sick. Save who I can. And make decisions based on medical necessity rather than political philosophy."

"That's still a choice," Tancred said. "Prioritizing individual lives over strategic necessity is defensive network thinking."

"And prioritizing strategy over individuals is offensive network thinking. I know." Alienor's voice was sharper than intended. "But maybe there's a third option. Maybe I can acknowledge that both networks have valid points while refusing to sacrifice either individuals or strategy entirely."

"That's not how it works."

"Then maybe it should be."

She turned and walked back into the hospital tent before Tancred could respond. Marguerite followed, and together they began the work of salvaging what supplies they could, discarding what was contaminated, and preparing for three more days of brutal marching through heat that killed as efficiently as any poison.

Behind her, she heard Tancred speaking to his guards in low, urgent tones. Arranging protection. Or surveillance. Probably both.

The crucible had begun.

And Alienor de Hauteville, Generation Fifty-Five, was about to be tested by fire.

---

## The March to Nicaea - Three Days Later

They lost another two hundred people during the march to Nicaea.

Heat. Disease. Turkish ambushes that struck the column's weakest points and melted away before the knights could respond. Accidents—men falling from horses, wagons overturning, fights that escalated into killings.

Alienor treated them all. Worked eighteen-hour days. Survived on three hours of sleep and meals snatched between patients. Watched men die despite her best efforts and some men live despite having no right to survive.

And through it all, she felt both networks watching.

The defensive network offered help. Extra water appeared in the medical tent from unknown sources. Supplies materialized to replace what had been contaminated. Patients who should have died stabilized mysteriously after unidentified scholars visited them with "blessings" that felt more like medical intervention disguised as prayer.

The offensive network offered efficiency. Lists appeared on Tancred's desk identifying which soldiers were most strategically valuable—treat these first, let those wait, don't waste resources on the others. Water rations were allocated based on military utility. The best physicians were directed toward treating knights and nobles, leaving common soldiers to barber-surgeons and hope.

Both strategies worked.

Both strategies failed.

The defensive network saved individuals who then slowed the march because they weren't fully recovered. The offensive network maintained military efficiency while creating resentment among common soldiers who noticed they received inferior care.

Alienor navigated between them, trying to find balance. Treating strategically important soldiers first when resources were scarce. But also treating common soldiers when she had time and supplies. Accepting that some patients would die while refusing to write anyone off entirely.

It satisfied neither network.

Which meant it probably offended both.

"You're trying to have it both ways," Marguerite said on the final night before reaching Nicaea. They sat outside the medical tent, too exhausted to sleep despite desperate need. "You're trying to be both networks simultaneously."

"I'm trying to be a physician."

"You can't separate the two. Not when resources are scarce and choices are necessary." Marguerite's voice was gentle but firm. "Every decision you make reveals which philosophy you actually believe. Whether you admit it or not, you're already choosing."

"Then what am I choosing?"

"Defensive network with offensive tactics. You want to save everyone—that's defensive thinking. But you're willing to triage ruthlessly when necessary—that's offensive execution. You're uncomfortable with breeding programs but you understand their logic. You reject pure utilitarianism but you acknowledge its practical value."

"That sounds confused."

"That sounds like someone who's seen both networks clearly and realizes neither has monopoly on truth." Marguerite smiled slightly. "Which makes you dangerous to both, because you might create something new. Something neither network can control."

Before Alienor could respond, shouting erupted across the camp.

They ran toward the noise and found soldiers gathered around the command tent, pointing at something nailed to the main support pole.

A parchment. Written in careful Latin. A message meant to be read.

Alienor pushed through the crowd close enough to read it:

*TO GENERATION FIFTY-NINE:*

*Your neutrality is noted. Your compromise is understood. Your attempt to serve both networks while committing to neither is clever but unsustainable.*

*Nicaea will fall within the month. When it does, both networks will offer you proof of their philosophies. The defensive network will show you how preservation saves lives. The offensive network will show you how selection wins wars.*

*You will choose then. Or the choice will be made for you.*

*The blood remembers. The pattern continues. And the crucible intensifies until you emerge refined or destroyed.*

*Choose wisely, Generation Fifty-Five. Humanity's next collapse may depend on your decision.*

No signature. No network identification. Just the message and the implicit threat.

"They're both cooperating," Alienor realized. "Both networks agreed to force my hand. They're working together to eliminate my neutrality option."

"Working together against a common threat," Marguerite corrected. "You. Because you represent the possibility that networks aren't necessary. That individuals can make these choices without ancient organizations directing them. That forty-one generations of breeding and selection and preservation might have produced something they didn't intend—someone capable of independent choice."

"That's insane."

"That's terrifying," Marguerite countered. "To them. Because if you prove networks aren't necessary, if you demonstrate that knowledge and choice matter more than organizational control, then both networks become obsolete. Forty-one generations of work, undermined by one woman who refuses to play by their rules."

Tancred appeared beside them, reading the message with cold efficiency.

"Well," he said after a moment. "That's both networks declaring war on your independence. Congratulations. You've managed to unite two factions that have been fighting for forty-one generations. Against you."

"What happens now?"

"Now we take Nicaea. And during the siege, both networks will demonstrate their philosophies with perfect clarity. The defensive network will try to minimize casualties through superior medical care, better hygiene, and strategic disease prevention. The offensive network will try to win the siege quickly through aggressive tactics, ruthless resource allocation, and accepting higher casualties for faster victory."

"And I'm caught in the middle."

"No." Tancred's expression was serious. "You're the prize. Whoever convinces you during the Nicaea siege wins Generation Fifty-Five. Wins your children's genetics. Wins the strongest carrier in four generations. Wins the possibility of shifting the network balance for the next forty-one generations."

He walked away, leaving Alienor and Marguerite standing in the pre-dawn darkness, staring at a message that had just transformed crusade into crucible.

The siege of Nicaea would begin tomorrow.

And somewhere in the brutal mathematics of siege warfare—the disease, the casualties, the impossible decisions about who lived and who died—Alienor would be forced to choose.

Or be chosen for.

The blood remembered.

The pattern continued.

And Generation Fifty-Five was about to learn whether forty-one generations of breeding had produced someone strong enough to survive what came next.

Or whether the networks would simply eliminate an uncontrollable variable.

## Siege Lines, Nicaea - Two Weeks Later

The siege was going badly.

Nicaea was a fortress city, triple-walled, with Turkish defenders who had no intention of surrendering to a Christian army. The crusaders had surrounded the walls, but taking them was proving impossible. Every assault was repelled with heavy casualties. Every section of wall they damaged was repaired overnight. Every strategy they tried, the Turks had counter-strategies ready.

And disease was ravaging the camp faster than Turkish arrows.

Dysentery had spread through half the army. Some new fever—possibly typhus—was killing dozens daily. The heat remained brutal. Water was scarce despite being near a lake, because the Turks controlled access and crusader supply lines were overstretched.

Alienor had lost count of how many patients she'd treated. How many had died. How many she'd had to turn away because she simply didn't have supplies or energy or time to save everyone.

And through it all, both networks demonstrated their philosophies with devastating clarity.

The defensive network, led by Theophania who'd traveled from Constantinople specifically for this, established model hygiene protocols. They dug proper latrines. They enforced water purification. They quarantined the sick. They treated everyone—knights and common soldiers equally.

Their sections of camp had half the disease rate of other sections.

They also were contributing fewer soldiers to assaults, because their healthier soldiers were being used for non-combat support roles like maintaining hygiene infrastructure.

The offensive network, led by Tancred and other commanders, accepted disease as inevitable. They focused medical resources on maintaining strike-force readiness. They pushed sick soldiers beyond recovery capability. They spent lives freely to maintain assault momentum.

Their sections of camp had twice the disease rate.

They also were winning the siege. Turkish defenders were breaking under sustained assault. The walls were weakening. Victory was perhaps a week away.

"Do you see now?" Theophania asked one evening, finding Alienor treating patients in the defensive network's hospital section. "Look at the disease rates. Look at the survival statistics. Prevention works. Organization works. Caring for everyone works."

"And yet you're not winning the siege," Alienor said, more sharply than intended. "Your soldiers are healthier but they're not fighting. Mine are dying but we're taking the walls."

"Is that victory? Achieving your military objective while losing half your army?"

"Is your version victory? Keeping everyone alive while failing to accomplish the mission?"

They stared at each other across a patient's bed—both physicians, both trying to save lives, both serving philosophies that seemed incompatible.

"I don't know anymore," Alienor admitted finally. "I see your point. I see Tancred's point. I see both networks' logic. And I can't figure out which is actually correct because they're both right about different things."

"Then choose based on values, not results." Theophania's voice softened. "Which philosophy aligns with who you are? Do you believe every life has equal worth? Or do you believe some lives are more strategically valuable than others?"

"I believe—" Alienor stopped, thinking. "I believe context matters. In stable civilizations with abundant resources, everyone should receive equal care. But in crisis situations with scarce resources, strategic triage is necessary for collective survival."

"That's still choosing offensive network philosophy. You're accepting that some lives matter more than others."

"No. I'm accepting that sometimes you can't save everyone, and when you can't, you make the hard choice about how to allocate limited resources. That doesn't mean I believe some people are inherently more valuable. It means I acknowledge tragic reality."

"And who decides which reality is tragic enough to justify strategic triage?"

That was the question, wasn't it? The one both networks had been fighting over for forty-one generations.

Who got to decide when circumstances justified abandoning the ideal of equal care? Who determined when prevention was no longer possible and selection became necessary? Who drew the line between crisis that required extraordinary measures and normal difficulty that should be solved through better organization?

Both networks had answers.

Neither answer satisfied Alienor.

Before Theophania could press further, screaming erupted near the walls.

"BREACH! The Turks have breached the northern section!"

Alienor ran.

---

## The Turkish Sortie - That Night

The Turks had done something brilliant and desperate—they'd tunneled under the crusader siege lines and erupted behind the northern camps, attacking from within while defenders on the walls assaulted from without.

Chaos consumed the crusader army.

Alienor found herself in her hospital tent as fighting raged outside. Wounded poured in—crusaders cut down by Turkish swords, burned by Greek fire, trampled in the panic. She worked with desperate speed, Marguerite beside her, trying to save whoever they could.

And she made the choices.

This knight was strategically important—treat him first. This common soldier's wounds were fatal—make him comfortable while she saved someone who might survive. These two patients had similar injuries, but one was younger, stronger, more likely to recover—prioritize him.

Strategic triage.

Offensive network logic.

The defensive network would have treated them in the order they arrived, regardless of military value or survival probability. Would have given everyone equal chance, equal resources, equal care.

Alienor was choosing who lived and who died based on strategic calculation.

And she hated herself for it.

But she also knew that if she didn't make these choices, if she tried to save everyone equally, she'd save fewer people overall. The mathematics were brutal but clear—strategic triage maximized total survivors even as it condemned specific individuals.

"Lady Alienor!" A guard burst into the tent. "Count Tancred is down. Critical wounds. They're bringing him—"

Tancred was carried in on a stretcher, his armor torn, blood pouring from a massive wound across his chest and shoulder. Turkish sword, probably, angled down through the shoulder joint.

He was dying.

Alienor knelt beside him immediately, assessing with practiced speed. The wound had severed major blood vessels. He'd lost enormous amounts of blood already. Without immediate intervention—surgery, cauterization, prayer and hope—he'd be dead within minutes.

But she had three other critical patients.

A common soldier with chest wound, survivable if treated immediately.

A knight with crushed leg, survivable with amputation.

A priest with head injury, might survive, might not, impossible to predict.

Four patients. One physician. She couldn't save all of them.

This was the choice both networks had been preparing her for.

Save Tancred—the offensive network's brilliant young commander, her betrothed, someone strategically critical to the crusade's success. Or let him die and save the others who were less important but collectively represented more total survivors.

Save the individual with highest strategic value, or save the most people possible with limited resources.

The offensive network's answer was obvious: save Tancred.

The defensive network's answer was also obvious: save whoever you encountered first, don't prioritize based on military rank.

Alienor looked at Tancred's face. He was conscious, aware of his situation, and surprisingly calm.

"Choose what you believe," he whispered. "Not what I'd want. What you actually believe."

She made her choice.

"Marguerite, take Tancred. Do what you can to stabilize him." To the barber-surgeon who'd just entered: "You, the knight with the crushed leg—amputate now before infection sets in." To the priest: "Get the head wound patient to Theophania's tent, they have better supplies for this." To herself: "I'll take the chest wound."

Not prioritizing based on rank. Not prioritizing based on who came first. Prioritizing based on who needed which specific expertise most critically—Marguerite's battlefield experience for Tancred's complex wound, the barber-surgeon's amputation skills for the knight, Theophania's head injury expertise for the priest, and her own surgical training for the chest wound.

Distributed specialization.

A third option neither network had presented.

She worked with absolute focus, opening the soldier's chest cavity, repairing the internal damage, praying her training was sufficient. Around her, Marguerite fought to save Tancred. The barber-surgeon amputated efficiently. Messengers ran to Theophania's tent.

An hour later, exhausted and blood-covered, Alienor had four outcomes:

The common soldier survived. Her surgery had worked.

The knight survived. His leg was gone but infection had been prevented.

The priest had died—Theophania had tried but the head injury was too severe.

And Tancred...

Tancred lived. Marguerite's battlefield experience and desperate skill had stabilized him long enough that Theophania's arrival with better supplies had completed what Marguerite started. He'd lost his arm—the shoulder joint was too destroyed to save—but he'd survived.

Three saved. One lost. Not the four-for-one trade the offensive network would have made. Not the first-come-first-served the defensive network would have employed.

A third way.

"You didn't choose either network," Marguerite said later, as they cleaned instruments in exhausted silence. "You chose distributed specialization. Used everyone's skills optimally regardless of network philosophy."

"Did it work?"

"Three people are alive who might have died with either network's approach. One died who might have survived with different choices. I don't know if that's better or worse than the alternatives. But it was your choice, made independently, based on medical judgment rather than network philosophy."

"And now both networks will be furious that I refused their frameworks."

"Probably." Marguerite smiled slightly. "But you saved Tancred, which the offensive network wanted. And you saved the common soldier, which the defensive network wanted. And you used distributed expertise, which is better than either network's centralized control model. So maybe you created something they'll both have to consider."

Or maybe she'd just made herself a target for both networks simultaneously.

The siege of Nicaea ended three days later when the Turks surrendered, exhausted and depleted. The crusaders had won, though at enormous cost—three thousand dead, thousands more wounded or sick, and the army's strength significantly reduced.

Both networks claimed vindication.

The defensive network pointed to their lower disease rates, their better survival statistics, their efficient use of resources.

The offensive network pointed to their victory, their maintained combat effectiveness, their strategic success despite accepting higher casualties.

Both were right.

Both were incomplete.

And Alienor, standing amid the aftermath, realized she'd made her choice without even recognizing it.

She believed in distributed specialization over centralized control. In contextual decision-making over rigid philosophy. In acknowledging that both networks had valuable insights while refusing to serve either exclusively.

She believed in independence.

Which meant both networks would now try to eliminate her.

The blood remembered.

The pattern continued.

And Generation Fifty-Five had just declared war on forty-one generations of network control.

Whether she survived that declaration remained to be seen.
