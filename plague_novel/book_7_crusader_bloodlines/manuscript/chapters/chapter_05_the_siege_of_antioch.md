# Chapter 5: The Siege of Antioch

**Timeline:** October 1097 - June 1098 CE
**Location:** Antioch, Syria
**Key Events:** Nine-month siege, famine, disease, Holy Lance discovery, Bishop Adhemar's death

---

## Part I: The Iron Winter

**October 1097**

Antioch rose before them like a judgment.

The city sprawled across the valley floor and up the mountainside, its walls stretching for miles in a circuit that seemed designed by madmen. Four hundred towers. Seven gates. The citadel perched on Mount Silpius so high that clouds touched its battlements.

Alienor stood with the other physicians on a ridge overlooking the Orontes River, watching the crusader army spread out across the plain. Forty thousand men preparing to besiege a city that could house ten times that number.

"It's impossible," Marguerite said quietly beside her. "Those walls—"

"Are twenty feet thick in places," Alienor finished. "I asked one of the Armenian guides. Built by Justinian, reinforced for eight hundred years. Never fallen to direct assault."

"Then why—"

"Because Jerusalem lies beyond it. And because both networks want to see what happens when you starve forty thousand men for months while testing their resistance to winter disease."

The blood-knowledge had been clear since Nicaea. This siege wasn't about taking Antioch. It was about the largest controlled famine experiment in network history.

Below, Count Tancred's banner moved through the camp. Alienor felt her pulse quicken—not with attraction anymore, but with the instinct of prey recognizing its hunter.

Since she'd stolen his breeding ledgers at Nicaea, he'd been searching. Politely. Carefully. With the patient determination of a man who knew his quarry couldn't run far in an army trapped before impregnable walls.

"We should move our hospital tent closer to the Bridge Gate," Sister Marguerite said. "Near Bohemond's section. Tancred commands on the opposite side—"

"He commands five hundred Norman knights who patrol everywhere," Alienor cut her off. "Geography won't save me. Only value will."

"Value?"

"I need to become so essential to the army's survival that killing me would damage both networks' experiments." Alienor turned from the view. "Which means I need to keep more crusaders alive through winter than either network expects."

Marguerite studied her. "That's not neutrality anymore."

"No," Alienor agreed. "It's independence."

The blood remembered forty-one generations of network control. But the blood didn't rule her.

Not anymore.

---

**November 1097**

The first month, they still had food.

The crusader army settled into siege positions around Antioch's southern and western walls, leaving the eastern approaches open—partly because the terrain made a complete encirclement impossible, partly because both networks wanted to observe what happened when you maintained hope of resupply while slowly withdrawing it.

Alienor recognized the experimental design. It was elegant, in its cruelty.

She established her hospital near the Bridge Gate, close enough to the fighting for quick access to wounded, far enough from the main camps to maintain independence. Marguerite helped recruit other healers—three more women with defensive network bloodlines, two monks with surgical training, and a Greek physician named Andreas who'd fled Constantinople after asking too many questions about the Copper Heart key.

"You're building a rival operation," Theophania observed one evening, visiting from the Byzantine section of camp. "The official army surgeons are complaining."

"The official army surgeons are killing patients through incompetence," Alienor replied, cleaning surgical tools in boiled wine. "Last week I watched one try to cauterize a leg wound with a red-hot sword pulled directly from a campfire. The patient died of infection two days later."

"You could teach them."

"They don't want to learn. They want to maintain their authority." Alienor set down the scalpel she'd been sharpening. "Besides, I'm not trying to compete with them. I'm trying to save enough lives that both networks notice."

"They've noticed." Theophania's tone went flat. "Brother Petros asked me yesterday if you were still 'ideologically uncommitted.' I told him you were committed to medicine."

"What did he say?"

"That medicine is never neutral. That every patient you save is a choice about who carries bloodlines forward and who doesn't."

The blood-knowledge stirred, confirming this. In a siege that would kill half the army, triage decisions were breeding decisions. Save the young knight with strong bloodlines, or the older sergeant who'd survived three campaigns? Save the nobleman's son who could pass genes to aristocratic lines, or the common soldier whose children would vanish into peasant populations?

Every choice shaped which bloodlines survived the siege.

Both networks were watching to see which pattern she'd choose.

"Then they'll be disappointed," Alienor said finally. "Because I'm choosing based on who has the best chance of survival with treatment, not whose children matter more to network breeding programs."

Theophania smiled slightly. "That's still a choice. One that will make enemies in both networks."

"I know."

Outside, the first snow began to fall.

---

**December 1097**

The cold came early and hard.

By mid-December, the Orontes River froze solid for the first time in living memory. The crusader army, which had marched from Western Europe expecting Mediterranean warmth, discovered that Syrian winters could kill as efficiently as Turkish arrows.

Alienor's hospital filled with frostbite cases.

"I'm amputating five toes a day," she told Andreas one freezing morning, her breath misting in the tent's dim interior. "We need more braziers, more blankets, more—"

"More everything," Andreas finished. "Which we don't have. The supply trains from Constantinople are three weeks overdue."

Because the Turkish garrison commander, Yaghi-Siyan, had closed the eastern approaches. The siege that was supposed to starve Antioch was starving the besiegers instead.

Alienor recognized this too. Another elegant variable in the experiment: when you starved the army faster than expected, which bloodlines proved most resilient?

The answer was becoming clear. Northern Europeans—bred for generations in cold climates—handled the winter better. Southern Italians and Provençals suffered worst. Germans and Franks maintained strength while Normans from Sicily shivered in inadequate clothing.

Both networks were documenting everything.

"We need to organize foraging parties," Alienor said. "Send men into the surrounding countryside before Turkish patrols make it impossible."

"Bohemond already tried. Lost thirty men to ambush yesterday."

"Then we send smaller groups. Two or three men, moving at night, targeting abandoned farms—"

"You're thinking like a defensive network operative." The voice came from the tent entrance.

Count Tancred stepped inside, brushing snow from his cloak.

Alienor's hand moved instinctively toward the surgical knife on her belt. Marguerite shifted position, placing herself between them.

Tancred smiled slightly. "Peace, Sister Marguerite. I'm not here to threaten anyone. I'm here because my uncle Bohemond wants to hire Lady Alienor as chief physician for the Norman contingent."

"I already serve the entire army," Alienor said carefully.

"You serve forty patients in an independent hospital. Bohemond can offer you four thousand Normans, proper facilities, and protection from..." He paused meaningfully. "Competing interests."

"Protection from you, you mean?"

"Among others." Tancred moved closer, lowering his voice. "The breeding ledgers you stole at Nicaea contained information about defensive network bloodlines too. Including Sister Marguerite's ancestry. Including the three women you recruited last month. If I wanted leverage against you, I have it."

The threat was clear. Join the offensive network's hierarchy, or watch everyone in her hospital become targets.

"And if I accept Bohemond's offer?"

"Then you're under Norman protection. Anyone who moves against you moves against the most powerful lord in the crusader army." Tancred studied her. "You'd still be independent—Bohemond doesn't micromanage his people. You'd just be independent inside a structure that keeps you alive."

"While serving offensive network goals."

"While serving Norman soldiers who'll die without competent medical care." Tancred's expression hardened. "I'm many things, Lady Alienor, but I'm not stupid enough to kill the best physician in the army during a siege that's going to slaughter half of us. Take the offer. Let both networks think you're choosing sides. Keep doing what you're doing."

"Why?"

"Because—" He stopped, reconsidering his words. "Because you were right at Nicaea. About distributed specialization. About using whoever had the right skills for each specific crisis. Three of my men are alive because of that philosophy. Including me."

"That doesn't change the breeding program."

"No," Tancred agreed. "It doesn't. The offensive network will keep engineering bloodlines. The defensive network will keep trying to stop us. And you'll keep trying to stand outside both while both try to kill you. But today, right now, I'm offering you a chance to survive winter. Take it."

Alienor looked at Marguerite, who gave the tiniest nod.

"I'll need to keep my current staff," Alienor said. "And control over all medical decisions."

"Agreed."

"And if I discover the Normans are conducting medical experiments on soldiers without consent—"

"You'll report it to Bohemond, and he'll gut whoever's responsible." Tancred extended his hand. "My uncle wants a physician, not a network operative. You have my word."

His word. The word of a man who'd admitted to honest manipulation. Who believed breeding programs were necessary. Who'd hunted her for two months.

But also a man who'd been willing to learn her philosophy. Who valued competence over ideology. Who needed her skills enough to offer genuine protection.

The blood-knowledge whispered warnings. Both networks would interpret this as choosing sides. Her independence would become harder to maintain, not easier.

But winter was here, and she couldn't save anyone if she froze to death in an inadequate hospital tent.

Alienor took his hand.

"Three months," she said. "If the siege lasts longer, we renegotiate."

"Done."

Outside, the snow fell harder.

## Part II: The Hunger Moon

**January - February 1098**

They ate the horses first.

By late January, the crusader army had consumed every mount that wasn't essential for knights or messengers. The destriers—the massive warhorses bred for battle—went last, slaughtered with ceremonies that felt like funerals.

Alienor stood in the Norman hospital compound, now a proper structure with wooden walls and braziers for heat, and watched malnutrition cases multiply.

"Scurvy," she told Bohemond during their weekly medical briefing. "Your men need fresh food. Vegetables, fruit—"

"Which don't exist." The Norman lord studied the figures she'd compiled. "How long before we start losing men to starvation rather than disease?"

"We already are. I'm listing them as 'fever' or 'flux' to avoid panic, but I lost three men last week whose bodies simply stopped. No obvious illness. They just... failed."

"Casualties?"

"Eleven dead in January. Forty-three too sick to fight. By February's end, those numbers will double."

Bohemond nodded slowly. He was a giant of a man, six and a half feet tall with pale hair and what the blood-knowledge recognized as classic Northern European genetics. Strong carrier. Bred for size and strength over generations.

"The Provençals are worse," he said. "Count Raymond lost sixty men last week. The German contingent buried forty. We're the healthiest section of the army."

"Because your bloodlines are more cold-resistant," Alienor said carefully. "Generations of Nordic ancestry, selected for survival in harsh winters."

Bohemond's eyes sharpened. "You sound like Brother Petros."

"I am Brother Petros's student. Or was. Before I decided both networks were conducting experiments I couldn't ethically support."

"And yet you're helping us survive those experiments more effectively than anyone else." Bohemond leaned back in his camp chair. "Do you know what that makes you, Lady Alienor?"

"A physician."

"A strategic asset." He smiled slightly. "Which is why Tancred was smart to recruit you before the defensive network could. Whatever philosophical objections you have to breeding programs, you're making the Norman contingent the strongest force in the army. That serves our interests regardless of your intentions."

The blood-knowledge confirmed this. She was being used. Not through manipulation or coercion, but through the simple fact that saving Norman lives advanced offensive network goals whether she wanted it to or not.

"Then we're using each other," Alienor said evenly. "You get medical expertise. I get protection and resources. But the moment this becomes about selecting which soldiers get treatment based on breeding value instead of medical need—"

"You'll walk out, and both networks will descend on you like wolves." Bohemond nodded. "Understood. Keep my men alive, Lady Alienor. That's all I'm asking."

"Even the ones with 'weak' bloodlines?"

"Even those. A soldier who can fight is worth more than breeding theory right now. We can worry about genetics after we take Jerusalem."

It was the most honest answer she'd heard from any network operative. Pragmatism over ideology, at least temporarily.

She could work with that.

"I'll need foraging parties," Alienor said. "Small groups, moving at night. There are abandoned farms within five miles that might still have stored grain or root vegetables—"

"You'll have them. Anything else?"

"Wine. As much as you can spare."

"For drinking?"

"For sterilizing surgical tools and cleaning wounds. Boiled wine kills whatever causes infection. I've tested it extensively."

Bohemond studied her. "The defensive network taught you that?"

"Genetics didn't teach me that. Observation did. A Roman surgeon sixteen generations back discovered it. The knowledge was preserved through blood memory, but anyone could have learned it through careful experimentation."

"But they didn't. Your bloodline did."

"Yes," Alienor admitted. "Which proves both networks have valuable insights. And that I don't have to join either to benefit from those insights."

"Dangerous philosophy."

"I know."

Bohemond stood, meeting ended. "Keep my nephew alive, Lady Alienor. Whatever else happens, whatever networks want, whatever breeding programs require—keep Tancred breathing. Do that, and you'll have Norman protection for as long as you need it."

The request was personal, not political. A uncle's concern, not a network directive.

"I'll do my best," Alienor said.

"That's all anyone can do."

He left. Alienor returned to her patients.

Outside, men were boiling leather harnesses to make soup.

---

**Late February 1098**

The defensive network moved first.

Brother Petros appeared in her hospital on a morning when freezing rain turned the camp to ice. He wore peasant clothing instead of monk's robes, and his face carried the hollowness of someone who'd been surviving on quarter rations.

"Generation Fifty-Five," he said quietly. "We need to talk."

Alienor finished bandaging a knight's frostbitten hand before responding. "If you're here to recruit me again—"

"I'm here to warn you. The offensive network is conducting a controlled starvation experiment in the Provençal section of camp. They're withholding food from soldiers with 'inferior' bloodlines to test survival rates under extreme deprivation."

The blood-knowledge confirmed this. She'd noticed the patterns—certain families receiving smaller rations, certain knights excluded from foraging parties. She'd assumed it was politics. Apparently it was eugenics.

"How many deaths?"

"Seventeen confirmed. Possibly more that were listed as other causes." Petros moved closer, voice dropping. "They're accelerating. Bishop Adhemar discovered the program and threatened to expose it. Three days ago, someone poisoned his food."

"The Bishop is—"

"Dying. Not dead yet, but it's only a matter of days. They made it look like camp fever." Petros's expression was carved stone. "When he dies, the offensive network loses its primary opposition in the army's leadership. They'll expand the program. More controlled experiments. More culling."

"And you want me to—"

"Stop them. You have Norman protection. Access to Bohemond. Evidence, if you look. The breeding ledgers Tancred kept." Petros gripped her arm. "You wanted independence. This is what it costs. Standing up. Choosing."

"Choosing your side, you mean."

"Choosing to save lives without conditions." His eyes burned. "Isn't that what you believe? That medical care shouldn't be based on breeding value? Then prove it. Expose the program. Force Bohemond to stop it."

The blood-knowledge whispered complications. If she exposed the offensive network's program, she'd make permanent enemies among the most powerful lords in the army. Norman protection wouldn't help—Bohemond himself was probably complicit.

But if she stayed silent, seventeen deaths would become a hundred. A thousand. The entire siege would become a culling operation disguised as holy war.

"I need evidence," Alienor said finally. "Real evidence, not accusations."

"Then get it. You have access to the Norman records now. Find the breeding ledgers. Document the pattern. Bring it to Count Raymond—he's dying but still lucid. He can confront Bohemond before Bishop Adhemar—"

A noise at the tent entrance cut him off.

Tancred stood in the doorway, water dripping from his cloak. His expression was unreadable.

"Brother Petros," he said mildly. "I didn't realize defensive network priests made house calls."

Petros straightened. "I was visiting the sick. That's permitted, even for network operatives."

"Certainly. Though usually the sick are lying in beds, not standing next to the physician having secret conversations." Tancred stepped inside, hand resting on his sword hilt. Not threatening. Just present. "What brings you to the Norman compound?"

"Spiritual counsel."

"For Lady Alienor? How kind." Tancred smiled slightly. "Though I should mention that spiritual counsel in the Norman camp requires my uncle's permission. Security concerns. I'm sure you understand."

The message was clear: leave, now, before this becomes formal.

Petros looked at Alienor. "Think about what I said. Some choices can't be avoided."

He left.

Tancred waited until his footsteps faded before speaking. "He's not wrong, you know. About the experiments. About Bishop Adhemar. About what happens when he dies."

Alienor stared at him. "You're admitting—"

"I'm admitting I know what the offensive network is doing because I help coordinate the breeding records." Tancred moved to the brazier, warming his hands. "Seventeen deaths. Controlled selection. Testing who survives extreme deprivation. Exactly as Petros said."

"And you're—"

"Conflicted." He turned to face her. "Philosophically, I believe the experiments are justified. We need to know which bloodlines carry the strongest survival traits. But practically, I think we're moving too fast. Killing soldiers during a siege we might lose seems strategically stupid."

"So you'll stop it?"

"No. I'm a junior member of the network. I don't have that authority." He studied her. "But you do. If you found evidence and brought it to Raymond or the other independent lords, they could force Bohemond to halt the program—at least until Jerusalem falls."

"Why are you telling me this?"

Tancred was silent for a long moment. "Because I told you at Nicaea that I hedge my bets. The offensive network might be right about breeding being necessary. But we might also be wrong. And if we're wrong, having someone like you—someone who believes in treating everyone regardless of bloodline—might be what saves humanity when collapse comes."

"That's defensive network philosophy."

"I know." He smiled slightly. "Told you. Hedging bets. Keep me alive if I'm wounded. I'll give you the access you need to investigate the program. Whatever you decide after that is your choice."

He left.

Alienor stood in the empty hospital, listening to rain hammer the roof.

Two network operatives. Two opposing philosophies. Both offering her the chance to choose sides by investigating the same program.

Both networks had just given her the rope to hang herself with, wrapped in different colors.

The blood-knowledge offered no guidance. Forty-one generations of ancestors had never faced this exact situation. She was alone.

So she did what she always did: she made rounds, checked patients, and kept people alive.

Tomorrow she'd decide about breeding programs and evidence and exposing atrocities.

Tonight, she had work.

---

## Part III: The Breaking Point

**March - April 1098**

Bishop Adhemar of Le Puy died on March 7th, 1098, officially of camp fever.

Alienor stood in the crowd watching his funeral, recognizing the symptoms Petros had described. Not fever. Poison. Slow-acting, designed to mimic disease. Elegant murder.

The offensive network had just removed its primary opposition.

Two days later, the controlled starvation program expanded.

Alienor discovered it while treating a young Provençal knight named Guillaume who'd collapsed during patrol. Extreme malnutrition, worse than camp averages. When she checked his ration records, she found systematic reduction over six weeks.

Then she found twenty more soldiers with identical patterns.

All from families the breeding ledgers marked as "inferior stock."

"You're going to confront Bohemond," Marguerite said when Alienor showed her the evidence. It wasn't a question.

"I'm going to ask for an explanation. Maybe there's a logistical reason—"

"There isn't." Marguerite's voice was flat. "It's eugenics. The offensive network is using starvation to cull bloodlines they don't want. You know this."

"I need to hear it from them."

"Why? So you can confirm what you already know? So you can feel morally justified before choosing sides?" Marguerite moved closer, voice urgent. "Alienor, the defensive network is right. This is exactly what we've warned about for generations. They're killing people based on breeding theory. You have to stop them."

"By joining your network?"

"By standing up for your principles! You said you believe in treating everyone equally. Prove it."

The blood-knowledge stirred with ancestral pressure. Forty-one generations of defensive network breeding, all pushing her toward this choice. Stand up. Speak out. Expose the atrocity.

But she remembered Constantinople. The Copper Heart vision showing her forty-one generations of conflict between the networks. Neither side purely right. Neither purely wrong. Both using any crisis to advance their philosophy at others' expense.

If she exposed the offensive network's program, she'd be defending defensive philosophy—even if she agreed with the principle. If she stayed silent, she'd be complicit in murders—even if she understood the offensive logic.

There was no neutral option.

So she went to Bohemond.

---

The Norman lord received her in his command tent, surrounded by maps and casualty reports. Count Raymond was there too, dying of wounds received during a skirmish three weeks prior. The old Provençal's presence meant this was formal.

"Lady Alienor," Bohemond greeted her. "I understand you've been reviewing ration records."

"I have." She laid out the evidence on his campaign table. "Twenty soldiers systematically underfed over six weeks. All from families your breeding records mark as inferior bloodlines. Seventeen died. Three survived. I want to know why."

Silence stretched. Raymond studied the papers, expression darkening. Bohemond remained impassive.

"It's a controlled experiment," Bohemond said finally. "Testing genetic resilience under extreme conditions. The data is valuable for understanding which bloodlines carry the strongest survival traits."

"It's murder," Raymond growled. "You're killing soldiers we need to take Jerusalem."

"We're documenting which soldiers are worth keeping in the bloodline." Bohemond's tone stayed level. "These men would have died anyway—starvation, disease, battle. We're simply controlling the variables to learn from their deaths."

"Except they wouldn't have died if you'd given them full rations," Alienor said. "You're not documenting natural survival rates. You're manufacturing deaths to prove your breeding theory."

"Yes." Bohemond didn't flinch. "Because breeding theory is correct. Bloodlines matter. Some families produce stronger, smarter, more resilient offspring than others. Understanding which traits correlate with survival lets us guide humanity toward better genetics. That's not murder. That's civilization."

"It's the same logic every tyrant uses," Raymond spat. "We know better. We're improving humanity. For the greater good." The old count struggled to stand, weakness from his wounds showing. "I'll take this to the other lords. They'll force you to stop—"

"They won't." Bohemond's certainty was absolute. "Half the army leadership is defensive network. Half is offensive. This will split the crusade right when we need unity to take Antioch. Is that what you want, Raymond? Civil war before we even reach Jerusalem?"

"Better civil war than systematic murder."

"Maybe. Or maybe you trigger a conflict that kills thousands instead of dozens, fractures the crusade, and ensures we all die here while achieving nothing." Bohemond turned to Alienor. "You brought this evidence. You're the neutral party both networks claim to respect. What's your judgment?"

The question was a trap. Whatever she said would be used against her by whichever network she opposed.

But it was also genuine. Bohemond was giving her the choice he'd promised: examine the situation, reach her own conclusion, let that guide her decision.

"My judgment," Alienor said slowly, "is that you're both right and both wrong."

Raymond stared at her. "That's not—"

"The offensive network is right that bloodlines matter. Generations of breeding have produced real differences in survival traits. I see it every day—Northern Europeans handle cold better, certain families resist disease more effectively, some bloodlines heal faster than others. That's genetic reality, not ideology."

Bohemond nodded slightly.

"But," Alienor continued, "the offensive network is wrong that those differences justify letting people die when they could be saved. Because bloodlines aren't destiny. The young Provençal knight I treated—Guillaume—survived your starvation test despite being 'inferior stock.' He's still weak, but he's alive and recovering. Which means your theory about who's worth saving is incomplete."

"One exception doesn't disprove—"

"It disproves your methodology. You can't learn which traits predict survival if you manipulate the variables to produce predetermined results. That's not science. It's confirmation bias wrapped in breeding theory." Alienor met Bohemond's eyes. "The defensive network is right that every life has value regardless of bloodline. Right that we should preserve knowledge and try to prevent collapses rather than engineering who survives them. I agree with those principles."

Raymond looked relieved. Marguerite, watching from the tent entrance, smiled.

"But," Alienor said again, "the defensive network is wrong that preventing collapses is always possible. I've seen the blood-knowledge. Forty-one generations of civilizations falling. Rome, Classical Greece, Bronze Age kingdoms. The pattern repeats. Maybe the offensive network is right that breeding programs are necessary because collapse is inevitable."

"So what's your judgment?" Bohemond pressed.

"Stop the controlled starvation. It's wasting soldiers we need and producing bad data. If you want to study genetic survival traits, document what happens naturally during the siege—we'll have more than enough death to analyze without manufacturing it. Keep breeding records, track bloodlines, study the patterns. But stop killing people to prove your theory."

She turned to Raymond. "And you—don't tear the crusade apart over this. The offensive network will document bloodlines whether you approve or not. Better to monitor what they're doing than drive them underground where you can't see it."

Raymond looked like he'd swallowed poison. "You're asking me to tolerate eugenics."

"I'm asking you to choose battles you can win. You can't stop the offensive network from studying genetics. But you can stop them from actively killing soldiers during a siege. Take that victory. Monitor the program. Document abuses. But don't start a civil war that ensures we all die here."

Silence.

Bohemond studied her for a long moment. "You're proposing compromise."

"I'm proposing survival. Both networks get something. Both networks give something. And maybe—maybe—we learn from each other instead of just fighting for control."

"That's not how this works," Marguerite said from the doorway. "The networks have been opposed for forty-one generations. You can't just—"

"Then maybe forty-one generations have been wrong." Alienor's voice hardened. "Maybe the reason we keep failing to prevent collapses is because we spend more energy fighting each other than solving problems. Maybe both philosophies have insights, and refusing to learn from each other makes both networks weaker."

Bohemond and Raymond exchanged glances. In that moment, Alienor saw the burden both men carried: network loyalties warring with practical necessities. Ideology confronting reality.

"I'll halt the controlled program," Bohemond said finally. "Natural documentation only. No manufactured deaths. But I'm keeping the breeding records. If you find that intolerable—"

"I find it acceptable," Raymond interrupted, looking exhausted. "Barely. But acceptable." He turned to Alienor. "You've just made enemies in both networks, you know. The defensive side will think you're too sympathetic to offensive philosophy. The offensive side will think you're too protective of 'inferior' bloodlines."

"I know."

"And you're still choosing this path?"

"I'm choosing to keep as many people alive as possible while both networks figure out I'm not going to help either achieve total victory." Alienor managed a slight smile. "Seemed better than the alternatives."

Raymond actually laughed—weak, painful, but genuine. "You're either brilliant or suicidal. I can't decide which."

"Probably both," Bohemond said dryly. He extended his hand to Raymond. "Truce. Monitor each other. No civil war. Agreed?"

Raymond took his hand. "Agreed."

They sealed the compromise that would let both networks continue their work while preventing immediate atrocity.

Alienor left the tent knowing she'd just bought herself enemies on both sides.

But also knowing she'd stopped seventeen deaths from becoming a hundred.

That seemed worth it.

---

**May 1098**

The food ran out completely in late May.

By the first week, soldiers were eating grass. By the second, they were boiling leather boots. By the third, whispers of cannibalism drifted through the camp—never confirmed, never quite denied.

Alienor's hospital became a triage center for starvation. She had nothing to offer except water and false hope.

"I lost six men yesterday," she told Andreas one evening. "Not from wounds. Not from disease. From hunger. Their bodies just... stopped."

"It's worse in the Provençal section. They're burying twenty a day." Andreas looked skeletal himself—he'd been giving half his rations to patients. "How long can this continue?"

"Until Antioch falls or we all die trying."

But Antioch wasn't falling. The walls held. Turkish sorties harassed foraging parties. Winter had broken but spring brought no relief. The besiegers were becoming the besieged, trapped before impregnable walls while Yaghi-Siyan waited for them to starve.

Then, on June 2nd, 1098, everything changed.

A minor nobleman named Peter Bartholomew claimed to have received a vision. God, he said, had shown him the location of the Holy Lance—the spear that pierced Christ's side during crucifixion. It was buried beneath the Church of St. Peter inside Antioch.

Most dismissed it as starvation-induced madness.

But Count Raymond, dying and desperate for divine intervention, ordered an investigation.

And Bohemond, recognizing a military opportunity when he saw one, negotiated with a Turkish officer named Firouz to open the city's Tower Gate for a bribe.

On June 3rd, 1098, the crusaders took Antioch.

Not through siege. Through bribery and betrayal.

Alienor was in the hospital when the shouts began. Within an hour, Norman soldiers were pouring through the opened gate. Within three hours, they controlled half the city. Within six, Antioch had fallen.

Yaghi-Siyan fled and was killed. Turkish defenders retreated to the citadel. Forty thousand crusaders flooded into a city that had resisted them for eight months.

The massacre began immediately.

Alienor spent the next three days in what had become an endless surgical rotation. Soldiers brought Turkish wounded to her hospital—not for treatment, but for interrogation about food stores. When she tried to help them, she was ordered to focus on crusaders only.

"They're human beings," she protested to a knight who'd dragged away a wounded Turkish boy who might have been twelve.

"They're the enemy. We need to know where they hid the food."

"He's a child—"

"With information."

The boy died before revealing anything. So did dozens more.

The blood-knowledge showed her similar massacres across forty-one generations. Rome sacking Carthage. Athens destroying Melos. Barbarians burning Alexandria. The pattern repeated. Starving armies slaughtered cities. Always had. Always would.

But knowing the pattern didn't make watching it easier.

On June 5th, a new Turkish army appeared outside Antioch.

Kerbogha of Mosul, commanding a massive relief force, surrounded the city the crusaders had just captured. The besiegers became the besieged again—this time trapped inside Antioch's walls with no food, no supplies, and no hope of relief.

"We're going to die here," Marguerite said that evening, watching Turkish forces deploy across the plain. "Forty thousand men, no food, surrounded by enemies who remember what we did when we took the city."

"Maybe," Alienor agreed. "Unless someone finds that Holy Lance Peter Bartholomew claimed God revealed."

"You think it's real?"

"I think men who believe in divine weapons fight harder than men who think they're already dead. Whether it's real doesn't matter. Whether they believe matters."

On June 10th, excavators dug beneath the Church of St. Peter.

On June 14th, Peter Bartholomew emerged from the dig claiming to have found the Holy Lance.

The crusader army exploded with renewed faith.

Alienor never saw the lance—she was treating patients when it was displayed. But she saw the effect it had on soldiers who'd been ready to surrender. They stood straighter. Spoke about victory instead of survival. Prepared for battle as if they believed they could win.

The offensive network called it mass delusion.

The defensive network called it divine intervention.

Alienor called it the power of belief to change outcomes—which was its own kind of miracle.

On June 28th, 1098, the crusader army marched out of Antioch to face Kerbogha's vastly superior force.

They should have been slaughtered.

Instead, they won.

---

## Part IV: The Aftermath

**July 1098**

The victory at Antioch made no sense.

Alienor reviewed casualty figures in the aftermath, trying to understand how a starving, outnumbered crusader force had routed Kerbogha's professional army. The defensive network credited the Holy Lance. The offensive network credited superior Northern European bloodlines. The historians credited Turkish disunity and tactical errors.

All were probably partially right.

"Six thousand crusader casualties," she told Bohemond during their first medical briefing after the battle. "Four thousand dead, two thousand wounded. But we inflicted twenty thousand Turkish casualties and broke their siege. Militarily, it's inexplicable."

"Militarily, we were desperate and they weren't," Bohemond replied. "Desperate men fight harder than professional soldiers with a safe retreat route. But you're right—we should have lost." He paused. "Both networks are claiming the victory proves their philosophy. The defensive side says faith and moral righteousness won. The offensive side says genetic superiority won. What do you say?"

"I say luck won. Kerbogha's coalition was fragile. Turkish princes didn't trust each other. Our timing caught them unprepared. We got lucky."

"That's not very inspirational."

"It's honest. Which is more useful than inspiration if we're trying to reach Jerusalem." Alienor set down her casualty reports. "Speaking of which—when do we march?"

"We don't. Not yet."

She stared at him. "We just won the most improbable victory in the crusade. The army's morale is at its peak. If we don't move now—"

"The army needs months to recover from starvation. We need to secure Antioch's defenses. And—" Bohemond's expression went carefully neutral, "—the lords are negotiating about who gets to keep Antioch. Whether it becomes a crusader state or gets returned to Byzantium. That's going to take time."

Politics. While men starved for eight months to take the city, lords were arguing about who owned it.

"How long?"

"Months. Maybe through winter. We'll march for Jerusalem in spring."

"Men will desert. They came for Jerusalem, not to watch you squabble over territory."

"I know. But they're too weak to march now anyway." Bohemond leaned back. "Use the time. Rest your staff. Document what you learned during the siege. Both networks will want reports."

"I'm not giving reports to either network."

"Then document it for yourself. Medical techniques that worked. Genetic patterns you observed. Survival traits. Whatever you want to remember about what we just survived."

It was good advice. And it gave her an excuse to avoid choosing sides while both networks regrouped after Antioch's chaos.

"I'll compile records," Alienor agreed. "Neutral medical documentation. Available to any physician who wants to learn from it."

"That's a defensive network approach—preserving and distributing knowledge."

"It's a physician's approach. Which happens to overlap with defensive philosophy on this topic."

Bohemond smiled slightly. "You're still trying to stand in the middle."

"I'm still succeeding at standing in the middle. Both networks compromised at Antioch. The controlled starvation stopped. You documented bloodlines without manufacturing deaths. Raymond monitored your records without starting civil war. It worked."

"This time. Because we were too desperate to afford conflict." His expression hardened. "But we're heading to Jerusalem now. Both networks want the city for different reasons. The defensive network sees it as a knowledge repository—Roman, Jewish, Christian, Islamic texts in one place. The offensive network sees it as the largest bloodline mixing experiment in history—three religions, dozens of ethnic groups, genetic diversity at scale. When we reach Jerusalem, the compromise ends. Both networks will push for total control. Where will you be then?"

The blood-knowledge whispered warnings. Jerusalem was Generation Zero. Where Tausret's bloodline split after the Bronze Age collapse. Where both networks traced their origins. Where the Bronze Hand key had been hidden since 70 CE.

Jerusalem wasn't just another city. It was the fulcrum point for forty-one generations of network conflict.

"I'll be in the hospital," Alienor said finally. "Treating patients. Staying neutral. Surviving."

"That won't be enough."

"Then I'll improvise. Like I did at Constantinople. Like I did here. Like I've been doing since Clermont."

Bohemond studied her for a long moment. "You're either the bravest person I've met or the most naive."

"Probably both," Alienor echoed his earlier assessment. "Like independence itself."

She left his tent and walked through Antioch's scarred streets, past buildings still smoking from the massacre, past Turkish bodies waiting for burial, past crusaders celebrating victory while knowing Jerusalem waited ahead.

The blood remembered forty-one generations leading to this moment.

The pattern continued.

And Generation Fifty-Five was running out of time to decide what she believed about bloodlines, breeding, collapse, and whether any philosophy justified the cost of survival.

But not today.

Today, she had patients to treat, records to compile, and an army to help recover from eight months of siege that should have killed them all.

Tomorrow's impossible choices could wait.

**Historical Note:**

The Siege of Antioch (October 1097 - June 1098) is one of the most brutal episodes in crusade history. Casualty estimates vary, but most historians agree that the crusader army lost roughly 40% of its strength to starvation, disease, and battle. The Turkish relief force under Kerbogha should have annihilated the weakened crusaders but was defeated due to a combination of Turkish disunity, crusader desperation, and possibly tactical errors.

The Holy Lance discovery is historical but controversial. Peter Bartholomew claimed divine revelation; skeptics suggested he planted it. Regardless, its discovery dramatically affected crusader morale.

Bishop Adhemar of Le Puy did die at Antioch in August 1098 (I've moved this to March for narrative purposes), and his death created a power vacuum in crusader leadership that contributed to delays in the march toward Jerusalem.

The eight-month siege represents the largest sustained population under extreme conditions in medieval history and provides a unique window into medieval survival rates, genetics, and human resilience—which both networks in this narrative would absolutely have documented.
