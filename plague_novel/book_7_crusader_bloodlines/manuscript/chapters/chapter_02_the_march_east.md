# CHAPTER 2: THE MARCH EAST

## Raymond de Toulouse's Camp, Southern France - April 1096 CE

Spring came late that year, wet and cold. The army gathered like a slow flood—first a trickle of knights and their retainers, then a steady stream of common soldiers, finally a deluge of pilgrims, merchants, prostitutes, thieves, and every desperate soul who saw crusade as escape from their ordinary miseries.

By the time Alienor arrived with her medical supplies loaded on three pack mules, Raymond de Toulouse's camp sprawled across three miles of French countryside. Fifteen thousand people, perhaps more. Impossible to count accurately when new arrivals appeared hourly and deserters slipped away in the night.

"Sweet Mother of God," Jehanne whispered, staring at the chaos. "It's a city. A city that moves."

Alienor had to agree. She'd grown up in Apulia's Norman settlements, lived in Salerno for her medical training, visited cities from Rome to Marseille. But she'd never seen anything like this—a complete human settlement assembled from nothing in just five months, organized around a single mad purpose.

Jerusalem.

The word rippled through the camp like fever. Old men whispered it like a prayer. Young knights shouted it like a battle cry. Prostitutes promised it would wash away sins. Priests preached it from makeshift pulpits erected in muddy fields.

And underneath all the religious fervor, Alienor sensed the same calculated direction she'd felt at Clermont. This wasn't spontaneous chaos. This was controlled. Managed. Orchestrated toward specific ends by hands that remained hidden.

"There." Tancred pointed toward the center of camp, where better pavilions clustered around Count Raymond's banner—a golden cross on crimson field. "The noble quarter. Your pavilion will be near the hospital tents, but I've arranged for you to take meals with the knights' households. Can't have my betrothed eating with common soldiers."

"I'm a physician." Alienor kept her voice level, though irritation prickled. "I should be eating with the other healers, learning who has what skills, coordinating our medical efforts."

"You're a countess-to-be." Tancred's tone was gentle but firm. "You have a position to maintain."

Since their betrothal had been formally announced in February, he'd become subtly more controlling. Nothing she could object to openly—he framed everything as protecting her reputation, ensuring her safety, maintaining her dignity. But the net was closing nevertheless.

She was property now. Valuable property that needed careful management until it could be properly claimed after his triumphant return from Jerusalem.

The thought made her want to hit him.

Instead, she smiled sweetly. "Of course. Though I'll still need to work in the hospital tents. Wounds and disease won't wait for social niceties."

"Naturally." Tancred gestured for his men to lead her mules toward the medical quarter. "I've assigned four guards to you. They'll escort you anywhere you need to go in camp."

*Or keep me from going anywhere you don't approve,* Alienor thought. But she nodded graciously, playing the dutiful betrothed while her mind catalogued escape routes and potential allies.

She'd been doing that since leaving Apulia. Watching. Learning. Preparing.

Because in the six months since Clermont, her blood memory had grown stronger. Not the overwhelming visions that had struck her at Pope Urban's speech, but steadier, more controlled access to ancestral knowledge. She'd learned to quiet her mind, let the memories surface, learn from lives lived centuries before her birth.

And what she'd learned terrified her.

The networks—defensive and offensive, as Brother Petros had named them—weren't just old. They were ancient. Older than Christianity. Older than Rome. The visions showed her Egyptian temples, Greek mystery cults, Jewish priesthoods, all somehow connected to the same source. The same split that had happened so long ago that even the blood memory couldn't show her clearly.

One network trying to preserve knowledge through catastrophes. The other trying to control who survived those catastrophes.

Both convinced they were saving humanity.

Both willing to sacrifice millions to achieve their goals.

And crusade—this enormous, chaotic, beautiful, terrible gathering of Christian Europe—was the largest opportunity either network had seen in centuries. A hundred thousand people mixing bloodlines across the continent. Disease testing at massive scale. Population reduction disguised as holy war. Knowledge-seeking pilgrimage masking strategic positioning.

The perfect storm for both networks to advance their agendas.

Alienor had to understand which was which before she chose sides.

If she chose sides at all.

---

## Hospital Tents, Medical Quarter - That Evening

The medical quarter occupied the camp's downwind section, where the stench of infected wounds and dysentery wouldn't reach the noble pavilions. Fifteen large tents served as hospital wards, with smaller tents for physicians, surgeons, midwives, and the various healers who'd attached themselves to the army.

Alienor was examining her assigned tent—basic but serviceable, with a cot, a chest for her supplies, and a brazier for warmth—when someone knocked on the tent pole.

"Come in."

A woman entered—mid-thirties, sturdy build, wearing a nun's habit so travel-stained and patched that it had clearly seen months of hard use. Her face was plain, weather-worn, but her eyes were intelligent and kind.

"Sister Marguerite," she introduced herself in heavily accented Norman French. "From Toulouse. I heard we had another woman physician joining us. Thought I'd welcome you properly before the men scare you off with their chest-beating."

Alienor liked her immediately. "Alienor de Hauteville. Though I'm not technically a physician—I trained at Salerno but never sought formal licensing. The Church doesn't generally approve of women physicians."

"The Church doesn't approve of much that's useful." Marguerite settled onto the spare stool without asking permission, comfortable in her own skin in a way Alienor envied. "But out here, away from bishops and their rules, we do what needs doing. You studied surgery?"

"Surgery, midwifery, herbal medicine. My teachers believed in comprehensive training."

"Your teachers were wise." Marguerite pulled out a wineskin, took a drink, offered it to Alienor. "You'll need all three disciplines. We haven't even left France and already I've treated two dozen infected wounds, delivered three babies, and set more broken bones than I can count. Men get drunk and fight each other. Women give birth in ditches. Children fall off wagons and break limbs. And that's before we face actual battle."

Alienor accepted the wine—rough but drinkable. "How many physicians does the army have?"

"Counting us? Fourteen. Mostly barber-surgeons and herbalists. A few priests who studied healing. One actual university-trained physician who treats only nobles and charges accordingly." Marguerite's tone suggested her opinion of that arrangement. "We're nowhere near enough for fifteen thousand people. When disease hits—and it will hit—we'll be overwhelmed."

"When, not if?"

"Always when." Marguerite's weathered face was grim. "I went on pilgrimage to Jerusalem fifteen years ago. Got as far as Constantinople before turning back. Half the pilgrims I traveled with died of flux before we crossed into Byzantine lands. Out here, away from cities and settled farms, with poor water and inadequate food, living in tents..." She shook her head. "Disease will kill more crusaders than Muslims ever will."

The certainty in her voice made Alienor's blood sing warning. This woman had seen it before. Knew the patterns. Could teach Alienor what to expect.

Could be a valuable ally.

Or could be working for one of the networks, positioned to watch Alienor and report back.

*Trust no one,* Petros had warned.

"Then we'll need to organize," Alienor said carefully. "Coordinate care. Establish protocols for treating common ailments before they become epidemics."

"Good luck with that." But Marguerite smiled. "Though if anyone can impose order on this chaos, it's probably a Salerno-trained woman who's too stubborn to realize it's impossible. I like you, Lady Alienor. We're going to be friends."

"Just Alienor. And I hope you're right."

They spent the next hour discussing medical logistics, comparing training, sharing techniques. Marguerite had fifteen years of practical experience that compensated for her lack of formal education. Alienor had theoretical knowledge that needed tempering with real-world application.

Together, they made a formidable team.

It was near midnight when Marguerite finally left, promising to introduce Alienor to the other healers in the morning. Alone, exhausted, Alienor collapsed onto her cot without bothering to undress fully.

Sleep came quickly.

The vision came quicker.

---

*She stood in a different tent, in a different army, in a different century. Roman legions this time, not crusaders. And the woman in the surgeon's tent looked like her—same height, same build, same dark eyes—but wore Roman clothing and spoke Latin with an Italian accent.*

*"The fever's spreading," the Roman surgeon told a centurion. "Third Legion lost forty men yesterday. If we don't quarantine—"*

*"The general wants the men ready to march." The centurion's face was hard. "We can't delay for illness. The weak will die. The strong will survive. That's how Rome has always done things."*

*"That's how Rome creates epidemics that kill entire legions." The surgeon's voice was urgent. "I've seen this pattern before. My mother saw it. Her mother saw it. Every generation, the same mistake—treating disease as if it's divine judgment instead of something we can prevent."*

*"You sound like a Christian." The centurion's lip curled. "Next you'll be claiming prayer heals better than surgery."*

*"I'm claiming knowledge heals better than ignorance. And the knowledge to prevent this epidemic exists. We're choosing not to use it because your general prioritizes speed over survival."*

*The centurion left without responding. The surgeon—Alienor's ancestor, Generation Forty-Something, she couldn't tell exactly—turned to her medical supplies, her face set in grim determination.*

*"They won't listen," she whispered. "They never listen. But we document anyway. We preserve. We teach the next generation. And eventually, eventually, enough knowledge accumulates that even generals can't ignore it."*

*The vision shifted. The same woman, older now, dying. Passing a bronze medallion to her daughter—the same medallion Petros had given Alienor.*

*"The blood remembers," the dying surgeon whispered. "Even when empires forget. The blood remembers. Teach your daughter. Make her teach hers. The pattern continues. The knowledge must survive."*

---

Alienor woke gasping, the medallion burning against her skin where she wore it on a chain beneath her clothing.

Blood memory. Stronger than ever before. Showing her not just images but full experiences—the frustration of that Roman surgeon, the certainty that knowledge existed to prevent suffering but was being ignored for military expediency.

The same pattern repeating.

And suddenly Alienor understood what Brother Petros had meant about defensive and offensive networks.

The defensive network wasn't trying to control humanity. It was trying to preserve the knowledge to prevent catastrophes—medical knowledge, agricultural knowledge, engineering knowledge. Everything civilizations needed to survive their own complexity.

The offensive network wasn't evil either. It was pragmatic. It accepted that catastrophes were inevitable, that civilizations would collapse, that the best you could do was ensure the "right" people survived to rebuild.

Two responses to the same horror. Two strategies for human survival.

Prevention versus management.

Knowledge versus selection.

And both networks using crusade to advance their goals, while a hundred thousand ordinary people caught between them marched toward Jerusalem dreaming of redemption, unaware they were pieces in a game that had been playing for centuries.

Alienor pressed her hand to the medallion. Three symbols: cross, star, crescent.

Three faiths.

But also, she suddenly realized, three branches of the same network. Christian, Jewish, Muslim—all descended from the same source, all preserving the same knowledge, all trying to prevent the same collapses.

The defensive network had survived Roman persecution, survived empire's fall, survived by fragmenting across religious lines and adapting to each era's dominant power structure.

And the offensive network had done the same.

Which meant both networks had members in the crusader camp. Christian knights and priests. But also Jewish merchants traveling with the army. Muslim guides and translators. All of them operating according to agendas that had nothing to do with liberating Jerusalem and everything to do with positioning for the next collapse.

The thought was dizzying.

It was also terrifying.

Because if both networks were here, both watching her, both knowing she was Generation Fifty-Five (whatever that meant), then she was a target. A prize. A piece both sides wanted to capture.

She needed to find Brother Petros. Needed answers before she made a wrong move and ended up dead in a ditch with her death written off as the kind of "tragic accident" that plagued armies.

But Petros had told her to wait until they were established in camp, away from immediate attention. To be patient.

Patience was hard when her blood was screaming warnings.

---

## The March Begins - Two Weeks Later, Late April 1096

Raymond de Toulouse's army finally departed France on April 28th, 1096.

Fifteen thousand people moving in a column that stretched for miles. Knights and their retainers in the front, marching in some semblance of order. Common soldiers behind them, less organized but trying. Then the civilians—pilgrims, merchants, families, prostitutes, thieves—a chaotic mass that transformed orderly military march into wandering human migration.

And at the rear, the baggage train. Wagons full of supplies, pack animals laden with equipment, hospital carts for the wounded and sick.

Alienor rode with the medical contingent, which put her near the column's end where dust was worst and pace slowest. Tancred had argued for her to ride with the noble ladies closer to the front, but she'd insisted on staying with the healers.

It was one of the few arguments she'd won.

Marguerite rode beside her, keeping up a steady stream of commentary that ranged from medical to theological to bawdy, depending on who was in earshot. The nun had a gift for reading people, for knowing exactly what to say to put them at ease or challenge their assumptions.

Alienor suspected Marguerite was smarter than she pretended. Much smarter.

Which made her either a valuable ally or a dangerous plant.

"First day's always chaos," Marguerite observed as they watched three wagons collide in a traffic jam. "Give it a week. By then the weak will have deserted, the stupid will have died doing stupid things, and the survivors will have learned how to march without killing each other. Probably."

"You're optimistic."

"I'm realistic. About half these people won't make it to Constantinople. Disease, desertion, accidents, bandits, stupidity—we'll lose thousands before we even reach Byzantine lands."

"And that doesn't bother you?" Alienor kept her voice carefully neutral, testing.

Marguerite was quiet for a moment. "It bothers me that it's preventable. Most of the deaths, anyway. But preventing requires organization, discipline, listening to physicians instead of commanders. And commanders always prioritize speed over survival."

The same thing the Roman surgeon had said in Alienor's vision.

The same pattern repeating.

"What if there were people trying to organize?" Alienor asked slowly. "Trying to preserve knowledge about preventing epidemics, treating disease, saving lives that would otherwise be lost?"

"Then they'd be fighting against a thousand years of ignorance." But Marguerite's eyes were sharp. "Why? You know of such people?"

"I've heard rumors." Alienor chose her words carefully. "Networks of scholars. Physicians. Teachers. People who preserve medical knowledge across generations."

"The physician's guild?" Marguerite's tone was skeptical. "They preserve knowledge, sure. But they also restrict it—won't teach women, won't treat the poor, charge nobles fortunes for basic care. If that's your network of saviors, I'm not impressed."

"What if it was different from the guilds? Older. More... distributed."

Marguerite studied her for a long moment. Then, so quietly Alienor almost missed it: "Which symbol did they give you? Cross, star, or crescent?"

Alienor's hand moved instinctively to her chest, to where the medallion hung beneath her clothing.

Marguerite saw the gesture and smiled sadly. "All three, then. You're Generation Fifty-Five."

The world tilted.

"You—" Alienor's voice came out strangled. "You're—"

"Not a carrier. Wrong bloodline." Marguerite kept her voice low, casual, as if they were discussing the weather. "But my mother was. Generation Fifty-Seven. She died bringing me into the world, but before that she taught me to watch for the signs. Certain families. Certain markers. Blood that remembers."

"Why didn't you tell me?" Accusation sharpened Alienor's voice. "You've been watching me for two weeks—"

"And you've been watching everyone, trying to figure out who's defensive and who's offensive, smart as your blood makes you." Marguerite's tone was gentle. "I waited to see if you'd approach me. Test whether you're as paranoid as you should be. Turns out you are. That's good. Paranoia keeps carriers alive."

Alienor's mind raced. "Are you defensive network?"

"I'm a nun who takes care of people because that's what Jesus actually preached, before the Church made it about power and gold." Marguerite shrugged. "The defensive network found me when I was twenty, after my mother died. They said I had the gift for healing, even without the blood memory. Said I could help preserve their knowledge, teach the next generation, be part of something larger. So I took vows and learned everything I could."

"To help prevent the next collapse."

"To help people survive the next collapse." Marguerite corrected. "I'm not convinced collapse is preventable. Civilizations fall. Rome fell. Before that Egypt, Greece, Babylon. It's what civilizations do when they get too complex to sustain themselves."

"Then what's the point?" Frustration leaked into Alienor's voice. "If collapse is inevitable, if we can't prevent it, why preserve the knowledge?"

"Because when Rome fell, they lost everything. Engineering, medicine, agriculture, governance—all gone. Took six hundred years to rebuild what the Romans knew in the first century. Next time civilization collapses, maybe we don't lose everything. Maybe we keep the knowledge alive through the dark ages. Maybe the next Rome rebuilds in two hundred years instead of six hundred."

"That's still defensive network thinking."

"Which is why I work with them." Marguerite's face was serious. "The offensive network thinks you can engineer who survives. Control the bloodlines, optimize the genetics, ensure the 'right' people rebuild. Maybe they're right. Maybe selective breeding produces better survivors. But I've delivered too many babies—high-born and low, genius and fool—to believe any of us can predict which bloodline will produce the next Marcus Aurelius versus the next Nero. So I choose preservation over selection."

Alienor digested this. Around them, the army continued its chaotic march. Knights shouted orders. Common soldiers sang bawdy songs. Pilgrims prayed. And hidden among them, two networks played their eternal game.

"Brother Petros," Alienor said quietly. "You know him?"

"Byzantine defensive network coordinator. Good man. Brilliant scholar. Terrible chess player." Marguerite smiled slightly. "He asked me to watch over you, make sure you didn't do anything stupid like trust Count Tancred or eat food from unmarked sources or wander off alone where offensive network operatives could arrange accidents."

"Tancred is offensive network." It wasn't a question.

"House of Hauteville has been offensive network since the Norman conquest of Sicily. They've been selectively breeding for military prowess, strategic thinking, cold pragmatism for four generations. Your betrothed is exactly what they're trying to produce—brilliant commander, ruthless when necessary, charming when useful."

"And they want to breed me to him."

"They want your defensive bloodline mixed with their offensive genetics. See if they can produce carriers who serve offensive goals instead of defensive ones. It's an experiment. You're the subject."

Nausea rose in Alienor's throat. "That's—"

"Disgusting? Yes. Effective? Also yes. Breeding programs work, Alienor. That's the horrible truth both networks dance around. You can selectively breed humans for desired traits the same way you breed horses for speed or dogs for hunting. The question isn't whether it works. The question is whether it's morally acceptable to do it."

"It's not."

"The offensive network disagrees. They've been doing it for—" Marguerite hesitated. "—longer than I can count. Since before Rome. Since before Egypt. Since the first time humans realized knowledge passed through bloodlines."

"How long?"

"Ask Petros. He knows the full history." Marguerite's voice dropped even lower. "But Alienor? Be careful how much you trust even him. Defensive network is still a network. Still uses people as tools. Still sacrifices individuals for collective survival. They're better than the offensive network—I believe that—but they're not innocent. Nobody who plays this game stays innocent."

Before Alienor could respond, shouting erupted ahead.

The column lurched to a halt. Riders raced back along the line, spreading news:

"Disease in the forward camps! Fever! Twenty men down!"

Alienor's blood went cold.

*When, not if,* Marguerite had said.

Disease had arrived.

And both networks would be watching to see who survived.

---

## Forward Camp - That Night

The fever tent stank of sweat and waste. Twenty-three men lay on rough pallets, burning with fever, vomiting blood, some already delirious.

Alienor moved among them with Marguerite and two barber-surgeons, checking symptoms, offering what comfort they could.

"Flux," one of the surgeons pronounced. "Common enough. Kill the weak ones, but most will recover."

"This isn't common flux." Alienor studied the rash spreading across one soldier's chest. "The blood in the vomit, the speed of onset, the rash pattern—this is something else."

"What?" Marguerite was taking pulses, noting which men had the fever worst.

"I don't know. I've never seen it before." Alienor's blood memory stirred, showing her fragments—Roman soldiers with similar symptoms, Byzantine physicians arguing about treatments, Jewish healers in Jerusalem's markets mixing remedies—but nothing clear enough to identify the disease or suggest a cure.

"Then we treat symptoms and hope." Marguerite's voice was grim. "Keep them hydrated. Control the fever as we can. Quarantine them from the healthy. Hope they're strong enough to fight through."

They worked through the night. By dawn, five men had died. Three more were clearly dying. The rest seemed to be stabilizing, though whether they'd recover fully or relapse later remained uncertain.

Alienor emerged from the fever tent at sunrise, exhausted and filthy, her hands stained with blood and worse.

Tancred waited outside.

"How bad?" His face was set in careful neutrality.

"Five dead. Three dying. Fifteen might survive." Alienor stripped off her stained gloves. "We need to quarantine anyone who shows symptoms. Establish clean water sources. Stop sharing cups and bowls. Basic hygiene protocols—"

"That will slow the march." Tancred's objection was immediate. "Count Raymond wants to reach Germany by May. Any delay means we arrive at Constantinople late in the season, which pushes the Jerusalem campaign into next year."

"Disease doesn't care about schedules."

"Soldiers do. Morale matters. If we start treating this march like a funeral procession, men will desert." Tancred's voice was reasonable, but his eyes were calculating. "We can't save everyone, Alienor. Some will die. That's war. We have to accept losses and keep moving."

It was exactly what the Roman centurion had said in her vision.

The same pattern.

The same choice.

And suddenly Alienor understood this was a test. Both networks watching to see what she'd do. Would she argue for prevention, for saving lives, for slowing the march to establish quarantine protocols? Or would she accept pragmatism, accept necessary losses, prioritize the mission over individual survival?

Would she choose defensive or offensive network thinking?

Or would she find a third way?

"You're right," she said slowly. "We can't save everyone. But we can save some. And saving some is better than saving none."

"Meaning?"

"Meaning I'll treat the sick without requiring army-wide quarantine. I'll establish hygiene protocols for the medical tents that don't impact the march. I'll train assistant healers to recognize early symptoms so we can isolate cases before they spread." Alienor met his eyes steadily. "And in return, you don't interfere with my medical decisions. You don't override my treatments. You don't sacrifice my patients for military expediency unless there's no other choice."

Tancred studied her for a long moment. Then, surprisingly, he smiled. "You're more clever than I thought. Compromise instead of confrontation. Pragmatism instead of idealism."

"I'm a physician in an army. Pragmatism is survival."

"So it is." He stepped closer, lowering his voice. "The offensive network is right about one thing, Alienor. The world requires hard choices. Civilization requires sacrifice. We can save everyone or accomplish nothing. Sometimes we have to choose who lives and who dies."

"And you think breeding programs give you the wisdom to make those choices?"

His smile widened. "I think forty-one generations of selective breeding has produced bloodlines with the intelligence and strength to survive catastrophe. Whether you call that wisdom or just superior genetics, the result is the same. My children—our children—will be among the survivors when the next Rome falls."

"Unless I choose not to marry you."

"You'll marry me." No threat in his voice, just certainty. "Because delaying our marriage was smart politics, but refusing it entirely would destroy your family's standing. You're too clever to let emotion override survival."

He was right. And that was perhaps what frightened Alienor most—that the offensive network's logic was compelling. That selective breeding did work. That engineering survival was pragmatic.

That the defensive network's idealism might be beautiful but unsustainable.

She needed to talk to Petros. Soon.

Before she made choices she couldn't unmake.

"I need to sleep," Alienor said, stepping back. "I've been awake for thirty hours."

"Of course." Tancred's mask of solicitousness slipped back into place. "Rest. You've done excellent work. Count Raymond has noticed your dedication. He speaks highly of you."

That should have pleased her. Instead, it felt like another net closing.

She made her way back to her tent, Marguerite falling into step beside her.

"Well?" The nun kept her voice low.

"He's testing me. Seeing which way I'll lean."

"And?"

"And I don't know." Alienor's exhaustion was soul-deep. "Both networks have valid points. Both are wrong in different ways. I don't know how to choose."

"Then don't choose yet." Marguerite squeezed her arm. "Petros will find you soon. He's waiting for the right moment—when we're far enough from France that offensive network can't easily eliminate you, close enough to Germany that defensive network resources can reach you if needed. Probably another week."

"How do you know?"

"Because I know Petros. And I know how this game is played." Marguerite's weathered face was sad. "You're Generation Fifty-Five. That means something specific. When you find out what, when Petros explains the full history, you'll understand why both networks want you so badly."

"What does it mean?"

"That you're the strongest carrier in four generations. That your blood memory is clearer than anyone's since your great-great-grandmother. That you have the potential to unite the fractured defensive network—or destroy it if you choose the offensive side instead." Marguerite paused. "That you're the most important piece on the board. And both sides will kill to possess you or prevent the other side from having you."

Alienor absorbed this. "So I'm dead either way."

"No. You're valuable either way. As long as both sides think they can win you, they'll keep you alive." Marguerite smiled grimly. "Welcome to the game, Generation Fifty-Five. Try not to die before you understand the rules."

She left Alienor standing outside her tent, the dawn light revealing the vast encampment spreading across the French countryside. Fifteen thousand people marching toward Jerusalem. Two networks playing for control. One woman caught between them.

And ahead, months of marching through foreign lands, facing disease and battle and politics and intrigue, with networks on all sides trying to claim her.

Alienor touched the medallion beneath her clothing.

Cross. Star. Crescent.

Three faiths. Two networks. One choice she'd have to make eventually.

But not today.

Today, she'd sleep. Tomorrow, she'd heal the sick. And when Brother Petros finally revealed himself, when she learned the full history of forty-one generations, when she understood what Generation Fifty-Five truly meant...

Then she'd decide which network deserved her loyalty.

If either did.

The blood remembered.

The pattern continued.

And Alienor de Hauteville, Generation Fifty-Five, took another step toward Jerusalem and the destiny waiting there.
